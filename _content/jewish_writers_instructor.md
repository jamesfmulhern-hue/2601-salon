## Preface — How to Use This Book

Dear Mr. Mulhern,

This companion is built to sit on the desk beside you in the hour before each class, and to be read more slowly in the days before that. It is not a script you must follow, and it is no substitute for your own reading of the primary texts, which remain the only indispensable preparation. It is, rather, a working scaffold: a place where the biography, the history, the close-reading, and the likely shape of the room have already been laid out so that your attention in the week of teaching can go where it belongs — into the texts and into the people in front of you.

Each session mirrors the others in structure: an "At a Glance" orientation, a biographical sketch long enough to teach from, historical and cultural context, the readings with freely available sources where they exist, a lecture script in your voice for the opening ten to fifteen minutes, close-reading notes on specific passages, discussion questions with model answers, a note on anticipated difficulties, further reading for you, and one line worth memorizing. Use what helps; cross out what doesn't. The lecture scripts in particular are meant to be spoken, revised, abandoned mid-sentence if the room takes you somewhere better.

A word on tone, because it matters more in this course than in almost any other you teach. You will most likely be standing in a largely non-Jewish room — adult learners, curious, generous, and in many cases encountering this literature for the first time as literature rather than as religion or as headline. The temptation in such a room runs two ways. One temptation is to flatten the material into universal humanism — "these are stories about the human condition" — which is true and which also quietly erases the specific Jewishness that is the whole point of the course. The other temptation is to overcorrect into solemn reverence, to treat every text as sacred and untouchable, which kills discussion and patronizes both the writers and the students. The path between is to treat this as what it is: a great literary tradition, particular in its languages and its griefs and its jokes, fully available to outsiders willing to learn its idiom. You can be warm and exacting at once. Assume intelligence. Explain the Yiddish word and move on.

Three of these sessions — Primo Levi, Paul Celan, and Cynthia Ozick (Sessions 8, 9, and 13) — bring the Shoah directly into the room. Handle them with a different weight. Do not open those classes with a joke. Build in silence; allow it. Tell students in advance, at the end of the preceding session, what is coming, so that no one is ambushed. Resist the urge to "teach through" the discomfort too quickly; some of these texts are designed to resist consolation, and your job is partly to protect that resistance from the room's natural wish to feel better. There are notes on this in each of those sessions.

As for the order: it is roughly chronological by the world each writer inhabited, beginning with the biblical bedrock, moving through the Yiddish-speaking world of Eastern Europe, into the great immigrant wave to America, then into European modernism and the catastrophe that consumed it, back across the Atlantic to the postwar American Jewish renaissance, and finally to modern Israel and our own contemporaries. The arc is meant to let students feel a tradition *moving* — carried in different languages, broken and re-made, comic and devastated by turns — rather than a list of names. Trust the arc. By Session 16 they will hear the echoes themselves.

With respect and with confidence in your hands,

*— The Companion*

## Recurring Threads Across the Course

It will help you, and your students, to name a small number of threads at the outset and then pull on them deliberately week after week. The pleasure of a course like this — the thing that turns sixteen separate authors into a single tradition in the students' minds — comes from recognition: *we have seen this before, and now it has changed.* Below are six threads worth tracking aloud. You need not raise all six every week, but you should know where each runs.

**Exile.** This is the oldest thread and the one that touches everything. The Jewish literary imagination is, to an unusual degree, an imagination of displacement: from Eden, from Jerusalem, from the shtetl, from Europe, and even — in the American and Israeli writers — an inner exile within apparent arrival. Watch how the *meaning* of exile shifts. For Emma Lazarus it is something America can heal; for Kafka it is a metaphysical condition with no homeland to return to; for Celan it becomes exile even from one's own mother tongue, which is also the murderers' tongue. Ask the students, late in the term, whether exile in this tradition is ever fully cured, or only transformed.

**The text as homeland.** A people repeatedly stripped of land learned to carry its home in a book. This is not a metaphor the writers invented; it is a fact of Jewish history — the portable Torah, the Talmud studied in a hundred countries, the idea (which you can attribute to George Steiner's famous formulation) that "the text is the homeland." You will see it literally in Session 1, where the biblical texts are themselves the foundation. You will see it again in Singer, who keeps a vanished Poland alive on the page, and in Amichai, who writes a modern secular Hebrew that is nonetheless thick with three thousand years of liturgy. Keep asking: where does this writer locate home? Often the honest answer is *in the writing itself.*

**The Shoah and after.** The murder of six million European Jews falls across the middle of this course like a shadow that reaches both backward and forward. The writers before it (Sholem Aleichem, Lazarus) were writing a world that would be destroyed; we read them now knowing what they did not. The writers of the catastrophe itself (Levi, Celan, Schulz, who was murdered) confront the central question of whether such suffering can or should be made into art at all — Adorno's much-quoted line about poetry after Auschwitz hovers here. The writers after (Ozick, and the contemporaries) inherit a trauma they did not personally live, and must decide what to do with it. This is the gravest thread. Treat it gravely.

**Comedy and survival.** And yet — this is crucial, and students are often surprised by it — Jewish literature is one of the great comic traditions in world literature. The laughter is not the opposite of the suffering; it is a response to it, a technology of survival. Sholem Aleichem's Tevye argues with God and makes us laugh while his world collapses. Singer's Gimpel is a fool who turns out to be wise. Bellow, Roth, and Paley are funny on nearly every page. Teach the students that the joke and the grief are not in different rooms in this tradition; they share a bed. Comedy here is a form of stubborn dignity.

**Who belongs to a tradition?** This is a live and contested question, and your course quietly stages it. Is a "Jewish writer" one who writes in Yiddish or Hebrew? One who keeps the commandments? A secular atheist born to Jewish parents, like much of the American canon? A writer like Kafka, who barely engaged with Judaism explicitly and yet seems to so many readers profoundly Jewish in sensibility? There is no settled answer, and you should not pretend to one. Let the students argue it. By the capstone they should see that the tradition is defined less by a checklist than by a set of recurring pressures, questions, and inheritances.

**The role of women.** Finally, track gender deliberately, because the tradition's classical center of gravity — the study house, rabbinic learning — was historically male, and the women in this course write partly against that. Yezierska gives voice to immigrant women crushed between old-world patriarchy and new-world poverty. Lazarus writes as a woman into a male literary establishment. Ozick and Paley are major artists who also think hard, in different keys, about what it means to be a woman in this inheritance. Ask whose voices the classical tradition left out, and how these writers answer.

Carry these six threads lightly but persistently. They are the rope that ties the sixteen weeks together.

## Session 1 — Welcome & The Tradition: Ecclesiastes 3 and Psalm 23

### At a Glance
We begin not with an author but with the source: two of the most quoted passages in the Hebrew Bible, read here as literature and as the bedrock every later writer in the course draws upon. The session establishes the central premise — that this is a tradition carried in texts — and gives students a shared vocabulary (covenant, exile, lament, wisdom) for the weeks ahead. The aim is to make the familiar strange again: to read "To everything there is a season" and "The Lord is my shepherd" as the strong, ancient poems they are.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

For this first session there is no single biography to recount, and that absence is itself the lesson. The Hebrew Bible is the work of many hands across many centuries, and the two texts before us — the third chapter of Ecclesiastes and the twenty-third Psalm — come out of distinct streams of ancient Israelite literature. What you can teach the students about authorship here is itself a doorway into how the tradition understands the relationship between a writer, a community, and a text.

*Ecclesiastes* is one of the books of *Ketuvim*, the "Writings," the third division of the Hebrew Bible after the Torah and the Prophets. In Hebrew the book is called *Qohelet*, a word usually rendered "the Preacher," "the Teacher," or "the Assembler" — one who gathers a congregation. By long tradition the book was attributed to King Solomon, the son of David, famed for wisdom; the opening verse calls its speaker "the son of David, king in Jerusalem." Modern scholarship, as the Encyclopaedia Britannica and standard reference works note, dates the book much later, most often to the post-exilic period, perhaps the third century BCE, on the evidence of its late Hebrew and its philosophical preoccupations ([Britannica, "Ecclesiastes"](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ecclesiastes)). For our purposes the attribution to Solomon is best taught as a literary and theological convention rather than a historical claim: the wisdom tradition liked to speak in the voice of its wisest king. *Qohelet* belongs to the international genre of ancient Near Eastern "wisdom literature," alongside Proverbs and Job — texts concerned not with law or prophecy but with how to live, and with the limits of human understanding.

*Psalm 23* belongs to the Psalter, the Book of Psalms — in Hebrew *Tehillim*, "praises" — the great hymnbook and prayerbook of ancient Israel and of Jewish and Christian worship ever since. Many psalms carry a superscription associating them with David, the shepherd-boy who became king; Psalm 23 is among the "Psalms of David." Here again, scholars treat Davidic authorship as traditional ascription rather than documentary fact, with the Psalter assembled and edited over a long span. What matters for the classroom is that the Psalms are *lyric* — first-person address, often to God — and that they have supplied the Western tradition with its most enduring vocabulary of comfort, complaint, and praise.

The text most of your students will arrive carrying in their ears is the King James Version of 1611, and you should honor that. The KJV is not the oldest or the most accurate English Bible, but it is one of the supreme monuments of English prose, and its cadences — "To every thing there is a season"; "Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death" — are woven into the language Shakespeare's near-contemporaries spoke and into every English-language writer who followed. Part of your work in this first session is to let students hear two things at once: the ancient Hebrew poem underneath, and the magnificent English surface the KJV gave it. Where the Hebrew is terse and concrete, the KJV is stately; comparing even a phrase or two against a modern Jewish translation — Robert Alter's, for instance — reveals how much interpretation lives inside every "literal" rendering ([Robert Alter, *The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary*, Norton, 2018](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Alter)).

A note on the Hebrew names of God, which will recur all term. The Psalm's "the LORD," printed in small capitals in most English Bibles, stands for the four-letter divine name, the *Tetragrammaton* (YHWH), which observant Jews do not pronounce, substituting *Adonai* ("my Lord") or *HaShem* ("the Name"). This is not trivia; it tells students something fundamental about a tradition in which the holiest word is the one you do not say, and in which reverence takes the form of restraint. You will return to this reticence — the unsayable, the gap, the silence — again and again, most painfully in the Shoah poets.

The legacy of these two short texts is incalculable. Ecclesiastes 3 alone has supplied a Byrds song, countless funerals, and a thousand essays on time; Psalm 23 is very likely the single best-known passage of the Bible in English, spoken at deathbeds and gravesides across the world. Beginning here tells the students that the tradition they are about to study does not start in a vacuum or in the nineteenth century — it starts in poems three thousand years deep, and every writer ahead is, in some sense, still answering them.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The Hebrew Bible took shape across roughly a thousand years of ancient Israelite and early Jewish history — a history of a small people repeatedly caught between empires (Egypt, Assyria, Babylon, Persia, Greece, Rome) and repeatedly displaced. The single most formative event behind much of the Hebrew Bible's final form is the Babylonian Exile of the sixth century BCE, when Jerusalem and its Temple were destroyed and the Judean elite were carried off to Babylon. Exile — the loss of land, Temple, and political sovereignty — is therefore not a theme imposed on this literature from outside; it is the crucible in which much of it was forged and edited. When students later meet exile in Singer, Kafka, and Celan, they are meeting a preoccupation older than any of them.

Out of that history came a revolution with no parallel in the ancient world: a people that learned to locate its identity not in a place or a king but in a *text* and the practice of reading it. The destruction of the Second Temple by Rome in 70 CE accelerated this turn. With sacrifice no longer possible, Judaism became a religion of study, interpretation, and the synagogue — of the book carried into every land. This is the deep background to one of our recurring threads, "the text as homeland." You can tell students that the entire later edifice of Jewish literature — the Talmud, the medieval commentators, the Yiddish and Hebrew literatures of the modern age — rests on this foundational act: a community deciding that its center of gravity would be a library.

Wisdom literature like Ecclesiastes reflects a particular cultural moment, probably under Persian or Hellenistic rule, when Jewish thinkers were in conversation with the wider intellectual currents of the ancient Near East and the Greek world. *Qohelet*'s bracing skepticism — its insistence that "all is vanity," that the wise and the foolish meet the same end — is startlingly secular in tone, and it sits inside the canon as evidence that the tradition could hold doubt and faith in the same hand. The Psalms, by contrast, come largely out of the world of Temple worship, the liturgical life of ancient Jerusalem, and preserve the full emotional range of that worship: ecstatic praise, bitter complaint, and the quiet trust of Psalm 23.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Ecclesiastes 3 (KJV).** The chapter opens with the famous catalogue of times: "To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven" (3:1). Verses 2–8 list fourteen paired opposites that govern human life — a time to be born and to die, to plant and to pluck up, to kill and to heal, to break down and to build up, to weep and to laugh, to mourn and to dance, to cast away stones and to gather them, to embrace and to refrain, to get and to lose, to keep and to cast away, to rend and to sew, to keep silence and to speak, to love and to hate, a time of war and of peace. Koheleth ("the Preacher") then asks what profit a worker gains from his toil (3:9). He concludes that God makes everything beautiful in its time and has set "the world" (or eternity) in human hearts, yet humans cannot grasp God's work from beginning to end (3:11). The good, he says, is to rejoice, eat, drink, and enjoy the fruit of one's labor as God's gift (3:12–13). God's work endures forever; nothing can be added or taken away; God acts so that people will fear Him (3:14). The chapter closes darkly: men and beasts share one breath and one death, all return to dust, and no one knows whether the human spirit rises (3:19–21). Therefore one should rejoice in present work, "for that is his portion" (3:22).

**Psalm 23 (KJV).** A six-verse psalm of trust. "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want" (23:1). God makes the speaker lie down in green pastures and leads him beside still waters, restoring his soul and guiding him "in the paths of righteousness for his name's sake" (23:2–3). Even walking "through the valley of the shadow of death," the speaker fears no evil, because God is with him; the rod and staff comfort him (23:4). God prepares a table before him "in the presence of mine enemies," anoints his head with oil, and his cup overflows (23:5). The psalm ends in confidence: "Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life: and I will dwell in the house of the LORD for ever" (23:6).

### Lecture Script

Good evening, and welcome. Before I tell you anything about this course, I'd like to read you something, and I'd like you to close your eyes if you're comfortable doing that, because I want you to hear it before you see it. [pause]

"To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven: A time to be born, and a time to die; a time to plant, and a time to pluck up that which is planted." [pause]

You know this. Almost everyone in this room knows this, even those of you who have never opened a Bible — you've heard it at a funeral, or in a song, or in a film. And that's exactly where I want to begin, because the whole adventure of this course depends on our being willing to take something we think we already know and hear it again, as if for the first time, as the strong and strange poem it actually is. [pause]

We are going to spend sixteen weeks with Jewish writers — with Sholem Aleichem and Kafka, with Primo Levi and Cynthia Ozick, with poets who wrote in Yiddish and Hebrew and German and English. They lived in shtetls and in Prague and in Auschwitz and in Newark and in Jerusalem. They are wildly different from one another. But they all, every one of them, draw water from the well we are standing beside tonight. And the thing about this well — this is the first and maybe the most important idea I'll give you all term — is that for the Jewish people, the *book* became the homeland. A people that kept losing its land learned to carry its home in a text. Tonight we look at the foundation of that text. [pause]

Now, who wrote these words? The honest answer is: we don't entirely know, and that's instructive. Ecclesiastes is traditionally attributed to King Solomon, the wise king, the son of David. Psalm 23 is traditionally a Psalm "of David," the shepherd-boy who became king — and notice that it's a shepherd's image, "The Lord is my shepherd," coming from the boy who actually kept sheep. But scholars will tell you these attributions are later, that Ecclesiastes especially comes from centuries after Solomon, in its skeptical, almost weary Hebrew. I don't want you to file that away as a debunking. I want you to notice what it tells us: that this tradition was comfortable speaking *in the voice of* its wisest figures, that authorship here is communal and layered, a thing built up over centuries. [pause]

Here's the anecdote I want you to hold. There is a word in Hebrew, the holiest word, the four-letter name of God — and observant Jews do not say it aloud. They will not pronounce it. When you see "the LORD" in small capital letters in your Bibles, that's standing in for a name too holy to speak, and the reader substitutes another word. Think about that for a moment. The most sacred word in the language is the one you keep silent. [pause] Hold onto that, because by the time we reach the poets who tried to write about the Holocaust, we will find them circling the same thing — the unsayable, the word that can't be spoken — and you'll feel the line running all the way back to here. [pause]

So tonight let's read these two short texts slowly. Let's notice the rhythm of Ecclesiastes, that great pendulum swinging between birth and death, and let's ask what kind of comfort — or what kind of unease — it actually offers. And let's sit inside the green pastures and the valley of the shadow of Psalm 23 and ask why these particular words have outlasted empires. Open your texts. Let's read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven" (Ecclesiastes 3:1).** Notice the architecture: a general thesis, then fourteen paired opposites unspooling beneath it. What to bring out: the form *enacts* its meaning — the relentless symmetry of "a time to... and a time to..." mimics the turning of seasons it describes. Ask students whether the catalogue is consoling or fatalistic. There is no escape clause; even "a time to kill" sits in the list, unjudged. This is wisdom literature's cool eye.

**2. "He hath made every thing beautiful in his time: also he hath set the world in their heart, so that no man can find out the work that God maketh from the beginning to the end" (Ecclesiastes 3:11).** A famously difficult verse — the Hebrew translated "the world" (*olam*) can also mean "eternity." What to notice: the ache built into being human — we are given a sense of the eternal but denied the ability to comprehend it. This is *Qohelet*'s central wound. Bring out how modern this sounds: meaning withheld, the human as the creature who knows there is a pattern but cannot read it.

**3. "The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want" (Psalm 23:1).** Pure assurance, stated as bare fact. What to notice: the metaphor's intimacy — God not as king or judge but as the keeper of sheep, humble and protective. The Hebrew is spare; the KJV's "I shall not want" carries an old sense of "want" as *lack*. Discussion: how does the poem earn this confidence before the dark valley arrives?

**4. "Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil: for thou art with me" (Psalm 23:4).** The turn. Note the pronoun shift — the psalm moves from speaking *about* God ("he maketh me," "he leadeth me") to speaking *to* God ("thou art with me"). What to bring out: the deepest comfort coincides with the most direct address; in the valley, the speaker stops describing and starts talking *to* God. That grammatical intimacy is the whole consolation.

**5. The two together.** Set Ecclesiastes' "a time to die" beside the Psalm's "valley of the shadow of death." What to notice: two ancient Jewish answers to mortality — one steady-eyed and almost stoic, one trusting and tender. Tell students they have just met, in miniature, the tonal range the whole course will travel: clear-eyed grief and stubborn comfort, often in the same writer.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why begin a course on Jewish *writers* with anonymous, ancient, sacred texts?**
   *Model answer:* Because every writer ahead inherits this language and these preoccupations — exile, covenant, lament, the holiness of words. Beginning here reframes the Bible as literature and gives us a shared vocabulary. It also introduces our master-thread: the text as homeland.

2. **Is Ecclesiastes 3 comforting or bleak? Can it be both?**
   *Model answer:* It is both, deliberately. The orderly seasons can console (everything has its place) or unsettle (we are swept along by forces we don't control). Wisdom literature refuses to resolve the tension, which is precisely its honesty.

3. **What does it mean that the holiest name of God is unspoken?**
   *Model answer:* It locates reverence in restraint rather than utterance, and it builds silence into the heart of the tradition. This reticence before the ultimate will return, transformed, in the Shoah poets who find ordinary language inadequate to their subject.

4. **The Psalm shifts from "he" to "thou" in verse 4. What does that grammatical move accomplish?**
   *Model answer:* It dramatizes intimacy arriving exactly when it is most needed. In the valley the speaker stops describing God and starts addressing him directly; the comfort is enacted in the grammar, not just stated.

5. **What is gained and lost in reading the King James Version rather than a modern translation?**
   *Model answer:* Gained: incomparable prose music and the version most embedded in English literature. Lost: precision and access to the Hebrew's concreteness. Comparing a verse against Alter's translation shows that every translation is already an interpretation.

6. **Why has Psalm 23 in particular outlasted empires and become the default text of grief in the English-speaking world?**
   *Model answer:* Its imagery is universal and humble (shepherd, water, table), its movement carries the reader from green pastures through death's valley to a house of dwelling, and its first-person voice lets anyone step inside it. It offers comfort without denying the darkness.

7. **What does "wisdom literature" mean, and how is Ecclesiastes different from law or prophecy?**
   *Model answer:* Wisdom literature concerns how to live and the limits of human understanding, not divine commands or predictions. Ecclesiastes is reflective, skeptical, almost philosophical — evidence that the canon could contain doubt as well as faith.

8. **If a people can lose its land but keep its book, what does that suggest about the relationship between literature and survival?**
   *Model answer:* It suggests literature can become a portable homeland and a technology of continuity. This is the founding insight of the whole tradition and the reason a course of *readings* can constitute a people's living memory.

9. **Solomon and David are traditional, not historical, authors. Does that diminish the texts?**
   *Model answer:* No — it tells us authorship here is communal and layered, a tradition speaking through its representative voices. The texts' authority comes from the community's long use of them, not from a single signature.

10. **What tone should we expect for the rest of the term, based on these two texts?**
    *Model answer:* A range that holds clear-eyed grief and stubborn comfort together, often within one writer. That doubled tone — facing the worst while refusing despair — is the tradition's signature, and we have just heard its first notes.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Some students will carry strong religious associations — devotional, or wounded, or hostile — and may find it strange to treat scripture "as literature." Acknowledge this openly and gently: we are reading these as the foundational texts of a literary tradition, which honors rather than diminishes them, and people of any faith or none are welcome to the conversation. Others will know these passages only as clichés (the funeral psalm, the Byrds song) and may resist taking them seriously. The cure is slow reading aloud. A few will be unfamiliar with the structure of the Hebrew Bible (Torah / Prophets / Writings) or the convention of the unspoken divine name; define these once, plainly, without making anyone feel ignorant.

## Session 2 — Sholem Aleichem: A Tevye Sketch

### At a Glance
This week we meet the comic genius of the Yiddish-speaking world and the character who would one day become *Fiddler on the Roof*: Tevye the dairyman, who argues with God in the language of the marketplace and keeps his dignity while everything he loves is taken from him. The session introduces the shtetl, the Yiddish language, and the tradition's astonishing fusion of laughter and loss. The goal is for students to laugh — and then to feel the floor shift beneath the laughter.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Sholem Aleichem was the pen name of Solomon Naumovich Rabinovich, born in 1859 in Pereiaslav, in the Ukrainian provinces of the Russian Empire, and raised in the nearby shtetl of Voronkov, which would later furnish the model for his fictional town of Kasrilevke ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Sholem Aleichem"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sholem-Aleichem); [YIVO Encyclopedia](https://yivoencyclopedia.org/article.aspx/Sholem_Aleichem)). The name he chose for himself is itself a teaching point: *sholem aleichem* is the everyday Yiddish (and Hebrew) greeting, "peace be upon you," the words one Jew says to another on the road. To publish under the name of a greeting is to declare oneself the people's familiar, the voice of the ordinary Jew — and Yiddish writers commonly used pseudonyms in part because writing in the "jargon," as Yiddish was condescendingly called, was not considered respectable for an educated man.

He grew up steeped in the languages and pressures of Jewish Eastern Europe. Like most boys of his world he was educated in the traditional religious system — the *kheyder* (elementary religious school) and Talmud study — but he also received a secular Russian education, and he came of age inside the *Haskalah*, the Jewish Enlightenment, which urged Jews toward secular learning and modern languages. He was multilingual in the characteristic way of his class: Yiddish was his mother tongue and the language of the folk; Hebrew was the sacred and literary language; Russian was the language of the surrounding empire and of secular advancement. He began writing in Hebrew and Russian before making the decisive choice to write in Yiddish — the despised vernacular of the masses — and to make it a literary language of the first rank.

His early adulthood reads like one of his own plots. He worked as a private tutor, fell in love with his pupil Olga, and after some years married her; when her wealthy father died, Sholem Aleichem inherited a fortune and promptly lost much of it through unlucky speculation on the Kiev stock exchange — an experience that fed his lifelong fascination with *luftmenschen*, "men of air," dreamers and schemers with no solid ground beneath them. Out of financial necessity and literary ambition he turned to writing in earnest, producing an enormous body of work: stories, novels, plays, and feuilletons. He effectively used his own funds to underwrite Yiddish literature, founding a literary annual and paying serious rates to other writers, helping to dignify the whole enterprise. Scholars rightly place him alongside Mendele Mocher Sforim (whom he called the "grandfather" of Yiddish literature) and I. L. Peretz as one of the three "classic" writers who founded modern Yiddish letters.

His greatest creations are characters who speak. Tevye the dairyman — *Tevye der milkhiker* — is the supreme example: a poor, pious, talkative milkman in a Ukrainian village whose monologues, addressed half to the narrator "Sholem Aleichem" and half to God, carry the reader through the marrying-off of his daughters and the slow disintegration of his world. Tevye misquotes scripture gloriously, bending sacred verses to fit his troubles, and argues with the Almighty as with a difficult business partner. The other great creation is Menachem-Mendl, the eternal speculator, and the boy narrator Motl, the cantor's son. What unites them is the voice — the *mouth* of the Jewish folk, rendered with such fidelity that reading Sholem Aleichem in Yiddish is, by all accounts, like overhearing one's own family.

History pressed hard on his last years. The wave of pogroms that swept the Russian Empire, especially after 1905, drove him, like millions of his readers, toward emigration. He left for the West, made an unsuccessful first attempt to settle in America, returned to Europe, and was caught there by the outbreak of the First World War, which trapped him as a Russian subject in Germany; he fled again to neutral Denmark and then, in 1914, to New York. He died there in 1916, in the Bronx, not yet fifty-seven, having never fully recovered his health or his fortune. His funeral was one of the largest New York had ever seen — by many accounts well over a hundred thousand mourners filled the streets — a measure of how completely he had become the beloved voice of a transplanted people ([YIVO Encyclopedia](https://yivoencyclopedia.org/article.aspx/Sholem_Aleichem)).

His legacy is double. He is the great comic chronicler of a civilization that would, within a generation of his death, be largely annihilated in the Shoah — which gives his laughter, read now, an almost unbearable poignancy. And he is, improbably, a figure of world fame, because the Tevye stories became the Broadway musical and film *Fiddler on the Roof* (1964; film 1971), which carried his shtetl into the imagination of millions who had never heard the word "shtetl." You should be candid with students that *Fiddler* both preserved and sentimentalized him: it gave the world Tevye, but a sweeter, more nostalgic Tevye than the sharp, grieving, God-arguing original. Reading the sketch returns them to the real thing.

### Historical & Cultural Context

To read Sholem Aleichem you must give students the *shtetl* and the *Pale of Settlement*. The Pale was the vast western region of the Russian Empire — taking in much of present-day Ukraine, Belarus, Lithuania, Poland, and Moldova — to which Jews were legally confined by the tsarist state from the late eighteenth century until the empire's collapse. Within the Pale, Jews lived in cities and, characteristically, in *shtetlekh* (singular *shtetl*), the small market towns whose population was substantially Jewish and whose rhythms were set by the Jewish calendar, the synagogue and study house, the market, and the surrounding non-Jewish peasantry. Life was poor, precarious, intensely communal, and saturated in religious observance and learning. It is the world of Tevye, and you should resist both sentimentalizing it (it was hungry and hard) and pitying it (it was rich in language, faith, and intellectual life).

The pressures on that world in Sholem Aleichem's lifetime were enormous and they all appear, lightly veiled, in the Tevye stories. The *Haskalah* and secularization were pulling young people away from tradition. Revolutionary and Zionist movements were drawing them toward new politics. Above all, official antisemitism and recurrent *pogroms* — violent, often state-tolerated mob attacks on Jewish communities — made life in the Pale increasingly untenable, triggering the great migration of roughly two million Eastern European Jews to America between 1881 and 1924. Tevye's daughters embody these forces: one marries for love against custom, one follows a revolutionary to Siberia, one marries outside the faith, and at the end Tevye himself is expelled from his village by decree. The comedy is real; so is the catastrophe gathering at its edges.

Yiddish itself deserves a moment. It is a fusion language — a Germanic core written in Hebrew characters, layered with Hebrew-Aramaic (the languages of sacred study) and Slavic elements — that was the everyday tongue of Ashkenazi Jewry for a thousand years. In Sholem Aleichem's hands it became a supple literary instrument capable of the full human range. Telling students this prepares them for later sessions: the Yiddishism of Singer, the Yiddish substratum under Bellow's and Roth's English, the loss that the Shoah inflicted on the language itself.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**A Tevye sketch — "Tevye Strikes It Rich" (the opening episode).** The story is a monologue addressed by the poor dairyman Tevye to "Pani Sholem Aleichem," laced with Tevye's own misquoted and creatively (mis)applied scriptural tags. Tevye begins with his philosophy: if you're meant to strike it rich, luck will find you at home in your slippers — neither brains nor brawn has anything to do with it. He then narrates the event that set him up in the dairy business. Years ago he was desperately poor, hauling logs for a pittance to feed his wife Golde and their many daughters. One evening, driving his half-dead horse home through the woods near Boiberik (the summer-dacha colony of rich Yehupetz Jews), the horse bolts and then halts, and Tevye sees two ghostly figures. Terrified they are demons, he recites prayers, only to discover they are two flesh-and-blood women — a mother and daughter — who have wandered off and gotten hopelessly lost during a walk. Exhausted, they beg him to drive them home. Tevye, debating with himself and with God the whole way about whether kindness will be rewarded or punished, takes them to a green dacha where a wealthy, anxious family greets them with joy and relief. He is fed a feast and, when invited to name his reward, is too flustered and proud to ask for much. The grateful household loads his wagon with food and money — enough for Tevye to buy a cow and set himself up as a dairyman. He returns home to a stunned Golde, and the two marvel at their sudden, God-sent "fortune." The episode introduces Tevye's two great subjects, God and money, and his habit of arguing with Heaven out loud in the language of the marketplace.

### Lecture Script

Let me start tonight with a confession on Tevye's behalf, in his own spirit. He would tell you that he is a poor man, a dairyman, that he has too many daughters and not enough money, that his horse is half-dead and his wife has a sharp tongue — and that despite all of this, God, in His infinite wisdom, has decided to keep him alive and arguing. And that's the thing I want you to listen for tonight: a man arguing with God, out loud, in the middle of the road, in the language of the marketplace. [pause]

Last week we stood at the well — Ecclesiastes, the Psalms, the ancient texts. Tonight we meet a man who has that whole library in his head and uses it the way you and I might use a Swiss Army knife. Tevye quotes scripture constantly — and constantly gets it slightly wrong, or bends it to mean exactly what he needs it to mean at that moment. It's one of the funniest things in literature, and it's also one of the most moving, because what it shows you is a man so saturated in sacred text that it has become his daily speech, his comfort, his weapon, his joke. The book has become the homeland. We talked about that idea last week; tonight you'll see it lived. [pause]

The author calls himself Sholem Aleichem — which, I should tell you, is not a name at all. It's a greeting. It's what one Jew says to another on the road: "peace be upon you." Imagine a writer who published everything under the byline "Hello, how are you." That tells you everything about his ambition: he wanted to be the voice of the ordinary person, the familiar at everyone's table. His real name was Solomon Rabinovich, born in 1859 in the Ukrainian provinces of the Russian Empire, in the world we call the shtetl — the small Jewish market town. [pause]

Here's the anecdote I want you to keep. He married for love — he'd been the tutor, she'd been the pupil, a real romance — and when his wealthy father-in-law died, he inherited a fortune. And then he lost most of it, speculating on the stock exchange in Kiev. So this great chronicler of poor dreamers and schemers, of men he called *luftmenschen*, "men of air" — men with no ground under their feet — knew exactly what he was writing about. He'd been one. [pause]

Now I have to tell you the hard thing, and I'm going to tell you now rather than spring it on you. Most of you know Tevye. You know him as *Fiddler on the Roof* — "Tradition," "If I Were a Rich Man," the whole beloved show. And *Fiddler* is wonderful, and it kept Tevye alive in the world's memory. But it sweetened him. The real Tevye is sharper, funnier, and far sadder. Because the world Sholem Aleichem was writing — this whole shtetl civilization, the Yiddish-speaking world of Eastern Europe — would be almost entirely destroyed within a generation of his death, in the Holocaust. He didn't live to see it; he died in the Bronx in 1916. But we read him knowing it. So when you laugh tonight — and you will laugh — I want you to notice the laughter, and then notice what's underneath it. [pause]

His funeral in New York drew, by many accounts, more than a hundred thousand people into the streets. A man who signed his work "peace be upon you," mourned by a city. Let's find out why. Open to Tevye, and let's hear him talk.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. Tevye's direct address to God and to "Sholem Aleichem."** Note the doubled audience: Tevye narrates *to* the writer-figure and simultaneously argues *with* God. What to bring out: the monologue form makes the reader a confidant; we are buttonholed on the road. The intimacy is the comedy and the pathos at once.

**2. The scriptural misquotations.** Tevye cites a Hebrew verse, then "translates" it into Yiddish in a way that twists or invents the meaning to fit his situation. What to notice (Halkin's notes are essential here): this is not ignorance — Tevye half-knows he's bending the text, and that wink is the joke. What to draw out: the sacred and the everyday are not separate rooms for him; scripture is a tool for living, comically and seriously at once. This is the tradition's intimacy with its own holy books.

**3. Tevye's response to disaster — a shrug, a proverb, a joke, and on he goes.** Pick a moment where misfortune lands and Tevye answers with humor. What to bring out: comedy here is *survival*, not avoidance. The joke is how he keeps his dignity and his footing when the ground is giving way. Connect explicitly to the course thread "comedy and survival."

**4. The daughters and the breaking of tradition.** In whichever episode you assign, locate the collision between Tevye's piety and his children's modern choices. What to notice: the historical forces — secularization, revolution, intermarriage, emigration — enter as *family drama*. The shtetl's dissolution is felt at the kitchen table. Discussion: is Tevye's eventual, partial acceptance of his children a defeat or a kind of love?

**5. The ending's expulsion (if your sketch reaches it).** Tevye is ordered out of his village. What to bring out: the comic voice does not break, even here — and that refusal to break is itself the statement. Ask students what it means that he meets exile with the same voice he met everything else.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does Tevye talk *to* God so casually and constantly?**
   *Model answer:* Because for Tevye, God is a real and present party to his life, like a landlord or a business partner — someone to thank, blame, and bargain with. The casualness is not irreverence but a deep, lived intimacy with the divine, characteristic of shtetl piety.

2. **What is the effect of Tevye's misquoted scripture?**
   *Model answer:* It is funny, but it also shows a mind so soaked in sacred text that scripture is his everyday speech. He bends the verses to his needs with a half-conscious wink, demonstrating the tradition's living, unstuffy relationship to its own holy books.

3. **Where exactly is the comedy, and where is the grief? Can you separate them?**
   *Model answer:* You cannot fully separate them — that is the point. Tevye jokes *because* his situation is dire; the humor is how he survives loss without surrendering his dignity. The laughter and the sorrow share the same sentence.

4. **How do large historical forces enter the Tevye stories?**
   *Model answer:* Through the daughters and their choices — love-marriage, revolution, intermarriage, emigration — and finally through Tevye's expulsion. Secularization, antisemitism, and migration are felt not as abstractions but as events at the family table.

5. **What is the "shtetl," and how does the story complicate any romantic image of it?**
   *Model answer:* The shtetl is the small, largely Jewish market town of the Pale of Settlement, poor and devout and communal. The story shows it as warm but also hungry, hidebound, and increasingly under threat — neither idyll nor mere misery.

6. **Why might Sholem Aleichem have chosen to write in Yiddish rather than Hebrew or Russian?**
   *Model answer:* Yiddish was the everyday language of the Jewish masses he wanted to reach and represent. Choosing the despised "jargon" and elevating it to high art was both a democratic and a defiant literary act.

7. **How does the monologue form shape our relationship to Tevye?**
   *Model answer:* It makes us his confidants, buttonholed on the road and unable to look away. We get his unfiltered voice with no narrator standing between us, which is the source of both the humor and the intimacy.

8. **Knowing what came later — the Shoah — how does it change the way we read this comedy?**
   *Model answer:* It charges every joke with elegy. We are laughing at a world we know is about to be destroyed, which deepens rather than cancels the comedy; the laughter becomes a way of honoring the vanished.

9. **How does the real Tevye differ from the Tevye of *Fiddler on the Roof*?**
   *Model answer:* The original is sharper, funnier, more argumentative with God, and far less sentimental. *Fiddler* preserved him for the world but smoothed his edges and warmed his ending; the text restores his complexity.

10. **Is Tevye wise, foolish, or both?**
    *Model answer:* Both, and the line is the point. His mangled learning and helpless schemes look foolish, but his endurance, his love for his daughters, and his refusal of despair are a form of hard-won wisdom — a recurring figure in this tradition, the wise fool.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Students will hit untranslated or transliterated Yiddish and Hebrew terms, and they will not catch the scriptural in-jokes on their own. Use a translation with good notes (Halkin) and read one misquotation aloud, explaining the "straight" verse and then Tevye's bent version, so they hear the joke at least once. Some students will know only *Fiddler* and may resist a darker reading; welcome the musical as a way in, then gently complicate it. Finally, the looming Shoah may tempt the class into premature solemnity — let them enjoy the comedy first; the grief will arrive on its own, and it lands harder if you have let them laugh.

Tevye's posture itself: a poor man on a dusty road, looking up to argue with the Almighty and then, getting no answer, picking up the reins and going on. Hold the image; it is the comedy and the courage of the whole tradition in one figure.

## Session 3 — Isaac Bashevis Singer: "Gimpel the Fool"

### At a Glance
The last great writer to make his life's work in Yiddish, and the only Yiddish author to win the Nobel Prize, Singer brings us a holy fool — Gimpel the baker, cheated and mocked by his whole town, who chooses faith and forgiveness over revenge. The session deepens our picture of the shtetl, introduces the figure of the trusting fool as a vessel of wisdom, and raises the question that haunts Singer: in a world full of deception, is belief foolishness or grace? The story arrives in English through Saul Bellow's famous 1953 translation — a literary event in itself.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Isaac Bashevis Singer was born Icek Hersz Zynger in 1902 (some sources give 1903) in Leoncin, a village near Warsaw in Russian-ruled Poland, into a deeply religious family ([NobelPrize.org, "Singer – Biographical"](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/literature/1978/singer/biographical/); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Bashevis-Singer)). His father was a Hasidic rabbi, his mother the daughter of a rabbi, and the household in which he grew up — for years on Krochmalna Street in the Jewish quarter of Warsaw — was saturated in piety, Talmudic argument, folklore, and the supernatural. This upbringing supplied the raw material of his entire fiction: the demons and dybbuks, the rabbinical courts, the world where the sacred and the uncanny pressed against each other at every turn. He studied for a time at a rabbinical seminary but turned away from the religious vocation toward secular literature, while never losing the religious imagination that animates his work.

He came of age in interwar Warsaw, one of the great centers of Yiddish culture, and worked as a proofreader and journalist in the Yiddish press while beginning to write fiction. He wrote always in Yiddish — this is the central fact of his career and a point to stress for students. By the time he matured, Yiddish was a language under mortal threat; he would become its great posthumous champion, the writer who kept it alive in world literature long after the civilization that spoke it had been destroyed. His older brother, Israel Joshua Singer, was already an established Yiddish novelist (author of *The Brothers Ashkenazi*), and Isaac always credited him as a guide and rival; their sister Esther Kreitman was also a writer, long overshadowed by her brothers.

The decisive biographical pressure was emigration ahead of catastrophe. In 1935, with antisemitism rising and the Nazi threat gathering, Singer left Poland and followed his brother to the United States, settling in New York. He could not have known it fully, but he was escaping the destruction of his entire world: the Polish Jewry he had grown up among, including his mother and a younger brother, would be murdered in the Shoah. This loss is the silent engine of his art. Much of his fiction is set in a Poland that no longer existed, peopled by Jews who had been annihilated — an act of resurrection and remembrance performed in a language most of his American readers could not read.

The arrival in America brought a long period of struggle. Working for the *Forverts* (the Yiddish *Jewish Daily Forward*), where much of his fiction first appeared in serial form, he wrote prolifically — novels, stories, memoirs, children's books — but remained, for years, a writer for a shrinking Yiddish readership. His breakthrough into the wider literary world came in 1953, when the *Partisan Review* published "Gimpel the Fool" in an English translation by Saul Bellow — a translation that became famous in its own right and effectively introduced Singer to American letters. From then on he was translated extensively (always working closely with his translators, treating the English versions as authoritative second originals), and his reputation grew steadily through novels such as *The Family Moskat*, *Satan in Goray*, *The Magician of Lublin*, and *Enemies, A Love Story*, and through story collections including *Gimpel the Fool and Other Stories*.

In 1978 Singer was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature — the first and only time the prize has gone to a writer in Yiddish ([NobelPrize.org](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/literature/1978/singer/biographical/)). The honor was, in a real sense, an honor to a murdered civilization and a vanishing language as much as to a single man, and Singer understood it that way; his Nobel lecture is partly a defense of Yiddish, "a language of exile, without a land, without frontiers." He died in Florida in 1991. His legacy is paradoxical and instructive: a writer who guarded a dying language and a destroyed world, yet reached millions through translation; a modern skeptic obsessed with demons and the divine; a chronicler of piety who lived a thoroughly secular, complicated life. Students should leave understanding that Singer is not a quaint folklorist but a sophisticated modern artist using folk forms to ask the hardest questions about faith, evil, and survival.

A note on the supernatural, which often confuses first-time readers. Singer's demons, imps, and spirits are not whimsy and not quite belief; they are a literary instrument inherited from Yiddish folklore and the Hasidic imagination, a way of dramatizing the moral and metaphysical forces inside human life. When a demon narrates a Singer story, it is a device — but a device freighted with the genuine theological seriousness of the world he came from. Tell students to take the demons seriously as *literature* without worrying whether Singer "believed" in them; the ambiguity is deliberate and is part of his greatness.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Singer wrote into, and out of, two worlds in collision. The first is the traditional Jewish Poland of the *shtetl* and the Hasidic milieu — the same Eastern European Jewish civilization we met with Sholem Aleichem, but seen from inside its religious and folkloric depths. Hasidism, the popular mystical-pietist movement that swept Eastern European Jewry from the eighteenth century, gave Singer's world its tone: an intense, joyful, sometimes superstitious piety, full of wonder-working rebbes, fervent prayer, and a vivid sense of the supernatural. "Gimpel the Fool" is set in the imaginary shtetl of Frampol, and its texture — the bakery, the rabbinical court, the gossiping townsfolk, the looming presence of the *yeytser-hore* (the evil inclination) and the demonic — comes directly from this milieu.

The second world is the catastrophe and its aftermath. Singer is writing after the Shoah, in America, about a Poland that has been physically destroyed. Even when a story makes no mention of the Holocaust — and "Gimpel" does not — the reader's knowledge that this world was annihilated colors everything, turning the fiction into an act of memorial. This is a crucial interpretive frame to give students: Singer's shtetl tales are not nostalgia; they are resurrection. He repopulates a graveyard.

The cultural moment of "Gimpel" in English is itself historically significant. Its 1953 appearance in *Partisan Review*, translated by Saul Bellow, placed a Yiddish story at the very center of the postwar New York intellectual scene — the same milieu we will meet again with Bellow and Roth. It marks the moment when Jewish American literature was moving from the margins to the center of American letters, and when the Yiddish past and the American present were being stitched together. Singer became a bridge between the destroyed Old World and the flourishing new Jewish American literature.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Gimpel the Fool" (Isaac Bashevis Singer, trans. Saul Bellow).** Gimpel, the orphaned baker of the town of Frampol, narrates his own life. He is called a fool because he believes everything anyone tells him — the townspeople constantly trick him with absurd lies (that the rabbi gave birth, that the messiah has come, that his dead parents are outside), and he goes along because, as he reasons, everything is possible and refusing to believe might wrong an honest person. The town marries him off to Elka, a sharp-tongued woman of bad reputation whom they pass off as a virgin. Seventeen weeks after the wedding she bears a child; she insists it was premature and Gimpel, scolded, accepts it. He loves the boy and works hard at the bakery to support the household. One night he comes home early and finds a man asleep beside Elka in his bed; she brazenly denies it and the rabbi orders Gimpel to divorce her, but after months of loneliness and longing for his child, Gimpel recants his testimony (claiming he imagined it) and returns to her. He goes on fathering and supporting children who are not biologically his, deceiving himself willingly. On her deathbed, Elka confesses that none of the children are his and begs his forgiveness. Devastated, Gimpel is then tempted by the Devil, who appears and urges him to take revenge on the town by urinating into the dough he bakes for them. Gimpel does it — but Elka's ghost appears, warning that by deceiving others he only damns himself. He buries the fouled bread, gives away his property, and becomes a wandering storyteller, telling tales to children. Old now, he concludes that the world is entirely an illusion, "once removed from the true world," and that in death — "the world to come" — there will be no deception. He waits for it gladly, certain that "whatever may be there, it will be real."

### Lecture Script

I want to begin tonight by asking you to think about the word "fool." We use it as an insult. A fool is someone who gets cheated, someone everybody laughs at, someone who believes things that aren't true. Hold that ordinary meaning in your mind, because tonight we meet a man named Gimpel, and the whole town calls him a fool — and by the end of his story you may not be sure whether they're right, or whether Gimpel is the only wise man in Frampol. [pause]

Listen to the famous opening: "I am Gimpel the fool. I don't think myself a fool. On the contrary." That's how the story begins, and it sets the whole problem in motion. Here is a man who is told, every day, by everyone, that he is a fool — and who keeps believing people anyway, keeps trusting, keeps forgiving. They tell him the rabbi's wife has given birth to a calf; he half-believes it. They marry him to a woman who deceives him over and over; he forgives her. The question Singer is asking — and it's one of the great questions — is this: in a world full of liars, is the man who keeps believing a fool, or is he something closer to a saint? [pause]

The man who wrote this was the last great master of a dying language. Isaac Bashevis Singer was born in Poland in 1902, the son of a Hasidic rabbi, and he grew up on Krochmalna Street in Warsaw in a house full of prayer and Talmud and, importantly, full of stories about demons and spirits and the unseen world. He wrote his entire life in Yiddish — the language of Eastern European Jews — and here's why that matters so much. [pause] In 1935 he left Poland for New York, ahead of the Nazis. The world he left behind — the Jewish Poland of his childhood, including members of his own family — was murdered in the Holocaust. So when Singer sits down in New York and writes a story set in a Polish shtetl, full of living, breathing, gossiping Jews, he is bringing the dead back to life. He is repopulating a graveyard. Every shtetl story he writes is, quietly, a memorial. [pause]

Here is the detail I most want you to carry. In 1978, Isaac Bashevis Singer won the Nobel Prize in Literature — the only writer in Yiddish ever to do so. And in his Nobel lecture he defended Yiddish as, in his words, "a language of exile, without a land, without frontiers." Think about that. A prize given, in a sense, not just to a man but to a murdered people and a homeless language. [pause]

One more thing before we read, because it trips people up. Singer's stories are full of demons and imps and spirits, and sometimes a demon even narrates. Don't get stuck on whether Singer "believed" in them. Treat them the way he did — as a way of making the moral forces inside us visible, the evil inclination, the temptation, the doubt. They come straight out of the folklore of his childhood, and they carry real weight. [pause] So: let's go to Frampol, and let's meet Gimpel, and let's decide for ourselves whether the town is laughing at a fool or at a holy man. Open the story.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The opening: "I am Gimpel the fool. I don't think myself a fool. On the contrary."** What to notice: the first-person voice, defensive and disarming at once, immediately splits the question of who is judging whom. What to bring out: we are inside the "fool's" head and find it lucid; the irony is established in three sentences. Voice is everything in this story.

**2. The townspeople's lies and Gimpel's reasoning that he had better believe, "for everybody can't be wrong."** What to notice: Gimpel's logic for credulity is half humble, half shrewd — he reasons that the cost of disbelief (calling everyone a liar) is higher than the cost of being deceived. What to draw out: this is a theology of trust. Connect to the question of faith: is belief in God, in a world of apparent evidence against it, the same kind of "foolishness"?

**3. The marriage to Elka and her repeated deceptions.** What to notice: Gimpel knows, again and again, that he is being betrayed, and chooses to stay, to provide, to love the children who are not his. What to bring out: forgiveness as a deliberate moral practice rather than mere weakness. Discussion: is Gimpel's endurance dignity or self-erasure?

**4. The Spirit of Evil's temptation and Gimpel's refusal.** Late in the story Gimpel is tempted to take revenge on the town — to befoul the bread. What to notice: the demonic appears precisely as the voice of cynicism and revenge. What to bring out: Gimpel's refusal to do evil even when "justified" is the moral climax; he buries the contaminated bread. The fool will not become a cynic.

**5. The ending: Gimpel becomes a wandering storyteller and looks toward a world "entirely imaginary" but "without deception."** What to notice: the final turn toward death and "the real world" where no one is deceived. What to bring out: the religious resolution — the trusting fool's faith is vindicated in the hope of a true world beyond this deceptive one. Ask whether this consoles us or unsettles us. Tie to Ecclesiastes' eternity "set in the heart."

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Is Gimpel a fool? Define the term as the story uses it.**
   *Model answer:* By the town's definition — easily deceived — yes. But the story redefines folly: Gimpel's trust and refusal of revenge look, by the end, like a higher wisdom and even holiness. The title is ironic.

2. **Why does Gimpel choose to believe people he suspects are lying?**
   *Model answer:* He reasons that disbelief would require calling everyone a liar, and he would rather risk being fooled than become a cynic. His credulity is partly a moral choice to trust the world rather than a simple deficiency.

3. **How is Gimpel's trust in people related to religious faith?**
   *Model answer:* The story aligns them: believing in an unseen, often unprovable good is structurally like Gimpel's believing his neighbors. Singer quietly asks whether faith in God, in a world full of suffering and deception, is "foolish" in the same way.

4. **What is the role of the Spirit of Evil near the end?**
   *Model answer:* It voices cynicism and the temptation to revenge, urging Gimpel to repay the town's cruelty. Its appearance externalizes the choice between bitterness and forgiveness, and Gimpel's refusal is the story's moral peak.

5. **Why does Gimpel forgive Elka rather than leave her?**
   *Model answer:* He loves her and the children, and he treats forgiveness as a practice rather than a feeling. Whether we read this as saintly or self-defeating is exactly the tension Singer wants us to hold.

6. **What does the supernatural element contribute, and how should we read it?**
   *Model answer:* It dramatizes inner moral forces and connects the story to Yiddish and Hasidic folklore. We need not decide whether Singer "believed" in demons; they function as serious literary embodiments of temptation and doubt.

7. **How does knowing the shtetl was destroyed in the Shoah affect our reading?**
   *Model answer:* It turns the vivid, living Frampol into a memorial. Singer resurrects a murdered world, so the story's warmth carries an undertow of elegy even though the Holocaust is never mentioned.

8. **Why does the story end with Gimpel as a wandering storyteller anticipating a world "without deception"?**
   *Model answer:* It vindicates his faith by projecting a true world beyond this deceptive one, recalling Ecclesiastes' eternity set in the human heart. The storyteller's role also makes Gimpel a stand-in for the artist who keeps faith and keeps telling.

9. **What is the significance of Bellow's translating this story into English in 1953?**
   *Model answer:* It carried a Yiddish story into the center of postwar American letters and introduced Singer to a vast new audience. It also symbolizes the stitching-together of the destroyed Old World and the rising Jewish American literature we will study later.

10. **Is the story's resolution consoling or troubling?**
    *Model answer:* Both. The promise of a deception-free world comforts, but it locates true justice only beyond death, which may unsettle readers who want vindication here and now. Singer leaves the tension open.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The Yiddish and Hebrew terms, the rabbinical-court setting, and especially the matter-of-fact demons can disorient students new to this world; explain them briefly and let the strangeness stand as part of the texture. Some students will read Gimpel's forgiveness as mere weakness and grow impatient with him — that reaction is useful, so draw it out rather than correct it, and let the class debate dignity versus self-erasure. As with Sholem Aleichem, hold the Shoah lightly here: the story does not name it, and you can let the elegiac dimension emerge through discussion rather than lecturing it in advance.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Isaac Bashevis Singer, *Nobel Lecture* (1978) — short, available via NobelPrize.org; his defense of Yiddish.
- Janet Hadda, *Isaac Bashevis Singer: A Life* (Oxford University Press, 1997).
- Ruth R. Wisse, *The Modern Jewish Canon* (Free Press, 2000) — chapter touching Singer and Yiddish modernism.
- Irving Howe, *World of Our Fathers* (Harcourt Brace Jovanovich, 1976) — on the Yiddish-speaking world and its American afterlife.
- Ilan Stavans, ed., *Isaac Bashevis Singer: An Album* / Library of America editions of Singer's *Collected Stories* (3 vols., 2004) — authoritative texts and apparatus.

### One Thing to Memorize

"I am Gimpel the fool. I don't think myself a fool. On the contrary." — the opening of the story, and the whole question of the course's wise-fool tradition in two sentences.

## Session 4 — Anzia Yezierska: "The Lost 'Beautifulness'" (from *Hungry Hearts*)

### At a Glance
We cross the Atlantic with the great migration and land on New York's Lower East Side, where Anzia Yezierska gives voice to the immigrant Jewish woman — poor, passionate, hungry not only for bread but for beauty, dignity, and a place in America. "The Lost 'Beautifulness'" follows Hanneh Hayyeh, who paints her tenement kitchen white and is destroyed for it by a landlord's greed. The session opens the American immigrant chapter of the course and foregrounds the thread of women's voices and the gap between the American promise and its delivery.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Anzia Yezierska was born around 1880–1885 (she herself obscured the date) in Płońsk, in Russian-ruled Poland, and emigrated as a child with her family to New York around 1890, settling in the immigrant Jewish world of the Lower East Side ([*Encyclopedia.com*, "Yezierska, Anzia"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/women/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/yezierska-anzia-c-1881-1970); [Jewish Women's Archive](https://jwa.org/encyclopedia/article/yezierska-anzia)). Her father was a Talmudic scholar who devoted himself to study and prayer while the women of the family supported the household — a domestic arrangement that became the central wound and theme of her fiction. The pull between the old-world father, who believed a woman's worth lay in serving the men who studied Torah, and the new-world daughter, who hungered for her own education and selfhood, runs through nearly everything she wrote.

She grew up in poverty and worked, as so many immigrant girls did, in sweatshops and as a domestic servant. Determined to educate herself, she won her way out through charity institutions and scholarships, living for a time at the Clara de Hirsch Home for Working Girls and eventually graduating from Teachers College at Columbia University in 1904 with training in domestic science — a credential she found stifling. Her languages were Yiddish (her mother tongue and the language of the home) and an English she acquired and then transmuted into a distinctive literary instrument: her prose deliberately carries the rhythms, inversions, and emotional directness of immigrant Yiddish-inflected speech, so that the reader hears the Lower East Side on the page. This invented "immigrant English" is one of her real artistic achievements and a point worth dwelling on with students.

Her personal life was turbulent in ways that mirror her fiction's restless heroines. She married twice in quick succession around 1910–1911 — the first marriage annulled or abandoned almost immediately, the second to Arnold Levitas, with whom she had a daughter, Louise — and she left her family to pursue writing, eventually relinquishing day-to-day custody of her child. For a period she was close to the philosopher John Dewey, then at Columbia; the intense, probably partly unconsummated relationship marked her deeply and surfaces, transmuted, in her later fiction. These facts are worth handling tactfully in class but they illuminate her central subject: the immigrant woman's struggle to claim a self against the competing demands of family, poverty, and respectability.

Her breakthrough came with *Hungry Hearts* (1920), a collection of stories about immigrant Jewish life on the Lower East Side, told largely from the perspective of women. The book caught the attention of Hollywood: Samuel Goldwyn bought the film rights for a then-enormous sum and brought Yezierska to California, where the press dubbed her "the sweatshop Cinderella" ([Women Film Pioneers Project, Columbia](https://wfpp.columbia.edu/pioneer/ccp-anzia-yezierska/)). The sudden fame was disorienting and, in the end, hollow; she was repelled by Hollywood's crassness and by the alteration of her work, and she returned to New York. She continued to publish — notably the novel *Bread Givers* (1925), her most fully realized treatment of the father-daughter conflict, and *Salome of the Tenements* (1923) — but her star faded with the Depression, whose mood had little appetite for immigrant uplift narratives, and she spent her later decades in relative obscurity and poverty.

Late in life she enjoyed a partial recovery of attention with the autobiographical *Red Ribbon on a White Horse* (1950), which carried an introduction by W. H. Auden, and she continued to write reviews and stories into old age. She died in 1970. Her fullest rediscovery came later still, with the feminist recovery of women's writing in the 1970s and after: Alice Kessler-Harris's reissues and Vivian Gornick's championing returned *Bread Givers* and *Hungry Hearts* to classrooms, where they now serve as central texts of the American immigrant and Jewish American canons.

Her legacy is precisely the voice the older Jewish literary tradition had largely left out. Against a classical center of gravity that was male, learned, and oriented toward the study house, Yezierska speaks for the women who kept the households running and who hungered — the word is hers — for beauty, learning, and a self of their own. Tell students that her limitations are real (critics have always debated the melodrama and repetition in her work) but that her achievement is to have made literature out of a perspective that had been mute: the immigrant Jewish woman as a full subject of desire and disappointment. She is the bridge in this course between the Old World we have just left and the American century to come, and the first woman writer we read closely.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The world of Yezierska's fiction is the Lower East Side of Manhattan in the first decades of the twentieth century — the densest urban district in America and the great gateway of Eastern European Jewish immigration. Between roughly 1881 and 1924, some two million Jews fled the poverty, persecution, and pogroms of the Russian Empire (the world of Sholem Aleichem and the young Singer) and poured into American cities, with New York's Lower East Side as the principal landing place. Students should picture the conditions: tenement apartments crammed with families and boarders, sweatshops in the needle trades, pushcart markets, and a teeming street life conducted largely in Yiddish. It was poor and overcrowded, but also intensely alive — a place of newspapers, theaters, unions, night schools, and fierce aspiration. Irving Howe's *World of Our Fathers* is the indispensable portrait.

Into this world came the American promise: the idea, embodied for many in the very immigrant journey, that one could remake oneself, escape poverty, and become a new person in a new land. Yezierska's fiction lives in the painful gap between that promise and its delivery. Her characters believe ardently in America — in education, in beauty, in the possibility of dignity — and they are repeatedly thwarted by economic exploitation, by the indifference of the prosperous, and by the persistence of old-world hierarchies. "The Lost 'Beautifulness'" is exactly such a parable: Hanneh Hayyeh's attempt to make a small beauty for herself collides with a landlord's power, and the American dream curdles.

There is also a specific gender dimension that distinguishes Yezierska within the immigrant story. In the traditional Jewish arrangement she grew up in, religious learning and public standing belonged to men; women's labor sustained the men's study. The American context cracked this open — daughters could go to night school, work for wages, imagine independence — and Yezierska's women are caught in the fault line, torn between the father's law and the new world's possibilities. This is the immigrant story told from the kitchen and the sweatshop rather than the study house, and it is the entry point for the course's sustained attention to the role of women in the tradition.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"The Lost 'Beautifulness'" (Anzia Yezierska, from *Hungry Hearts*).** Hanneh Hayyeh, a poor Lower East Side washerwoman, spends her own hard-earned savings to paint her tenement kitchen a shining white — partly inspired by the home of Mrs. Preston, the wealthy American woman whose fine laundry she does and who has taught her the word "democracy" and made her "feel like a person." She dreams of welcoming her son Aby home from the war (he is fighting in France) to a beautiful spotless kitchen. Her careworn husband, Jake Safransky, scolds her for wasting money that should have been saved and reminds her the flat belongs to the landlord. Proud and joyful, Hanneh Hayyeh shows off the gleaming kitchen to admiring neighbors at the butcher shop (Mr. Sopkin says the landlord should give her a medal), and Mrs. Preston calls her "an artist." But when the landlord, Benjamin Rosenblatt, comes to collect rent and sees the improvement, he raises the rent five dollars a month — precisely because the painted flat is now worth more. He coldly tells her, "In America everybody looks out for himself." Hanneh Hayyeh pleads, then fights: she refuses Mrs. Preston's offer of charity (she wants justice, not "hush money"), feels betrayed when Mrs. Preston counsels patience with America's imperfect laws, and goes to court — where the judge confirms the landlord's right to raise the rent or evict her. Crushed, taunted by her husband as a "starved beggarwoman" who thought she could live like Mrs. Preston, she snaps. On the last night before eviction, rather than let the landlord profit, she takes an axe and destroys the kitchen herself — scratching off the paint, tearing up floorboards, blackening the white ceiling with gas. At dawn she grieves the "lost beautifulness" she has ruined. The story ends as Aby, returning decorated from the war on a surprise leave, arrives to find his mother huddled and broken on the curb, the family's belongings dumped in the rain after the dispossession.

### The Text(s) for This Session

- **"The Lost 'Beautifulness'"** from *Hungry Hearts* (1920). The collection is in the **public domain** (published 1920).
- **Free sources:** [Project Gutenberg, *Hungry Hearts* by Anzia Yezierska](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/author/?query=Yezierska) (search "Yezierska"); also at the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org) (search "Hungry Hearts Yezierska") and full text via [Wikisource](https://en.wikisource.org) where digitized.
- If "The Lost 'Beautifulness'" is hard to locate alone, assign the full short collection and focus discussion on that story; "The Fat of the Land" is an excellent backup from the same volume.

### Lecture Script

I want to start tonight with a kitchen. Just a kitchen — a poor woman's kitchen, in a tenement on the Lower East Side of New York, a hundred years ago. The walls are gray and grimy. And a woman named Hanneh Hayyeh decides, against all sense, to do something extravagant: she is going to paint that kitchen white. Clean, shining white. She scrimps, she saves, she does without, and she paints her kitchen white — for the joy of it, for the beauty of it, and to be ready for her son who is coming home from the war. [pause] And tonight we're going to watch what happens to a poor woman who dares to make something beautiful. I'll warn you now: it does not end the way we want it to.

Last week and the week before, we were in the Old World — the shtetls of Eastern Europe, the world of Sholem Aleichem and Singer. Tonight we make the crossing that two million Jews actually made: the great migration to America, between the 1880s and the 1920s, most of them landing right here in the Lower East Side of Manhattan, the most crowded neighborhood in the country. They came hungry — hungry for bread, yes, but also, in Yezierska's great word, *hungry-hearted*: hungry for beauty, for dignity, for a self. [pause]

Anzia Yezierska came on one of those boats, as a child, around 1890, from Poland. And here's the family she came from — listen, because it's the engine of everything she wrote. Her father was a Talmudic scholar. He studied and prayed all day, which in the old tradition was the highest calling — and the women of the family worked to support him. So Anzia grew up watching a world in which the men studied the holy books and the women carried the burden, and in which a girl's hunger for her own learning, her own beauty, her own life, was treated as a kind of disobedience. She spent her whole career writing against that — for the women the tradition had left out. She is the first woman we read in this course, and that is not an accident; she is giving voice to people who didn't get to speak in the books we read so far. [pause]

Here's the anecdote. *Hungry Hearts*, the book this story comes from, was such a sensation that Samuel Goldwyn — the Hollywood mogul — bought the film rights and brought Yezierska to California. The newspapers called her "the sweatshop Cinderella." Picture it: a girl from the tenements, suddenly famous, suddenly rich, in Hollywood. And she hated it. She was repelled by the money, by the falseness, by what they did to her stories, and she went back to New York. She had spent her life writing about the hunger for beauty and dignity — and when the dream finally arrived, gift-wrapped, it felt like a betrayal of everything real. [pause]

Listen, too, to *how* she writes. Her English is unlike anyone else's — it's built out of Yiddish rhythms, the word order tilted, the emotion right on the surface. "The Lost 'Beautifulness'" — even the title, that word "beautifulness," is not standard English. She made an English that sounds like the immigrant street. Don't correct it in your head; listen to it. [pause] So: let's go to Hanneh Hayyeh's white kitchen, and let's watch what America does with a poor woman's dream of beauty. Open the story.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The word "beautifulness" and Hanneh Hayyeh's speech generally.** What to notice: the deliberately non-standard English — inversions, exclamations, Yiddish cadence ("Like a baby") — that renders immigrant speech as a literary medium. What to bring out: this is not "broken" English but a crafted voice; the very word "beautifulness" expresses a hunger that standard English doesn't have a word for. Ask why Yezierska refuses to "clean up" the diction.

**2. The painting of the kitchen white.** What to notice: the act is economically irrational and emotionally enormous; white is the color of cleanliness, dignity, and hope, and she does it partly to honor her returning soldier-son. What to bring out: beauty as a claim to full humanity by someone the world treats as disposable. The white kitchen is Hanneh Hayyeh's small declaration that she, too, deserves loveliness.

**3. The landlord raising the rent because the apartment is now "improved."** What to notice: the brutal economic logic — her improvement becomes the pretext for her exploitation. What to bring out: the bitter irony at the heart of the immigrant promise; the reward for striving is punishment. Connect to the course thread on the gap between America's promise and its delivery.

**4. Hanneh Hayyeh's destruction of the kitchen (or her devastation when evicted).** What to notice: the violence of her grief — the same passion that built the beauty turns to ruin. What to bring out: this is the emotional climax; her rage is dignity refusing humiliation. Discussion: is her act self-defeating, or the only protest available to the powerless?

**5. Any invocation of "America" as an ideal.** What to notice: characters speak of America as a near-sacred promise of fairness and possibility. What to bring out: Yezierska sets the ideal and the reality violently against each other; the story is a test of whether the promise is real for the poor. Tie to Emma Lazarus next week — the welcoming "Mother of Exiles" — and let the contrast do its work.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What does "beautifulness" mean to Hanneh Hayyeh, and why coin the word?**
   *Model answer:* It names a hunger for beauty and dignity that ordinary English doesn't quite capture. The coinage signals that her desire exceeds the vocabulary available to her, and Yezierska honors that excess by inventing the word.

2. **Why does Yezierska write in a non-standard, Yiddish-inflected English?**
   *Model answer:* To make the immigrant voice itself the medium of the story, so we hear the Lower East Side directly. It is a deliberate artistic choice that asserts the dignity and expressiveness of immigrant speech rather than apologizing for it.

3. **Why does Hanneh Hayyeh paint the kitchen white when she can barely afford food?**
   *Model answer:* Because beauty is, for her, a basic human need and a claim to dignity, not a luxury. The act declares that a poor woman, too, deserves loveliness, and it prepares a worthy home for her returning son.

4. **What is the significance of the landlord raising the rent?**
   *Model answer:* It turns her self-improvement into the instrument of her exploitation, exposing a system in which the poor are punished for striving. It is the story's central, devastating irony.

5. **Is Hanneh Hayyeh's final act of destruction self-defeating or justified?**
   *Model answer:* It can be read both ways: it loses her the very thing she loved, yet it is the only protest left to the powerless and an assertion that she will not be quietly humiliated. The ambiguity is the point.

6. **How does this story revise the immigrant "American Dream" narrative?**
   *Model answer:* It dramatizes the gap between the promise of fairness and self-making and the reality of economic exploitation. The dream is not denied outright but shown to be cruelly conditional for the poor.

7. **How does Yezierska's own biography illuminate the story?**
   *Model answer:* Her own poverty, sweatshop labor, hunger for education, and conflict with a study-devoted father all feed the fiction. Even her Hollywood disillusionment echoes the story's theme that the dream, when it arrives, may betray.

8. **Why is it significant that this is the first woman we read in the course?**
   *Model answer:* The classical tradition centered on male religious learning and largely omitted women's voices. Yezierska gives the immigrant Jewish woman full subjectivity — desire, ambition, rage — and opens the course's sustained attention to gender.

9. **What role does the father-figure tradition play, even offstage, in Yezierska's world?**
   *Model answer:* The old-world expectation that women serve the men who study shapes the constraints her heroines push against. The hunger for a self of one's own is, in part, a rebellion against that inherited arrangement.

10. **How does the story prepare us for Emma Lazarus next week?**
    *Model answer:* Lazarus wrote the welcoming words of the Statue of Liberty, the "Mother of Exiles." Reading Yezierska first lets us measure that generous promise against the lived experience of the immigrants who actually arrived, sharpening both texts.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Some students will read Yezierska's heightened, melodramatic register as a flaw rather than a feature; acknowledge the critical debate honestly, but redirect them to hear the emotional logic and the crafted immigrant voice. The non-standard English may read as "errors" to careful readers — say plainly that it is intentional and artful. A few students may find Hanneh Hayyeh's choices (spending on paint, destroying the kitchen) baffling or exasperating; that frustration is productive, so turn it into the debate about dignity and powerlessness rather than smoothing it away.

### One Thing to Memorize

The image of the white kitchen: a poor woman painting her tenement walls white for the sheer dignity of beauty — and the rent rising the moment she does. Hold both halves; they are the American promise and its betrayal in a single picture.

## Session 5 — Emma Lazarus: "The New Colossus," "1492," "In Exile"

### At a Glance
This week we read the poet who gave the Statue of Liberty its voice — and discover that "The New Colossus" was written by a proud, learned American Jewish woman whose later work turned fiercely toward her own people's history of persecution and exile. Reading "The New Colossus" beside "1492" and "In Exile" reveals a poet far deeper than the famous sonnet alone suggests, and it lets us test the American promise we watched curdle in Yezierska against the generous welcome Lazarus imagined. The session also returns women's voices to center stage and braids together our threads of exile and belonging.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Emma Lazarus was born in New York City in 1849 into a wealthy and long-established Sephardic Jewish family — a crucial fact that sets her apart from nearly everyone else in this course ([Poetry Foundation, "Emma Lazarus"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/emma-lazarus); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Emma-Lazarus)). Her people were not recent immigrants from the Pale but descendants of Sephardic Jews (of Spanish-Portuguese origin) who had been in America since the colonial era, members of the old New York congregation Shearith Israel. She grew up in privilege and cultivation, educated privately in literature and languages — she read and translated German, French, and Italian, and engaged seriously with classical and European literature. This patrician, assimilated, fourth-generation American background is essential context: Lazarus did not come to Jewish themes out of the shtetl or the tenement, but found her way to them, increasingly and deliberately, as a mature artist responding to history.

She was a prodigy. Her first volume, *Poems and Translations*, appeared when she was seventeen, and it drew the attention of Ralph Waldo Emerson, who became a correspondent and mentor (their relationship had its strains, notably when he omitted her from an anthology). She moved in elevated literary circles and was, for much of her early career, a poet of refined, conventional, European-facing verse — sonnets, translations (including of Heine and of the medieval Hebrew poets), and a verse drama. Had she stopped there she would be a minor figure. What made her major was a turn.

That turn was historical. The wave of violent pogroms that swept the Russian Empire after the assassination of Tsar Alexander II in 1881 drove masses of destitute Eastern European Jews to America, and Lazarus — encountering these refugees, some of them housed in miserable conditions on Ward's Island — was galvanized. The comfortable, assimilated Sephardic poet became an impassioned advocate for her people. She wrote essays defending Jews against antisemitism, argued (strikingly early) for a Jewish homeland in Palestine, worked practically to aid the refugees, and produced a body of explicitly Jewish poetry — most importantly the collection *Songs of a Semite* (1882), which includes "1492" and the cycle "By the Waters of Babylon," and the dramatic poem *The Dance to Death*, about the medieval massacre of Jews. Students should see this as the central drama of her career: a privileged American artist choosing solidarity, claiming a Jewish identity and history that her assimilated upbringing might have let her ignore.

"The New Colossus" belongs to this period. She wrote the sonnet in 1883 as a contribution to an auction raising funds for the pedestal of the Statue of Liberty. At the time it attracted modest notice; the statue itself was conceived by its French makers as a monument to liberty and republican friendship, not to immigration. It was Lazarus's poem — and its eventual inscription on a bronze plaque inside the pedestal in 1903, sixteen years after her death — that decisively reimagined the statue as the "Mother of Exiles," the welcomer of the world's poor and huddled masses ([National Park Service, Statue of Liberty](https://www.nps.gov/stli/learn/historyculture/colossus.htm)). It is one of the most consequential acts of interpretation in American history: a Jewish woman's sonnet that taught a nation what its great statue meant.

Lazarus's life was short. She traveled twice to Europe in her last years and returned from the second trip seriously ill; she died in New York on November 19, 1887, at thirty-eight, most likely of Hodgkin's lymphoma ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/emma-lazarus)). For a long time she was remembered, if at all, only as the author of the famous sonnet's closing lines. The fuller recovery of her work — the fierce, learned, prophetic Jewish poet behind "Give me your tired, your poor" — is relatively recent, and it reveals a writer of real range and conviction.

Her legacy is layered. She gave America its self-image as a refuge, in lines schoolchildren still memorize. She was an early Jewish proto-Zionist voice in America, decades before Herzl. And she was a woman who used a position of privilege not to escape her people's history but to claim it and speak for it. In a course tracing exile and belonging, Lazarus is a pivotal figure: the insider who chose to stand with the outsiders, and who wrote the welcome that the immigrants of our previous session — and the next century of newcomers — would arrive beneath.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Lazarus stands at the intersection of two Jewish histories. On one side is the long, proud, assimilated Sephardic-American presence she was born into — Jews who had been in the New World for generations, prosperous and at home. On the other is the sudden arrival, in her adult years, of impoverished Ashkenazi refugees fleeing the post-1881 pogroms in the Russian Empire — the same migration that produced Yezierska's Lower East Side. Lazarus's great theme emerges precisely from the meeting of these two worlds: the established American Jew confronting the suffering of her newly arriving co-religionists and choosing identification over distance.

Behind her explicitly Jewish poems lies the deeper history of the Jewish diaspora and its expulsions, which she read with a poet's and a partisan's eye. "1492" turns on the double event of that year: the expulsion of the Jews from Spain (the end of a golden age of Sephardic culture) coinciding with Columbus's voyage that opened the New World. For Lazarus, descended from Sephardic Jews, this was personal history and providential pattern — the year that cast her ancestors out of Spain was the year that opened the land that would receive their descendants. "In Exile" and the Babylon poems reach back to the ancient template of Jewish exile — the Babylonian captivity, the loss of Jerusalem — that we first met in Session 1.

The American context matters too. Lazarus wrote in the Gilded Age, amid mass immigration and rising nativism, and her vision of America as a refuge for the world's oppressed was a deliberate moral argument, not a bland platitude. To proclaim the new nation the "Mother of Exiles" was to take a side in a live debate about who America was for — a debate, you can gently note, that has never closed. Read against Yezierska, her sonnet's generosity becomes poignantly aspirational: the welcome was real as an ideal and unreliable as a lived experience.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"The New Colossus" (1883).** An Italian sonnet. The octave contrasts the Statue of Liberty with the ancient "brazen giant of Greek fame" (the Colossus of Rhodes): unlike that conquering figure, here at America's "sea-washed, sunset gates" stands "a mighty woman with a torch" whose flame is "imprisoned lightning," named "Mother of Exiles," her beacon glowing "world-wide welcome" over the harbor between New York and Brooklyn. The sestet gives her speech: with "silent lips" she cries, "Keep, ancient lands, your storied pomp!" and instead demands, "Give me your tired, your poor, / Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free, / The wretched refuse of your teeming shore. / Send these, the homeless, tempest-tost to me, / I lift my lamp beside the golden door!"

**"1492" (1883).** A sonnet addressed to the year 1492 as a "two-faced year, Mother of Change and Fate." The octave recalls its dark face: that year Spain "cast forth with flaming sword" the Jews — "Prince, priest, and people, spurned by zealot hate" — who were hounded "from sea to sea, from state to state," with the West refusing them and the East abhorring them, "Close-locked was every port, barred every gate." The sestet turns to the year's other face: smiling, it "unveil'dst... a virgin world where doors of sunset part" (Columbus's discovery of America), proclaiming, "Ho, all who weary, enter here!" — a New World where "each ancient barrier" of "race or creed or rank" and "Grim bulwarked hatred between heart and heart" would fall.

**"In Exile" (from *Songs of a Semite*, 1882).** Prefaced by an epigraph from "a letter of a Russian refugee in Texas" describing life as "one unbroken paradise" in which the settlers gather each evening under "the mighty oak" to sing. The poem depicts Russian Jewish refugees who have become farmers on the Texas prairie. It opens at twilight as the day's toil ends, oxen are unyoked, and the laborers rest. Lazarus celebrates their hard-won freedom: "Freedom to love the law that Moses brought, / To sing the songs of David," to think the thoughts of Gabirol and Spinoza, "to dig the common earth, to drink / The universal air" — for which they crossed wave and continent, linking "Egypt with Texas in their mystic chain." The closing stanza describes their song floating "through the quiet evening air" with "wild sweet rhythm and glad refrain," celebrating the soul's victory over pain; in their singing one hears both "Rustle of palms and Eastern streams" as "the broad prairie melts in mist of tears."

- **"1492" (1883).** [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/emma-lazarus); [Wikisource](https://en.wikisource.org).

### Lecture Script

I'm going to begin tonight by reciting something most of you know by heart, or nearly. [pause] "Give me your tired, your poor, / Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free, / The wretched refuse of your teeming shore. / Send these, the homeless, tempest-tost to me, / I lift my lamp beside the golden door!" [pause] You know those words. They're on the Statue of Liberty. They are, for many people around the world, what America *means*. And here is what I want you to sit with tonight: those words were written by a woman. A Jewish woman. A poet named Emma Lazarus, in 1883. The Statue of Liberty did not come with that meaning attached — the French built it as a monument to liberty and friendship between republics. It was Emma Lazarus's sonnet that turned a statue of liberty into the *Mother of Exiles*. A Jewish woman taught America what its most famous monument means. [pause]

Now here's what's surprising about her, and it connects directly to last week. You'd assume the woman who wrote "Give me your tired, your poor" must have *been* one of the tired and poor — an immigrant herself, fresh off the boat. She was the opposite. Emma Lazarus was born in 1849 into one of the oldest, wealthiest Jewish families in New York — Sephardic Jews, descended from Spanish and Portuguese Jews, in America since colonial times. She grew up in privilege, reading German and French and Italian, corresponding with Ralph Waldo Emerson. She could very easily have spent her whole life as a refined, assimilated, comfortable poet of pretty verses. [pause]

And then history reached her. In 1881, after the Tsar was assassinated, a wave of vicious pogroms swept across the Russian Empire, and masses of destitute Jewish refugees began arriving in New York — some of them housed in wretched conditions on an island in the harbor. And Emma Lazarus went to see them. The comfortable poet met her suffering people face to face, and it changed her. She became an advocate, an essayist defending Jews against hatred, an early voice — decades before Zionism had a name — calling for a Jewish homeland. And she began to write a fierce new poetry about exile and persecution and her own people's history. [pause]

So tonight we read three poems together, and the order matters. We read "The New Colossus," the famous welcome. But then we read "1492" — about the year the Jews were expelled from Spain, *her* ancestors, the very same year Columbus opened the New World. For Lazarus that was personal: the year that threw her people out of Spain was the year that opened the land that would one day take them in. And we read "In Exile," which reaches all the way back to the ancient exile we started this course with, by the waters of Babylon. [pause] Here's the anecdote to hold: she died young, at thirty-eight, in 1887, and the sonnet wasn't even inscribed on the statue until 1903 — sixteen years after her death. She never knew she'd written America's welcome. [pause] Let's read all three, and let's discover the deep, fierce poet hiding behind five famous lines. Open to "The New Colossus."

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "The New Colossus," the octave: "Not like the brazen giant of Greek fame... / A mighty woman with a torch."** What to notice: Lazarus deliberately contrasts her statue with the ancient Colossus of Rhodes — not a conqueror but a woman, not domination but welcome. What to bring out: she feminizes and re-genders the icon of national power; "Mother of Exiles" replaces the warrior. Connect to the course's thread on women's voices: a woman reimagines the meaning of national strength as nurture.

**2. "The New Colossus," the sestet: "Give me your tired, your poor..."** What to notice: the statue *speaks*, in the imperative; the poem's power comes from giving the mute monument a voice, and that voice is one of radical hospitality. What to bring out: the audacity of "wretched refuse" — Lazarus takes the era's contemptuous language for immigrants and turns it into a banner of honor. Discussion: read against Yezierska, is this promise fulfilled or aspirational?

**3. "1492," its turning structure.** What to notice: the sonnet hinges on the double meaning of the year — expulsion from Spain and discovery of the New World — addressing the year itself as "Two-faced." What to bring out: Lazarus reads catastrophe and refuge as a single providential pattern; the door that closed in the Old World opened in the New. The personal stake (her Sephardic ancestry) charges the abstraction. Tie to exile-and-belonging.

**4. "In Exile," its pastoral imagery of freed Russian-Jewish immigrants on American soil.** What to notice: the poem imagines former refugees finding peace and dignity in rural American freedom, with biblical resonance. What to bring out: the contrast between the old persecution and the new liberty, and the way Lazarus sacralizes the American refuge. Discussion: how does the idealism here compare with Yezierska's harder realism?

**5. The Babylon/ancient-exile resonance across the poems.** What to notice: Lazarus repeatedly reaches back to the ancient template of exile we met in Session 1. What to bring out: she places the modern immigrant story inside a three-thousand-year history of displacement and return — the long memory that distinguishes this tradition. Memorize-worthy connective tissue for the whole course.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why is it significant that "The New Colossus" was written by an assimilated, wealthy Jewish woman rather than an immigrant?**
   *Model answer:* It shows solidarity chosen rather than inherited: Lazarus could have ignored the refugees but identified with them. It also complicates the poem's voice — the welcome is spoken on behalf of newcomers by someone already securely American.

2. **How does Lazarus redefine the Statue of Liberty?**
   *Model answer:* She transforms it from a generic monument to liberty into the "Mother of Exiles," a feminine figure of welcome rather than a "brazen giant" of conquest. Her interpretation, not the sculptor's intent, became the statue's enduring meaning.

3. **What does Lazarus do with the phrase "wretched refuse"?**
   *Model answer:* She reclaims contemptuous nativist language and turns it into an emblem of honor, insisting that the despised and discarded are precisely whom America should receive. It is a deliberately provocative reversal.

4. **What is the "two-faced" structure of "1492," and why is it personal for Lazarus?**
   *Model answer:* The year both expelled the Jews from Spain and opened the New World, so it is at once catastrophe and refuge. As a descendant of Sephardic Jews, Lazarus reads her own family's history into the pattern, making the providential reading intimate.

5. **How does reading these three poems together change your view of Lazarus?**
   *Model answer:* The famous sonnet alone suggests a single sentimental note; the trio reveals a learned, fierce poet steeped in Jewish history and exile. She is a major voice of identification and protest, not just the author of a welcome.

6. **How does Lazarus's welcome read against Yezierska's Lower East Side?**
   *Model answer:* Lazarus offers the ideal — the lamp, the golden door — while Yezierska shows the lived reality of exploitation and disappointment. Together they frame the American promise as genuine in aspiration and unreliable in delivery.

7. **What is the effect of giving the statue a speaking voice?**
   *Model answer:* It converts a silent monument into a moral agent making a demand of the world. The imperative "Give me..." turns description into ethics and gives the poem its lasting force.

8. **How do these poems connect to the ancient exile we read in Session 1?**
   *Model answer:* Lazarus repeatedly invokes Babylon and the long history of Jewish displacement, placing the modern immigrant inside a three-thousand-year story. She makes the contemporary refugee an heir to an ancient pattern of loss and return.

9. **Was Lazarus's vision of America naive?**
   *Model answer:* It was idealistic and deliberately so — a moral argument in an age of rising nativism rather than a naive description. She knew persecution intimately; the welcome is a chosen aspiration, not innocence.

10. **Why does it matter that Lazarus is the second woman writer in this course?**
    *Model answer:* With Yezierska she establishes that women are central, not marginal, to this tradition. Lazarus in particular shows a woman authoring a nation's self-understanding, reclaiming public and prophetic voice.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Most students will know "The New Colossus" only as a patriotic cliché and will be surprised — even resistant — to find a fierce Jewish particularism behind it; lead with the surprise, it tends to energize the room. "1492" requires the historical fact of the Spanish expulsion and its coincidence with Columbus; supply it in a sentence. The sonnet form and Lazarus's somewhat formal nineteenth-century diction can feel stiff to modern ears, so read the poems aloud and let the sound carry them. Take care, in a contemporary classroom, that the immigration theme not collapse into present-day politics; keep the focus on Lazarus's own moment and let students draw their own connections.

## Session 6 — Franz Kafka: "Before the Law" and "A Hunger Artist"

### At a Glance
We turn to the great riddle of modern literature: Franz Kafka, the German-speaking Jew of Prague whose parables of locked doors, inexplicable authority, and self-starving artists have given us the very word "Kafkaesque." Reading "Before the Law" and "A Hunger Artist" together, we confront a writer whose Jewishness is everywhere and nowhere — never named in these stories, yet felt by generations of readers as the deep structure of his vision. The session asks one of the course's hardest questions: what makes a writer "Jewish," and can a tradition claim someone who barely claimed it himself?

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Franz Kafka was born in 1883 in Prague, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, into a German-speaking Jewish family ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Franz Kafka"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Franz-Kafka); [Franz Kafka Museum, Prague](https://kafkamuseum.cz/en/biography)). His situation as a writer was defined from the start by a triple marginality that you should lay out plainly for students, because it is the key to everything: he was a German-speaker in a majority-Czech city; a Jew among German-speakers; and a secular, assimilated, ambivalent Jew among Jews. He belonged fully to no group. He once described, in a famous letter, the German-speaking Jewish writer as caught among "impossibilities" — unable to write convincingly in German, unable not to. This condition of belonging nowhere is, many readers feel, the secret engine of his fiction, with its perpetual outsiders barred from a Law, a Castle, a Court they can never reach.

His father, Hermann Kafka, was a self-made, domineering businessman, and Franz's tortured relationship with him is among the most documented in literary history — most directly in the long, unsent *Letter to His Father*, in which Kafka traces his sense of inadequacy, fear, and smallness to the paternal shadow. This biographical material matters because authority — vast, arbitrary, unappeasable, often paternal — is the central subject of his work. Kafka was educated in German-language schools and took a doctorate in law at the German university in Prague. He then spent his working life as a respected official at a workmen's accident-insurance institute, writing his fiction at night — a double life of bureaucrat and visionary that itself feels Kafkaesque.

He published relatively little in his lifetime and was tormented by doubt about his work. The stories that did appear — including "A Hunger Artist" (*Ein Hungerkünstler*, published in the year of his death) and "Before the Law" (*Vor dem Gesetz*, which he also embedded in his novel *The Trial*) — show the qualities we now call Kafkaesque: lucid, calm prose describing situations of nightmarish, inescapable logic; ordinary people crushed by incomprehensible systems; guilt without a nameable crime. His major novels — *The Trial*, *The Castle*, and *Amerika* — were all left unfinished and unpublished at his death.

The crucial biographical pressure of his later years was illness. Kafka contracted tuberculosis in 1917 and struggled with it for the rest of his short life, dying in a sanatorium near Vienna in June 1924, not yet forty-one ([Franz Kafka Museum](https://kafkamuseum.cz/en/biography)). In his final years he had grown somewhat more engaged with Jewish life — he was drawn to Yiddish theater, studied Hebrew, took an interest in Zionism, and his last companion, Dora Diamant, came from an observant Eastern European Jewish background. This late turn complicates any picture of him as wholly detached from his people. Still, the relationship was searching and unresolved rather than settled.

The most famous fact about Kafka's legacy is also the most poignant: he asked his friend Max Brod to burn his unpublished manuscripts after his death. Brod refused, and instead edited and published *The Trial*, *The Castle*, and *Amerika*, single-handedly creating the Kafka the world knows. Without that act of disobedience, one of the central figures of modern literature would have vanished. There is a dark coda students should know: three of Kafka's sisters were murdered in the Shoah, and his beloved Milena Jesenská died in a concentration camp. Kafka did not live to see the catastrophe, but it consumed the world he came from and many of those closest to him — one reason his visions of faceless, lethal bureaucracy read, in retrospect, as eerily prophetic.

His legacy is immense and contested. He is one of the inescapable writers of the twentieth century, and the question of his Jewishness is one of the great interpretive debates in modern literature. Some read his work as universal allegory with no specifically Jewish content; others — and this is the more compelling case for our course — find in it the deep structure of Jewish experience: the outsider before an inaccessible Law (a word with profound Jewish resonance), the exile with no homeland, the figure who waits a lifetime for an admission that never comes. Tell students they do not have to resolve this; the productive thing is to *feel the question*, which is precisely the course's thread about who belongs to a tradition.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Kafka's Prague was a city of layered tensions, and students need a quick map of it. It was the capital of Bohemia within the Austro-Hungarian Empire, a majority-Czech city with a significant German-speaking minority, within which the Jews formed a further minority — many of them, like the Kafkas, German-speaking and culturally assimilated. The German-speaking Jews of Prague occupied a precarious middle ground: not Czech, not quite accepted as German, marked as Jews by a rising and increasingly racialized antisemitism. This is the social reality behind Kafka's metaphysics of exclusion. His outsiders are not arbitrary inventions; they distill a real condition of belonging nowhere securely.

The intellectual world Kafka wrote in was the ferment of central European modernism in the years around the First World War — the Vienna of Freud, the crisis of religious faith, the dawning sense that the great rational systems (law, bureaucracy, science, the state) had become impersonal machines indifferent to the individual. Kafka gives this modern condition its definitive images. But you should also place him within a specifically Jewish frame. The "Law" before which his man from the country waits is, in German, *das Gesetz* — and for a Jewish reader the word inevitably evokes Torah, the divine Law, *Halakhah*. To stand before the Law and be denied entrance, to spend a lifetime seeking admission to one's own birthright, has a charge in this tradition that it cannot have for anyone else.

Finally, the catastrophe that came after. Kafka died in 1924, but the bureaucratic, faceless machinery of dehumanization that he imagined was realized, with literal horror, in the Nazi genocide that murdered his sisters and so much of European Jewry. We must be careful not to turn Kafka into a mere prophet of the Shoah — he was writing about his own moment — but it is honest to say that readers after 1945 cannot unsee the resemblance, and that this resemblance is part of why his work feels so terribly central to the Jewish twentieth century.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Before the Law" (1915).** A brief parable (also embedded in *The Trial*). A man "from the country" comes seeking entrance to the Law and finds a doorkeeper standing before an open door. The doorkeeper refuses him admittance "now," warning that even if he forces past, there are more powerful doorkeepers beyond, hall after hall. The man decides to wait. He waits for years, sitting on a stool the doorkeeper provides; he questions the doorkeeper, bribes him with everything he owns (the doorkeeper accepts it all, saying he takes it only so the man won't feel he left something untried), and grows childishly fixated, even talking to the fleas in the doorkeeper's collar. As he nears death, his eyesight failing, he sees a radiance streaming from the doorway of the Law. With his last strength he asks why, in all these years, no one else has ever come seeking entrance. The doorkeeper, seeing the man is dying, roars into his ear: "No one else could ever be admitted here, since this gate was made only for you. I am now going to shut it." The door is closed; the man dies having never entered.

**"A Hunger Artist" (1922/1924).** A "hunger artist" once enjoyed great public fame for professional fasting: crowds paid to watch him sit starving in a barred cage, supervised by watchers (often skeptical butchers), his fasts capped at forty days by his impresario, who knew interest waned afterward. The artist is privately tormented — not by hunger but by the fact that no one believes he never cheats, and by his own dissatisfaction, since fasting is in truth easy for him and he can never fast as long as he wishes. Over time public taste changes and interest in fasting collapses; the artist's day passes. He hires himself to a circus, where his cage is placed near the menagerie, ignored by crowds rushing to see the animals. No one even counts the days of his fast anymore; the placard goes unchanged. He fasts on, forgotten, indefinitely. Eventually an overseer, noticing the long-neglected cage, finds him buried in the straw, near death. Asked why he persisted, the dying artist confesses his secret: he should not be admired, because he had to fast — he could never find a food that he liked; had he found it, he would have eaten like everyone else. He dies and is cleared away with the straw. A young, vigorous panther is put in the cage in his place — full of life and appetite, drawing delighted crowds.

- **"A Hunger Artist"** (*Ein Hungerkünstler*, 1922/1924). Older translations (e.g., the Muirs') and the German original are in the **public domain** in many jurisdictions; [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/author/1735) and the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org).
- **Recommended edition (library copy):** *Kafka: The Complete Stories*, trans. Willa and Edwin Muir (Schocken), or the newer Michael Hofmann translations (Penguin). Hofmann's notes are excellent for discussion.

### Lecture Script

There is a word in English that came from a single writer's name — "Kafkaesque." You've used it, probably: when the bureaucracy traps you, when you're caught in a system whose rules you can't learn and can't satisfy, when you feel guilty without knowing your crime. That word comes from the man we read tonight, Franz Kafka, and by the end of this class I want you to understand it from the inside. [pause]

Let me start with the first of our two stories, because it's only a page long and it will haunt you. A man comes from the country to seek entrance to "the Law." There's a doorway, and a doorkeeper. The man asks to be let in. "Not now," says the doorkeeper. So the man waits. He waits days, then years, then his whole life, sitting before that open door, never daring to go through, always asking, always told "not yet." And at the very end, dying, he asks one last question — and the answer is one of the most devastating sentences in all of literature. I won't spoil it. But hold the image: a man waiting his entire life before an open door he never walks through. [pause]

Who was Kafka? He was born in Prague in 1883, and I want you to understand his situation exactly, because it explains everything. He was a German-speaker in a Czech city — minority. He was a Jew among the German-speakers — minority within the minority. And he was a secular, doubting, assimilated Jew, not fully at home among other Jews either. He belonged, fully, nowhere. He once described the German-Jewish writer as caught among impossibilities — couldn't write in German, couldn't not. Now think about that man, who belongs nowhere, writing a story about a man who waits his whole life before a door he's never allowed to enter. [pause]

He had a day job — he was an insurance official, a respected one — and he wrote at night, in secret, tormented by doubt about whether any of it was good. And here is the fact I most want you to carry out of this room. When he was dying of tuberculosis in 1924, at forty, he asked his closest friend, Max Brod, to burn all his unpublished manuscripts. Everything. *The Trial*, *The Castle* — burn it. And Brod refused. Brod disobeyed his dying friend, and edited and published the books, and that disobedience is the only reason we have Kafka at all. One of the central writers of the twentieth century survived by an act of betrayal of his own wishes. [pause]

Now, the hard question, and it's the question I want hanging over the whole class. Is Kafka a *Jewish* writer? These two stories never mention Jews, never mention Judaism. And yet — that word "the Law," in German *das Gesetz*, is the word for Torah, the divine Law. A man who spends his whole life trying and failing to enter the Law that was meant for him — does that sound like a particular history to you? Some readers say Kafka is universal, that this is everyone's story. Others say it's the deep structure of Jewish exile itself. I'm not going to settle it for you tonight. I want you to feel the question, because "who belongs to a tradition?" is one of the great questions of this whole course, and Kafka is where it gets hardest. [pause] Three of his sisters, by the way, were later murdered in the Holocaust — the faceless machinery he imagined became real. Let's read "Before the Law," and then the hunger artist who starves in his cage. Open the page.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "Before the Law": the open door and the doorkeeper's "not now... but not at the moment."** What to notice: the door is *open* the entire time; nothing physically prevents entry except the man's own deference to authority. What to bring out: the self-imposed nature of the prohibition — the most Kafkaesque horror is that the trap may be one we keep ourselves inside. Discussion: why does the man never simply walk through?

**2. "Before the Law": the final revelation — the door was meant for him alone, and is now to be shut.** What to notice: the doorkeeper's closing words reveal that this entrance existed solely for this one man, who never used it. What to bring out: the unbearable particularity — a salvation designed for *you*, missed through a lifetime of waiting for permission. The Jewish resonance (the Law that was your birthright, never entered) is available without being forced.

**3. "A Hunger Artist": the artist's pride in fasting and the public's waning interest.** What to notice: the hunger artist is a *performer* whose art is self-deprivation, once celebrated, then ignored as fashions change. What to bring out: art as starvation, the artist as someone who consumes himself for an audience that drifts away. Tie to Kafka's own tormented, secret writing life.

**4. "A Hunger Artist": the dying confession — he fasted only because "I couldn't find the food I liked."** What to notice: the deflating, devastating admission that his heroic asceticism was not transcendence but an inability to be satisfied by the world. What to bring out: the anti-heroic turn; his "art" is reframed as a kind of incapacity for ordinary life. Discussion: is this tragic, comic, or both?

**5. "A Hunger Artist": the panther that replaces him in the cage.** What to notice: the closing image of the vital, hungry, joyously alive panther where the starving artist died. What to bring out: the brutal contrast between life lived fully and the self-denying artist; the world's appetite moves on. Ask what Kafka is saying about the cost of the artistic vocation.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does the man in "Before the Law" never walk through the open door?**
   *Model answer:* Because he defers endlessly to the doorkeeper's authority, treating a "not now" as a permanent barrier. The horror is that the obstacle is largely self-imposed — a lifetime of waiting for a permission that was never required.

2. **What is the effect of the story's final revelation?**
   *Model answer:* Learning that the door existed only for him, and is now to be closed, makes the loss unbearably specific. A salvation designed for one person is forfeited through obedience and delay.

3. **In what sense might these stories be specifically Jewish, despite never mentioning Judaism?**
   *Model answer:* "The Law" evokes Torah and the divine Law; the figure of the outsider barred from his own birthright echoes a long history of exile and unreachable belonging. The reading is suggestive rather than explicit, which is why the question stays open.

4. **What does the word "Kafkaesque" mean, and where do you see it in these stories?**
   *Model answer:* It names situations of inescapable, irrational systems that crush the individual with calm, lucid logic and unappeasable authority. Both the endless waiting before the Law and the artist trapped in his self-consuming performance embody it.

5. **What is the hunger artist's "art," and why does the public lose interest?**
   *Model answer:* His art is public fasting, self-deprivation as spectacle, which depends entirely on an audience's attention. When fashions change the audience drifts away, exposing how fragile and contingent his vocation always was.

6. **How should we read the hunger artist's dying confession?**
   *Model answer:* It deflates his apparent heroism: he fasted not from transcendence but because nothing in the world satisfied him. This reframes his asceticism as incapacity, which is at once comic and deeply sad.

7. **What does the panther at the end signify?**
   *Model answer:* It embodies vital, unselfconscious life and appetite, everything the artist renounced. The contrast suggests the world's hunger for living energy and the loneliness of the self-denying artist.

8. **How does Kafka's biography (the outsider, the night-writer, Brod's disobedience) illuminate the work?**
   *Model answer:* His belonging-nowhere feeds the theme of exclusion; his secret, doubted writing mirrors the self-consuming artist; and the fact that he wanted the work burned makes the hunger artist's failed vocation feel autobiographical.

9. **Is the missed salvation in "Before the Law" a tragedy of the individual, or of a system?**
   *Model answer:* Both: the man fails through his own deference, but the doorkeeper's authority and the opaque "Law" form a system designed to defeat him. Kafka holds personal and structural guilt in unresolved tension.

10. **Can a tradition claim a writer who barely claimed it? What is at stake in answering?**
    *Model answer:* To include Kafka expands "Jewish writing" to sensibility and structure rather than explicit content; to exclude him narrows it to declared identity and theme. The choice reveals what we think a literary tradition fundamentally is — our central course question.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Kafka frustrates students who want stories to "mean" one thing; reassure them that productive irresolution is the point and resist nailing the parables to a single key. The Jewish-or-universal debate can become heated or, worse, abstract — anchor it in the specific word "Law" and in concrete textual evidence rather than letting it float. Some students will find "A Hunger Artist" merely bleak or absurd; draw out its dark comedy so they see Kafka's wit, not only his gloom. Finally, take care with the prophetic-of-the-Shoah framing: note the resemblance honestly while reminding students that Kafka was writing about his own pre-Holocaust moment, not foretelling.

## Session 7 — Bruno Schulz: A Piece from *The Street of Crocodiles*

### At a Glance
We meet a writer of dazzling, dreamlike prose from a small Galician town — Bruno Schulz, painter and teacher, who transformed his provincial childhood into one of the most luxuriant imaginative worlds in modern European literature, and who was murdered in the street by a Gestapo officer in 1942. The session lets students experience prose as a kind of intoxication, introduces magical realism and the mythologizing of childhood and the father, and brings the Shoah into the course not as subject matter but as a brutal interruption of a living art. We read Schulz partly for his genius and partly for what was taken.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Bruno Schulz was born on July 12, 1892, in Drohobycz, a town in the Galician province of Austria-Hungary (today Drohobych, in western Ukraine), into an assimilated Jewish family ([Wikipedia/standard reference; EBSCO Research Starters, "Bruno Schulz"](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/bruno-schulz)). His father, Jakub, was a cloth merchant who kept a fabric shop on the town square — a figure who would be transfigured, in Schulz's fiction, into a mythic, demiurgic, ultimately disintegrating patriarch, one of the great father-figures in modern literature. Schulz spent almost his entire life in Drohobycz, a provincial oil-boom town on the edge of empires, and he made that small, ordinary place into a cosmos. This rootedness in one spot is essential to his art: he did not range the world; he excavated a single childhood until it became myth.

His languages and culture place him precisely. Schulz wrote in Polish — he is one of the supreme prose stylists of the Polish language — though he came from the German-inflected culture of Austrian Galicia and knew German well. He was, like Kafka (with whom he is often compared, and whose *Trial* he was long credited with helping translate into Polish), a Jew writing in the language of the surrounding majority culture rather than in Yiddish or Hebrew. He trained in architecture and studied art; he was a gifted graphic artist and draftsman, and he earned his living, unhappily, as a drawing and crafts teacher in a Drohobycz secondary school — a job he found draining and beneath his gifts but which he held for most of his adult life.

His literary career was brief and late-blooming. Encouraged by the writer Zofia Nałkowska, he published his first collection, *Sklepy cynamonowe* — literally *Cinnamon Shops*, known in English as *The Street of Crocodiles* — in 1934, when he was already in his forties. A second collection, *Sanatorium pod Klepsydrą* (*Sanatorium Under the Sign of the Hourglass*), followed in 1937. These two slender books, plus a few uncollected pieces, are essentially his entire surviving output — a tragically small body of work for so original a writer. He won the Polish Academy of Literature's Golden Laurel in 1938. He worked on a novel, *The Messiah*, which was lost; its disappearance, along with other late manuscripts, is one of the painful might-have-beens of twentieth-century literature.

His death is among the most terrible facts in this course, and you must tell it with care and exactness. When Germany invaded the Soviet-occupied region in 1941, the Jews of Drohobycz were forced into a ghetto, and most were murdered, many at the Bełżec extermination camp. Schulz survived for a time because a Gestapo officer named Felix Landau, who admired his artistic talent, took him as a kind of protected "personal Jew" and set him to painting murals (including fairy-tale scenes) on the walls of his villa. On November 19, 1942, during a Gestapo killing spree in the town, Schulz was shot dead in the street — by some accounts while carrying home a loaf of bread — by another SS officer, Karl Günther, reportedly in a tit-for-tat grudge against Landau ([Wikipedia, "Bruno Schulz"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruno_Schulz); [EBSCO Research Starters](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/bruno-schulz)). He was fifty. The casualness of the murder — a great artist shot like a stray dog over a quarrel between his killers — is something students should be allowed to sit with. It is the Shoah in a single, unbearable anecdote.

There is a strange and instructive afterlife. The murals Schulz painted in Landau's villa were rediscovered in 2001 and became the subject of an international controversy when fragments were removed, partly by Yad Vashem in Israel — a dispute about who owns the relics of a murdered Galician-Polish-Jewish-Ukrainian artist that itself dramatizes the contested memory of the Shoah. Schulz's literary reputation, meanwhile, has grown steadily; he is now recognized internationally as a master, championed by writers from Isaac Bashevis Singer to Cynthia Ozick (whose novel *The Messiah of Stockholm* imagines the recovery of his lost manuscript) to Philip Roth. His legacy, then, is double: an extraordinary, intact achievement in two small books, and an immense absence — the work he did not live to write, murdered with him in a provincial street.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Schulz's Drohobycz sat at one of Europe's great crossroads of peoples and empires, and students need a sense of it. Galicia was the easternmost crownland of the Habsburg Empire, a region of mixed Polish, Ukrainian (Ruthenian), and Jewish populations; Drohobycz in particular boomed in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries as a center of the Galician oil industry. After the First World War and the collapse of Austria-Hungary, the town became part of the newly independent Poland; after 1939 it fell under Soviet occupation per the Nazi-Soviet pact; and after 1941 under German occupation, which destroyed its Jewish community. Schulz lived his whole life in one place while that place passed through three states and toward annihilation — a vertiginous instability that contrasts strangely with the dreamy timelessness of his fiction.

His art belongs to the great current of European modernism, but on its eastern, mythic edge. He is regularly grouped with Kafka (the assimilated, German-cultured Jewish outsider writing fantastical parables) and identified as a forerunner or kin of *magical realism* — the mode, later associated with writers like Gabriel García Márquez, in which the marvelous erupts within the ordinary as if it were perfectly natural. In Schulz, a father metamorphoses into a bird or a cockroach; cloth bolts breed in the shop; time grows extra, illegitimate months; a whole town becomes a tawdry, cardboard "street of crocodiles." This is not whimsy but a serious metaphysics: Schulz believed reality was a thin, provisional crust over a roiling mythic substratum, and that the task of art (he called it the "mythologizing of reality") was to break through the crust.

It is worth setting Schulz beside the broader Jewish literary world we have been tracing. Unlike Sholem Aleichem or Singer, he did not write of Jewish religious life or in a Jewish language; his fiction barely mentions Jewishness explicitly. Yet he was murdered precisely *as* a Jew, and his work is now read within the Jewish canon partly because of that fate and partly because of its kinship with Kafka's. He raises, again, the course's question of who belongs to the tradition — and he adds a darker dimension: the Shoah did not only kill those who wrote *about* Jewish themes; it killed Jewish artists indiscriminately, and so it impoverished all of literature.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**A chapter from *The Street of Crocodiles* — the title story "The Street of Crocodiles."** The collection as a whole is narrated by Joseph, a boy in a small Galician town (modeled on Schulz's Drohobycz), recounting his strange merchant father, the dominant servant girl Adela, and a mythologized, dreamlike provincial world. In the title chapter, the narrator describes a particular degraded district of the town by way of an old map hanging in his father's drawer. On that map the rest of the town is drawn in rich detail, but the area called the Street of Crocodiles is left a blank white space, as if the cartographers disowned it. This quarter is the town's seedy, modern, parasitic commercial district — a zone of cheap pseudo-progress and shabby imitation, where everything is tawdry, second-rate, and faintly fraudulent. The narrator portrays it in a deliberately flat, "paper" tone: the grey daylight is dull, the architecture a tasteless mimicry of metropolitan modernity, the trams run without proper conductors, the shops sell goods that are somehow not quite real — display windows full of dubious, suggestive merchandise, dressmaking establishments and "houses of ill repute" with an air of vice and moral laxity. Everything in the district has the quality of a cheap, hastily improvised stage set, a "concession to modernity" that the dignified old town tolerates with embarrassment. The narrator stresses the quarter's essential unreality and impotence: nothing there ever fully happens, gestures dissolve before they are completed, "nothing in that city was ever realized definitively." He concludes with characteristic irony — that the town must, after all, be granted "no more than the honor" of such a district: a paper city, a parody of true vitality, where the corruption is itself too half-hearted and provisional to amount to real sin.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The opening descriptions of summer / the town / "the dog days."** What to notice: the prose's density — adjectives piling, metaphors metastasizing, the ordinary swelling toward the mythic. What to bring out: Schulz's style *is* his metaphysics; the over-rich language enacts his belief that reality is bursting with hidden myth. Read a sentence twice and ask what the excess accomplishes.

**2. The figure of the father (Jakub) and his transformations.** What to notice: the father is by turns a heroic demiurge, a mad inventor, a creature shrinking into a bird or insect — never stable. What to bring out: this is the mythologizing of the parent, the childhood awe and terror of the father turned to literature. Compare to Kafka's overwhelming father and to Yezierska's patriarch — the father is a recurring pressure in this tradition. Discussion: is Jakub god, victim, madman, or all three?

**3. The "Cinnamon Shops" night-wandering / the magical city at night.** What to notice: the boy's nocturnal journey into a city that has grown new, dreamlike districts. What to bring out: the dream-logic, the way space and time become elastic; this is magical realism as a mode of childhood perception, where the world is not yet fixed. Ask how Schulz makes the impossible feel inevitable.

**4. "The Street of Crocodiles" itself — the tawdry, cardboard modern quarter.** What to notice: in contrast to the rich mythic town, this district is described as cheap, gray, second-rate, "paper" reality. What to bring out: Schulz's critique of soulless modernity and commerce; the "street of crocodiles" is where the mythic crust has worn thin into shoddiness. A useful counter-image to the luxuriant passages.

**5. Any moment of metamorphosis (cloth, birds, weather).** What to notice: transformation is constant and unremarked; the marvelous is narrated in the same calm tone as the mundane. What to bring out: the defining technique of magical realism, and the source of the work's dreamlike authority. Connect to the reader's knowledge that the man who imagined endless transformation was himself, brutally, ended.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **How would you describe Schulz's prose, and what does its excess accomplish?**
   *Model answer:* It is dense, baroque, and intoxicating, piling metaphor on metaphor until ordinary things swell into myth. The excess enacts his belief that reality is a thin crust over a teeming mythic depth, so the style is inseparable from his worldview.

2. **What is "magical realism," and how does Schulz exemplify it?**
   *Model answer:* It is a mode in which the marvelous erupts within the ordinary and is narrated as if perfectly natural. Schulz's metamorphosing father, self-multiplying cloth, and elastic time are all related in the same calm tone as everyday life.

3. **What kind of figure is the father, Jakub?**
   *Model answer:* He is unstable and mythic by turns — demiurge, mad inventor, and shrinking creature — embodying a child's mingled awe and terror. He is among the great father-figures of modern literature and resonates with Kafka's and Yezierska's patriarchs.

4. **Why does Schulz set the rich, mythic town against the cheap "street of crocodiles"?**
   *Model answer:* The contrast critiques soulless modern commerce, where the mythic crust has worn thin into shoddy "paper" reality. It clarifies, by opposition, what Schulz values: the deep, living myth beneath the everyday.

5. **How does Schulz compare with Kafka?**
   *Model answer:* Both are assimilated Jews writing in a majority language and both make fantastical transformations central, but Schulz is lush and sensuous where Kafka is austere and lucid. They are kin in sensibility and in the question of Jewish belonging.

6. **In what sense is Schulz a "Jewish writer," given his work barely mentions Judaism?**
   *Model answer:* His themes are not explicitly Jewish, yet he was murdered as a Jew and is read within the canon for that fate and his Kafkaesque kinship. He sharpens the course's question of whether the tradition is defined by content or by history and sensibility.

7. **What does the manner of Schulz's death teach about the Shoah that statistics cannot?**
   *Model answer:* Being shot casually in the street over a grudge between his killers shows the genocide's indiscriminate, dehumanizing cruelty. It registers the loss as the murder of a specific, irreplaceable imagination, not an abstract number.

8. **What is lost with *The Messiah* and his other vanished manuscripts?**
   *Model answer:* An entire further body of work by a major artist, gone forever. The absence stands for the broader cultural amputation the Shoah inflicted on literature itself.

9. **How does childhood function in Schulz's fiction?**
   *Model answer:* Childhood is the realm where the world is not yet fixed and the mythic is still visible, so the boy-narrator perceives transformations adults have learned to ignore. Schulz mythologizes memory rather than reporting it.

10. **Why read Schulz "for what was taken" as well as for what he made?**
    *Model answer:* Because his case forces us to reckon with the Shoah as the destruction of living art and future masterpieces, not only of lives. Honoring the achievement and mourning the absence are, for him, inseparable acts.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Schulz's prose is demanding; some students will find the density exhausting or "purple." Read passages aloud, slow the pace, and frame the style as deliberate metaphysics rather than decoration. The plotlessness — these are atmospheres more than stories — can frustrate readers expecting narrative; tell them to surrender to image and mood. Above all, handle the death with steadiness: the murder is gratuitous and may shock, so deliver it plainly, without melodrama, and allow a beat of silence. This is the course's first direct encounter with a writer murdered in the Shoah, and it should feel like one; Sessions 8 and 9 will go further still.

## Session 8 — Primo Levi: From *If This Is a Man* and the poem "Shema"

### At a Glance
This is the first of three sessions that bring the Shoah directly into the room. Primo Levi, an Italian Jewish chemist who survived Auschwitz, wrote what many consider the indispensable testimony of the camps — clear, measured, scientific in its precision, and morally unflinching. We read an excerpt from *If This Is a Man* alongside the searing poem "Shema" that stands at its threshold. The aim is not to "cover" the Holocaust but to encounter one survivor's attempt to make the unspeakable into precise, durable language — and to honor it with the gravity it demands. Treat this session differently from all the others.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Primo Levi was born in 1919 in Turin, Italy, into an assimilated, cultured, middle-class Italian Jewish family ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Primo Levi"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Primo-Levi); [Biography.com](https://www.biography.com/authors-writers/primo-levi)). His Jewishness, before the catastrophe, was a matter of family heritage rather than intense religious observance; he thought of himself, first, as an Italian, a Piedmontese, a citizen of a country where Jews had been relatively well integrated. This starting point is important for students: Levi did not come from the shtetl or a world of fervent piety, but from European bourgeois modernity — which makes his fate, and his clear-eyed analysis of it, all the more chilling. He trained as a chemist, earning his degree from the University of Turin in 1941 despite Italy's Fascist racial laws of 1938, which had begun stripping Jews of rights and made his graduation difficult. The scientist's mind — precise, observant, committed to truth and to the careful description of phenomena — would become the defining instrument of his writing.

When Germany occupied northern Italy in 1943, Levi joined a small, ill-organized partisan resistance group. He was captured in December 1943, declared himself a Jew (calculating, probably correctly, that this was safer than being shot as a partisan), and was deported in February 1944 to Auschwitz — specifically to Monowitz (Auschwitz III), the labor camp attached to a synthetic-rubber plant. He survived eleven months there, until the camp's liberation by the Red Army in January 1945. His survival owed something to chance, something to his usefulness as a chemist (he was eventually assigned to a laboratory), and something to small acts of human solidarity — but Levi was always scrupulous to insist that survival was overwhelmingly a matter of luck, and that "the best" did not survive; the drowned, he wrote, were the true witnesses, and they cannot speak. This refusal of any consoling moral about survival is central to his integrity.

After a long, circuitous journey home through the chaos of postwar Europe (recounted in *The Truce*), Levi returned to Turin, resumed work as an industrial chemist, married, and raised a family. And he wrote. His first book, *Se questo è un uomo* (*If This Is a Man*, 1947; published in the United States as *Survival in Auschwitz*), was initially rejected and sold poorly, then gradually came to be recognized as one of the essential books of the twentieth century. He wrote in Italian — luminous, restrained, classical Italian — and he brought to the description of the camps the discipline of the scientist: exact, unhysterical, refusing both sentimentality and the temptation to look away. Over the following decades he produced a remarkable and varied body of work: *The Periodic Table* (1975), a memoir organized around the chemical elements, often called one of the best science books ever written; *The Drowned and the Saved* (1986), his last, darkest, most analytical reckoning with the camps and with memory; poems, stories, essays, and a novel.

Levi's death in 1987 — he fell from the interior landing of his Turin apartment building, where he had lived most of his life — was ruled a suicide by most, though some who knew him have disputed it, and the question remains genuinely open ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Primo-Levi)). Students sometimes want a tidy reading of this (the survivor finally destroyed by his memories); you should resist that neatness. What is certain is that Levi spent forty years bearing witness with extraordinary clarity, and that he grew, if anything, more pessimistic about whether the lesson had been learned — *The Drowned and the Saved* is haunted by the fear of forgetting and of the "gray zone" of moral compromise that the camps produced.

His legacy is foundational. Levi is, for many readers, the witness — the one who made the camps comprehensible without making them bearable, who insisted on the dignity of precise testimony against both denial and kitsch. He coined or crystallized concepts (the "gray zone," the problem of the survivor's shame, the impossibility of full communication of the experience) that shape how we think about the Shoah and about atrocity generally. He also insisted, against despair, on a rational, secular humanism — even in Auschwitz he sought to understand, to retain the mind, to remain *a man*. The very title *If This Is a Man* poses the question the whole book answers: under conditions designed to destroy the human, what, if anything, of the human remains?

### Historical & Cultural Context

Students need a clear, sober frame for the Shoah itself, delivered without euphemism. Between 1941 and 1945, Nazi Germany and its collaborators murdered approximately six million European Jews — about two-thirds of the Jewish population of Europe — in a systematic, industrialized, state-organized genocide. This is the central event toward which the first half of our course has been building and from which the second half recoils. Auschwitz, where Levi was imprisoned, was the largest of the Nazi camps, a complex that combined a death camp (Birkenau, with its gas chambers) and slave-labor camps; over a million people, the great majority of them Jews, were murdered there. These are facts, and you should state them as facts.

Levi's particular vantage is Italian and Western, which usefully complicates the more familiar Eastern European story. The Italian Jews were a small, ancient, deeply assimilated community; Fascist Italy's antisemitism arrived later and more reluctantly than Germany's, with the racial laws of 1938, and the mass deportations of Italian Jews came only after the German occupation of 1943. Levi thus represents the European Jew who believed himself securely a citizen of a modern nation, and discovered that this belief was no protection — a fact that gives his testimony a distinctive, disabused quality. He also writes as a man of science and reason confronting a system built on irrational hatred, which produces the book's signature tension: the lucid mind describing the systematic unmaking of minds.

The poem "Shema," which stands as the epigraph-poem to *If This Is a Man*, draws its title and structure from the central prayer of Judaism — the *Shema Yisrael* ("Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One"), the declaration of God's oneness that observant Jews recite daily and that is meant to be the last words on the lips of the dying. Levi takes this most sacred Jewish text and turns it into a fierce commandment to the reader: not "hear that God is one," but *hear what was done, and never forget it, and teach it to your children, or be cursed.* You must explain the original prayer for students to feel the force of Levi's appropriation. It is one of the most powerful uses of liturgical tradition in modern literature, and it connects directly to our course-long thread of sacred text repurposed.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Excerpt from *If This Is a Man* / *Survival in Auschwitz* (the opening chapters and the "Canto of Ulysses").** In "The Journey," Levi, a twenty-four-year-old Italian Jewish chemist captured as a partisan in late 1943, recounts being held at the Fòssoli camp and then deported in February 1944: 650 Jews packed into sealed freight cars for a days-long journey without water, the old and the children among them, arriving at night at Auschwitz, where on the ramp SS men perform a swift "selection" — most are sent straight to the gas, a minority (Levi among them) to forced labor. In "On the Bottom," Levi describes the systematic destruction of the prisoner's humanity: the men are stripped, shaved, tattooed with numbers (he becomes 174517), dressed in rags, denied water (a guard breaks off the icicle he reaches for, telling him "Hier ist kein warum" — "here there is no why"), taught that the camp's only law is to survive the day, and reduced to the condition of the Häftling, the prisoner-thing. In the "Canto of Ulysses" chapter, Levi, walking to fetch the daily soup with a young Alsatian named Jean ("Pikolo"), tries to teach him Italian by reciting from memory Dante's *Inferno* Canto XXVI — Ulysses' speech urging his crew onward to knowledge: "Consider your origins: you were not made to live like brutes, but to follow virtue and knowledge." For a moment the poetry restores Levi's sense of being human, before the line of prisoners reaches the soup queue and the everyday brutality returns.

**"Shema" (1946).** The poem that stands as the book's epigraph, echoing the central Jewish prayer ("Hear, O Israel"). Addressed to readers living safely "in your warm houses" with hot food and friendly faces, it commands them to "Consider" (or "Meditate") whether the camp victims were still men and women: the man worn to nothing, "who labours in the mud / Who knows no peace / Who fights for a crust of bread / Who dies because of a yes or a no," and the woman "without hair and without name," eyes empty and womb cold "as a frog in winter." The poem then turns the *Shema*'s injunction into a curse-tinged commandment: engrave these words on your heart, repeat them to your children at home and abroad, brooding on them lying down and rising up — or "may your house fall apart, / May illness impede you, / May your children turn their faces from you."

I want to begin tonight by changing the way we begin. Usually I open with a hook, sometimes a joke. Not tonight. He believed himself securely a citizen of a modern European nation. And he learned that this belief protected him not at all. In 1943 he joined the resistance, was captured, declared himself a Jew, and was deported to Auschwitz, where he survived for eleven months until the Red Army arrived. [pause]

Here is what makes Levi's testimony unlike almost any other, and why we read him first among the witnesses. He brought the mind of a scientist to the camp. Where others might give us a cry, Levi gives us *precision* — exact, measured, unhysterical observation. He describes the unspeakable in clear, calm, classical Italian, refusing both sentimentality and the urge to look away. He once said that the true witnesses, the complete witnesses, were not the survivors but "the drowned" — those who went to the bottom and died — and that they cannot speak. He never let himself off the hook with a comforting story about why he lived. He said: it was luck. The best did not survive. [pause]

The title of his book is a question: *If This Is a Man.* And the whole book answers it. It comes from a poem he placed at the very front, called "Shema." Now I have to explain that word, because it carries the whole weight. The *Shema* is the central prayer of Judaism — "Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One" — the declaration a Jew is meant to say every day, and to have on the lips at the moment of death. And Levi takes that most sacred prayer and rewrites it. He turns "Hear, O Israel" into a fierce command to *us*, the readers: consider whether this is a man, this starved creature in the mud; consider whether this is a woman; carve these words on your heart; teach them to your children; and if you don't — he says — may your house fall, may illness strike you. He takes the holiest text and makes it a curse against forgetting. [pause]

Levi bore witness for forty years, and grew more afraid, not less, that the world would forget. He died in 1987 — most believe by suicide, though it is genuinely uncertain, and I'd ask us not to reach for a tidy explanation. [pause] Tonight we read the opening of his account and we read "Shema." We will read some of it aloud. We will leave room for silence. And we will try to do the one thing Levi asked of his readers: not to look away. Let's begin.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "Shema" — the rewriting of the prayer.** The poem commands the comfortable reader ("You who live secure / In your warm houses") to consider the man and the woman of the camps, then turns the *Shema*'s injunction to "repeat them to your children" into a duty of memory, sealed with a curse. What to notice: the inversion of a prayer of faith into a prayer of memory and warning. What to bring out: Levi, a secular man, uses the deepest liturgical form to bind the reader morally. Read it aloud, slowly. Let the curse land. This is the session's center of gravity.

**2. *If This Is a Man*, the deportation and arrival ("The Journey" / "On the Bottom").** What to notice: the precise, almost clinical registering of the process of dehumanization — the cattle cars, the selection, the stripping, the tattooing, the loss of name and possessions. What to bring out: Levi's method — description so exact it needs no editorializing. The systematic *unmaking* of the person is shown step by step. Discussion: why is restraint more powerful here than outcry?

**3. The reduction to a number and the loss of the name.** What to notice: the tattooed number replacing the name; Levi's insistence on the meaning of this. What to bring out: in a tradition that holds names and the unspoken Name as sacred (recall Session 1), the obliteration of the name is a precise spiritual atrocity. The dehumanization is also a desecration.

**4. The "Canto of Ulysses" chapter (if assigned).** What to notice: Levi, in the camp, struggles to recall and translate for a fellow prisoner the lines from Dante's *Inferno* about Ulysses urging his men toward "virtue and knowledge." What to bring out: the desperate assertion that culture, memory, the human mind still matter even here — a flicker of the human against the machine designed to extinguish it. This is the affirmative counter-note to the horror, and it must not be sentimentalized.

**5. The concept of "the drowned and the saved" / the gray zone (if you draw on his later framing).** What to notice: Levi's refusal of heroic narratives of survival; his insistence on luck and on the moral murk the camps created. What to bring out: his intellectual integrity — he will not let testimony become consolation. Connect to the course's larger refusal of easy redemption in Shoah literature.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

*Note: lead these slowly, with patience for silence. Do not rush to fill pauses.*

1. **Why does Levi write with scientific precision rather than emotional outcry?**
   *Model answer:* Because exact, restrained description gives the facts an authority that hysteria would undercut, and because as a chemist that was his discipline. The restraint also respects the reader's capacity to feel without being told how, and it resists turning atrocity into spectacle.

2. **What does Levi do by titling the poem "Shema" and rewriting that prayer?**
   *Model answer:* He takes Judaism's central declaration of faith and converts it into a commandment to remember, sealed with a curse on those who forget. A secular survivor uses the holiest liturgical form to bind the reader morally — sacred text repurposed as the duty of witness.

3. **What is the significance of the title *If This Is a Man*?**
   *Model answer:* It poses the book's central question — whether the starved, numbered creature of the camp is still a human being — and forces the reader to answer. The whole testimony is an argument that the victim's humanity survived even a system built to destroy it.

4. **How is dehumanization shown in the arrival chapters?**
   *Model answer:* Through the precise, step-by-step stripping of name, clothing, possessions, hair, and dignity, ending in a tattooed number. Levi documents the systematic unmaking of the person without editorial comment, which makes it more devastating.

5. **Why does Levi insist that "the drowned" are the true witnesses?**
   *Model answer:* Because those who died experienced the camp's full destruction and cannot speak, while survivors are, by definition, the exceptions. This refuses any heroic or consoling narrative of survival and confronts the limits of testimony itself.

6. **What does the "Canto of Ulysses" episode assert?**
   *Model answer:* That memory, culture, and the human mind retain meaning even in the camp, as Levi clings to Dante's verse about pursuing virtue and knowledge. It is a fragile affirmation of humanity against a system designed to extinguish it.

7. **Why might restraint be more powerful than explicit emotion here?**
   *Model answer:* Restraint lets the facts speak and trusts the reader, avoiding the numbing or distancing effect of melodrama. It also models Levi's central act — preserving the rational, observing mind under conditions meant to destroy it.

8. **How does Levi's pre-war identity as an assimilated Italian shape his testimony?**
   *Model answer:* It gives his account a disabused clarity: he believed himself a secure modern citizen and learned that belief was no protection. His vantage shows that the catastrophe reached the assimilated and the pious alike.

9. **What is gained by reading the poem and the prose together?**
   *Model answer:* The poem states the moral demand — remember, or be cursed — and the prose enacts the experience that demand refers to. Together they unite the imperative of witness with the substance of what must be witnessed.

10. **Levi grew more pessimistic over time about whether the lesson was learned. Why does that matter for us as readers?**
    *Model answer:* It implicates us directly: the value of testimony depends on the listener, and forgetting is an active danger. Reading Levi is not a passive act of sympathy but the acceptance of a charged responsibility.

### Anticipated Difficulties

This is the gravest material in the course, and the room will feel it. Some students will go silent; some may become visibly upset; a few may deflect with intellectualizing or, rarely, nervous humor. Hold the seriousness without scolding anyone for how they cope. Build in silence and do not rush to fill it. Do not, under any circumstances, open or close this class with a joke. Students may not know the *Shema* prayer; explain it plainly so the poem's force lands. Some will want a redemptive takeaway ("but he survived," "but love endures") — gently resist that, because Levi himself resisted it; the integrity of his witness depends on refusing false comfort. Remind students at the end of the previous session that this is coming, and remind them at the end of *this* session that Sessions 9 and 13 continue the reckoning, so no one is caught off guard.

## Session 9 — Paul Celan: "Death Fugue" and Late Short Poems

### At a Glance
The second Shoah session, and in some ways the most difficult: where Levi sought clarity, Paul Celan, the supreme poet of the catastrophe, pressed the German language itself — the language of the murderers and of his murdered mother — to its breaking point. "Death Fugue" is the most famous poem of the Holocaust; his later poems grow ever more compressed, broken, and silent. The session confronts the question Adorno made famous — whether poetry is even possible after Auschwitz — and asks how language can carry what language was used to destroy. Handle this session with the same gravity as Session 8.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Paul Celan was born Paul Antschel in 1920 in Czernowitz, the capital of Bukovina — then a province at the eastern edge of Romania, formerly Habsburg Austria, today in Ukraine ([Poetry Foundation, "Paul Celan"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/paul-celan); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Paul-Celan)). "Celan" is an anagram of the Romanian form of his surname, a name he made for himself. He grew up in a German-speaking Jewish family in a famously multilingual city; his mother in particular cherished German language and literature and raised him in it. This is the central, agonizing fact of his life and art: German was his mother tongue, the language of his beloved mother, and it became the language of those who murdered her. Celan would spend his career writing his greatest poetry *in German* — refusing to abandon it, while forcing it to register what had been done.

The Shoah destroyed his family and his world. In 1941 the German and Romanian forces took Czernowitz; the Jews were ghettoized and then deported. In 1942 Celan's parents were deported to a camp in Transnistria; his father died there, probably of typhus, and his mother was shot — murdered, in the accounts Celan absorbed, with a bullet to the neck ([Danish/German Wikipedia and standard sources](https://da.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Celan)). Celan himself, away from home the night of the deportation, survived; he was then held in Romanian forced-labor camps until 1944. He emerged having lost his parents, his community, and the secure ground of his native language — carrying what he would call an *Erinnerungswunde*, a "memory-wound" that would not close. Nearly all his poetry flows from this wound.

After the war he lived briefly in Bucharest and Vienna, then settled in 1948 in Paris, where he remained for the rest of his life, working as a teacher of German language and literature and as a translator (of Shakespeare, Rimbaud, Mandelstam, Dickinson, and others). He married the French artist Gisèle Lestrange and they had a son. He published his early masterpiece "Todesfuge" ("Death Fugue") — written in the years just after the war and printed in his collections of the early 1950s — and went on to produce a series of increasingly spare, difficult, and original volumes. Over time his poetry moved away from the relative lyricism and musicality of "Death Fugue" (which he later grew wary of, fearing it was too beautiful, too consumable) toward radical compression: broken words, coined compounds, silences, a language pared toward the unsayable.

His later years were shadowed by recurring mental crises and by a painful, prolonged plagiarism accusation (the "Goll affair," in which the widow of the poet Yvan Goll falsely accused Celan of plagiarism) that wounded him deeply and which he experienced as a renewed antisemitic persecution. He was hospitalized several times. In late April 1970 — the exact date is uncertain — Paul Celan drowned himself in the Seine; his body was recovered on May 1, near Courbevoie outside Paris ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Paul-Celan); [German Wikipedia](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Celan)). He was forty-nine. He is buried in the Thiais cemetery near Paris.

His legacy is immense and forbidding. Celan is widely regarded as the most important European poet of the postwar period and the indispensable poet of the Shoah — the writer who confronted, more radically than anyone, the question of whether and how the catastrophe could be put into language at all. His response to Theodor Adorno's famous dictum that "to write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric" was not to fall silent but to remake poetry from the inside, wresting from German a strange, broken, utterly original speech adequate to grief. He also articulated, in his great prose statement *The Meridian* (1960), a vision of the poem as a message in a bottle, sent toward an uncertain "you," an act of reaching across an abyss toward another person. Students should leave understanding that Celan's difficulty is not obscurity for its own sake but the necessary shape of an attempt to say what ordinary language cannot, in the very tongue that had been turned to murder.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Celan's Bukovina deserves a moment, because its world is gone. Czernowitz was a jewel of the old Habsburg Empire — a cultivated, multilingual city where German served as the language of high culture among a large and flourishing Jewish population, alongside Romanian, Ukrainian, Yiddish, and Polish. It produced an extraordinary German-language Jewish literary culture (the poet Rose Ausländer was among Celan's circle). The Shoah annihilated this world utterly: the Jews of Czernowitz were murdered or scattered, and the German-Jewish culture of Bukovina ceased to exist. Celan is, in a sense, its last great voice, carrying a destroyed civilization's language into exile in Paris.

The deepest context for Celan is the problem of language itself after the Shoah, and you should put this squarely before students. German was the language of Goethe and Rilke and Celan's own mother — and it was the language of the camps, the orders, the propaganda, the murderers. For a German-speaking Jewish poet whose mother was shot, every word in his native tongue was contaminated and beloved at once. Celan's whole project is the attempt to use this language while registering its corruption — to make it bear witness against itself. This is the most intense version of the course's recurring concern with sacred and ordinary language strained past its limits (recall the unspoken Name in Session 1, Levi's rewritten prayer in Session 8).

Adorno's dictum — "to write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric" — frames the entire postwar debate and should be introduced here, with the qualification that Adorno himself later complicated it, and that Celan's work is the great counter-argument: not that poetry is impossible, but that it must be utterly transformed. Set against this the long Jewish tradition of lament (the Book of Lamentations, the elegies for destroyed Jerusalem) and you can show students that Celan is both unprecedented and ancient — the newest and the oldest of grief.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Death Fugue" (*Todesfuge*, c. 1945–48).** Celan's most famous poem, structured musically like a fugue, with phrases that recur and interweave. It is spoken in the collective voice of Jewish camp prisoners. The hypnotic refrain — "Black milk of daybreak we drink it at evening / we drink it at midday and morning we drink it at night" — runs through the poem; the prisoners "drink and drink," dig a grave in the air "where you won't lie too cramped." A German camp commandant figure dominates: "A man lives in the house he plays with the serpents he writes / he writes when it grows dark to Germany your golden hair Margareta." This man whistles for his Jews and his dogs, orders the prisoners to dig and to play dance music, and brandishes a gun ("a man lives in the house... he plays with his vipers"). The poem repeatedly counterposes two women — "your golden hair Margareta" (the German ideal) and "your ashen hair Shulamith" (the murdered Jewess of the Song of Songs). The commandant calls "death is a master from Germany"; he sets the prisoners to play for dancing and shoots them, then "grants us a grave in the air." The poem ends with the two haunting lines that fuse victim and ideal: "your golden hair Margareta / your ashen hair Shulamith."

**Late short poems (e.g., "Psalm," "Tenebrae").** *"Psalm"* is a bitter anti-prayer addressing a God who is "No One": "No one moulds us again out of earth and clay, / no one conjures our dust. / No one." It declares the speakers "the Nothing-, the / No one's-Rose," blooming "towards, against" a "Nothing," with a thread-thin style and a "crown red / from the purple-word" — a flower praising and accusing absence. *"Tenebrae"* (Latin for "darkness," echoing Good Friday liturgy) inverts conventional prayer: "We are near, Lord, / near and graspable." Here it is the murdered who tell God to "Pray, Lord, / pray to us, / we are near." They describe being "clawed into each other," driven "to the trough," drinking blood, and looking out toward God — a blasphemous, anguished reversal in which the dead summon the absent God to pray to *them*. The late lyrics are characteristically compressed, dense, and broken, pushing the German language toward silence.

### Lecture Script

Again tonight I'm going to ask us to begin in silence, the way we did last week. [pause — hold it] We are still in the literature of the Shoah, and tonight we read its greatest poet. I warned you this was coming, and I'll warn you again at the end about the weeks ahead, so no one is caught unprepared. [pause]

Last week, Primo Levi gave us clarity — the scientist's exactness, the calm precise sentence laid against the horror. Tonight, Paul Celan gives us the opposite and the equal: language pushed to its breaking point, because for Celan clarity was not possible and maybe not honest. And I have to tell you the fact that explains everything about him, the fact you must hold in your mind the whole hour. [pause] Paul Celan was born into a German-speaking Jewish family. German was his mother tongue — the language his mother loved, the language she raised him in, sang to him in. And in 1942 his mother was deported and shot. Murdered. In the language of the people who murdered her. [pause] So here is a poet whose mother tongue is also the murderers' tongue — the language of his dead mother and of her killers, the same words. And he made the impossible decision to keep writing his poetry in German. Not to flee the language, but to stay inside it and force it to carry what it had been used to destroy. [pause]

His most famous poem is called "Death Fugue." A fugue — like the musical form, where voices chase and overlap. And it begins with one of the most unforgettable and terrible images in modern poetry: "Black milk of daybreak we drink it at evening." Black milk. Milk is the first nourishment, the mother's gift, white, life-giving — and Celan makes it black, and makes the prisoners drink it, morning and night. The poem circles and repeats like music, and through it moves a figure, a camp commandant, who "plays with the serpents and writes," who orders the prisoners to dig graves and to dance and make music while they do. And it sets against each other two women — "your golden hair Margarete," the German ideal, and "your ashen hair Shulamith," the Jewish woman reduced to ash. [pause]

Now there is a famous saying I want you to know. The philosopher Theodor Adorno wrote that "to write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric." Think about what that means — that after such horror, the beauty of art is somehow obscene, a betrayal. And Celan is the great answer to that. He did not stop writing. But he transformed what a poem could be. His later poems — and we'll read one or two — get shorter and shorter, more broken, full of silences and invented words, language pared down toward what cannot be said. He wasn't being difficult to show off. He was trying to find a speech honest enough for what he had to carry — a wound he called the *Erinnerungswunde*, the memory-wound that would not close. [pause]

He lived out his life in Paris, teaching and translating, married, a father — and in 1970 he drowned himself in the Seine. He was forty-nine. [pause] Tonight we read "Death Fugue," and we'll read it aloud, slowly, and let the repetitions work on us. Then we'll read a late poem and feel how much further into silence he went. As last week: we will leave room for quiet. We will not rush. Let's begin.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

*Read "Death Fugue" aloud, slowly, ideally in a translation that preserves the German refrains.*

**1. "Black milk of daybreak we drink it at evening / we drink it at midday and morning we drink it at night."** What to notice: the oxymoron "black milk" (nourishment turned to poison/death) and the disorienting collapse of time ("evening... midday... morning... night"). What to bring out: the perversion of the most basic, maternal sustenance; time made meaningless in the camp. The repetition is incantatory, fugue-like — beauty and horror fused. Discussion: why is "black milk" so much more powerful than a literal description?

**2. The commandant: "A man lives in the house he plays with his serpents he writes / he writes when it grows dark to Deutschland your golden hair Margarete."** What to notice: the murderer is cultured — he writes, he loves a German woman, he is sentimental about home — even as he orders atrocity. What to bring out: the chilling coexistence of high culture and mass murder, a theme Celan shares with the whole German question. The "golden hair Margarete" evokes Goethe's *Faust* — German high culture implicated.

**3. "your golden hair Margarete / your ashen hair Shulamith."** What to notice: the paired women — the blond German ideal and the Jewish woman whose hair is "ashen" (the ash of the crematoria, and Shulamith from the Song of Songs). What to bring out: in two lines, the whole opposition of murderer-culture and murdered-people; "ashen" is unbearably literal. This couplet is the poem's devastating close.

**4. "Death is a master from Germany" (*der Tod ist ein Meister aus Deutschland*).** What to notice: the refrain that names the catastrophe's source directly and turns "mastery" — craft, expertise, the German cultural ideal of *Meisterschaft* — into the mastery of killing. What to bring out: the indictment folded into the language's own vocabulary of excellence. Memorable, quotable, terrible.

**5. A late short poem (e.g., "Psalm": "No one kneads us again out of earth and clay, / no one conjures our dust. / No one. // Praised be your name, No One.").** What to notice: the compression, the address to "No One," the wrenching of liturgical praise toward a God who is absent or annulled. What to bring out: the movement from the relative fullness of "Death Fugue" toward broken, near-silent speech; the theological devastation (a prayer to "No One"). Connect to Levi's rewritten *Shema* and to the unspoken Name of Session 1 — the sacred language pushed to its limit.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

*As with Session 8, lead slowly and allow silence.*

1. **Why did Celan keep writing in German, the language of his mother's murderers?**
   *Model answer:* Because German was also his mother's language and his own, and to abandon it would surrender it to the murderers. He chose instead to stay inside it and force it to bear witness against itself — the central paradox of his art.

2. **What does the image "black milk" accomplish?**
   *Model answer:* It fuses nourishment and death, turning the white, maternal, life-giving substance into something black and poisonous that the prisoners must drink endlessly. Its impossibility conveys the camp's reality better than any literal description could.

3. **Why is the murderer in "Death Fugue" depicted as cultured — writing, loving, sentimental?**
   *Model answer:* To expose the terrible coexistence of high German culture and mass murder. The commandant's refinement makes the horror more, not less, monstrous and implicates a whole cultural inheritance.

4. **What is the effect of pairing "golden hair Margarete" with "ashen hair Shulamith"?**
   *Model answer:* It sets the idealized German woman against the Jewish woman reduced to ash in two stark lines. "Ashen" is at once a color and the literal residue of the crematoria, compressing the genocide into an image.

5. **What does the refrain "Death is a master from Germany" indict?**
   *Model answer:* It names the source of the catastrophe and corrupts the German ideal of mastery and craft into expertise at killing. The indictment is built from the culture's own prized vocabulary.

6. **How and why does Celan's later poetry differ from "Death Fugue"?**
   *Model answer:* It grows shorter, more broken, full of silence and coined words, moving toward the unsayable. Celan came to distrust the relative beauty and accessibility of "Death Fugue" and sought a more honest, pared-down speech for grief.

7. **How should we understand Adorno's claim that poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric, and Celan's response?**
   *Model answer:* Adorno warned that art risks making beauty out of atrocity, an obscenity. Celan answered not by silence but by transforming poetry from within, making a broken language that registers the catastrophe rather than aestheticizing it.

8. **In "Psalm," what is the significance of addressing "No One"?**
   *Model answer:* It turns a prayer of praise toward an absent or annulled God, voicing theological devastation after the Shoah. Yet the act of praising at all keeps a fragment of the liturgical impulse alive even in negation.

9. **How does Celan's difficulty serve his subject rather than obstruct it?**
   *Model answer:* The fractured, compressed language enacts the impossibility of ordinary speech adequate to the catastrophe. Difficulty is the honest shape of the attempt, not obscurity for its own sake.

10. **How does Celan relate to the course's thread of sacred and broken language?**
    *Model answer:* From the unspoken Name of Session 1 to Levi's rewritten *Shema*, the tradition strains language before the ultimate. Celan pushes this furthest, wresting from a corrupted tongue a speech adequate to grief and to the silence at its center.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Celan is hard — harder than anything else in the course — and some students will feel locked out by the difficulty. Reassure them that not "solving" the poems is acceptable; the goal is to dwell in them, read aloud, and feel their pressure. Provide the *Shema*/liturgical and *Faust*/Margarete background so the allusions land. As with Levi, expect strong emotion and protect silence; do not open or close with levity. Some students may resist the idea that beauty and atrocity can coexist in one poem; that discomfort is exactly the Adorno problem, so make it explicit rather than smoothing it over. Remind the class, kindly, at the end of the previous session and at the end of this one, that Session 13 (Ozick) returns to this ground.

## Session 10 — Bernard Malamud: "The Magic Barrel"

### At a Glance
After the descent into the Shoah, the course returns to America and to a writer who found the moral and spiritual weight of the Jewish tradition in the lives of ordinary, struggling immigrants and their children. Bernard Malamud's "The Magic Barrel" — about a rabbinical student, a marriage broker, and a photograph that undoes him — is funny, tender, and finally mysterious, a story about love, redemption, and the hidden moral economy of suffering. The session restores comedy and warmth to the room after two grave weeks, while introducing the postwar Jewish American renaissance and Malamud's signature: the Jew as everyman, suffering toward grace.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Bernard Malamud was born on April 26, 1914, in Brooklyn, New York, the son of Russian Jewish immigrants who ran a small grocery store ([Wikipedia, "Bernard Malamud"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Malamud); [Library of Congress, Read.gov](https://www.read.gov/fiction/malamud.html)). The grocery is essential to him: he grew up amid the hard, marginal economic life of the immigrant small shopkeeper, and that world — the failing store, the long hours, the moral pressures of poverty — became the recurring landscape of his fiction (most fully in his novel *The Assistant*). His childhood was not bookish or affluent; his father's store struggled, his mother died when he was young, and the family knew hardship. Out of that unliterary beginning Malamud built himself into one of the major American writers of his generation, which tells students something about the autodidactic, upward-striving energy of the immigrant Jewish world.

He was educated in the New York City public system, took a bachelor's degree from City College of New York in 1936, and later earned a master's from Columbia (his thesis on Thomas Hardy). He worked at a series of jobs — including in a factory and as a clerk — and taught in New York evening high schools before beginning, slowly and with great discipline, to write. He was a famously dedicated craftsman, a reviser and a worker, not a prodigy; his breakthrough came relatively late. In 1949 he took a teaching post at Oregon State College (later University), far from the Jewish New York of his upbringing — an experience of cultural displacement that fed his work — and he later taught for many years at Bennington College in Vermont.

His major work belongs to the great flowering of postwar Jewish American literature. His first novel, *The Natural* (1952), a mythic baseball story, was unlike most of what followed; it was *The Assistant* (1957), set in a failing Brooklyn grocery and drawing on his father's life, that established his characteristic mode — the moral seriousness, the suffering, the redemption sought through hardship. *The Magic Barrel* (1958), his first collection of short stories, won the National Book Award for Fiction — a signal recognition ([Library of Congress](https://www.read.gov/fiction/malamud.html)). His novel *The Fixer* (1966), about a Jew falsely accused of ritual murder in Tsarist Russia, won both the National Book Award and the Pulitzer Prize, making it one of the rare books to take both. He continued to publish novels and stories — *A New Life*, *The Tenants*, *Dubin's Lives*, *God's Grace* — until his death in 1986.

Malamud's languages and milieu place him squarely in the American-born second generation. He wrote in English — but an English haunted by Yiddish, full of inverted syntax, Yiddish-inflected rhythms, and a characteristic music that evokes immigrant speech without merely transcribing it. His Jews are mostly poor, urban, struggling figures — grocers, students, tailors, repairmen — and his great subject is the moral life lived under economic and spiritual pressure. He is often grouped with Saul Bellow and Philip Roth as one of the triumvirate of major postwar Jewish American novelists, but his sensibility is distinct: more parabolic, more drawn to suffering and moral testing, more explicitly concerned with what it means to be a *mensch* — a decent, responsible human being. He famously, and revealingly, resisted the label "Jewish writer" while writing some of the most Jewish fiction in American letters; "All men are Jews," he once said, by which he meant that the Jewish condition of suffering and moral obligation was a universal human condition.

He died in Manhattan in 1986. His legacy is that of a moral fabulist — a writer who took the materials of immigrant Jewish life and made from them parables of conscience, love, and the painful road to grace. For students he is a crucial bridge: he carries the Yiddish-inflected, suffering, ethically serious world of the immigrants forward into mid-century American literature, and he does so with a tenderness and a touch of the magical (note the "magic" in our title) that distinguishes him from the more satirical Roth and the more philosophical Bellow whom we meet in the next two weeks.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Malamud writes out of the world of the American-born children and grandchildren of the great immigration — the second generation that we first glimpsed in Yezierska's tenements, now grown, educated, and moving (sometimes) into the middle class, but still shaped by the immigrant grocery, the Yiddish-speaking parents, the marginal economic life. "The Magic Barrel" is set in this milieu: its protagonist, Leo Finkle, is a rabbinical student in New York; Salzman, the marriage broker (*shadchan*), is a figure straight out of the Old World transplanted to the new. Students should understand the *shadchan* and the institution of the arranged match (the *shidduch*) as living holdovers of traditional Jewish life persisting into modern urban America — the old ways surviving, comically and poignantly, in a changed world.

The larger context is the emergence, after the Second World War, of Jewish American writing as a central force in American literature — arguably *the* defining strand of mid-century American fiction. For complex reasons — the upward mobility of the second generation, the entry of Jews into universities and publishing, the moral authority conferred (terribly) by the Shoah, and the sheer talent of a remarkable cohort — Bellow, Malamud, Roth, Singer (in translation), Paley, Ozick, and others moved from the margins to the center of American letters in the 1950s and after. The New York intellectual scene around journals like *Partisan Review* and *Commentary* was a crucible of this flowering. Malamud is one of its founding figures, and "The Magic Barrel," with its National Book Award, is one of its early monuments.

Finally, the moral-spiritual context. Malamud's fiction is deeply concerned with suffering as a path to moral growth and even redemption — a sensibility with roots in Jewish ethical and Hasidic traditions, though Malamud was himself secular. His characters are tested; they fail and strive and sometimes, painfully, grow. The recurring word and ideal is the *mensch*. "The Magic Barrel" enacts this: a young man who has studied the Law without love discovers, through a photograph and a longing, the possibility of a fully human, fully moral life. The "magic" of the title points to the strain of the marvelous — a residue of the folk and Hasidic imagination we met in Singer — that runs through Malamud's realism.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"The Magic Barrel" (Bernard Malamud).** Leo Finkle, a lonely, bookish rabbinical student about to be ordained at Yeshiva University in New York, decides that having a wife would help him win a congregation, so he engages a marriage broker, Pinye Salzman — a shabby, herring-eating, seemingly itinerant *shadchan* who arrives with a portfolio of client cards he keeps in "a barrel." Salzman presents candidates: a widow, a young woman who turns out to be older than advertised (a "thirty-two" who is really older), a lame girl Salzman tries to pass off favorably. Leo, increasingly dispirited, agrees to meet a schoolteacher named Lily Hirschorn; on their walk Lily, fed romantic exaggerations by Salzman, presses Leo about his passionate love of God — and Leo, mortified, realizes the broker has misrepresented him and that he himself feels no real love of God or even of people. The encounter throws Leo into a crisis: he concludes he came to God not out of love but loveless, and that he has never truly loved anyone. He resolves to find love before God. Rejecting Salzman, Leo nonetheless rifles through the photographs the broker has left behind and is electrified by one — a face that is not young or conventionally beautiful but seems to have "lived... and lived in suffering," a face that draws him irresistibly. He hunts Salzman down to demand to meet her. Salzman, agitated, says she is not for him: she is "a wild one... she is dead to me now," and finally admits, "she is my daughter Stella." Leo, convinced he is meant to redeem her (and perhaps she him), insists on the meeting. The story ends ambiguously and famously: Leo meets Stella under a street lamp at night — she stands smoking, in white with red shoes (suggesting both innocence and sin) — and rushes toward her "with flowers outthrust," while around the corner Salzman, "leaning against a wall, chanted prayers for the dead." The ending leaves open whether this is redemption or a final con.

He wasn't a prodigy; he was a worker, a reviser. *The Magic Barrel*, the collection this story comes from, won the National Book Award. All of Malamud's characters are, in some way, trying — and often failing, and trying again — to become menschen. [pause]

So listen tonight for two things. Listen to the language — it's English, but it's haunted by Yiddish, the word order tilted, a particular music underneath it. And listen for the "magic" in the title, because Malamud is a realist with a touch of the marvelous, a little of that Hasidic, Singer-like sense that the supernatural is just around the corner. ### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. Leo Finkle's reason for seeking a wife — that a congregation prefers a married rabbi.** What to notice: the marriage begins as a career calculation, not a desire. What to bring out: the gap between Leo's bookish, dutiful religiosity and any lived experience of love. Discussion: what is missing from Leo's relationship to the tradition he has studied so devotedly?

**2. Salzman the marriage broker — his shabby appearance, his "magic barrel" of cards, his herring and his patter.** What to notice: Salzman is comic, almost a caricature, yet shrewd and finally uncanny; he smells of fish and seems to materialize and vanish. What to bring out: the Old World *shadchan* transplanted to New York; the comedy of the type, and the hint of something more-than-natural about him. Connect to the marvelous strain (the "magic").

**3. Leo's crisis after meeting Lily Hirschorn — the realization that he does not love God or man.** What to notice: prompted by Lily's questions, Leo confronts the emptiness at the center of his learned piety; he has come to God "not... because I love Him, but because I do not." What to bring out: the story's moral heart — knowledge of the Law without love is hollow; Leo must be broken open. This is Malamud's characteristic suffering-toward-growth.

**4. The photograph of the woman who "had lived, or wanted to — more than just wanted, perhaps regretted it."** What to notice: among the staid cards, Leo finds a face marked by suffering and life, and falls for it instantly and helplessly. What to bring out: love arrives through an image of suffering, not innocence; Leo is drawn to a woman who has known pain. The face, we come to suspect, is Salzman's own daughter, Stella — possibly "fallen," which is why she is not meant to be in the barrel.

**5. The ending — Leo rushing to meet Stella under a streetlamp while Salzman "chanted prayers for the dead."** What to notice: the famously ambiguous final image: redemption, or doom, or both. What to bring out: Malamud refuses to resolve whether this is Leo's salvation (love and full humanity at last) or his ruin, and whether Salzman has engineered it all. The prayers for the dead cast a shadow over the romance. Discussion: is the ending hopeful? Whose redemption is it?

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does Leo Finkle decide to find a wife, and what does his reasoning reveal?**
   *Model answer:* He seeks a wife mainly because a congregation prefers a married rabbi — a career calculation, not desire. It reveals that his devotion to the Law is dutiful but loveless, the central lack the story will expose.

2. **What kind of figure is Salzman, and what is "magic" about him?**
   *Model answer:* He is a comic, shabby Old World marriage broker with a "barrel" of cards, but also shrewd and faintly uncanny, seeming to appear and vanish at will. The magic suggests he may be orchestrating Leo's fate, a touch of the marvelous within the realism.

3. **What does Leo realize during his crisis after meeting Lily Hirschorn?**
   *Model answer:* He realizes he has come to God without love — that his learning has no living feeling beneath it. This breaking-open is the necessary suffering that opens him to genuine human connection.

4. **Why does Leo fall in love with the woman in the photograph?**
   *Model answer:* Because her face shows someone who has lived and suffered, unlike the staid candidates in the cards. Malamud has love arrive through an image of pain rather than innocence, which fits his moral vision of redemption through suffering.

5. **Who is the woman in the photograph likely to be, and why does it matter?**
   *Model answer:* She appears to be Stella, Salzman's own daughter, possibly "fallen," which is why her card was not meant to be among the others. It means Leo is drawn toward someone the conventional world would reject, deepening the story's theme of redemption.

6. **How should we read the ambiguous ending?**
   *Model answer:* It can be salvation — Leo finally choosing love and full humanity — or ruin, or both at once, with Salzman's mourning prayers casting doubt. Malamud deliberately withholds resolution so the reader must weigh hope against foreboding.

7. **What does the ideal of the *mensch* mean in Malamud, and how does it operate here?**
   *Model answer:* A *mensch* is a fully decent, responsible human being. Leo's journey from loveless learning toward risky, compassionate love is a movement toward becoming a real human being rather than a credentialed one.

8. **How does Malamud's English carry the trace of Yiddish?**
   *Model answer:* Through inverted syntax, particular rhythms, and the cadence of immigrant speech, especially in Salzman's dialogue. The music evokes the Old World within American English without simple transcription.

9. **What does Malamud mean by "All men are Jews," and how does the story bear it out?**
   *Model answer:* He means the Jewish condition of suffering and moral obligation is universally human. Leo's very particular Jewish story — a rabbinical student and a matchmaker — dramatizes a universal need to move from duty to love.

10. **How does "The Magic Barrel" connect backward to Singer and forward to the postwar canon?**
    *Model answer:* Its touch of the marvelous and its Old World types recall Singer's folk-Hasidic world, while its American setting and moral seriousness mark the postwar Jewish American renaissance. Malamud bridges the immigrant past and the mid-century literary center.

## Session 11 — Saul Bellow: A Short Essay or the Opening of *Seize the Day*

### At a Glance
We meet the writer many consider the giant of postwar American letters — the only American novelist to win both the Nobel Prize and three National Book Awards — and the man who, more than anyone, made the Jewish American voice the central voice of American fiction. Reading the opening of *Seize the Day* (or a short essay), we encounter Bellow's restless, brilliant, talky prose and his great subject: the modern urban soul, "disaffected by society but not destroyed in spirit," seeking meaning amid material American abundance. The session deepens the postwar canon and introduces the New York intellectual world.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Saul Bellow was born in 1915 — the date conventionally given, though some scholars argue for 1910 — as Solomon Bellows, in Lachine, Quebec, a suburb of Montreal, to Jewish immigrants who had left Russia (Saint Petersburg) for Canada just before his birth ([NobelPrize.org, "Bellow – Biographical"](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/literature/1976/bellow/biographical/); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saul-Bellow)). When he was a child the family moved to Chicago, the city that would become his great subject and home, and which he would render as fully as Dickens rendered London. He grew up multilingual in the characteristic immigrant way: Yiddish at home (he was fluent, and it deeply shaped his English — he later translated Singer's "Gimpel the Fool"), along with Hebrew, French (from his Montreal years), and the vigorous American English of the Chicago streets. This linguistic richness is audible everywhere in his prose, which fuses high European intellectual culture with the demotic energy of immigrant and urban American speech.

He studied at the University of Chicago and then Northwestern, taking his degree in 1937 with honors in anthropology and sociology — disciplines that left their mark on his fiction's restless, idea-saturated quality. He did graduate work, served briefly in the Merchant Marine during the Second World War, and began writing. His early novels, *Dangling Man* (1944) and *The Victim* (1947), were taut and somewhat Europeanized; his breakthrough into his own expansive, exuberant voice came with *The Adventures of Augie March* (1953), which opens with the famous declaration "I am an American, Chicago born" — a sentence that, spoken by the son of Jewish immigrants, effectively claimed the whole American experience for the Jewish American writer. *Augie March* won the National Book Award and changed the trajectory of American fiction.

There followed one of the great careers in American letters: *Seize the Day* (1956), the tight, painful novella we may read from; *Henderson the Rain King* (1959); *Herzog* (1964), his masterpiece, about a cuckolded intellectual writing unsent letters to the living and the dead; *Mr. Sammler's Planet* (1970); *Humboldt's Gift* (1975); and later works including *The Dean's December* and *Ravelstein*. He won the National Book Award three times, the Pulitzer Prize for *Humboldt's Gift*, and in 1976 the Nobel Prize in Literature — the crowning recognition that confirmed the Jewish American writer at the very center of world literature. His Nobel citation honored "the human understanding and subtle analysis of contemporary culture that are combined in his work."

Bellow's relationship to his Jewishness was complex and central. He was thoroughly secular, impatient with piety and with being pigeonholed as a "Jewish writer," and he insisted on his Americanness and on the universality of his concerns. Yet his sensibility — the intellectual restlessness, the moral seriousness, the comic-elegiac voice, the Yiddish substratum, the preoccupation with suffering, memory, and the soul — is unmistakably formed by the immigrant Jewish world of his childhood. He represents, perhaps more fully than anyone, the second-generation American Jew who absorbed the whole inheritance of Western culture and made it his own without ceasing to be marked by where he came from. His personal life was turbulent (five marriages, much-publicized feuds), and his later politics grew conservative, generating controversy; he is not a saint, and you need not present him as one.

Bellow died in 2005 in Brookline, Massachusetts, at eighty-nine ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Saul-Bellow)). His legacy is foundational: he raised the Jewish American novel to the highest level of literary ambition and achievement, fused European intellectual tradition with American vitality, and created a prose style — dense, allusive, funny, soulful — that influenced generations. For students, he is the summit of the postwar renaissance: where Malamud is parabolic and Roth (next week) is satirical, Bellow is philosophical and capacious, the novelist of the examined urban soul.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Bellow is the central figure of the postwar Jewish American literary ascendancy, and his career maps its arc. He emerged from the Depression-era immigrant world of Chicago into the cultural confidence of mid-century America, when, for the reasons sketched in the previous session, Jewish writers moved decisively to the center of American letters. He was closely tied to the world of the *New York intellectuals* — the largely Jewish circle of writers and critics around journals like *Partisan Review*, *Commentary*, and later *The New York Review of Books* (Bellow published *Seize the Day* in *Partisan Review*). This was a milieu of fierce argument, high cultural seriousness, leftist and ex-leftist politics, and enormous intellectual energy, and Bellow both belonged to it and stood apart from it. Students should understand it as the engine room of mid-century American intellectual culture, disproportionately Jewish, and the context in which the Jewish American voice became, briefly, the defining American voice.

Bellow's great subject is the modern self adrift in material abundance — the soul's hunger amid plenty. His Nobel-era America was prosperous, powerful, and spiritually restless, and his protagonists (Augie, Tommy Wilhelm, Herzog, Sammler) are intelligent, suffering men trying to make sense of their lives against the noise and money of the American city. As his Nobel materials put it, he wrote of "modern urban man, disaffected by society but not destroyed in spirit." This places him in a recognizable Jewish lineage — the questioner, the sufferer who endures and seeks meaning — even as he writes in a thoroughly American idiom about thoroughly American lives.

*Seize the Day* (1956) crystallizes this. Set over a single day on Manhattan's Upper West Side, it follows Tommy Wilhelm — a middle-aged failure, estranged from his cold father, who loses his last money in a commodities speculation and ends, famously, weeping at a stranger's funeral. The novella's title is the old *carpe diem*, ironized: Tommy cannot seize his day; he is paralyzed, drowning. It is a small masterpiece of the suffering urban soul, and a useful contrast to Bellow's more expansive, exuberant novels. The world it depicts — the residential hotel, the speculative market, the father-son coldness, the spiritual drowning amid Manhattan plenty — is the postwar American Jewish bourgeoisie at its most anxious.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**The opening of *Seize the Day* (1956).** The novella is set over a single day in a residential hotel, the Hotel Gloriana, on Manhattan's Upper West Side (Broadway in the upper 80s–90s), populated largely by elderly retirees. Its protagonist is Tommy Wilhelm (born Wilhelm Adler) — a heavyset, failing, fortyish man who has lost his job as a salesman, is separated from his wife (who refuses divorce and presses him for money), and is estranged from his children. He came to New York chasing a Hollywood acting career that never materialized (encouraged years earlier by a phony talent scout), and even his name change marks his lifelong self-deception and drift. The opening pages establish his situation as he descends from his hotel room for breakfast, dreading another encounter with his father, the retired and prosperous Dr. Adler, who also lives in the Gloriana. Tommy is broke, panicked about money, and desperate for his cold, self-satisfied father's approval and financial help — which Dr. Adler withholds, ashamed of his son's failures and unwilling to be dragged into his troubles. The opening introduces the day's looming crisis: Tommy has handed over the last of his money to the slippery, voluble Dr. Tamkin, a self-styled psychologist and market guru, to speculate on commodities (lard) in the stock market. The narration immerses us in Tommy's anxious, self-pitying, yet sympathetic inner voice — a man choking on his failures, his appetite, and his need to be loved, on a day that will end with him losing everything in the market and, in the novella's climactic close, weeping uncontrollably at the funeral of a stranger.

### Lecture Script

I want to start tonight with a sentence — maybe the most famous opening sentence in postwar American fiction. It's the first line of a novel called *The Adventures of Augie March*, and it goes: "I am an American, Chicago born." [pause] Now, that's a simple sentence. But listen to who's saying it. It's written by Saul Bellow, the son of Jewish immigrants from Russia, born in Canada, raised speaking Yiddish at home. And he opens his big American novel by declaring, flatly, magnificently: *I am an American.* With that sentence, the son of Yiddish-speaking immigrants reached out and claimed the whole American experience as his to write. That's the moment the Jewish American writer walked into the center of American literature and sat down. And the man who wrote it is the giant of his generation. [pause]

Saul Bellow is, by most reckonings, the most important American novelist of the second half of the twentieth century. He won the National Book Award three times. He won the Pulitzer Prize. And in 1976 he won the Nobel Prize in Literature. No other American novelist has that combination. He was born in 1915, near Montreal, to immigrants from Russia, and grew up in Chicago — and Chicago is to Bellow what London is to Dickens, the great city rendered whole. He was fluent in Yiddish — in fact, he's the one who translated "Gimpel the Fool," the Singer story we read back in week three, into English. So Bellow literally carried the Old World into the new with his own hands. [pause]

Tonight we read from a short, painful, perfect book called *Seize the Day*. The title is the old Latin tag — *carpe diem*, seize the day — but Bellow means it bitterly, because his hero, Tommy Wilhelm, is a man who cannot seize anything. He's a middle-aged failure. He's lost his job, he's losing his money, his marriage is gone, and his father — a successful, icy old doctor living in the same Manhattan hotel — will not help him, will barely acknowledge him. Over the course of a single day, Tommy goes down: he sinks the last of his money into a foolish speculation, and the book ends with him weeping — drowning, really — at the funeral of a man he never knew. [pause]

Here's the anecdote, and it's about the voice. Bellow's prose is unlike anyone else's: it's dense and allusive and full of big European ideas, and at the same time it's full of the noise of the American street, the energy of immigrant speech, the rhythms of Yiddish underneath the English. He once said he wanted to write like a man "who had read everything and lived in a tough city." That's the fusion: the whole library of Western culture and the Chicago sidewalk, in one sentence. He was thoroughly secular, by the way — he resisted being called a "Jewish writer." And yet listen to what his subject is: the suffering soul, the questioner, the man disaffected by the world but not destroyed in spirit, hungry for meaning in the middle of all that American money. That's a very old figure in the tradition we've been studying, wearing a new American suit. [pause] Let's open *Seize the Day* and meet Tommy Wilhelm on the worst day of his life.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The opening description of Tommy Wilhelm descending in the hotel elevator, "concealing his troubles."** What to notice: Bellow establishes Tommy's interior turmoil beneath a performance of ease; the prose moves fluidly between external detail and inner anxiety. What to bring out: the free-indirect immersion in a suffering consciousness; we are inside a drowning man pretending to swim. Discussion: how does Bellow make us feel Tommy's panic without stating it baldly?

**2. The relationship with Dr. Adler, the cold, successful father.** What to notice: the father's withholding, his embarrassment at his failed son, his refusal of money and of love. What to bring out: the father-son coldness — a recurrent pressure in this tradition (recall Kafka, Yezierska) — here rendered as American assimilated respectability that has hardened into contempt. The successful father cannot forgive the unsuccessful son.

**3. Tommy's name and self-invention (he changed it from Wilky Adler).** What to notice: Tommy reinvented himself, Americanized his name, chased Hollywood and success, and failed. What to bring out: the immigrant/second-generation drama of self-fashioning gone wrong; the American dream of remaking oneself curdles, echoing Yezierska. Discussion: what does the name-change cost him?

**4. Dr. Tamkin, the charlatan-philosopher who counsels "seize the day" and takes Tommy's money.** What to notice: Tamkin is a fraud who also speaks, amid his cons, real if garbled wisdom about living in the present and the "real soul" versus the "pretender soul." What to bring out: Bellow's characteristic mixing of the ridiculous and the profound; truth arrives through a swindler. Connect to the course's wise-fool / holy-fool figures, here inverted into a foolish sage.

**5. The ending — Tommy weeping at the stranger's funeral.** What to notice: the famous final scene where Tommy, swept into a stranger's funeral, breaks down into deep, anonymous weeping that exceeds the occasion. What to bring out: the cathartic, ambiguous release — grief for the dead man, for himself, for everything; a drowning that may also be a kind of opening. Discussion: is this Tommy's defeat or his moment of true feeling, his "seizing" of the day at last?

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why is the title *Seize the Day* ironic?**
   *Model answer:* The classical *carpe diem* urges decisive living, but Tommy is paralyzed and cannot seize anything — he loses his money, his dignity, and his day. The title measures the gap between the ideal of vital action and Tommy's drowning passivity.

2. **How does Bellow render Tommy's inner suffering?**
   *Model answer:* Through free-indirect immersion that slides between external detail and anxious interior monologue, so we feel his panic beneath a surface of composure. We experience him as a drowning man pretending to swim.

3. **What is the significance of Tommy's relationship with his father, Dr. Adler?**
   *Model answer:* The cold, successful father withholds money and love, embarrassed by his failed son. It dramatizes the father-son coldness recurrent in this tradition, here as assimilated respectability hardened into contempt.

4. **What does Tommy's name change (from Wilky Adler to Tommy Wilhelm) reveal?**
   *Model answer:* It marks his attempt to reinvent himself and chase American success, an effort that failed. The self-fashioning of the second generation curdles, echoing the broken American dream we saw in Yezierska.

5. **How should we read Dr. Tamkin?**
   *Model answer:* He is a charlatan who fleeces Tommy yet also voices real, if garbled, wisdom about living in the present and distinguishing the "real soul" from the "pretender soul." Bellow characteristically lets truth arrive through a fraud.

6. **What happens, and what does it mean, at the funeral that ends the novella?**
   *Model answer:* Tommy is swept into a stranger's funeral and breaks into overwhelming, anonymous weeping. The release is ambiguous — defeat and breakdown, but also perhaps his first moment of true, unguarded feeling.

7. **How does Bellow's prose style fuse European intellect and American vitality?**
   *Model answer:* It is dense and allusive, full of big ideas, yet alive with the noise and rhythm of the American city and a Yiddish-inflected energy. The fusion lets him write the soul's crisis in a thoroughly urban American voice.

8. **In what sense is Bellow's secular fiction still part of the Jewish tradition?**
   *Model answer:* His subject — the suffering, questioning soul seeking meaning, disaffected but unbroken — is an old Jewish figure in modern dress, and his English carries a Yiddish substratum. The sensibility is formed by the immigrant world even where the content is secular and American.

9. **What does "I am an American, Chicago born" accomplish as a literary gesture?**
   *Model answer:* Spoken by the son of Yiddish-speaking immigrants, it claims the whole American experience as the Jewish American writer's to render. It marks the moment Jewish American fiction moved to the center of American letters.

10. **How does *Seize the Day* compare with Malamud's "The Magic Barrel" in its treatment of suffering?**
    *Model answer:* Both see suffering as central, but Malamud's is parabolic and oriented toward possible redemption, while Bellow's is psychological and philosophical, ending in ambiguous catharsis rather than clear grace. Together they show two modes of the postwar Jewish American soul.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Bellow's prose is dense and digressive, and some students will find it hard to follow the movement between event and idea; encourage them to read for voice and mood as much as plot, and to read passages aloud. The novella's bleakness — a man losing everything in a day — can feel airless after the relief of Malamud; frame Tommy's final weeping as a genuine, if painful, opening. The figure of Tamkin confuses readers who want a clear hero or villain; lean into Bellow's deliberate mixing of fraud and wisdom. Finally, some may want to debate Bellow's later conservative politics or his personal life; acknowledge briefly that he was a flawed and contentious man, then keep the focus on the work.

## Session 12 — Philip Roth: *Goodbye, Columbus* Opening or "Writing American Fiction"

### At a Glance
The youngest and most combative of the great postwar triumvirate, Philip Roth turned a merciless, hilarious, and deeply controversial eye on Jewish American life — its strivers and suburbanites, its hypocrisies and longings — and in doing so asked what it means to write honestly about one's own people. Reading the opening of *Goodbye, Columbus* (or his essay "Writing American Fiction"), we encounter Roth's sharp comic realism and the firestorm it provoked about loyalty, self-criticism, and the writer's freedom. The session foregrounds satire as a mode of belonging and the perennial charge of the "self-hating" Jewish writer.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Philip Roth was born in 1933 in Newark, New Jersey, and raised in the Weequahic neighborhood, a heavily Jewish, lower-middle-class enclave that he would chronicle across his entire career ([Philip Roth Society, "Biography"](https://www.philiprothsociety.org/biography); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Philip-Roth)). His father was an insurance manager; the family was second-generation American Jewish, secure in its identity but anxious about its place, and Roth's fiction would return obsessively to this Newark world — its parents and sons, its ambitions and embarrassments, its particular American Jewishness. He was the great anatomist of the second and third generations: not the immigrants of Yezierska, but their assimilated, striving, suburbanizing children and grandchildren.

He was educated at Bucknell University and the University of Chicago (where he overlapped with the world of Bellow), and he served briefly in the army before an injury. His debut, *Goodbye, Columbus* (1959) — a novella and five stories — announced a major comic talent and won the National Book Award when Roth was just twenty-six. It also ignited the controversy that would shadow much of his career: stories like "Defender of the Faith" and "Epstein" depicted Jews unflatteringly — as social climbers, adulterers, hypocrites — and many in the Jewish community, including rabbis, denounced him as a traitor and a "self-hating Jew" who handed ammunition to antisemites. The young Roth defended himself in essays (notably "Writing About Jews") arguing that the novelist's loyalty is to truth and to the full human reality of his characters, not to communal public relations. This argument — the writer's freedom versus communal loyalty — is one of the richest in the course.

His breakthrough to fame and infamy came with *Portnoy's Complaint* (1969), a scandalous, hilarious, taboo-shattering monologue by a guilt-racked young Jewish man on his analyst's couch — a book that made Roth a celebrity and intensified the charges against him. He then produced one of the longest and most varied major careers in American literature, much of it organized around alter-ego narrators — Nathan Zuckerman and "Philip Roth" himself — through whom he explored the relationship between life and fiction with dizzying ingenuity. The masterpieces accumulated: *The Ghost Writer* (1979), *The Counterlife* (1986), *Operation Shylock* (1993), *Sabbath's Theater* (1995), and the great "American trilogy" — *American Pastoral* (1997, Pulitzer Prize), *I Married a Communist* (1998), and *The Human Stain* (2000) — in which the Jewish American story opens out into the story of postwar America itself. *The Plot Against America* (2004) imagined a fascist, antisemitic United States.

Roth wrote in a vigorous, voice-driven American English, comic and ferocious and supple, and he was perhaps the most relentless self-examiner in American letters — turning his own life, ethnicity, sexuality, and literary identity into endlessly recursive fiction. He won nearly every major American literary honor (multiple National Book Awards and Critics Circle Awards, the Pulitzer, the PEN/Faulkner three times, the PEN/Nabokov and PEN/Bellow lifetime awards) and was, for decades, perennially mentioned for the Nobel he never received. He announced his retirement from writing in 2012 — a rare, clean exit — and spent his last years as the elder statesman and chronicler of his own corpus. He died in 2018, at eighty-five.

His relationship to Jewishness was, like Bellow's, secular but inescapable, and more combative than anyone's in the course. He insisted on his right to portray Jews as flawed human beings rather than as exemplary victims or saints, and he spent a career dramatizing the tension between the individual (especially the artist) and the demands of family, community, and tribe. His legacy is that of the great satirist and self-anatomist of American Jewish life, who expanded what a Jewish writer was permitted to say, and who ultimately used the particular materials of Newark Jewishness to write a panoramic history of the American twentieth century. For students he completes the postwar triumvirate: where Malamud suffers toward grace and Bellow philosophizes, Roth satirizes, provokes, and interrogates the very act of writing about one's own.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Roth's world is postwar, suburbanizing Jewish America — the third act of the immigrant story. The grandchildren of the Lower East Side had, by the 1950s, achieved unprecedented prosperity and security; they moved from the old urban neighborhoods like Weequahic toward the suburbs, into the professions and the middle class, into a confident American identity. Roth is the satirist of this arrival and its discontents: the country-club aspirations, the anxieties about money and status and assimilation, the generational conflict between immigrant-shaped parents and fully American children. *Goodbye, Columbus* skewers exactly this — its romance between Neil Klugman, a working-class Newark library clerk, and Brenda Patimkin, daughter of a newly rich suburban family, is a comedy of class and assimilation within the Jewish community itself.

The deeper context is the controversy over Jewish self-representation, which you should put squarely before students because it animates one of the course's central threads (who belongs to a tradition, and on what terms). In the shadow of the Shoah — barely a decade past when *Goodbye, Columbus* appeared — many American Jews felt that public airing of Jewish flaws was dangerous, even a betrayal: it might feed the antisemitism that had just murdered six million. Roth's insistence on writing Jews as fully human, including unflatteringly, struck these readers as reckless or treacherous, and the epithet "self-hating Jew" was hurled at him repeatedly. Roth's counter-argument — that to deny the novelist the right to portray Jews honestly is to deny that Jews are fully human, and that the antisemite is not appeased by Jewish silence — is a serious position, and the clash between communal loyalty and artistic freedom is genuinely difficult. Let students feel both sides.

His essay "Writing American Fiction" (1961), if you teach it, broadens the frame: there Roth famously argued that postwar American reality had become so extreme and absurd that it outstripped the novelist's imagination — "the American writer in the middle of the twentieth century has his hands full in trying to understand, and then describe, and then make credible much of the American reality." It is a key document of postwar American literary self-understanding and shows Roth thinking beyond the Jewish frame about the writer's task in a media-saturated, sensational America.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**The opening of *Goodbye, Columbus* (1959).** The novella is narrated by Neil Klugman, a working-class Jewish young man from Newark who lives with his Aunt Gladys and Uncle Max and works at the Newark Public Library. In the opening scene, at a country club swimming pool one summer, Neil meets Brenda Patimkin, a beautiful, confident, and affluent Jewish college girl from the prosperous suburb of Short Hills. He famously first sees her at the pool when she asks him to hold her glasses; watching her dive, he is smitten. He phones her and they begin dating. The opening chapters sharply contrast the two worlds: Neil's cramped, anxious, lower-middle-class Newark home, dominated by his fretful Aunt Gladys (who worries over food and the refrigerator), versus the Patimkins' lavish Short Hills house — with its sporting goods (the father, Ben Patimkin, made his fortune in "Patimkin Kitchen and Bathroom Sinks"), its refrigerator in the finished basement stocked with fruit, its lawn, its trees, and its air of arrived, assimilated American abundance. Brenda's family embodies the new postwar suburban Jewish bourgeoisie — athletic, materially comfortable, slightly crude in its prosperity (her brother Ron, the basketball-and-records jock who plays his "Goodbye, Columbus" Ohio State graduation record; her mother's suspicion of Neil's prospects). The novella's central tensions — class envy and aspiration, sexual freedom (the later episode of the diaphragm), the satire of assimilated suburban Jewishness, and Neil's ambivalent attraction to and judgment of the world Brenda represents — are all set in motion by these opening pages, which Roth renders with sharp comic detail and a keen, sometimes acid social eye.

### Lecture Script

Tonight we meet the troublemaker. Of the three great writers we're spending these weeks with — Malamud, Bellow, and tonight Philip Roth — Roth is the youngest, the funniest, the most ferocious, and by far the most controversial. When his first book came out, rabbis denounced him from the pulpit. He was called a traitor, a "self-hating Jew," a writer who handed weapons to antisemites. He was twenty-six. And the book won the National Book Award. So tonight we have to talk not just about a writer but about a fight — a fight about what a writer is allowed to say about his own people. [pause]

Here's the book. *Goodbye, Columbus.* It's a comic love story, set in and around Newark, New Jersey, where Roth grew up. A young man named Neil Klugman — working-class, a clerk at the public library — falls for a young woman named Brenda Patimkin, whose family has just gotten rich and moved to the leafy suburbs. And what Roth does, with a scalpel, is anatomize the world of newly prosperous American Jews: the country club, the nose jobs, the fridge in the finished basement stuffed with fruit, the anxieties about money and status, the whole comedy of a people that has just made it in America and isn't quite sure how to hold its new wealth. It is very funny. It is also merciless. [pause]

Roth was born in 1933 in Newark, in a Jewish neighborhood called Weequahic — and he spent his whole life writing about that world, the world of the second and third generation. Not the immigrants of Anzia Yezierska anymore — their grandchildren, the assimilated, striving, suburbanizing American Jews. And here's the thing that got him in so much trouble. He wrote them as *human beings* — which means he wrote them as flawed: social climbers, adulterers, hypocrites, alongside the decent and the loving. And remember when this was: 1959. The Holocaust was barely a decade in the past. Many Jews felt that to show Jewish flaws in public was dangerous — that it fed the very hatred that had just murdered six million people. So when Roth wrote a Jewish character behaving badly, a lot of people heard betrayal. [pause]

And Roth fought back, in essays, with an argument I want you to take seriously, because it's one of the real questions of this whole course. He said: to forbid me from showing Jews as fully human — including their faults — is itself to deny that Jews are fully human. The novelist's loyalty, he said, is to the truth, not to public relations. And he said the antisemite is not appeased by Jewish silence anyway. [pause] Now — is he right? Or did his critics have a point, that in a dangerous world a writer bears some responsibility to his vulnerable community? I genuinely want you to argue this tonight, because there's weight on both sides, and it goes to the heart of what we've been asking all term: who belongs to a tradition, and what do they owe it? [pause] One more thing to listen for: the voice. Roth writes American English at full throttle — fast, funny, alive, the sound of a smart wise-guy who has read everything. Let's open *Goodbye, Columbus* and walk into the Patimkins' world.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The opening: Neil's first sight of Brenda at the country-club pool, asking him to hold her glasses.** What to notice: the comedy of class and gaze; Neil, the poor Newark cousin, looking up (literally) at the suburban Brenda. What to bring out: the social precision — Roth maps intra-Jewish class distinctions with a satirist's exactness. The romance is also a class transit. Discussion: what does Neil desire — Brenda, or what she represents?

**2. The Patimkin house and its abundance (the famous fruit refrigerator, the sporting goods, the finished basement).** What to notice: Roth lavishes detail on the material plenty of the newly rich suburban family. What to bring out: abundance as the subject — the comedy and slight grotesquerie of arrival, of a people suddenly surrounded by more than it knows what to do with. Echo of Bellow's "plenty amid which the soul hungers," but in a satirical key.

**3. Mr. Patimkin and the immigrant-to-suburb arc (Patimkin Kitchen and Bathroom Sinks).** What to notice: the self-made father whose plumbing-supply fortune built this world; his rough vitality versus his children's softness. What to bring out: the generational drama — the striving immigrant-shaped father and the assimilated children he made possible; assimilation as both triumph and loss. Connect to Yezierska's and Bellow's fathers.

**4. Moments of satire on Jewish self-consciousness (Brenda's nose job; anxieties about appearance and status).** What to notice: Roth's needling of assimilation's bodily and social anxieties. What to bring out: precisely the material that enraged critics — Jews depicted as vain, status-anxious, assimilation-driven. Use this to open the self-representation debate directly. Discussion: is this contempt, or honest comedy, or love?

**5. (If using "Writing American Fiction") Roth's claim that reality outstrips the novelist's imagination.** What to notice: the argument that postwar American actuality is so sensational it defeats invention. What to bring out: Roth thinking about the writer's task beyond the Jewish frame; a key statement of postwar literary self-understanding. Connect to the comedy of *Goodbye, Columbus* as a response to an absurd, abundant America.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What is the social comedy at the center of *Goodbye, Columbus*?**
   *Model answer:* The romance between working-class Neil and newly rich, suburban Brenda stages class difference *within* the Jewish community. Roth maps intra-Jewish status distinctions with satirical precision, so the love story is also a study of assimilation and arrival.

2. **What does the Patimkins' material abundance signify?**
   *Model answer:* It embodies the postwar triumph of the immigrant story — a people suddenly prosperous and suburban — and its slight grotesquerie. The overflowing fruit refrigerator is comic and faintly disquieting, plenty that the family scarcely knows how to inhabit.

3. **What generational drama plays out between Mr. Patimkin and his children?**
   *Model answer:* The self-made, rough-edged father built the wealth that made his children soft and assimilated. It dramatizes assimilation as both achievement and loss, echoing the father-figures in Yezierska and Bellow.

4. **Why did *Goodbye, Columbus* and the accompanying stories provoke such anger?**
   *Model answer:* They depicted Jews unflatteringly — as vain, status-anxious, hypocritical, or adulterous — barely a decade after the Shoah, when many felt such portrayals were dangerous. Critics called Roth a traitor and "self-hating," feeling he fed antisemitism.

5. **What was Roth's defense, and is it persuasive?**
   *Model answer:* He argued that denying the novelist the right to show Jews' faults denies that Jews are fully human, that his loyalty is to truth not public relations, and that antisemites are not appeased by silence. It is a serious position, though one can still weigh it against a writer's responsibility to a vulnerable community.

6. **Is Roth's satire an act of contempt, of honesty, or of love?**
   *Model answer:* A strong case exists for all three, and the ambiguity is the point. Mockery this precise usually requires intimate knowledge and attachment; Roth skewers the world he came from because he knows and, arguably, loves it.

7. **How does the question Roth raises connect to the course's thread about belonging to a tradition?**
   *Model answer:* It asks what a member of a tradition owes it — fidelity, flattery, or honest critique. Roth claims that the freedom to criticize from within is itself a form of belonging, not a betrayal of it.

8. **How does Roth's prose voice differ from Bellow's and Malamud's?**
   *Model answer:* It is faster, funnier, more colloquial and combative — a wise-guy American voice at full throttle. Where Bellow philosophizes and Malamud parables, Roth performs and provokes.

9. **(If using the essay) What does Roth mean that reality outstrips the imagination?**
   *Model answer:* He argues postwar American actuality is so extreme and sensational that it defeats the novelist's powers of invention and credibility. It reframes the writer's task as wrestling with an absurd, media-saturated America.

10. **What does the postwar suburban Jewish world tell us about the immigrant story's arc?**
    *Model answer:* It is the third act: the grandchildren of the Lower East Side have arrived into prosperity and security. Roth shows that arrival brings its own discontents — status anxiety, assimilation's losses, and the comedy of not knowing how to hold one's success.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The self-representation controversy is the live wire of this session, and in a contemporary classroom it can touch present-day debates about identity, "airing dirty laundry," and who may criticize whom; let students argue it, but keep it anchored in Roth's specific 1959 context and the shadow of the Shoah. Some students will find Roth's satire cruel or off-putting; press them on whether precise mockery can coexist with affection. Others may not register the intra-Jewish class distinctions that drive the comedy; supply a sentence on Newark versus the suburbs. Finally, Roth's reputation (the later scandalous books, debates about his treatment of women) may surface; acknowledge it briefly and keep the focus on the text at hand.

## Session 13 — Cynthia Ozick: "The Shawl"

### At a Glance
The third and final Shoah session, and the one that asks how the catastrophe reaches those who came after. Cynthia Ozick — among the most learned and morally serious of American writers, and a fierce defender of the Jewish particularity of her art — wrote in "The Shawl" a brief, devastating story of a mother, an infant, and a death in a concentration camp. We read it as the culmination of the course's reckoning with the Shoah and as a meditation on memory, transmission, and whether art may touch such suffering at all. Handle this session with the gravity of Sessions 8 and 9.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Cynthia Ozick was born in 1928 in New York City and grew up in the Pelham Bay section of the Bronx, the daughter of Russian Jewish immigrants who ran a pharmacy ([Poetry Foundation / Jewish Women's Archive, "Cynthia Ozick"](https://jwa.org/encyclopedia/article/ozick-cynthia); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Cynthia-Ozick)). Unlike the immigrants of Yezierska's generation, she was American-born, but she grew up close to the immigrant world — her family's pharmacy, the Yiddish and the Jewish learning around her — and she absorbed early both a deep American literary culture and a serious engagement with Judaism and its texts. She has described the antisemitism she encountered as a child, and the formative experience of being made to feel an outsider, as shaping her sense of herself as a Jew and a writer. She studied at New York University and took a master's at Ohio State, writing a thesis on Henry James — and James's influence, the high-literary devotion to the art of fiction, runs alongside her Jewish commitments throughout her work.

Her path to recognition was slow and arduous, a fact worth sharing with students as a portrait of literary vocation. She spent years on an ambitious, James-influenced novel and labored in relative obscurity well into her thirties; her first novel, *Trust*, appeared in 1966, when she was nearly forty. But she emerged as one of the most formidable American writers of her generation, distinguished above all by the union of two qualities that rarely coexist at her level: a dazzling command of English prose and a profound, learned engagement with Jewish tradition, text, and theology. She is, with Saul Bellow, among the few major American writers genuinely steeped in Jewish learning, and she has thought harder than almost anyone about what it means to make Jewish art.

Ozick is celebrated above all as an essayist and a writer of short fiction. Her essay collections (*Art & Ardor*, *Metaphor & Memory*, *Quarrel & Quandary*, and others) are among the finest in contemporary American letters, and they articulate her central preoccupations: the danger and seduction of idolatry (she is wary of art that becomes a graven image, a substitute for the moral and the holy), the relationship of the writer to tradition, the ethics of imagination. Her fiction includes the celebrated stories of *The Pagan Rabbi*, the novella *The Shawl* with its sequel *Rosa*, and novels such as *The Messiah of Stockholm* (her homage to Bruno Schulz, whom we met in Session 7) and *The Puttermesser Papers*. Her work is allusive, intellectually demanding, and morally intense.

"The Shawl" (first published in *The New Yorker* in 1980) and its companion "Rosa" together form her most widely read work and her direct confrontation with the Shoah. "The Shawl" is very short — a few pages — and almost unbearable: it depicts Rosa, a young mother in a concentration camp, her infant daughter Magda hidden in a shawl, and the niece Stella; and it moves to the moment of Magda's death at the electrified fence. Ozick, who did not experience the camps herself, approached this material with great hesitation and a sense of trespass; she has spoken of her wariness about making fiction of the Shoah, of the risk that imagination might violate or aestheticize the unspeakable. That hesitation is itself part of the story's meaning and a crucial point for students: "The Shawl" is written by someone acutely conscious that she has no right to the experience and writes anyway, under the pressure of memory and obligation.

Now in her nineties, Ozick continues to write, and her legacy is that of a moral and intellectual conscience of American letters — a defender of the seriousness of literature and of the specificity of the Jewish imagination against assimilation into a generic universalism. For our course she is the essential figure of the *second generation* of Shoah writing: not a survivor like Levi or Celan, but an American Jew who inherited the catastrophe as memory and responsibility, and who confronts the question of whether and how those who came after may speak of it. She closes the course's Shoah arc by turning it toward the future — toward transmission, inheritance, and the long aftermath.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Ozick writes from a particular and important vantage: postwar American Jewry living in the shadow of an event it did not directly experience but cannot escape. By the time she came to maturity as a writer, the Shoah had become the central fact of modern Jewish consciousness, and a generation of American Jews — safe, prosperous, American — had to reckon with their relationship to a catastrophe that had consumed their European cousins. This is the condition of *postmemory* (a term you may borrow from the scholar Marianne Hirsch): the inheritance of trauma by those who did not live it but who grew up in its aftershadow, for whom the event is at once intimate and inaccessible. "The Shawl" and "Rosa" are central texts of this condition. The sequel "Rosa" is set decades later, in Florida, among elderly survivors — showing the long, distorting afterlife of the trauma in American exile.

The deepest context is the ethical debate, running through the whole second half of our course, about representing the Shoah in art — the debate we framed with Adorno in Session 9. Ozick is an unusually self-conscious participant. Her wariness about idolatry — about art that sets itself up as a substitute for the holy — makes her acutely uneasy about turning Auschwitz into beautiful fiction, and yet she wrote "The Shawl," one of the most powerful imaginative treatments of the catastrophe. The story holds this tension inside itself: it is spare, refusing spectacle, and its central image — a shawl that shelters, nourishes (Magda sucks it), and finally fails to save — works by suggestion rather than description. Students should understand that Ozick's hesitation is not weakness but conscience, and that "The Shawl" earns its right to exist precisely by its restraint and its sense of trespass.

Finally, place Ozick within the course's thread on women. Like Yezierska, Lazarus, and Paley (next week), she is a major woman writer in a tradition whose classical center was male; unlike them, she is deeply learned in the very textual tradition that historically excluded women, and she writes from inside it as well as against it. "The Shawl" is also, centrally, a story of *mothers* — of maternal love and its annihilation — which gives the Shoah a specifically female face, the mother who cannot save her child.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"The Shawl" (Cynthia Ozick).** A very short, intense story set during the Holocaust, on a forced march and then in a concentration camp. It follows three figures: Rosa, a young mother; her fifteen-month-old infant daughter, Magda; and Rosa's niece, the starving fourteen-year-old Stella. Magda is hidden inside Rosa's shawl, which the baby clings to and sucks for comfort and nourishment in place of Rosa's dried-up breasts; the shawl is "magical" — it keeps Magda quiet, hidden, and somehow alive even as she should have died of hunger. Magda is described with strange beauty, with round blue eyes and yellow hair "as light as air," so fair she could almost pass for an "Aryan" child. In the camp, the perpetually freezing, ravenous Stella covets the warmth of the shawl. One day Stella takes the shawl for herself. Deprived of it, the now-walking Magda toddles out from the barracks into the open roll-call arena (the *Appellplatz*), wailing aloud for the first time in her life — her voice exposing her. Rosa is paralyzed by an impossible choice: run out to grab Magda, or run back for the shawl to silence her and lure her back. She races for the shawl, but it is too late: a helmeted German guard has already seen the child. The guard scoops Magda up and, in the story's horrifying climax, hurls the baby against the electrified fence; Magda flies "like a butterfly" through the air and is killed on the wires. Rosa, knowing that if she screams or runs she too will be shot, stuffs the shawl into her own mouth to stifle her cry, "swallowing up the wolf's screech," and stands frozen, drinking in the taste of the shawl, until the screaming inside her subsides.

### Lecture Script

I'm going to ask us, one more time this term, to begin in silence. [pause — hold it] This is the last of our three weeks with the literature of the Shoah, and in some ways it is the hardest, though the story is only a few pages long. I told you it was coming. We will read it with the same care we brought to Levi and to Celan. [pause]

Here is the difference this week. Primo Levi was there. Paul Celan lost his mother to it. They are witnesses. But Cynthia Ozick was not there. She was a young woman in the Bronx during the war, safe in America. And so tonight we ask a new and difficult question: what is the relationship to this catastrophe of those who came *after* — who did not live it, but who inherited it, who grew up in its shadow, who carry it as memory and as obligation even though it is not, strictly, their own? That is the condition of most of us in this room, in fact. And it was Cynthia Ozick's condition, and she made from it one of the most devastating short stories in the language. [pause]

I have to tell you that Ozick herself was deeply uneasy about writing this. She is one of the most learned and morally serious writers we have — a great essayist, steeped in Jewish tradition — and she has a deep wariness about art, about the danger of making something beautiful out of something holy or something unspeakable. She felt she had no right to this material, that to imagine Auschwitz was a kind of trespass. And she wrote it anyway, under a pressure she couldn't refuse. I want you to hold that as you read: this is a story written by someone who is not sure she should be writing it. And that hesitation, that conscience, is *in* the story — it's why the story is so spare, why it refuses spectacle, why it works by suggestion and leaves so much in silence. [pause]

The story is called "The Shawl." It is about a young mother named Rosa in a concentration camp, and her baby daughter, Magda, whom she hides and shelters in a shawl — a shawl that the baby sucks on for comfort, that keeps her hidden, that is, for a while, the baby's whole world and her protection. And there is an older child, Rosa's niece Stella, who is cold, and starving, and who does something — I won't say what — that leads to the most terrible thing in the story, at the electrified fence of the camp. It is a story about a mother's love and the annihilation of that love. It gives the Holocaust a mother's face: the mother who cannot save her child. [pause]

We will read it, perhaps partly aloud, and we will leave a great deal of room for silence — more than usual. I will not rush us toward any comfort, because the story does not offer one, and it would be a betrayal of it to pretend otherwise. What the story offers instead is memory — the refusal to let this be forgotten — which is, you'll remember, exactly what Primo Levi demanded of us in "Shema," all those weeks ago: carve it in your hearts. Tonight we keep that promise. [pause] Let's begin, gently.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

*Read with care; allow silence around these passages.*

**1. The opening: the three figures on the march — Rosa, Stella, and Magda hidden in the shawl.** What to notice: the compressed, sensory, almost hallucinatory prose; the reduction of human beings to bodies, hunger, cold. What to bring out: Ozick's spareness — she gives us the camp through a few intense images, not panorama. The shawl is introduced at once as shelter and concealment. Discussion: how does the restraint intensify rather than soften the horror?

**2. The shawl as a "magic" object that nourishes the baby.** What to notice: Magda sucks the shawl "instead of milk"; the shawl substitutes for the mother's failed body, keeping the child alive and quiet. What to bring out: the terrible transformation of an ordinary object into the thin thread of a child's survival; maternal love displaced into a piece of cloth. Connect to the failure of nourishment in Celan's "black milk."

**3. Magda's first steps and her cry — the loss of the shawl.** What to notice: when Stella takes the shawl, Magda, uncovered, cries out and toddles into the open of the camp yard. What to bring out: the unbearable mechanics of the catastrophe — a child's natural need (the shawl, the cry) becomes the cause of her death. Ozick withholds judgment of Stella; the camp, not the starving child, is the murderer.

**4. The death at the fence.** What to notice: the moment is rendered obliquely, at a distance, with strange beauty (Magda "swimming through the air"), and Rosa's stifling of her own scream. What to bring out: the aestheticized image is morally fraught — Ozick risks beauty at the worst moment, and we must ask why. Rosa cannot cry out (a scream would mean her own death); the shawl that saved the child now stifles the mother's grief. The image is meant to sear, not to console.

**5. Rosa stuffing the shawl into her own mouth to keep from screaming.** What to notice: the final use of the shawl — to silence the mother's anguish so she will not be shot. What to bring out: the shawl's full arc, from shelter to nourishment to the gag that stifles a mother's scream; love, survival, and the suppression of grief compressed into one object. Tie to the course's long thread of the unspeakable and the stifled voice — from the unspoken Name to Celan's broken speech.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

*Lead slowly; protect silence.*

1. **How does Ozick's restraint shape our experience of "The Shawl"?**
   *Model answer:* By giving the camp through a few intense images rather than panorama, she forces the reader's imagination to supply what she withholds, which intensifies the horror. The spareness also enacts her sense that the material must not be aestheticized or exploited.

2. **What does the shawl come to mean across the story?**
   *Model answer:* It moves from shelter and concealment, to a substitute for milk that keeps Magda alive, to the gag Rosa stuffs in her own mouth to stifle her scream. The single object carries love, survival, and the suppression of grief in turn.

3. **Why does Magda die, and how does Ozick handle blame?**
   *Model answer:* Magda is uncovered when Stella takes the shawl, cries, and wanders into the open, where she is killed. Ozick withholds judgment of the starving Stella; the camp itself, not the child, is the murderer.

4. **How should we read the strange beauty of the death scene ("swimming through the air")?**
   *Model answer:* The aestheticized image is deliberately fraught: Ozick risks beauty at the worst possible moment, which can sear rather than console. It raises the Adorno problem directly — whether beauty has any place in representing atrocity.

5. **What is distinctive about Ozick's position relative to Levi and Celan?**
   *Model answer:* She was not a witness; she inherited the Shoah as an American Jew who did not live it. Her work embodies the second generation's "postmemory" and the question of whether those who came after may speak of the catastrophe at all.

6. **Why was Ozick hesitant to write this story, and how does that hesitation appear in it?**
   *Model answer:* She feared that imagining Auschwitz was a trespass and that art might aestheticize the unspeakable. The hesitation shows in the story's extreme restraint, its refusal of spectacle, and its reliance on silence and suggestion.

7. **How does "The Shawl" give the Shoah a specifically maternal face?**
   *Model answer:* It centers a mother's love and its annihilation — Rosa nourishing and hiding Magda, then forced to stifle her own grief. The catastrophe is felt as the mother who cannot save her child.

8. **How does the story connect to Levi's demand in "Shema"?**
   *Model answer:* Levi commanded us to remember and never forget; Ozick's act of writing, despite her misgivings, is an act of memory and transmission to those who came after. The story keeps the promise the course made in Session 8.

9. **What does the suppressed scream add to the course's thread of the unspeakable?**
   *Model answer:* From the unspoken Name in Session 1 to Celan's broken language, the tradition repeatedly confronts what cannot be said; here a mother must literally stifle her voice to survive. The silenced scream is the thread's most physical, terrible form.

10. **Does "The Shawl" offer any consolation, and should it?**
    *Model answer:* It offers none, and that refusal is faithful to its subject; the only "redemption" is the act of remembrance itself. To impose comfort would betray both the story and the dead it honors.

### Anticipated Difficulties

This is the most intense single text in the course, and a brief story can hit harder than a long one. Expect strong emotion; protect silence and do not rush. As with Sessions 8 and 9, never open or close with levity, and remind students at the end of the previous session that it is coming. The aestheticized death image troubles some readers, who feel beauty is obscene here; do not resolve this for them — make it the discussion, framed by the Adorno problem you raised with Celan. Some students may want to blame Stella; gently redirect them to Ozick's withholding of judgment and to the camp as the true agent. Finally, signal clearly at this session's close that the course now turns away from the abyss — toward Paley's comedy, Amichai's modern Hebrew, and the living contemporary voices — so the term does not end in the dark.

## Session 14 — Grace Paley: "A Conversation with My Father"

### At a Glance
After the gravity of the Shoah sessions, we turn to a writer of extraordinary warmth, wit, and humanity — Grace Paley, the great chronicler of working-class New York women, whose short, deceptively casual stories carry enormous moral and emotional weight. "A Conversation with My Father" is a story about storytelling itself: a daughter and her dying father argue about what a story should be, and in their argument we find Paley's whole vision of art, hope, and the refusal of tragic inevitability. The session restores comedy and ordinary life, returns women's voices to the center, and quietly reflects on the course's own enterprise — what stories are for.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Grace Paley was born Grace Goodside in 1922 in the Bronx, New York, the daughter of Jewish immigrants from Ukraine (then the Russian Empire) who had fled tsarist persecution; her father, who had been exiled to Siberia for socialist activity before emigrating, became a doctor in New York ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Grace Paley"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Grace-Paley); [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/grace-paley)). She grew up in a household alive with Russian, Yiddish, and English — her first languages, she said, were Russian and Yiddish — and with passionate political talk; the family's socialist, secular, immigrant Jewish culture shaped her permanently. This is essential background: Paley's Jewishness is secular, left-wing, and Yiddish-inflected rather than religious, and her sensibility — the irreverence, the moral seriousness, the love of talk, the solidarity with the struggling and the ordinary — comes straight out of that immigrant-radical Bronx world.

She studied briefly at Hunter College and the New School, where she heard W. H. Auden lecture, but she did not complete a degree; she married young, raised two children, and lived the life of a New York mother and homemaker before and during the years she began to write. This matters because Paley's great subject is precisely that life — the lives of women, mothers, neighbors, divorcées, the everyday dramas of apartments and playgrounds and kitchens that "serious" literature had largely overlooked. She came to writing fiction relatively late and slowly, and her output was famously small: just three slim collections of stories over decades. But the quality and originality were such that she became, on the strength of that small body of work, one of the most admired American short-story writers of the century.

Her first collection, *The Little Disturbances of Man* (1959), announced a wholly distinctive voice — comic, vernacular, formally inventive, attentive to the speech and inner lives of working-class New York women. It was followed by *Enormous Changes at the Last Minute* (1974), which contains "A Conversation with My Father," and *Later the Same Day* (1985). Many of her stories feature Faith Darwin, a recurring semi-autobiographical figure — a writer, mother, and activist navigating the disorder of urban life. *The Collected Stories* (1994) gathered them and was a finalist for both the Pulitzer Prize and the National Book Award. She also wrote poetry and essays. Her prose is unmistakable: short, elliptical, packed with idiomatic speech, refusing conventional plot, finding the comic and the heartbreaking in the same breath, deeply attentive to voice.

Paley was, equally, a lifelong political activist — a pacifist, feminist, and anti-war organizer whose commitments were inseparable from her art. She protested the Vietnam War (traveling to Hanoi), the nuclear arms race, and many other causes; she helped found organizations and was repeatedly arrested for civil disobedience. She taught for many years at Sarah Lawrence College. This activism is not a sidelight but part of her vision: her belief that ordinary lives matter, that the powerless deserve voice and solidarity, and — crucially for our story this week — that the future is *open*, that nothing is fated, that people can change. She died in 2007 in Vermont, at eighty-four.

Her legacy is that of a writer who made high literary art out of the talk of ordinary women, who proved that the short story could carry as much moral and emotional weight as the novel, and who united aesthetic radicalism with political conscience. For our course she is the essential later voice of secular, left, Yiddish-inflected American Jewish culture, and — with Yezierska, Lazarus, and Ozick — a major woman in the tradition. She also offers, after the abyss of the Shoah sessions, a vision of hope as a deliberate, almost ethical choice: the refusal, dramatized in our very story, to accept that any life's ending is foreclosed.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Paley's world is the secular, progressive, immigrant-descended Jewish New York of the twentieth century — a culture distinct from both the religious tradition and the suburbanizing strivers Roth satirized. Her people are the children and grandchildren of the radical immigrant Jews: the union members, the socialists and communists, the secular intellectuals and activists, the inhabitants of the Bronx and the Lower East Side and Greenwich Village who carried Old World Yiddish culture into American left politics. The talk-saturated, argumentative, morally engaged texture of her fiction comes from this milieu. Students should understand that "Jewish" here means a cultural and political inheritance — Yiddish rhythms, a passion for justice, a love of argument — rather than religious observance.

Her work also belongs to two important literary-historical currents. First, the flowering of the American short story and of formally adventurous "postmodern" fiction in the 1960s and '70s: Paley's elliptical, anti-plot, voice-driven stories are formally radical, and "A Conversation with My Father" is, among other things, a metafiction — a story about the making of stories. Second, the emergence of women's writing and feminist literary culture: Paley gave literary dignity to domestic and female experience that the canon had marginalized, and she is a foundational figure for later women writers. These two currents meet in her work: formal experiment in the service of women's ordinary lives.

"A Conversation with My Father" sits at a precise intersection of the personal and the aesthetic. The narrator's father, eighty-six and dying, asks her to write "a simple story" of the old-fashioned kind — "the kind de Maupassant wrote, or Chekhov" — with a recognizable plot and characters who meet their fates. The daughter resists, because she rejects plot, "the absolute line between two points," as a lie about life: "not for literary reasons, but because it takes all hope away. Everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life." The argument between them is generational, aesthetic, and finally philosophical — and it is, not incidentally, an argument between a daughter and a dying father, which gives the abstract debate an unbearable tenderness. It is the perfect penultimate text, because it asks, openly, what stories are *for* — the question underlying the entire course.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"A Conversation with My Father" (Grace Paley).** A metafictional story narrated by a writer-daughter at the bedside of her eighty-six-year-old father, who is dying of a weak heart. The father asks her to write him "a simple story" of the kind he admires — "the kind de Maupassant wrote, or Chekhov" — a recognizable tale with real people to whom "something happens." The daughter, who dislikes plot ("the absolute line between two points") because it "takes all hope away," reluctantly obliges and tells a brief, bare story: a woman in their neighborhood had a son; to be close to him she became a drug addict like him; then the son grew up, kicked the habit, and left her, disgusted with her addiction, leaving her alone. The father criticizes the story as too sketchy — he wants detail, background, the woman's appearance, her history. So the daughter retells it more fully and richly: now the mother is a smart, lapsed woman who, to stay connected with her teenage son, joins his drug-and-hippie counterculture; she becomes a successful junkie; the son eventually goes clean, becomes scornful of her weakness, and abandons her; a former sweet young man from the local "Headbusters" coffeehouse who admired the son tries to comfort her. The father is still dissatisfied and, more importantly, insists on the tragic logic of the ending: the woman is finished, "Poor woman. Poor girl... The end. The end. You were right to put that down. The end." The daughter resists his pessimism, arguing the woman need not be doomed — she could change, get a job, find a new life ("She's only about forty," she could become a counselor in a clinic). The father, speaking from the perspective of a dying man who knows tragedy is real, rebukes her hopefulness as a refusal to face life's truth: "Tragedy! You too. When will you look it in the face?" The story dramatizes a generational and artistic argument — between the father's belief in fixed, fated endings and the daughter's insistence on open-endedness and the possibility of change — that is also, beneath it, a conversation about her father's own approaching death.

### Lecture Script

After three hard weeks, I want to begin tonight by telling you something hopeful, because the writer we meet tonight believed in hope as a kind of moral duty, and so do I, and we've earned a little of it. Grace Paley once wrote a story in which a daughter and her dying father argue about what a story should be — and in that small domestic argument, she put her whole vision of life. Tonight that's our story, and I think you're going to love her. [pause]

Here's the setup. The narrator — a writer, much like Paley herself — is sitting with her father, who is eighty-six and dying. And the father makes a request: write me a simple story, he says, the kind Chekhov wrote, or de Maupassant — a real story, with a plot, with people you can recognize, where things happen and characters meet their fate. And the daughter doesn't want to. She tries — she writes a little story to please him — but she keeps refusing to give it a real plot, a fixed ending. And when he pushes her, she finally says why. She says she hates plot — "the absolute line between two points" — not for literary reasons, but because, she says, it "takes all hope away. Everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life." [pause]

Sit with that line, because it's the heart of everything. *Everyone deserves the open destiny of life.* The daughter refuses to write an ending that forecloses her character's future — because to her, fixing a fate, saying "this is how it must end," is a kind of despair, even a kind of death. And of course the terrible irony, the thing that makes the story break your heart, is that she's saying this to her father, who *is* dying, whose story *is* ending, and who knows it. He says to her, gently, brutally: "When will you look it in the face?" [pause]

The woman who wrote this, Grace Paley, was born in the Bronx in 1922, the daughter of Jewish immigrants from Ukraine — her father had been exiled to Siberia as a young socialist before he got out and became a doctor in New York. Her first languages were Russian and Yiddish. She grew up in a house full of political argument and Yiddish warmth, and that's the kind of Jewish she is — not religious, but secular, left-wing, passionate about justice, in love with the talk of ordinary people. And here's something remarkable: she wrote only three thin books of short stories in her whole life. That's it. And on the strength of those three small books she became one of the most beloved and important American short-story writers of the century. She proved a story could hold as much as a novel. [pause]

She was also, her whole life, an activist — a pacifist, a feminist, arrested again and again for protesting war and injustice. And that's not separate from her writing; it's the same thing. She believed ordinary lives matter, that the powerless deserve a voice, and that the future is *open* — that nothing is fated, that people can change. That belief is the soul of tonight's story. [pause] So listen for two things: listen to the voice, which is pure New York, funny and quick and warm; and listen to the argument between the daughter and the father, because it's really an argument about what we've been doing together all term — what stories are *for*. Let's read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The father's request: "I would like you to write a simple story just once more... the kind de Maupassant wrote, or Chekhov, the kind you used to write."** What to notice: the father wants traditional, plotted, "recognizable" storytelling; he equates it with the real and the honest. What to bring out: the generational and aesthetic divide — old-world clarity versus the daughter's modern openness. The request is also a father asking his child to do something for him before he dies. Discussion: why does the kind of story matter so much to him?

**2. The daughter's first attempt — a bare, plotless paragraph — and the father's dissatisfaction ("You misunderstood me on purpose").** What to notice: she gives a deliberately skeletal "story" and he sees through it. What to bring out: the comedy and the love in their sparring; she is withholding the satisfactions of plot, and he knows it. The metafiction — a story containing the writing of a story — lets us watch art being argued into being.

**3. The expanded second version (the mother who becomes a drug addict to be close to her son, then is left alone).** What to notice: the daughter fills in the story but stops at the bleak point, refusing to let the character's fate be sealed. What to bring out: her insistence that the woman *could* change, could recover, could have a future — that the ending is not fixed. This is the philosophical core enacted in craft.

**4. "Everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life."** What to notice: the daughter's explicit credo, given "not for literary reasons" but as an ethic of hope. What to bring out: Paley's whole vision — that to foreclose a fate is a small despair, and that art should keep the future open. Connect to the course: against the deterministic horror of the Shoah, this is a deliberate, hard-won insistence on openness and possibility. Memorize-worthy.

**5. The father's reply: "When will you look it in the face?"** What to notice: the dying father's gentle, devastating insistence on tragic reality — some stories *do* end, his among them. What to bring out: Paley does not let the daughter win the argument; the father's mortality is the unanswerable counter-evidence. The story holds hope and tragic fact in unresolved tension — and that refusal to resolve is the deepest honesty. Discussion: who is right?

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does the father want "a simple story" with a real plot?**
   *Model answer:* He values the clarity and honesty of traditional, plotted storytelling and equates it with facing reality. The request is also a dying father asking his daughter to do something for him, which charges the aesthetic preference with love and urgency.

2. **Why does the daughter refuse to write plot?**
   *Model answer:* She rejects "the absolute line between two points" because fixing a character's fate "takes all hope away." For her, leaving the ending open is an ethical act, since "everyone deserves the open destiny of life."

3. **How does the story function as metafiction?**
   *Model answer:* It is a story about the writing of a story, letting us watch the daughter compose and revise within the larger narrative. The form embodies the very debate about what fiction should do.

4. **What is the effect of the daughter revising her invented story across the conversation?**
   *Model answer:* Each revision dramatizes her refusal to seal the character's fate, stopping short of a fixed bleak ending. We see her aesthetic of openness enacted in real time as craft, not just stated as theory.

5. **What does the credo "everyone deserves the open destiny of life" mean?**
   *Model answer:* It holds that to foreclose any fate, real or fictional, is a small despair, and that art should keep the future possible. Against determinism it asserts hope, change, and human possibility as ethical commitments.

6. **Why is it significant that this argument is between a daughter and a dying father?**
   *Model answer:* The father's mortality is the unanswerable counter-evidence to the daughter's optimism: his story really is ending. The tender, painful context turns an aesthetic debate into a confrontation with death.

7. **Does the story take sides between the father and the daughter?**
   *Model answer:* No — it holds their positions in unresolved tension. The daughter's hope and the father's "When will you look it in the face?" are both honored, and that refusal to resolve is the story's deepest honesty.

8. **How does Paley's voice and form differ from the writers we have read?**
   *Model answer:* Her prose is short, elliptical, idiomatic, and anti-plot, finding comedy and heartbreak in the same breath. She makes high art from the everyday talk of ordinary women, with a formal radicalism distinct from Bellow's density or Roth's performance.

9. **How is Paley's Jewishness expressed if not through religion?**
   *Model answer:* Through a secular, left-wing, Yiddish-inflected culture of argument, warmth, and solidarity with the struggling. Her sensibility comes from the immigrant-radical Bronx world rather than from observance.

10. **How does this story reflect on the whole course's enterprise?**
    *Model answer:* It directly asks what stories are for — clarity and fate, or openness and hope — which is the question underlying everything we have read. Coming after the Shoah sessions, its insistence on the "open destiny of life" is a hard-won answer to determinism and despair.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Paley's deliberate plotlessness can frustrate students who, like the father in the story, want a "real" plot — which is, delightfully, the story's own subject, so turn that frustration into the discussion. Her elliptical, idiom-heavy prose moves fast and assumes a New York ear; read passages aloud to catch the rhythm and humor. Some students may try to declare a "winner" of the daughter-father debate; resist that and keep both positions alive. After three Shoah weeks, the lighter tone is welcome, but note that Paley is not merely light — the dying father gives the comedy its gravity, so do not let the class read it as simply charming.

## Session 15 — Yehuda Amichai: Selected Short Poems

### At a Glance
We arrive in modern Israel and meet its most beloved poet — Yehuda Amichai, who wrote a new kind of Hebrew poetry: colloquial, tender, ironic, intimate, and saturated with the ancient liturgy even as it described love affairs, wars, and ordinary days in Jerusalem. Reading a selection of his short poems, students encounter the rebirth of Hebrew as a living spoken and literary language and the distinctively Israeli fusion of the sacred and the secular, the ancient and the everyday. The session opens the course's final movement — the tradition arriving at a homeland and a revived tongue — and prepares the turn to contemporary voices.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Yehuda Amichai was born Ludwig Pfeuffer in 1924 in Würzburg, Germany, into an Orthodox Jewish family, and grew up speaking both German and Hebrew ([Poets.org / Academy of American Poets, "Yehuda Amichai"](https://poets.org/poet/yehuda-amichai); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Yehuda-Amichai)). This German-Jewish, observant childhood is the foundation beneath his later secular, Israeli identity, and its traces — the rituals, the prayers, the Hebrew of the synagogue — never left his poetry. In 1936, as the Nazi threat rose, his family emigrated to Mandatory Palestine, settling eventually in Jerusalem, the city that would become the great subject and beloved ground of his life's work. He thus belongs to the generation that left Europe just in time; the world he was born into was destroyed, and like so many in this course he carried the memory of a vanished European Jewish life into a new home and a new language.

His languages tell the story of modern Jewish history. Born to German and the Hebrew of prayer, he became one of the supreme poets of *modern, spoken* Hebrew — the language revived, against all linguistic odds, from a sacred and scholarly tongue into the living, daily, colloquial speech of a new nation. Students should grasp how extraordinary this is: Hebrew had not been an everyday spoken language for some two thousand years, surviving as the language of liturgy and study while Jews spoke Yiddish, Ladino, Arabic, and the languages of their host countries. The Zionist movement and the modern state revived it as a vernacular, and Amichai is one of the first great poets to write in this reborn everyday Hebrew — and crucially, one of the first to bend it toward the conversational, the ironic, the intimate, rather than the prophetic or the grand. He has been called the poet who taught modern Hebrew poetry to speak in a human, ordinary voice.

He came of age as the State of Israel was being born, and war marked his life as it marks his work. He served with the British Army's Jewish Brigade in the Second World War, then fought in Israel's 1948 War of Independence and in later conflicts. War — its losses, its absurdity, its intrusion into private life — runs through his poetry, never glorified, always seen from the vantage of the individual human being who would rather be making love or sitting with his child. After the war he studied Hebrew literature and biblical texts at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, became a teacher, and began publishing the poetry that would make him, in time, the most widely translated and internationally celebrated Israeli poet, and the most beloved within Israel itself.

His major collections (in Hebrew, and in the English translations by Chana Bloch, Stephen Mitchell, Robert Alter, and others) established his characteristic voice: deeply personal, frequently about love and loss and fatherhood and the city of Jerusalem; wittily ironic; and constantly weaving the language and imagery of Jewish liturgy and the Bible into utterly secular, contemporary scenes. He takes the sacred phrases that every Hebrew speaker knows from prayer and turns them, tenderly or ironically, toward a woman's body, a soldier's death, a quarrel, a bus ride. This double exposure — the ancient holy language laid over modern secular life — is his signature and his deepest theme. He was honored with the Israel Prize, the nation's highest, and was a perennial figure in conversations about the Nobel. He died in Jerusalem in 2000.

His legacy is foundational to Israeli literature and beloved far beyond it. He made modern Hebrew poetry intimate and human; he gave secular Israelis a way to feel their connection to the ancient tradition through irony and tenderness rather than piety; and he wrote some of the most quoted love poems and elegies in the modern Jewish world. For our course he is the essential representative of the tradition's arrival in a homeland and a revived language — the answer, in a sense, to the long exile that began in Session 1. Yet his work complicates any triumphant reading: his Jerusalem is layered with war, loss, and the weight of all that history, and his exile, students will see, has become an *inner* condition even after the physical homecoming. He is the bridge from the European and American chapters to the living present.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Amichai's context is the birth and life of modern Israel, and students need a brief, fair sketch. The Zionist movement, arising in the late nineteenth century partly in response to European antisemitism (recall Lazarus's early proto-Zionism in Session 5), sought a Jewish homeland in the ancestral land; waves of Jewish immigration to Ottoman and then British-Mandate Palestine culminated, after the Shoah, in the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948. Amichai's adult life is coextensive with the state's: he fought in its founding war and lived through its subsequent conflicts. His poetry is steeped in this reality — the wars, the bereavements, the militarized everyday — but always from the human, individual angle, skeptical of grand national rhetoric and tender toward private life. You can present him as a poet who loves his country and his city while remaining wary of the abstractions that send the young to die.

The single most important cultural fact for reading Amichai is the revival of Hebrew, which we should dwell on. The transformation of Hebrew from a sacred, frozen, scholarly language into a living vernacular — accomplished within a few generations, an event without real parallel in linguistic history — is the precondition of his art. And it created a peculiar and rich situation: every word of modern spoken Hebrew carries the echo of its ancient, biblical, liturgical uses. When an Israeli says an ordinary word, the prayer-book and the Bible murmur underneath it. Amichai exploits this constantly, and it is the reason his apparently simple, conversational poems are so resonant: the sacred substratum is always there, available for tenderness or irony. This is the culmination of the course's "text as homeland" thread — here the homeland and the text (the language itself) have, astonishingly, fused.

Finally, place Amichai within the long arc of Jewish exile. From Babylon (Session 1) through the shtetl, the immigrant tenement, and the European catastrophe, the tradition has been an imagination of displacement. With Amichai we reach the apparent end of exile — a Jewish state, a Jewish city, a revived Jewish language. And yet his poetry is full of loss, transience, ruins, and an ironic awareness that arrival does not cure the human condition. Exile, in Amichai, becomes interior and existential even after the physical homecoming. This is a subtle and important point for the capstone: the homeland is real, and the longing does not end.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Selected short poems of Yehuda Amichai (representative paraphrases).** *"God Has Pity on Kindergarten Children"*: God pities little children, less the schoolchildren, and not at all the grown-ups, whom He leaves to crawl on hands and knees through the burning sand toward the first-aid station, "bleeding." But, the poem says, perhaps He will watch over true lovers and shelter them as a tree shelters a sleeper on a public bench — and to such lovers we should give the last rare coins of compassion that our mother left us, so their happiness will protect us now and on other days. *"A Pity. We Were Such a Good Invention"*: a lover's lament built on the metaphor of the body as an invention or apparatus. The beloved's arms were unfastened from the speaker; the lovers were "a good and loving / invention," like "an airplane made from a man and wife," but now they have been disassembled — "they took us apart / each from the other" — and the speaker mourns that so fine a working device has been dismantled. *"Tourists"*: a sardonic poem about visitors to Jerusalem who reduce the living city and its suffering people to photo-ops and historical sights. The speaker mocks tour guides who point at biblical monuments and walls; the bitter punch line comes when a guide directs tourists to an ancient Roman arch — and the speaker thinks redemption will come only when a guide tells the group, "You see that man with the baskets? Just right of his head there's an arch from the Roman period," and adds, "But never mind: a little to the left and lower down there's a man buying fruit and vegetables for his family." *"Jerusalem"* and the other short lyrics typically fuse the city's ancient, sacred weight with ordinary modern human life — love, war, parents, laundry — in plain, ironic, deeply tender language, finding eternity inside the everyday.

For about two thousand years, Hebrew was not a language anyone spoke at home. It was the holy language — the language of the Bible, of prayer, of study. And then, within just a few generations, as part of the Zionist movement and the building of modern Israel, Hebrew was *woken up* — revived as an everyday spoken language, the language of buses and newspapers and lovers' quarrels and children's games. There is no other example like it in human history: a sacred language brought back to ordinary life. [pause]

And here is what that miracle did to poetry. When an Israeli speaks modern Hebrew, every ordinary word still carries the echo of its ancient use, because the same words have been in the Bible and the prayer book for three thousand years. He writes simple, conversational, tender, funny poems — about love, about his father, about a bus ride, about war — and underneath every line, the old holy language is humming. He takes the sacred phrases that every Hebrew speaker knows from prayer and lays them over a woman's body, or a soldier's death, or an argument with someone he loves. [pause]

He was born in Germany in 1924, into an Orthodox Jewish family — so his childhood was full of that prayer-Hebrew — and his family got out, to Palestine, in 1936, just in time. The world he was born into was destroyed. He grew up in Jerusalem, fought in Israel's wars, and became the most beloved poet his country has produced. And here's the thing I most want you to feel about him: he loved his country and his city, and he never, ever glorified war. His poems look at war from the point of view of the individual human being who would much rather be in bed with the person he loves than dying for an abstraction. Listen to one title: "A Pity. So we've come, in a way, to the end of the long exile we started with — Babylon, the shtetl, the tenement, the camps — and here is a homeland, a city, a living holy-and-ordinary language. ### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "God Has Pity on Kindergarten Children" — the descent from the liturgical to the human.** What to notice: the poem opens with a prayer-like cadence about God's pity for children, less for schoolchildren, and none at all for adult lovers, whom it then tenderly defends. What to bring out: the signature move — sacred language ("God has pity") turned toward secular love; the irony that God's mercy runs out exactly where adult human love begins, so the poet must supply the tenderness. Discussion: is this irreverent, devout, or both?

**2. "A Pity. We Were Such a Good Invention" — love rendered in the language of machinery.** What to notice: the lovers described as a dismantled invention, "taken apart"; the tonal mix of wit and grief. What to bring out: Amichai's characteristic irony as a vehicle for real feeling; the playful conceit carries genuine loss. The modern, secular imagery (invention, flying) against the ache of separation.

**3. A Jerusalem poem (e.g., "Tourists" or "Jerusalem").** What to notice: the city layered with history, religion, tourists, and ordinary life; Amichai's insistence on the human being amid the monuments ("redemption will come only if... a man... buys fruit and vegetables for his family"). What to bring out: his refusal to let the sacred city's grandeur eclipse the individual; the everyday as the true holy. Connect to "text as homeland" — here homeland and history press on daily life.

**4. "My Father" or another elegy.** What to notice: the tender, concrete memory of the father, often set against war and loss; the German-Jewish past surfacing. ### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why is the revival of Hebrew so important for reading Amichai?**
   *Model answer:* Hebrew was revived from a sacred, unspoken language into an everyday vernacular, so every ordinary word carries an ancient liturgical echo. Amichai's apparently simple poems resonate because the Bible and prayer book hum beneath his conversational lines.

2. **What is Amichai's signature poetic move?**
   *Model answer:* He lays the sacred language of prayer and scripture over secular, modern, intimate scenes — love, war, family, the city. The double exposure of holy and everyday is both tender and ironic and is his deepest theme.

3. **How does "God Has Pity on Kindergarten Children" use liturgical language?**
   *Model answer:* It borrows the cadence of prayer to claim God's pity for children but not for adult lovers, whom the poet then defends himself. The irony is that divine mercy runs out where human love begins, so tenderness becomes a human task.

4. **How does Amichai treat war?**
   *Model answer:* Never with glory, always from the standpoint of the individual who would rather be loving and living. War appears as loss and absurd intrusion into private life, seen with skepticism toward national abstraction.

5. **What does Amichai's Jerusalem look like, and what does he insist on?**
   *Model answer:* It is layered with history, religion, tourists, and ordinary life, but he insists on the human being amid the monuments — the man buying fruit for his family. He refuses to let sacred grandeur eclipse the everyday, which he treats as the true holy.

6. **How does Amichai's European, German-Jewish past surface in his Israeli poetry?**
   *Model answer:* Through elegies and memories, especially of his father, and an awareness of the world destroyed in the Shoah. The lost European life is carried, tenderly, into the homeland and its revived language.

7. **In what sense does Amichai represent the "end" of the course's exile arc?**
   *Model answer:* He writes from a Jewish homeland, a Jewish city, and a revived Jewish language — the apparent answer to the displacement that began in Babylon. Homeland and text have, astonishingly, fused in the living tongue.

8. **Yet how does exile persist in Amichai even after the homecoming?**
   *Model answer:* His poems are full of loss, ruins, and transience, suggesting that arrival does not cure the human condition. Exile becomes interior and existential, a longing that continues even at home.

9. **Is Amichai irreverent or devout toward the tradition?**
   *Model answer:* Both at once: he handles sacred language with playful irony yet with evident love and intimacy. He offers secular Israelis a way to feel connected to the ancient tradition through tenderness rather than piety.

10. **How does Amichai bring together the course's thread of sacred text repurposed?**
    *Model answer:* From the unspoken Name, through Levi's rewritten "Shema" and Celan's "Psalm," the tradition strains and transforms sacred language. In a living Hebrew, Amichai shows the sacred inescapably present within the secular, completing the thread.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Students reading Amichai only in translation miss the buried Hebrew allusions that give the poems their depth; flag a few of the most important liturgical echoes explicitly so the resonance is not lost. The history of Israel may invite present-day political debate; keep the focus on the poems and on Amichai's own humane, war-skeptical vantage, and gently defer contemporary controversy. Some students find the apparent simplicity of the poems deceptive and may underrate them; slow down and show how much the plainness carries. The miracle of Hebrew's revival is unfamiliar to most; take the time to explain it, as it is the key to everything.

## Session 16 — Capstone & Contemporary Voices: Krauss, Englander, Keret

### At a Glance
The final session brings the tradition into our own moment and then hands it to the students. We read a very short piece by one of three living writers — Nicole Krauss, Nathan Englander, or Etgar Keret — to see how the threads of the whole course (exile, the text as homeland, the Shoah and after, comedy and survival, who belongs, the role of women) live on and transform in contemporary fiction. ### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Rather than a single life, this session offers three contemporary writers, each carrying the tradition forward in a distinct key. Sketch all three briefly, then let your students' interests and the assigned piece determine the focus.

**Nicole Krauss** was born in 1974 in New York and educated at Stanford, Oxford, and the Courtauld Institute ([standard reference; Granta "Best Young American Novelists" 2007](https://granta.com); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nicole-Krauss)). She emerged as a major literary novelist with *The History of Love* (2005), an intricate, moving novel that braids the Old World and the new, a lost Yiddish manuscript, Holocaust survivors, and contemporary New York — a book deeply engaged with memory, loss, and the survival of texts. Her later novels — *Great House* (2010), *Forest Dark* (2017) — continue her preoccupation with inheritance, Jewish identity, and the weight of the past. She is, with several others, central to a generation of American Jewish writers exploring the long aftermath of the Shoah and the meaning of Jewishness for the secure, assimilated American descendant. Her work directly takes up the course's threads of the text as homeland and of postmemory.

**Nathan Englander** was born in 1970 in New York and raised in an Orthodox Jewish community on Long Island, a background he left and has written about with both intimacy and critical distance ([standard reference; *Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nathan-Englander)). His debut story collection, *For the Relief of Unbearable Urges* (1999), announced a major talent and ranged across Orthodox life, Soviet Jewry, and the Shoah. His later collection *What We Talk About When We Talk About Anne Frank* (2012) — its title story a brilliant rewriting of Raymond Carver that turns on a grim game about who would hide whom in a second Holocaust — is a touchstone of contemporary Jewish American fiction and a superb capstone text. Englander writes with comic energy, moral seriousness, and a deep engagement with religious tradition and its discontents, carrying forward the line from Singer and Malamud (the religious world) and from Roth (the satirical, self-examining one).

**Etgar Keret** was born in 1967 in Ramat Gan, Israel, the son of Holocaust survivors, and is the most internationally celebrated Israeli writer of his generation ([standard reference; *Encyclopaedia Britannica*](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Etgar-Keret)). He is a master of the very short story — surreal, funny, deadpan, often only a page or two, written in a colloquial, slangy modern Hebrew that descends directly from the conversational revolution Amichai began. His collections (*The Bus Driver Who Wanted to Be God*, *The Nimrod Flipout*, *Suddenly, a Knock on the Door*) blend absurdism, tenderness, violence, and humor, capturing the anxious, militarized, media-saturated texture of contemporary Israeli life. As the child of survivors, he also carries the Shoah as inheritance, often obliquely and through dark comedy. He completes the course's arc by representing the living Hebrew literature of contemporary Israel and the tradition's enduring fusion of comedy and survival.

Together these three writers embody the contemporary tradition: American (Krauss, Englander) and Israeli (Keret); secular-assimilated and Orthodox-raised; engaged with the Shoah as postmemory; comic and grave by turns; written in a revived Hebrew and in a Yiddish-haunted English. They demonstrate that the tradition is alive, mutating, and continuous with everything the course has read. You need not present all three biographies in depth; choose according to the piece you assign and let the students lead.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The context of this session is the present — the condition of Jewish writing in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, a few generations after the immigrant wave, after the Shoah, after the founding of Israel. Several large facts frame contemporary Jewish literature. First, the centrality of *postmemory*: the dominant living writers did not experience the Shoah but inherited it, and much contemporary Jewish fiction (Krauss, Englander, Keret as a survivor's child) circles the long aftermath — how trauma travels down generations, how the descendants relate to a catastrophe at once intimate and inaccessible (recall Ozick in Session 13). Second, the security and assimilation of contemporary American Jewry, which raises, in a new key, the course's question of who belongs to a tradition and what Jewishness means when it is no longer enforced by persecution or poverty. Third, the maturity of modern Israeli literature, writing in a Hebrew now several generations into its revival, fully colloquial and capable of every register from the sacred to the street.

The deeper purpose of this session, however, is synthesis. The whole course has been pulling on six threads — exile, the text as homeland, the Shoah and after, comedy and survival, who belongs to a tradition, the role of women — and the capstone is where students gather them and see the pattern whole. The contemporary writers are ideal for this because every thread is visible in them: Krauss's lost manuscript (the text as homeland; postmemory); Englander's "Anne Frank" game (the Shoah and after; comedy and grief together); Keret's survivor-child surrealism (comedy and survival; the Israeli homeland). Use the contemporary piece as a lens to look back across the entire term, and then turn the lens over to the students.

This is also the moment to make explicit what the course has been quietly arguing: that the "Jewish tradition" of the title is not a checklist (a language, a religion, a set of themes) but a living conversation defined by recurring pressures and questions, carried across centuries, languages, and continents, and now continuing in living writers — and, in a small way, in the students themselves, who have spent sixteen weeks inside it. The tradition is not closed; they have joined the conversation.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Recommended capstone text — "What We Talk About When We Talk About Anne Frank" (Nathan Englander).** Modeled on Raymond Carver's "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love," the story is narrated by a secular Jewish husband whose family has lived in America for generations and who lives in Florida with his Holocaust-obsessed wife, Deb, and their pot-smoking sixteen-year-old son, Trevor. They host Deb's childhood best friend from yeshiva, who has become ultra-Orthodox, moved to Jerusalem, taken the name Shoshana (her husband Mark is now Yerucham), and had ten children. Over a long afternoon, the two couples spar — gently and then sharply — about Jewish identity, observance, intermarriage, and who is "really" Jewish; the opinionated Mark needles the narrator and suggests Trevor isn't a Jew. The couples raid Trevor's marijuana and get high, loosening into intimacy. Finally they play the "Anne Frank game," a grim parlor game from Deb and Shoshana's girlhood: pick a Gentile you know and decide whether, in a second Holocaust, that person would hide you — or turn you in. As they apply the game to friends and neighbors, the talk turns to the secular couple themselves, and the question lands on the marriages in the room. In the quiet, devastating ending, Shoshana realizes — and the reader feels — that she does not believe her devout husband Yerucham would hide *her*; the game exposes the fault lines of trust, faith, and fear running beneath contemporary Jewish life.

**Alternative — "Suddenly, a Knock on the Door" (Etgar Keret).** A very short, comic-absurd metafiction. A writer in Tel Aviv is confronted in his own apartment by a bearded man who pulls a gun and demands, "Tell me a story" — anything to escape "reality." As the writer begins ("Two people are sitting in a room. Suddenly there's a knock on the door"), there is a real knock: a pollster doing a survey forces his way in and, when threatened, also produces a gun and demands a story. Another knock brings a pizza deliveryman, who likewise pulls a weapon and wants a tale. The captors argue about the rules — the Swede forbids any story that begins with a knock on the door. Cornered and terrified, the writer fumbles for a story about a man alone in a room who wants to write but draws a blank because the "human condition" doesn't seem worth a story — until, inevitably, there is a knock on the door. The piece is a playful, anxious parable about Israeli life, the violent hunger for stories, and the writer's compulsion to keep producing them under pressure.

**4. (If Keret) the surreal premise treated as ordinary; the deadpan voice.** What to notice: the matter-of-fact narration of the impossible. What to bring out: the line from Singer's demons and Schulz's metamorphoses to Keret's contemporary surrealism, now in living Israeli Hebrew. The Shoah inheritance often present obliquely.

**5. (If Krauss) the surviving manuscript / the text that outlives its people.** What to notice: a book or document that travels across generations and oceans. What to bring out: the literal embodiment of "the text as homeland" — the course's founding idea, made into plot. Tie back explicitly to Session 1.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

*Most of this session is student-led. Use these to seed and steer.*

1. **What is a "Jewish writer"? Propose a working definition and test it against our sixteen weeks.**
   *Model answer:* No single criterion (language, religion, theme, ancestry) fits all the writers we read, since Kafka barely practiced, Schulz rarely wrote of Judaism, and Bellow and Roth were secular. The most defensible answer is a living tradition defined by recurring pressures and questions — exile, text, catastrophe, comedic survival, belonging — carried across languages and continents.

2. **Which of the six threads is most visible in tonight's contemporary piece, and how?**
   *Model answer:* Answers will vary; in Englander, the Shoah-and-after thread dominates through the inherited "who would hide us" test, fused with comedy. Students should point to specific moments and name the thread precisely.

3. **How does contemporary Jewish writing handle the Shoah differently from Levi, Celan, and Ozick?**
   *Model answer:* As postmemory rather than witness — inherited by those who did not live it, often approached obliquely, through comedy, surrealism, or surviving objects. The catastrophe becomes a permanent inheritance and a measure applied to present life rather than a directly recounted experience.

4. **Is comedy still doing the same work in Keret/Englander that it did in Sholem Aleichem and Singer?**
   *Model answer:* Largely yes — laughter remains a technology of survival and dignity, inseparable from grief. The settings have changed (suburban America, contemporary Israel), but the fusion of the comic and the devastating is continuous with Tevye and Gimpel.

5. **How do Krauss, Englander, and Keret carry the "text as homeland" idea forward?**
   *Model answer:* Krauss most literally, through a surviving manuscript that outlives its people; Englander and Keret through the act of writing the inherited story itself. The course's founding image persists: the book is what endures across exile and catastrophe.

6. **Has the long exile ended, given Israel and a secure American Jewry?**
   *Model answer:* Physically, in important ways, yes — there is a homeland and a revived language. But Amichai and the contemporaries suggest exile has become interior and existential, and the questions of belonging and loss persist even in security.

7. **What has the role of women been across the tradition, and how does it look now?**
   *Model answer:* From the male-centered classical world, women's voices entered powerfully with Yezierska, Lazarus, Ozick, and Paley, and now Krauss stands among the central living figures. The tradition has moved from largely excluding women's voices to being substantially shaped by them.

8. **What did reading the biblical texts in Session 1 give you that you used later?**
   *Model answer:* A shared vocabulary — exile, covenant, lament, sacred language, the text as homeland — and an ear for liturgical echo that recurs in Levi, Celan, and Amichai. The foundation made the later transformations legible.

9. **Which writer changed your mind, surprised you, or stayed with you most, and why?**
   *Model answer:* Open; press students to be specific about passage and reason, and to connect their choice to a thread or a moment of recognition across the term.

10. **Now that you have spent sixteen weeks inside it, what do you owe this tradition as a reader?**
    *Model answer:* Open; useful answers include the duty of memory (Levi's "Shema"), honest attention, the willingness to hold comedy and grief together, and the recognition that careful reading is itself a way of keeping a tradition alive.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The capstone's openness can make some students anxious — they may want the "right" answer to "what is a Jewish writer?"; reassure them that the question is genuinely open and that thoughtful disagreement is the goal. A student-led session can stall if no one volunteers first; prime two or three students at the previous meeting to come ready to share, so the room starts warmly. Some students may bring favorites from the Shoah sessions and reopen heavy material; honor that, allow the gravity, but steer the session toward synthesis and toward the living, continuing tradition so the term ends with a sense of life and continuity rather than only loss. Keep your own talking minimal tonight; this class belongs to them.

## Glossary for the Instructor

*This scholarly glossary is intended for Mr. Mulhern's own preparation and is more technical than the student-facing glossary. Terms are grouped loosely by domain but listed for quick reference.*

**Pale of Settlement.** The vast western region of the Russian Empire (encompassing much of present-day Ukraine, Belarus, Lithuania, Poland, and Moldova) to which Jews were legally confined from the late eighteenth century until the empire's collapse in 1917. Within it, Jews lived in cities and in small market towns (shtetlekh). It is the geographic and social world behind Sholem Aleichem, the young Singer, and the immigrant generation of Yezierska and Lazarus's refugees.

**Haskalah.** The Jewish Enlightenment, a movement beginning in the late eighteenth century (with roots in the German thought of Moses Mendelssohn) that urged Jews toward secular education, engagement with the surrounding culture, and modern languages, often in tension with traditional religious authority. Sholem Aleichem and the founders of modern Yiddish and Hebrew literature are its heirs. An adherent is a *maskil* (plural *maskilim*).

**Shtetl** (Yiddish; plural *shtetlekh*). A small market town in Eastern Europe with a substantial Jewish population, whose rhythms were set by the Jewish calendar, the synagogue and study house, the market, and the surrounding non-Jewish peasantry. Poor, communal, and intensely religious, it is the iconic setting of Yiddish literature and must be taught without either sentimentality or condescension.

**Yiddishism.** The ideology and cultural movement that championed Yiddish — the everyday fusion language of Ashkenazi Jewry (Germanic core, written in Hebrew characters, with Hebrew-Aramaic and Slavic elements) — as a legitimate national language and the basis of a modern secular Jewish culture, against those who privileged Hebrew or assimilation. Relevant to Sholem Aleichem and Singer, who made Yiddish a literary language of the first rank.

**Hebraism.** The corresponding orientation toward Hebrew — the sacred and scholarly language — as the proper vehicle of Jewish literary and national revival. The Hebraist current, tied to Zionism, ultimately triumphed in the revival of spoken Hebrew in modern Israel (see Amichai). The Yiddishism/Hebraism contest is a key axis of modern Jewish culture.

**Halakhah.** Jewish religious law — the entire body of binding legal rulings derived from the Torah and rabbinic tradition, governing every aspect of observant life. The "Law" before which Kafka's man waits resonates, for a Jewish reader, with Torah and Halakhah, which is why the parable carries a charge it cannot have for others.

**Aggadah.** The non-legal portion of rabbinic tradition — the stories, parables, ethical teachings, legends, and theology that accompany the legal (halakhic) material in the Talmud and Midrash. The storytelling, parabolic strain in Jewish literature (Singer, Malamud) draws on the aggadic imagination.

**Talmud.** The central text of rabbinic Judaism after the Bible: a vast compilation of law (Halakhah) and lore (Aggadah) comprising the Mishnah and the later Gemara (rabbinic commentary and debate upon it). Studied for centuries across the diaspora, it is the supreme embodiment of "the text as homeland." There are two: the more authoritative Babylonian Talmud and the Jerusalem (Palestinian) Talmud.

**Mishnah.** The foundational compilation of Jewish oral law, edited around 200 CE by Rabbi Judah the Prince; it forms the core upon which the Talmud's later discussion (Gemara) is built. Knowing the Mishnah/Gemara structure helps explain the depth of the textual culture underlying the whole tradition.

**Midrash.** A method and body of rabbinic interpretation that probes, expands, and fills the gaps of the biblical text, often through imaginative narrative (aggadic midrash) or legal derivation (halakhic midrash). Tevye's creative, self-serving "interpretations" of scripture are a folk echo of the midrashic habit of mind.

**Parable / Mashal.** *Mashal* (Hebrew) is the traditional Jewish term for a parable or illustrative story, a central form in biblical wisdom literature, rabbinic Aggadah, and Hasidic teaching. Kafka's "Before the Law," Singer's "Gimpel," and Malamud's "The Magic Barrel" all operate, in different ways, as modern *meshalim*.

**Kabbalah.** The tradition of Jewish mysticism, developed especially in medieval Spain and Safed, concerned with the hidden structure of the divine, creation, and the soul (its central text is the *Zohar*). Its imagery feeds the supernatural and mystical strain in Singer and the folk-Hasidic background of several writers.

**Tetragrammaton.** The four-letter name of God (YHWH) in the Hebrew Bible, considered too holy to pronounce; observant Jews substitute *Adonai* ("my Lord") or *HaShem* ("the Name"). Introduced in Session 1, it establishes the tradition's reverence-as-restraint and the motif of the unsayable that recurs in the Shoah poets.

**Golem.** In Jewish folklore, an artificial being (most famously the clay man created by the Maharal of Prague) animated by sacred language to protect the community. A touchstone of the Jewish supernatural imagination, relevant to the folk-mystical background of Singer and to Prague (Kafka's city).

**Holocaust vs. Shoah.** Two terms for the Nazi genocide of European Jewry (1941–1945). "Holocaust" (from Greek, "burnt offering") is the most widely used English term but is contested by some because of its sacrificial connotation. "Shoah" (Hebrew, "catastrophe") is preferred by many scholars and survivors precisely because it carries no implication of sacrifice or divine purpose. This companion uses both, leaning on "Shoah" for the gravest contexts.

**Sho'ah.** The Hebrew word (שואה) meaning "catastrophe" or "calamity," used to denote the Nazi genocide; the term was adopted to avoid the theological overtones of "Holocaust." Yad Vashem and much Israeli and scholarly usage prefer it. (Spelled here both "Shoah" and "Sho'ah" to match the variants Mr. Mulhern will encounter.)

**Vichy.** The collaborationist French regime (1940–1944), based in the town of Vichy, that governed unoccupied France after the German defeat of 1940 and actively participated in the persecution and deportation of Jews. Useful background for the European-wide machinery of the Shoah; relevant context when discussing how the genocide operated across occupied and collaborating states (and to Celan's France, where he later lived).

**Sonderkommando.** The units of (mostly Jewish) prisoners forced by the SS to work in and around the gas chambers and crematoria of the death camps, disposing of bodies — and themselves periodically murdered. Primo Levi's concept of the "gray zone" of moral compromise centers on such coerced complicity; handle with care when discussing Levi.

**Yiddish theatre.** The vibrant tradition of professional Yiddish-language drama, melodrama, musical theatre, and revue that flourished in Eastern Europe and on New York's Lower East Side (Second Avenue, the "Yiddish Rialto") from the late nineteenth century. It was a central institution of immigrant Jewish culture and a formative influence (Kafka was drawn to a touring Yiddish troupe; the world of Sholem Aleichem and Yezierska is steeped in it).

**Forward (Forverts).** *The Jewish Daily Forward* (Yiddish: *Forverts*), the most important Yiddish-language newspaper in America, founded in New York in 1897, a pillar of immigrant Jewish life, socialist politics, and Yiddish letters. Isaac Bashevis Singer published much of his fiction in its pages. Its famous advice column was the *Bintel Brief*.

**Partisan Review.** The influential American literary and political journal (founded 1934), the flagship of the largely Jewish "New York intellectuals," which fused literary modernism with (initially) anti-Stalinist left politics. It published Singer's "Gimpel the Fool" in Bellow's translation (1953) and Bellow's *Seize the Day*; it is the crucible of the postwar Jewish American literary ascendancy.

**New York intellectuals.** The loosely defined, predominantly Jewish circle of writers and critics (Lionel Trilling, Irving Howe, Alfred Kazin, Philip Rahv, Clement Greenberg, Susan Sontag, and others) associated from the 1930s onward with journals like *Partisan Review*, *Commentary*, and *The New York Review of Books*. Marked by fierce argument, cultural seriousness, and ex-radical politics, they are the intellectual milieu surrounding Bellow and the postwar canon.

**Israeli Hebrew literature.** The body of modern literature written in revived, spoken Hebrew in Israel from the twentieth century onward, encompassing poets (Bialik, Amichai, Dahlia Ravikovitch) and novelists (Agnon, Oz, Yehoshua, Grossman, Keret). It represents the linguistic miracle of Hebrew's revival from a sacred to a vernacular tongue and the tradition's arrival at a homeland and a living language (Sessions 15–16).

**Magical realism.** A narrative mode in which marvelous or impossible events are related in the same matter-of-fact register as the everyday, so the fantastic and the real coexist without rupture. Bruno Schulz is an early European exemplar (with Kafka as kin); the mode continues in Singer's folk-supernatural and in contemporary writers like Keret. Distinguish from pure fantasy: the marvelous erupts *within* a realist world.

**Witness literature.** Writing — testimony, memoir, poetry, fiction — that bears witness to historical atrocity, especially the Shoah, and that grapples with the adequacy of language to convey extreme experience. Primo Levi (*If This Is a Man*) is its paradigm; the genre raises the central problem of representation framed by Adorno (Session 9) and the duty of memory framed by Levi's "Shema" (Session 8).

**Generational trauma (and postmemory).** The transmission of the psychological and cultural weight of catastrophe to descendants who did not directly experience it. The literary scholar Marianne Hirsch's term *postmemory* names the relationship of the "second generation" to the Shoah — intimate yet inaccessible, inherited as image and story. It is central to Ozick (Session 13) and to the contemporary writers (Session 16: Krauss, Englander, Keret as a survivor's child).
