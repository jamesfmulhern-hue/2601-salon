## Preface — How to Use This Book

Dear Mr. Mulhern,

This is not a companion like the others on your shelf. The literature manuals gave you biographical sketches of the masters; this one gives you the machinery of the trade. It is built for a different kind of course — not the study of what has been published, but the practice of getting published — and so it is built differently. Where the literature volumes asked you to teach *Moby-Dick*, this one asks you to teach the cover letter that might one day accompany a writer's own *Moby-Dick* on its way to an editor's slush pile. The subject is mechanics, money, timelines, and temperament. The authority you bring to it is unusual and worth naming at the outset: you have stood on both sides of the desk. You acquired and edited manuscripts at Houghton Mifflin in Boston and at Clark Boardman Callaghan in New York; you have read slush, written rejection letters, and made the cold call to an agent that turns a maybe into a contract. And you have also been the writer on the other end — more than three hundred journal placements and, by your own honest accounting, many more rejections than that, plus novels, a novella, story collections, and a memoir carried through to print. Almost no one teaching this material has held both ends of the wire. That is the whole reason this course can be honest.

Use this book the way you used the others: as a scaffold, not a script. Each session is laid out in the same recurring order — At a Glance, Session Goals, Industry Context, Key Teaching Points written as your own lecture notes to yourself, a Lecture Script in your voice with [pause] markers, Sample Documents you can photocopy and hand out, free online Resources, Anticipated Student Questions with model answers, a note on Anticipated Difficulties, a time-boxed Workshop element, Further Reading for you, and One Thing to Remember. Cross out what doesn't fit the room. The scripts are meant to be spoken and then abandoned the moment a student says something better than what you'd planned to say.

A word on tone, because here it governs everything and the stakes are higher than in a literature seminar. You will be standing in the community room of 2601 before adult writers at wildly different stages — a retired attorney with a finished novel in a drawer, a young mother with nine poems and enormous talent, a man who has been told for forty years that he should "write a book" and now wants to know how. Three temptations attend this room, and all three are forms of dishonesty. The first is to **over-promise** — to let them believe that talent plus persistence reliably produces a book deal, an agent, an income. It does not, and you of all people know it. The second, the opposite error, is to **crush hope** — to so thoroughly inventory the brutality of the odds that a gifted person walks out and never submits again. The third is the most seductive: to **romanticize the work** — the garret, the muse, the noble suffering. You have lived the actual writing life, which is mostly spreadsheets, postage, three-month silences, and form rejections, punctuated rarely and gloriously by a yes. Teach that life. It is better than the romance because it is real and because it is achievable.

You will face three hard conversations, and they will recur. **First:** the student whose work is, candidly, not publishable yet — and may not become so. You owe this person honesty delivered as craft guidance, never as a verdict on their worth. **Second:** the student who hands you a manuscript and asks you to read it. You cannot read them all, and a teacher who reads one must read all or play favorites; set the boundary warmly on day one. **Third — and you must decide your own policy before week one:** the student who wants you to publish them through Silver Current Press. The cleanest answer, and the one this book recommends, is a standing firewall: *Silver Current does not consider work from anyone enrolled in this course, during the course or for one year after.* Say it out loud in Session 1. It protects them from the appearance of a sales pitch and protects you from the reality of one. Everything in this book assumes you will draw that line and keep it.

With respect and confidence in your hand,
— *The Companion*

## Recurring Threads Across the Course

Sixteen sessions can feel like sixteen unrelated errands — letters here, contracts there, a week on agents, a week on contests. They are not unrelated. Beneath the mechanics run a half-dozen convictions that you should let surface again and again, in your own words, until the room can finish your sentences. Name them early; return to them often. They are what turns a how-to seminar into an education in a life.

**The long quiet career is the only career worth wanting.** The publishing imagination is dominated by the lottery winner — the debut that sold at auction, the seven-figure advance, the festival circuit. These exist. They are also, statistically, almost no one. Roughly ninety-eight percent of traditionally published titles sell fewer than five thousand copies, and the great majority of working writers earn, from their books, something closer to a part-time wage than a living. The career you should teach them to build is the other kind: the slow accumulation of placements, the second book that is better than the first, the body of work assembled over decades. That career is available to people of ordinary luck and extraordinary persistence. The lottery is not teachable. This is.

**Rejection is the curriculum, not an interruption of it.** A writer who has been rejected three hundred times is not a failed writer; that is the ordinary texture of a serious submitting life. You will teach students to read rejections the way an editor reads them — to tell a form letter from a tiered "please send more" from a genuine personal note, and to treat the gradations as the only feedback the market offers. The discipline is not in avoiding rejection. It is in not letting any single rejection mean more than it does.

**Marketing is not writing, and confusing the two will cost you both.** There is a whole industry now — platforms, newsletters, follower counts, "author brands" — that promises that visibility precedes and produces good work. For literary writers this is mostly false and frequently corrosive. The writing is done in silence and solitude; the marketing, such as it is for our kind of book, is a separate and much smaller job done afterward. You will teach students to give the writing its due first, and to spend on platform only the energy that is left over and only where it demonstrably helps.

**The writer's relationship to the gatekeeper is neither servile nor adversarial.** Editors and agents are not enemies to be outwitted, nor gods to be appeased. They are overworked readers looking, genuinely, for something to love and say yes to. You read slush at Houghton Mifflin; you can tell them what it actually feels like on the other side of the desk — the fatigue, the hope, the gratitude when a manuscript is simply *clean and ready*. That insider view dissolves both the servility and the resentment.

**Most published writers are not famous, and that is the normal and dignified condition.** Fame is a separate lottery layered on top of publication, and it correlates only loosely with the quality of the work. A poet with four collections from good small presses, a teaching life, and the respect of a few hundred serious readers has a complete and honorable career. Teach them to want that, and the disappointment that ends so many writing lives loses most of its power.

**The small press is where literary writing actually lives, and there is no shame in it — there is honor.** The mid-list at the large houses has contracted for thirty years; advances for debut literary fiction now commonly run one to four thousand dollars at independent presses, and the editorial care, the patience, and the willingness to publish the strange and the quiet increasingly reside with the small and the independent. You run such a press yourself. You can teach the small press not as a consolation prize but as the natural home of the work — which it is.

**The honest economics, finally, set everyone free.** The fifteen-percent agent commission, the ten-percent hardcover royalty, the advance that is never earned out, the reading fee that is sometimes legitimate and sometimes a tax, the contest that publishes the winner and quietly funds itself on the losers' entry fees — once a writer understands the money, the mystification falls away and the decisions become clear. Nobody in this room needs to be rich. Everybody in this room deserves to know exactly how the money moves, so that no one can take advantage of them. That is the gift of having been an editor. Spend it generously.

## Session 1 — Welcome & The Honest Landscape

**At a Glance**
The first night sets the contract for the whole course: this room tells the truth about publishing. We introduce the real odds, the real money, and the real timeline — and we frame all of it not as discouragement but as liberation.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands that publishing is a craft and a business with knowable rules, not a mystery or a lottery.
- Each student can state, roughly, the real acceptance rates at literary journals and the real economics of a first book.
- Each student leaves with a working definition of "a writing life" that does not depend on fame or money.
- Each student understands the firewall: Silver Current Press is off-limits to this class.
- Each student has named, privately, one publishing goal for the next twelve months.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The landscape a writer enters in 2026 is the product of thirty years of contraction at the top and explosion at the bottom. At the top: the "Big Five" trade houses (Penguin Random House, HarperCollins, Hachette, Simon & Schuster, Macmillan) have consolidated through mergers, the most recent being the blocked PRH–Simon & Schuster deal of 2022 and Simon & Schuster's subsequent sale to the private-equity firm KKR. Their mid-list — the modestly selling literary titles that once sustained careers — has thinned steadily. BookScan-based analyses of Big Five output indicate that roughly ninety-eight percent of titles sell under five thousand copies and close to ninety percent never reach two thousand across tracked channels ([For The Writers, 2025](https://www.forthewriters.com/post/the-economic-realities-of-traditional-publishing-today)). Median author book income sits in the low four figures.

At the bottom — meaning the entry points available to a new writer — the picture is paradoxically abundant. The literary magazine ecosystem has exploded online: where a writer in 1990 had perhaps a few hundred print journals to submit to, a writer in 2026 has thousands, the great majority of them online-only, free to read, and actively soliciting work. The tracking platform Chill Subs catalogues thousands of active markets; only about thirteen percent of the magazines it tracks charge any submission fee at all ([Literary Hub, 2025](https://lithub.com/the-rise-of-the-submission-industrial-complex/)). This is the most important single fact for a beginning submitter to internalize: the front door is wide open and mostly free.

Between these poles sits the engine of literary publishing as most writers will experience it: the independent and small press, the university press, the regional and nonprofit publisher. Advances here are modest — commonly one to four thousand dollars for debut literary fiction at an independent press, and frequently zero for poetry, where a small honorarium and author copies are the norm ([Barnett Ghostwriting, 2026](https://www.barnettghostwriting.com/blog/average-book-advance-for-first-time-authors/)). The trade is the work; the editorial care is real; the audience is small and devoted.

The organizing institution of the field is AWP — the Association of Writers & Writing Programs — whose annual conference and bookfair is the largest literary gathering in North America, drawing on the order of ten to twelve thousand writers, teachers, editors, and publishers to a single city each spring ([San Antonio Report](https://sanantonioreport.org/12000-writers-to-descend-on-san-antonio-for-literary-super-bowl/)). AWP is the closest thing the field has to a town square; understanding it is part of understanding the landscape, and we return to it in Session 13.

The honest summary you should be able to deliver from memory: the door in is open and largely free; the path through is slow and unglamorous; the money is small for almost everyone; and a complete, dignified writing life is nonetheless available to the persistent. That is not a discouraging story. It is the true one, and the true one is the only one that protects a writer from being either crushed or fleeced.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Open with the firewall and the manuscript-reading boundary. Say both out loud, warmly, in the first ten minutes. Get them out of the way so the rest of the term is clean.
2. Publishing is a business with knowable rules. Demystify it. Nothing here is magic; all of it is learnable.
3. The two-poles model: contracted top (Big Five, shrinking mid-list), exploding bottom (thousands of journals, mostly free). Draw it on the board.
4. The single liberating fact: ~87% of journals charge no submission fee; the front door is open. No one needs money or connections to start.
5. Real acceptance rates: competitive journals accept roughly 1–2% of submissions; top-tier venues well under 1%. This is normal. It is not a referendum on talent.
6. Real first-book economics: $1–4K advance at small/indie presses for literary fiction; often $0 plus copies for poetry; ~98% of titles sell <5,000 copies. Say the numbers plainly.
7. The timeline is measured in months and years, not days. A journal response of three to six months is ordinary. A book from acceptance to shelf is twelve to twenty-four months.
8. Define "a writing life": a body of work built slowly, a few hundred readers who care, the second book better than the first. Fame is a separate, mostly-unteachable lottery.
9. Distinguish the four lanes early: journals/magazines, agented trade books, small-press books, self-publishing. We will spend the term mapping all four.
10. Name my dual authority honestly — editor at Houghton Mifflin and Clark Boardman Callaghan; 300+ placements and far more rejections. I have read slush and I have been slush.
11. Ask each student to write down one twelve-month goal tonight. Keep it; we revisit it in Session 16.
12. End on the promise: by week sixteen each person leaves with a real, written submission plan — not hope, a plan.

**Lecture Script**

Good evening. [pause] I want to start with a number, and then a confession.

The number is three hundred. That's roughly how many times my work has appeared in literary journals and anthologies — poems, stories, essays, scattered across magazines you've never heard of and a few you have. It's the number that goes in my bio. [pause] Here's the confession: I don't actually know how many times my work has been *rejected*, because I stopped being able to count somewhere past a thousand. The real ratio of my writing life — and I'd guess of every honest writer's life in this room or out of it — is something like one yes for every four or five noes, on a good year. On a bad year, worse. I'm telling you that in the first ten minutes because everything I want to teach you over the next sixteen weeks depends on you believing it. [pause]

I have spent my life on both sides of a desk. I was an editor — at Houghton Mifflin in Boston, and later at a legal publisher in New York — and part of my job was reading the slush pile. The slush pile is the stack of unsolicited manuscripts that arrives without an agent, without an introduction, from people who simply found the address and sent the work. And I want to tell you what it actually felt like to read slush, because it is the opposite of what you imagine. [pause] You imagine a cynical gatekeeper looking for reasons to say no. The truth is that I sat down to that stack *wanting* to find something. Every editor does. The fatigue is real — by the fortieth manuscript on a Friday your eyes are gone — but underneath the fatigue is hope. We were looking for someone to fall in love with. And when a manuscript arrived that was simply clean, and ready, and *alive* on the first page, the feeling was gratitude. [pause] I need you to hold both of those truths at once: the odds are brutal, and the person on the other side of the desk is rooting for you. Both. Always.

Now let me tell you what this course is and what it is not. [pause] It is not a course that will make you famous, and I want to dispose of fame right now, tonight, so it stops haunting us. Most published writers are not famous. A poet with four good books from small presses, whose name you have never heard, who is read carefully by maybe three hundred people — that poet has a complete and honorable career. Fame is a second lottery stacked on top of the first one, and it correlates only loosely with how good the work is. We are not going to chase it. We are going to build something better and more reliable: a *writing life*. [pause]

Here is the shape of the real landscape. At the top you have the big New York houses — five of them now, after thirty years of mergers — and their world is contracting. The mid-list, the modestly selling literary book that used to sustain a quiet career, has been squeezed for a generation. Most books, even most books from the big houses, sell fewer than five thousand copies. Advances for a debut literary novel at an independent press today commonly run one to four thousand dollars. For a first book of poems, often nothing but copies and a small honorarium. I am not saying this to frighten you. I am saying it so that no one can ever lie to you about it. [pause]

And at the *bottom* of the landscape — by which I mean the door you and I actually walk through — the news is wonderful. There have never been more places to publish. Thousands of literary magazines, most of them online, most of them free to read, and here is the number to write down: only about thirteen percent of them charge any fee at all to submit. The front door is open. It costs you nothing but postage and nerve. [pause]

So here is our contract for sixteen weeks. I will tell you the truth about the odds, the money, and the time — always. In exchange, I ask you not to be crushed by the truth, because the truth is survivable and even kind once you understand it. By the end of this course you will not leave with hope. Hope is cheap. You will leave with a *plan* — a written, specific, twelve-month submission plan that you could start executing the next morning. [pause]

Two housekeeping matters, and then we begin. First: I cannot read your manuscripts. Not because I don't care — because if I read one I must read all forty, and I'd be doing none of you justice. Second, and please hear this clearly: I run a small press, Silver Current. It is completely off-limits to everyone in this room, during this course and for a year after. I will not publish you, and you will never have to wonder whether I'm teaching you or selling to you. [pause] Good. Now — tonight, before you leave, I'm going to ask you to write down one thing you want to have happen with your writing in the next twelve months. One. We'll come back to it in week sixteen. Let's begin.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *Am I too old to start submitting?* — No. Editors do not see your age; they see your pages. Writers debut in their sixties and seventies regularly, and the long-game career we'll build rewards patience more than youth. Start tonight.
2. *Do I need an MFA?* — No. An MFA buys you time, teachers, and a network, but it buys you nothing an editor requires. Many of the writers in any journal have no graduate degree at all. We cover this fully in Session 13.
3. *Is it really almost free to start?* — Yes. About eighty-seven percent of literary journals charge nothing to submit, and the platforms writers use to find and track them have robust free tiers. Your only required investments are time and postage.
4. *What are my actual chances at a good journal?* — At a competitive journal, acceptance runs roughly one to two percent of submissions; at the very top, under one percent. That is the normal weather, not a judgment of your worth.
5. *How long until I see a book in print?* — Plan in years. A journal may take three to six months to respond; a book from acceptance to shelf typically runs twelve to twenty-four months. Speed is not how this game is won.
6. *Should I write under a pseudonym?* — You may, and we'll discuss when it makes sense (privacy, a day job, genre separation) in Session 3. For most writers your own name is fine and simplest.
7. *Can I make a living at this?* — Almost no one makes a living from literary writing alone. Most working writers teach, edit, or hold other jobs. Plan for a writing *life*, not a writing *salary*.
8. *Will you publish my book through Silver Current Press?* — No, and I want you to be glad I said no: this course stays clean of any sales relationship. Silver Current does not consider work from anyone in this class during the course or for a year after.
9. *Do connections matter more than the work?* — The work comes first, always; connections can open a door but cannot keep you in the room if the pages aren't ready. We'll build the few connections that genuinely help in Sessions 11 and 13.
10. *What if no one ever publishes me?* — Then you will have written, which is the part no one can take away, and we'll have spent sixteen weeks making sure that outcome is far less likely than you fear.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
Two reactions tend to surface on night one. Some students arrive over-confident — certain a finished manuscript is a publication — and the real numbers can read as a personal affront; deliver them as weather, not verdict, and keep the tone warm. Others deflate the instant they hear the odds; for them, end every hard fact with the open front door and the achievable long career. The hardest moment, which will recur all term and which you should rehearse now: a student declares their work is ready for *The New Yorker*. Almost never is it. Do not contradict them in front of the room. Say, "Wonderful — let's build the ladder that gets you there, because even brilliant work takes a stack of smaller credits first," and redirect the energy into a tiered submission strategy. You preserve the dream and the dignity while quietly correcting the readiness.

**Workshop / Action Element** (90 minutes)
- (0–15) Instructor framing: the firewall, the boundary, the two-poles map on the board.
- (15–35) Lecture script and the honest numbers.
- (35–55) Round-the-room: each student says their name, their stage, and one thing they've written that they're proud of. Low stakes; builds the cohort.
- (55–75) Small-group exercise: in threes, students name what they *thought* publishing required versus what they just learned. Surface the myths aloud.
- (75–90) Each student writes a single twelve-month goal on an index card. Collect copies; return originals. Take-home: read one literary magazine cover to cover before next week (any one, online is fine).

**Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor**
- Betsy Lerner, *The Forest for the Trees: An Editor's Advice to Writers* (rev. ed., Riverhead, 2010) — the definitive editor's-eye account; sets the tone for the whole course.
- Jane Friedman, *The Business of Being a Writer* (2nd ed., University of Chicago Press, 2024) — the clearest single map of the current landscape and economics.
- Carolyn See, *Making a Literary Life* (Random House, 2002) — on the long, quiet, dignified career; pairs perfectly with our central thread.
- John B. Thompson, *Merchants of Culture* (2nd ed., Polity, 2012) — the scholarly anatomy of how trade publishing actually works.
- Poets & Writers Magazine, "The Practical Writer" department — ongoing, current, free in part online at https://www.pw.org.

**One Thing to Remember**
The door in is open and nearly free; the path through is slow and unglamorous — and the editor on the other side of the desk is rooting for you.

## Session 2 — Reading Like a Submitter: The Literary Magazine Ecosystem

**At a Glance**
Tonight we teach students to read magazines the way a submitter must — not as a fan, but as a strategist learning a venue's taste, tier, terms, and turnaround. The journal is the writer's apprenticeship and the foundation of every later credential.

**Session Goals**
- Each student can describe the tiered structure of literary journals and place specific magazines within it.
- Each student can read a journal's masthead, guidelines, and recent issue to infer its taste and fit.
- Each student understands print vs. online, paying vs. non-paying, and what "fit" actually means.
- Each student can build a starter list of five to ten journals genuinely suited to their work.
- Each student understands why journal credits matter for later books and agents.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The literary magazine is the oldest continuous institution in American letters and, for the working writer, the most important. The lineage runs from the nineteenth-century quarterlies through the little magazines of high modernism — *Poetry* (founded 1912, still publishing), *The Dial*, *The Little Review* that serialized *Ulysses* — to the postwar university quarterlies (*The Kenyon Review*, *The Sewanee Review*, *The Paris Review*, founded 1953) and finally to the digital explosion of the last twenty years. Today the field is best understood in tiers, though every writer and editor would draw the lines slightly differently.

At the top sit the venues that confer the most prestige and, often, the most money: *The New Yorker*, *The Atlantic*, *The Paris Review*, *Granta*, *Tin House* (now book-and-workshop only), *Ploughshares*, *AGNI*, *The Kenyon Review*, *The Sewanee Review*, *One Story*. Acceptance rates here run well under one percent; *One Story* pays $500 and publishes a single story per issue. The middle tier — *The Missouri Review*, *Colorado Review*, *Crazyhorse*/*swamp pink*, *The Cincinnati Review*, *Prairie Schooner*, *New Ohio Review*, and hundreds of strong university and independent quarterlies — is the realistic and honorable target for most serious submitters and where most published literary writers actually accumulate their credits.

Then there is the vast, vital, mostly-online lower and emerging tier — *SmokeLong Quarterly*, *Wigleaf*, *CRAFT*, *Hobart*, *Pithead Chapel*, *Pidgeonholes*, *Maudlin House*, and thousands of others, including the international magazines that have published your own work: Impspired, The Galway Review, Vita Brevis Press, CC&D/Scars Publications, Sparks of Calliope, Fragmented Voices, Academy of the Heart and Mind. These pay little or nothing but publish quickly, build a writer's confidence and credit list, and are themselves often beautiful and serious. Crucially, work first published online is now fully respectable; the old prejudice against digital publication has largely collapsed.

Payment models run the full range: from nothing (contributor copies only) at most magazines, to token amounts, to professional rates of roughly twenty-five cents per word or more at top venues like *One Story* ([Creative Writing Authority, 2026](https://creativewritingauthority.com/submitting-to-literary-magazines)). Response times of three to six months are standard; the writer who needs faster validation is in the wrong art form.

What the journal credit *buys* a writer is the through-line of this session: it is the apprenticeship in which you learn to finish and submit; it is the acknowledgments page of a future book; it is the evidence an agent or book editor scans to see whether anyone else has vouched for you; and it is, occasionally, the doorway to a Pushcart nomination or an anthology selection that compounds into real standing.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Teach the tier model — top / middle / emerging-online — but stress the lines are soft and that "fit" beats "tier" every time.
2. "Reading like a submitter" means reading the masthead, the guidelines, and at least one recent issue before sending. Demonstrate live.
3. The single most common rookie error: submitting without ever reading the magazine. Editors detect it instantly. Don't.
4. Print is not better than online anymore. Online journals are fully respectable and often pay and respond faster.
5. Most journals pay nothing but copies. That is normal and not exploitative; the currency is the credit, not the cash, at this stage.
6. Acceptance rates: ~1–2% middle tier, <1% top tier. Place yourself in the middle tier to start and let the rare top acceptance be a gift.
7. Define "fit": tone, length, aesthetic, subject. A perfectly good story sent to the wrong magazine is a guaranteed no.
8. Teach them to mine acknowledgments pages and "where I've published" notes — the fastest way to find magazines that take work like yours.
9. Response time is three to six months; simultaneous submission (covered Session 4) is how you survive that math.
10. Journal credits compound: they feed bios, acknowledgments, agent queries, Pushcart and Best-of anthologies. Start the snowball now.
11. Name my own journal homes (Impspired, Galway Review, Vita Brevis, CC&D/Scars, Sparks of Calliope, Fragmented Voices, Academy of the Heart and Mind) as living proof the emerging tier is real and worth it.
12. Warn against "pay-to-submit" reflex — fees are sometimes legitimate (covered Session 6), but never required to build a career.

**Lecture Script**

I want to begin tonight with a rejection I deserved. [pause] Years ago, early on, I sent a story — a fairly dark, fairly violent story — to a magazine I had never actually read. I'd seen the name somewhere, it sounded prestigious, I sent the thing off feeling rather pleased with myself. The rejection came back in about two weeks, which should already have told me something. And it said, very politely, that while the writing was strong, the magazine "publishes exclusively gentle, nature-centered lyric essays and had not in its forty-year history published fiction of any kind." [pause] I had sent a knife fight to a birdwatching journal. And the editor was right, and I was an idiot, and it was entirely my fault — because I had not done the one thing every submitter must do before sending a single page anywhere. I had not read the magazine. [pause]

That is the lesson tonight. We are going to learn to read literary magazines not as fans — though you should love them — but as *submitters*. And reading like a submitter is a different skill. A fan reads for pleasure. A submitter reads for *fit*. [pause]

Let me give you the map first. Imagine the literary magazine world as a mountain. At the very top, a thin band: *The New Yorker*, *The Paris Review*, *Granta*, *Ploughshares*, *AGNI*, *One Story*. These pay well, confer enormous prestige, and accept well under one percent of what's sent to them. *One Story* publishes exactly one story per issue. One. Think about what that means about your odds. [pause] Now — I am not telling you never to send there. I'm telling you to understand where "there" is on the mountain.

The broad middle of the mountain is where the real climbing happens, and where most published writers you'll ever meet actually live: *The Missouri Review*, *Colorado Review*, *The Cincinnati Review*, *Prairie Schooner*, dozens and dozens of strong university and independent quarterlies. Acceptance rates here run one to two percent. That sounds brutal, and it is, but it's a different order of brutal than the summit, and it is where you should aim the bulk of your effort. [pause]

And then there's the base of the mountain — broad, green, alive, and where I want you to *start*. The emerging and online journals. I'll name some of my own homes, because I want you to know I'm not sending you anywhere I haven't been. My work has appeared in Impspired, in The Galway Review, in Vita Brevis Press, in CC&D from Scars Publications, in Sparks of Calliope, in Fragmented Voices, in Academy of the Heart and Mind. [pause] Most of those paid me nothing but the deep pleasure of being read and a line for my bio. And every one of them is a real, serious magazine with a real editor who chose my work over other work. Do not let anyone tell you online publication or unpaid publication "doesn't count." That snobbery is twenty years out of date. The credit counts. The reader counts. The line in your bio counts. [pause]

So here is how you read like a submitter. Three documents, every time, before you send. First, the *masthead* — who edits this magazine, and what else have they written or championed? Taste is a person. Second, the *submission guidelines* — and read them as if your life depended on it, because your acceptance does. Word counts, reading periods, simultaneous-submission policy, whether they pay, whether they charge. Third — and this is the one everyone skips — at least *one recent issue*. Read three or four pieces. Ask yourself honestly: does my work belong in this company? Not "is my work good enough" — "is my work the same *kind* of thing." [pause] A wonderful story in the wrong magazine is a guaranteed rejection. A merely-good story in exactly the right magazine gets published all the time.

Here's a professional trick I'll give you tonight, and it will save you years. When you find a writer whose work feels like a cousin to yours, go find their acknowledgments page or their "previously published in" note. That list — those magazines — is your starter list. Someone has already done the matching for you. [pause] You are not looking for the *best* magazines. You are looking for *your* magazines. The ones that already publish work that rhymes with yours.

One more thing about the calendar, because it governs everything. A literary magazine will typically take three to six months to respond to you. Some take a year. You will send a story into a silence that feels, the first time, unbearable. We will deal with how to manage that silence next week, when we talk about tracking and about the thing called simultaneous submission, which is the only reason this math is survivable at all. [pause] For tonight: read the magazine. Read the magazine. Read the magazine. If you take nothing else from week two, take that.

**Recommended Resources for This Session**
- **Chill Subs** — free; filter thousands of magazines by genre, fee, response time, acceptance rate: https://www.chillsubs.com
- **Duotrope** — paid (~$50/yr) but worth it; deep market data, response-time stats, deadline calendar: https://duotrope.com
- **Poets & Writers Literary Magazines database** — free: https://www.pw.org/literary_magazines
- **The Submission Grinder** — free, skews toward genre/speculative markets but useful broadly: https://thegrinder.diabolicalplots.com
- **CLMP (Community of Literary Magazines and Presses)** — the field's trade group, with a free directory: https://www.clmp.org

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *Is it worse to publish online than in print?* — No. That prejudice is largely dead. Online journals are read more widely than most print ones, often pay and respond faster, and the credit counts identically on a bio or in an agent query.
2. *Why would I submit somewhere that pays nothing?* — Because at this stage the currency is the credit, the reader, and the apprenticeship in finishing and submitting. Payment comes later and to few; the credit compounds for everyone.
3. *How do I know if my work "fits" a magazine?* — Read one recent issue and ask whether your piece is the same *kind* of thing — in tone, length, and ambition — not merely whether it's good. Fit is about kinship, not quality.
4. *Should I aim high or low?* — Both, simultaneously, as a tiered list: a couple of reach venues, a solid core of realistic middle-tier targets, and a few emerging journals where acceptance is likeliest. Never all-reach or all-safe.
5. *How many magazines should be on my starter list?* — Five to ten to begin, drawn from acknowledgments pages of writers like you. You'll expand it all term.
6. *What's a masthead and why do I care?* — It's the list of editors. Taste is personal; knowing who reads your genre, and what they've championed, tells you more about your odds than any prestige ranking.
7. *Can I submit the same poem to ten magazines at once?* — Often yes — that's simultaneous submission, which we cover next week — but you must check each magazine's policy and withdraw promptly when one accepts.
8. *Do editors really notice if I didn't read their magazine?* — Instantly. A mismatched submission, a wrong editor's name, a genre they don't publish — these read as carelessness and earn a fast no.
9. *Should I subscribe to the magazines I submit to?* — When you can afford to, yes — it supports the ecosystem you want to join and deepens your sense of fit. It's a courtesy, not a requirement, and never a quid pro quo for acceptance.
10. *How long before I should expect my first acceptance?* — Highly variable; for many serious submitters, several months and dozens of submissions. The math improves dramatically once you're sending to the right tier and the right fit.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
The intimidation of the summit is the chief hazard tonight: a talented student reads *The Paris Review* and concludes their work is worthless by comparison. Reframe — the summit is one band of a large mountain, and almost no living writer started there. The opposite difficulty is the student who refuses to read any magazine ("I just want to write"); be gentle but firm that submitting blind is the single most reliable way to waste years collecting form rejections. The recurring hard moment returns here in a new costume: the student who will only submit to top-tier venues. Don't forbid it; build the tiered list *with* them, slipping the reach venues in among realistic ones so the dream survives inside a strategy that can actually produce a first acceptance.

**Workshop / Action Element** (90 minutes)
- (0–10) Instructor framing and the mountain map.
- (10–30) Lecture and the "read the magazine" discipline.
- (30–55) Live demonstration on the room's screen: pull up two real journals (one middle-tier, one emerging), read the masthead, guidelines, and a sample piece aloud; infer the taste together.
- (55–80) Hands-on: each student uses Chill Subs (or printed listings) to find five magazines that fit their work, recording for each: tier, fee, response time, simsub policy, what they pay.
- (80–90) Share two finds aloud. Take-home: read one full issue of one magazine on your new list and write three sentences on why your work does or doesn't fit.

- Ian Morris & Joanne Diaz, eds., *The Little Magazine in Contemporary America* (University of Chicago Press, 2015).

- Becky Tuch's *Lit Mag News* newsletter (Substack), the field's best current reporting on the magazine world: https://litmagnews.substack.com.

**One Thing to Remember**
Read the magazine before you submit to it — every time, without exception. Fit beats prestige, and reading is the only way to find fit.

## Session 3 — The Cover Letter & The Bio

**At a Glance**
The cover letter is the smallest, most over-thought document in a writer's life: three short paragraphs that get read in fifteen seconds and almost never decide anything by themselves. Tonight we demystify it, write real ones, and craft the author bio that will follow students for years.

**Session Goals**
- Each student can write a clean, correct, three-paragraph cover letter for a literary magazine.
- Each student understands what the cover letter does and — crucially — what it does not do.
- Each student can write a short author bio and understands how it grows with their career.
- Each student knows the conventions: salutation, length, what to include, what to omit.
- Each student can adapt the same letter for a journal that wants more vs. one that wants almost nothing.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The cover letter occupies a strange place in literary culture: writers agonize over it for hours, and editors spend on it roughly the time it takes to confirm you can follow instructions and spell their magazine's name correctly. This asymmetry is the single most useful thing to teach. The letter is not a sales document and cannot make a mediocre story publishable; the work is read on its own merits, almost always blind to or unswayed by the letter. What the letter *can* do is small but real: it signals professionalism, confirms you've read the magazine, provides the handful of facts the editor needs (genre, title, word count, simultaneous-submission status), and offers a brief, factual bio. What it cannot do is persuade. There is no magic sentence.

The conventions have stabilized over the last two decades, largely because submission platforms like Submittable standardized the fields. The contemporary literary cover letter is three short paragraphs: a greeting and the practical facts; a one-or-two-sentence bio; a closing thank-you. It is *not* a query letter (that's Session 8, for agents and books) and should not summarize or "pitch" the creative work — for a story or poems, the work speaks for itself and a plot summary reads as amateurish. The bio, meanwhile, is a genuinely portable asset: it appears in the cover letter, in the magazine's contributor notes, on the author's website, on book jackets, and in conference programs. Writers should maintain it in two or three lengths (a one-liner, a fifty-word version, a hundred-word version) and update it after every acceptance.

A persistent and useful debate concerns the bio of the unpublished writer. The honest convention: if you have no publication credits, do not apologize for it and do not pad. A first-time submitter may simply write a true sentence about who they are ("Maria Delgado is a nurse and lifelong reader who lives in Philadelphia; this is her first submission") or, equally acceptably, include no bio at all. Editors publish unknowns constantly; the absence of credits is not a mark against you, and pretending otherwise — listing a community workshop as if it were a fellowship, or claiming a "forthcoming" piece that is merely submitted — reads as exactly the insecurity it is. The pseudonym question also belongs here: writers use pen names legitimately for privacy, day-job separation, or to distinguish bodies of work, and a magazine will publish under a pseudonym as long as it can pay the real person.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Lead with the asymmetry: writers agonize, editors skim in fifteen seconds. The letter rarely decides anything. Relax them immediately.
2. The cover letter is NOT a query letter and NOT a pitch. Do not summarize the story or "sell" the poems. Amateur move.
3. The three-paragraph structure: (1) greeting + practical facts (genre, title, word count, simsub status); (2) one-or-two-sentence bio; (3) thank-you.
4. Address a real human when you can find one ("Dear Ms. Okafor" / "Dear Fiction Editors"). Never "Dear Sir or Madam." Spell the magazine's name right.
5. Keep it under 150 words. Brevity reads as confidence and respect for the editor's time.
6. Confirm you read the magazine — one true, specific, unfawning sentence is plenty, and optional. Do not grovel.
7. The unpublished bio: do not apologize, do not pad, do not invent "forthcoming." A true plain sentence, or no bio, is perfectly professional.
8. Maintain the bio in three lengths (one-liner / ~50 words / ~100 words) and update after every acceptance.
9. Pseudonyms are legitimate — privacy, day job, separating genres. The magazine just needs to be able to pay the real you.
10. List credits "best first, then most relevant," capped at three or four. Don't list everything; curate.
11. Never lie or inflate. Editors talk to each other and a padded bio is easy to puncture and impossible to recover from.
12. Hand out the three samples; read the flawed one aloud and let the room diagnose it before I do.

**Lecture Script**

I'm going to take something away from you tonight, and I think you'll be relieved to lose it. [pause] I'm going to take away the belief that the cover letter matters as much as you think it does.

When I was reading submissions as an editor, do you know how long I spent on the cover letter? About the length of time it just took me to say that sentence. [pause] I glanced at it to confirm three things: Did this person follow our guidelines? Did they spell our magazine's name correctly? And is there anything here I actually need to know — the title, the word count, whether they've sent it elsewhere too. That's it. Then I went straight to the work, because the work is the only thing that was ever going to decide it. [pause] I have accepted stories with cover letters that were two flat sentences. I have rejected stories with cover letters that were dazzling little essays. The letter did not save the second writer, and the brevity did not hurt the first. The work decides. Always the work.

So why are we spending a whole evening on it? [pause] Two reasons. First, because the letter *can* hurt you, even though it can't really help you — and that's worth understanding precisely. A letter that's too long, too boastful, that summarizes your story like a movie trailer, that misspells the editor's name, that opens with "Dear Sir or Madam" — these don't get you rejected on their own, but they whisper *amateur* into the editor's ear before they've read a word, and you do not want to start in that hole. The second reason is the bio. The bio inside that letter is going to follow you for the rest of your writing life, and tonight we're going to build it right.

Let me give you the whole structure in one breath, because it's that simple. [pause] Three short paragraphs. Paragraph one: a greeting to a real human if you can find one, and the facts — "Please consider my short story 'The Inheritance,' 3,200 words, for *The Cincinnati Review*. This is a simultaneous submission; I'll notify you immediately if it's accepted elsewhere." Paragraph two: one or two sentences of bio. Paragraph three: "Thank you for your time and for the work you do at the magazine." Sign your name. [pause] That's the whole thing. Under a hundred and fifty words. If it's longer than that, you're probably doing something I'd advise you to stop doing.

Now — what you do *not* do. You do not summarize your story. [pause] You do not write, "In this haunting tale, a young woman returns to her childhood home and confronts the ghosts of her past." The editor is about to *read the story*. Telling them what's in it is like narrating a meal while you serve it. For poems, you don't explain the poems. The work is right there. Let it speak. [pause]

Let's talk about the hardest case in this room: the writer with no publications yet. I want to free you from a particular anxiety. The empty bio is not a problem. Editors publish unknown writers every single week — that's most of what we do, frankly; the famous ones are the exception. So if you've never published, you have two honest options, and both are completely professional. You can write one true sentence — "Maria Delgado is a nurse and lifelong reader in Philadelphia; this is her first submission" — or you can simply leave the bio out. [pause] What you must *not* do is the thing insecurity tempts everyone toward: padding. Don't list a weekend workshop as if it were a prestigious fellowship. Don't write "forthcoming" about a piece that's merely been submitted somewhere — that's a lie, it's an easy lie to catch, and editors talk to each other. A clean empty bio beats a padded one every time, because the padding is the tell. [pause]

A few of you have asked me privately about pen names. Use one if you have a real reason — you're a schoolteacher writing frank fiction, you want to protect your family's privacy, you're keeping your literary novels separate from the thrillers you write for money. All legitimate. The magazine only needs to be able to pay the actual human being; they don't care what name is on the page. [pause]

I'm going to hand out three sample letters now. One is short and excellent. One is acceptable — it does the job, nothing wrong with it. And one is a mess, and I want *you* to tell *me* what's wrong with it before I say a word. Because once you can diagnose the bad letter, you will never write one. [pause] Let's read them.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *What if I can't find the editor's name?* — Use "Dear Fiction Editors" or "Dear Poetry Editors." A correct generic greeting is far better than a wrong specific one, and infinitely better than "Dear Sir or Madam."
2. *Should I mention I've read the magazine?* — One true, specific, unfawning sentence is a nice touch and entirely optional. Skip it rather than fake it; editors smell flattery.
3. *I have no publications — won't that hurt me?* — No. Editors publish unknowns constantly. Write one honest sentence about yourself or include no bio at all; never pad or invent credits.
4. *Can I say a piece is "forthcoming" if it's been accepted but not printed?* — Yes — "forthcoming" means accepted-and-scheduled. It does *not* mean submitted; using it that way is a falsehood editors easily catch.
5. *How many credits should I list?* — Three or four, best or most relevant first. A long list reads as padding; a short curated one reads as a writer who knows their own work.
6. *Do I summarize my story or poems?* — Never. The work is right there to be read; a summary signals inexperience and wastes the one virtue the letter has, which is brevity.
7. *Should I use a pen name?* — Only if you have a real reason (privacy, day job, separating genres). The magazine just needs to be able to pay the real person behind the name.
8. *How long should the letter be?* — Under 150 words; three short paragraphs. Brevity reads as confidence and respect for the editor's time.
9. *Do I need a different letter for every magazine?* — Change the greeting, the title/word count, and any "I read your magazine" line; the bio and structure stay the same. Maintain one master template.
10. *Will a great cover letter get a weak story accepted?* — No. The work decides, always. A strong letter can only avoid hurting you; it cannot rescue the pages.

A second difficulty is the unpublished writer's shame, which produces either paralysis or padding; address it directly and kindly, normalizing the empty bio with examples of unknowns who were published. The recurring hard moment appears as the writer who wants to "pitch" their genius in the letter (Sample C is this person). Don't humiliate them; use the anonymous sample to let the room diagnose the instinct, then redirect that energy into making the *story* undeniable, since that is the only thing the letter's brevity is meant to get out of the way.

## Session 4 — Tools of the Trade: Tracking Submissions

**At a Glance**
Tonight is the least glamorous and most career-saving session in the course: the systems that let a writer keep dozens of pieces in motion across dozens of magazines for months without losing track. We build a tracking spreadsheet and learn the rules of simultaneous submission.

**Session Goals**
- Each student can set up and maintain a submission-tracking system (spreadsheet or platform).
- Each student understands simultaneous submission, multiple submission, and how to withdraw correctly.
- Each student knows the major tools (Submittable, Duotrope, Chill Subs, QueryTracker) and which are free.
- Each student understands response times and how to "keep work in the air."
- Each student leaves with a tracker started and at least one piece logged.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
Tracking is the invisible discipline that separates writers who get published from equally talented writers who don't. The reason is arithmetic. If a typical literary magazine takes three to six months to respond and accepts one to two percent of submissions, then a writer who sends one piece to one magazine and waits will spend a decade collecting a handful of rejections. The only way the math works is to keep many pieces moving through many venues at once — which immediately creates a bookkeeping problem. Which poems are where? Sent when? If "The Inheritance" is accepted at magazine four, which other three magazines must be told to withdraw it, today, before they waste their readers' time? A writer running twenty or thirty active submissions cannot hold this in their head, and the failure mode — accepting at two places, or letting a piece sit "lost" for a year — is both embarrassing and avoidable.

The conventions that make this survivable are **simultaneous submission** (sending the same piece to several magazines at once) and its etiquette. Simultaneous submission is now the default and is accepted by the great majority of magazines; the writer's obligation is to disclose it ("this is a simultaneous submission") and to withdraw promptly and graciously everywhere else the moment a piece is accepted. A minority of magazines forbid it and demand exclusivity — *The Sun* and *Narrative*, for example, read exclusively — and a writer must honor those policies. **Multiple submission** is different and means sending several different pieces to the *same* magazine at once (e.g., a batch of three to five poems), which most poetry venues expect and many fiction venues forbid; read the guidelines.

The toolset has matured. **Submittable** is the dominant submission *platform* — the place where you actually upload and where many magazines run their reading — and it is free for submitters; its dashboard doubles as a partial tracker, showing every piece you've sent through it and its status. It is sufficient for a serious submitter; the paid tier is unnecessary. **Duotrope** (about $50/year) is a tracking-plus-market-data service whose value is its statistics — response times, acceptance rates, who is slow, who is open — and many committed submitters find it worth the cost. **Chill Subs** is the free modern alternative: a market database with a free built-in tracker that exports to CSV. **QueryTracker** is the equivalent tool for the *book* world — tracking agent queries rather than magazine submissions — and is free at its basic tier (covered in Session 8). Beneath all of these sits the humble, eternal, entirely sufficient option: a spreadsheet. Many of the most published writers alive track everything in a single Google Sheet, and a writer who learns to build one well never needs anything else ([Lit Mag Lab, 2025](https://litmaglab.substack.com/p/the-most-common-ways-writers-and)).

The deeper point to teach is psychological as much as logistical: a good tracker converts the unbearable silence of submission into a manageable pipeline. When work is "in the air" — many pieces out, new ones going out weekly — no single rejection lands as a catastrophe, because the writer's attention is on the system, not on any one envelope. The tracker is, quietly, the chief instrument of the writer's emotional survival.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Frame tracking as career-saving arithmetic, not bureaucracy. Without it the publication math is impossible.
2. Teach simultaneous submission as the default: send the same piece to many magazines; disclose it; withdraw promptly when accepted.
3. The withdrawal obligation is sacred — the moment a piece is accepted, withdraw it everywhere else *that day*. Show them how (Submittable button, polite email).
4. Distinguish multiple submission (several pieces to one magazine — common for poetry, often banned for fiction) from simultaneous submission.
5. Honor exclusivity: a minority of magazines (The Sun, Narrative) read exclusively. Track which ones; never simsub those.
6. Submittable: free for submitters, dominant platform, doubles as partial tracker. The free tier is enough; the paid tier is unnecessary.
7. Duotrope (~$50/yr): worth it for the response-time and acceptance-rate data, not required.
8. Chill Subs: free database + free tracker, exports CSV. QueryTracker: free, for agent/book queries (Session 8).
9. The spreadsheet is sufficient forever. Columns: Piece / Magazine / Date sent / Simsub? / Response date / Outcome / Notes / Tier.
10. "Keep work in the air": as soon as a piece comes back rejected, send it out again — same day if possible. The rule is the piece is never resting on your desk.
11. Track tiered rejections in the Notes column ("personal note," "send more") — that data tells you which magazines liked you and are worth returning to.
12. The tracker is emotional armor: it turns silence into a pipeline so no single no can sink you. Say this plainly.

**Lecture Script**

Tonight is the dull session. [pause] I want to be honest about that — there are no beautiful sentences in this one, no Houghton Mifflin war stories about the manuscript that made me weep. Tonight is about a spreadsheet. And I am going to tell you that this dull session has saved more writing careers than any inspiration ever did, including mine.

Let me start with the most embarrassing thing that ever happened to me as a submitting writer. [pause] Early on, before I had a system, I had a poem I rather liked, and over about eight months I sent it to — I think — six magazines, in dribs and drabs, whenever I remembered. One day I got an acceptance from a small journal I admired. I was thrilled. I wrote back yes. And then, about three weeks later, I got a *second* acceptance, for the same poem, from a magazine I admired even more. [pause] Now — that sounds like a happy problem. It is not a happy problem. It is a humiliating problem, because I now had to write to one of two editors who had said yes to me and tell them I had to take it back. I had to break a promise to a person who had chosen my work. And the reason I was in that position was not bad luck. It was that I had no system. I did not know where my own poem was. [pause] I never let that happen again, and tonight I'm going to make sure it never happens to you.

Here's the arithmetic that makes a system non-negotiable. [pause] A magazine takes three to six months to answer you. It accepts one or two percent of what it gets. So if you send one piece to one magazine and wait — like a gentleman, one at a time — you will spend the next ten years collecting maybe a dozen rejections, and you will quit, and who could blame you. The only way the math works is to keep many pieces moving through many magazines at the same time. Twenty submissions in the air. Thirty. [pause] And the second you're running twenty submissions, you cannot hold it in your head. You will lose track. So you need a tracker. That's the whole logic. The tracker exists because the only survivable strategy is volume, and volume is impossible to manage by memory.

So let me teach you the magic word, and its rules. The word is *simultaneous submission*. [pause] It means you send the same story or the same batch of poems to several magazines at once. It is completely normal — it is the default now — and the great majority of magazines expect and allow it. You just have to do two things. First, disclose it: one line in your cover letter, "this is a simultaneous submission." Second — and this one is sacred — the *moment* a piece is accepted somewhere, you withdraw it everywhere else, that same day, politely. [pause] You do not make an editor read a piece that's no longer available. That's the one unforgivable discourtesy in this whole business, and the tracker is how you never commit it, because the tracker tells you instantly: "The Inheritance" is also out at these three places — withdraw it from all three right now.

One distinction, because people mix them up. *Simultaneous* submission is the same piece to many magazines. *Multiple* submission is many different pieces to *one* magazine — like sending a batch of four poems. Most poetry magazines expect a batch. Most fiction magazines want one story at a time. Read the guidelines; the answer is always in the guidelines. [pause] And a small number of magazines — *The Sun*, *Narrative* — read *exclusively*: they want the piece to be at no other magazine while they have it. You honor that. You note it in your tracker so you never accidentally simsub them.

Now the tools. [pause] You do not need to spend a dime. Submittable is the platform where most of this actually happens — you upload there, magazines read there — and it's free for submitters, and its dashboard shows you everything you've sent through it. That's a partial tracker right there. There's a service called Duotrope, about fifty dollars a year, and what you're paying for is *data* — how slow is this magazine, what's their real acceptance rate — and a lot of serious writers think it's worth it. There's a free version of that idea called Chill Subs. And for the book world, when you get there, there's QueryTracker, also free. [pause] But here is what I actually want you to build tonight, because it will outlast every app: a spreadsheet. Eight columns. The piece. The magazine. The date you sent it. Is it a simsub. The date they answered. What they said. Notes. And the magazine's tier. That's it. That single sheet is a complete professional submission system, and many of the most-published writers alive use nothing else. [pause]

Two last habits. First: *keep the work in the air*. The day a piece comes back rejected, you send it somewhere else — ideally the same day, before the sting sets in. The rule is that no piece ever rests on your desk; a rejected piece is just a piece that's between magazines. Second: in that Notes column, write down when a rejection was *personal* — when an editor wrote anything beyond the form letter, "we'd love to see more from you," anything human. Because that magazine is now on a special list. They liked you. You go back to them. [pause] We'll talk about reading rejections like that next week — that's a whole session. For tonight: build the sheet. The sheet is your armor. With it, no single no can ever sink you, because your eyes are on the pipeline, not on the wound.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *Is simultaneous submission considered rude?* — No; it's the default and most magazines expect it. Your only obligations are to disclose it and to withdraw promptly everywhere else the instant a piece is accepted.
2. *How many submissions should I have out at once?* — As many as you can manage honestly — twenty or thirty active submissions is normal for a serious writer. Volume is what makes the acceptance math work.
3. *What if two magazines accept the same piece?* — You honor the first acceptance and withdraw from the second with a gracious email. A good tracker prevents this; without one it's a real and avoidable risk.
4. *Do I really need to pay for Duotrope?* — No. Its data is genuinely useful and many writers value it, but a free spreadsheet plus Submittable and Chill Subs covers everything you strictly need.
5. *How do I know which magazines forbid simsubs?* — It's in their guidelines, always; note exclusivity in your tracker the moment you see it so you never violate it. *The Sun* and *Narrative* are common examples.
6. *What's the difference between simultaneous and multiple submission?* — Simultaneous is the same piece to many magazines; multiple is several different pieces (like a batch of poems) to one magazine. Poetry usually wants a batch; fiction usually wants one story.
7. *When should I follow up on a slow magazine?* — After the response time stated in their guidelines has passed, usually with one polite query; if there's no policy, three to six months is a reasonable wait before nudging.
8. *Should I track rejections too, not just acceptances?* — Especially rejections — and note any personal or "send more" responses, because those magazines belong on a return list and are your warmest future leads.
9. *What do I put in the tracker for a "tiered" rejection?* — Record exactly what they said ("higher-tier form," "personal note," "send more") in Notes; that gradation is the only feedback the market gives you, and it guides where to resubmit.
10. *Is it okay to resubmit a rejected piece the same day?* — Yes — keeping work "in the air" is the healthiest habit you can build. A rejected piece is simply between magazines, not finished.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
The difficulty here is rarely emotional and usually behavioral: students agree the system is wise and then never build it, because tracking feels like procrastination disguised as work — the opposite of "real" writing. Counter this by building the sheet *in the room*, tonight, so each person leaves with a working file and one piece logged; a started system gets maintained, an intended one does not. A few students will resist simultaneous submission out of a misplaced courtliness ("isn't it rude to send to several at once?"); reassure them it's the universal norm and that the only real discourtesy is failing to withdraw. The recurring hard-conversation thread surfaces mildly here as the perfectionist who won't submit a piece anywhere until it's "perfect" — gently reframe submission as part of revision's feedback loop, not its enemy.

**Workshop / Action Element** (90 minutes)
- (0–10) Instructor framing: the arithmetic of volume.
- (10–30) Lecture; the embarrassing double-acceptance story; the rules of simsub.
- (30–55) Build-along: every student opens a blank spreadsheet and creates the eight columns live with the instructor; everyone logs at least one real piece to at least three magazines from their Session 2 list.
- (55–75) Etiquette drill: students draft a withdrawal email and a partial-withdrawal email from the samples.
- (75–90) Set the habit: each student commits to a weekly "submission hour" and writes the day/time on their tracker. Take-home: get at least three real submissions actually sent this week, logged in the sheet.

Keep your work in the air and never lose track of where it is. The spreadsheet is humble, free, and the single most career-saving tool you will ever build.

## Session 5 — Writing & Revising for Submission

**At a Glance**
A craft session aimed squarely at the submission moment: not how to write, but how to know a piece is *ready to send* — and how the disciplines of revision, opening pages, and ruthless self-editing change a writer's acceptance rate more than any cover letter ever could.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands that the work, not the letter, is what gets accepted — and what "ready" actually means.
- Each student can apply concrete readiness tests to their own pieces (the opening, the ending, the proofread).
- Each student understands the editor's-eye reasons manuscripts get rejected in the first paragraph.
- Each student has a revision checklist they can run before every submission.
- Each student practices reading their own work aloud as a revision tool.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
This session sits at the hinge of the course, because everything else — the letters, the trackers, the agents, the contests — is machinery in service of one thing the machinery cannot supply: work that is genuinely ready. The uncomfortable truth, visible to anyone who has read slush, is that the majority of rejected manuscripts are not rejected for taste or fit; they are rejected because they are not *finished*. They are first or second drafts wearing the confidence of final ones. An editor reading sixty submissions in an afternoon develops an almost physical sensitivity to the difference between a manuscript that has been read aloud, slept on, and cut by a fifth, and one that was typed last week and sent in a flush of excitement. The first survives the opening paragraph; the second rarely does.

The most important and least romantic fact about reading slush is the speed of the first judgment. Editors and first readers decide whether to keep reading within the first paragraph — sometimes the first sentence — not out of laziness but out of necessity and pattern recognition. A clichéd opening (the alarm clock, the weather, the character waking up), a paragraph of throat-clearing before the story starts, a single grammatical error in the first line, a manuscript formatted in a way that signals inexperience — any of these can end it before the writing has a chance. This is not unfair; it is the same instinct any reader uses in a bookstore, compressed and professionalized. Teaching students to survive the first paragraph is the single highest-leverage craft intervention available.

The disciplines that produce "ready" work are old and unglamorous and were articulated long before the submission era: the rest period (put it in a drawer), reading aloud (the ear catches what the eye forgives), cutting (most drafts improve by losing ten to twenty percent), and the willingness to start the story later and end it earlier than instinct suggests. Craft books from Strunk and White through Annie Lamott's *Bird by Bird* (the "shitty first drafts" chapter is the gentlest permission ever granted to write badly first), through Francine Prose's *Reading Like a Writer* and Ursula K. Le Guin's *Steering the Craft*, all converge on the same humbling point: the difference between an amateur and a professional is mostly the willingness to revise long after the amateur has declared the piece done.

The instructor's dual authority is decisive here. Mr. Mulhern has both made the first-paragraph judgment as an editor and submitted into it as a writer three hundred times over. He can say, with standing, exactly what makes him stop reading — and exactly what made him, on the rare good day at the desk, sit up and keep going.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Open with the hinge: the work, not the letter, gets accepted. Most rejections are for unfinished work, not bad taste or wrong fit.
2. The first-paragraph judgment is real and fast. Teach them: an editor decides whether to keep reading in the first sentence-to-paragraph. Make the opening earn it.
3. Catalogue the clichéd openings I learned to dread: waking up, the alarm, the weather, the mirror-description of the protagonist, throat-clearing before the story starts.
4. The single biggest improvement: start later, end earlier. Most drafts have a wind-up and a wind-down that should both be cut.
5. The rest period: put a finished draft in a drawer for at least two weeks before deciding it's done. You cannot proofread your own enthusiasm.
6. Read it aloud. The ear catches the clumsy sentence, the repeated word, the dead patch the eye skates over. Non-negotiable.
7. The "lose 10–20%" rule: nearly every draft is improved by cutting a fifth. Tighten relentlessly.
8. Proofread for the fatal first-line typo. A grammatical error in sentence one can end it; the editor reasonably assumes the rest is just as careless.
9. Distinguish revision (rethinking, restructuring) from editing (sentence-level) from proofreading (errors). Do them in that order, last things last.
10. Format conventions: standard manuscript format (12-pt readable serif, double-spaced prose, name/contact, page numbers). Don't signal inexperience through formatting.
11. The hardest lesson: "done" is a decision you make after the work is ready, not a feeling you have when you're excited. Separate the two.
12. Hand out the revision checklist; have them run a real piece through it in class.

**Lecture Script**

I want to tell you about two manuscripts that crossed my desk in the same week, thirty years ago, because together they taught me everything I know about what "ready" means. [pause]

The first was by a writer with obvious talent. The sentences were alive. There were images in it I still remember. And I stopped reading on the second page — not because it was bad, but because it wasn't *finished*. The story didn't start until page three; the first two pages were the writer warming up, clearing his throat, telling me about the weather and the character's morning routine and what the kitchen looked like, before anything actually happened. [pause] I could feel the gifted writer underneath it. But I had sixty manuscripts to read that afternoon, and I made the judgment every editor makes, the one I'm going to teach you to survive tonight: I decided, fast, whether to keep reading. And the warming-up cost him. The talent was real and it didn't matter, because the piece wasn't ready.

The second manuscript that week was, frankly, less dazzling. The sentences were plainer. But it started in the right place — it dropped me into a moment that was already moving — and it had clearly been read, and reread, and cut. Nothing was wasted. By the bottom of the first page I trusted the writer, and trust is the whole game. I kept reading. We published it. [pause] So here is the lesson, and I want it to sting a little, because it stung me when I finally understood it as a *writer*: most manuscripts are not rejected because the editor has bad taste or because your work doesn't fit. Most manuscripts are rejected because they are not finished. They are first drafts wearing the costume of final drafts. [pause]

Let me tell you what actually happens on the other side of the desk, because once you see it you can't unsee it, and it will change how you submit forever. An editor reads the first sentence. Then, if that sentence earns it, the first paragraph. Somewhere in there — the first sentence, the first paragraph, almost never later than the first page — the editor decides whether to keep going. [pause] This sounds brutal. It isn't, really. It's the same thing you do in a bookstore when you read the opening of a novel and put it back on the table. We've just professionalized it and sped it up. Which means your opening is not a warm-up. Your opening is the entire audition. [pause]

So I'm going to give you the list of openings that made me, and every editor I ever worked with, sigh and reach for the form rejection. The character waking up to an alarm clock. The weather. The character looking in a mirror so the author can describe them to us. A page of backstory before the story starts. Any sentence that exists only to clear the writer's throat. [pause] And I'm going to give you the single most powerful revision move in this entire art form, the one that fixes more manuscripts than any other: *start later and end earlier.* Cut your first paragraph. Often cut your first page. You will almost always find that the real opening — the live one — was sitting there underneath the warm-up the whole time. And do the same at the end: most stories go on for a beat or two after they're actually over, explaining what they've already shown. Cut that too. [pause]

Now, how do you get a piece *to* ready? Three disciplines, all of them unglamorous, all of them ancient. First: the drawer. When you finish a draft and you're sure it's done, you are not capable of judging it, because you're still in love with it. Put it away for two weeks. Minimum. You cannot proofread your own enthusiasm. [pause] Second: read it aloud. Out loud, every word, to the empty room. Your ear is a better editor than your eye, because your eye reads what you *meant* and your ear hears what you actually *wrote*. Every clumsy sentence, every word you used twice in three lines, every dead patch where the energy drops — your ear catches all of it. Third: cut. Take a finished draft and challenge yourself to lose ten, fifteen, twenty percent. It will hurt. The piece will get better. It almost always does. [pause]

And then — proofread. The fatal one is the typo in the first sentence. If your opening line has a grammatical error, the editor makes a perfectly reasonable assumption: that the other ten thousand words are just as careless, and they don't have to find out. They reject. [pause] So: revise first — that's the big stuff, the rethinking, the restructuring. Then edit — the sentences. Then, dead last, proofread — the errors. In that order. Never proofread a piece you're still restructuring; you'll polish sentences you're about to delete.

I'll close with the hardest thing I know about this, as a writer who's done it three hundred times. [pause] "Done" is not a feeling. The feeling you get when you finish a draft — that warm certainty that it's brilliant and complete — that feeling is a liar, every time, for everyone. "Done" is a *decision* you make coldly, after the drawer and the reading-aloud and the cutting, when you've run out of things you can honestly improve. Learn to distrust the warm feeling and trust the cold decision. That's the whole difference between an amateur and a professional. The professional just keeps revising long after the amateur has hit send. [pause] Tonight we're going to build the checklist that makes the cold decision for you. Let's go.

> **SAMPLE — The Pre-Submission Revision Checklist** *(hand out; run a real piece through it)*

> 3. Is every scene/stanza earning its place? Could the piece survive losing its weakest section?

> 10. Spelling, punctuation, tense consistency, name consistency throughout.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *How do I know when a piece is truly finished?* — When it has rested at least two weeks, you've read it aloud, you've cut what you can, and you can no longer honestly improve it. "Done" is that cold decision, not the warm feeling you get on finishing a draft.
2. *Do editors really reject after one paragraph?* — Often after one sentence. It's pattern recognition under volume, the same judgment a reader makes in a bookstore. Your opening is the whole audition, so make it earn the next line.
3. *What's the fastest way to improve a draft?* — Start later and end earlier: cut the warm-up opening and the over-explained ending. This single move fixes more manuscripts than any other.
4. *Why read aloud — isn't that for poetry?* — For everything. The ear catches clumsy sentences, repeated words, and dead patches that the eye, reading what you meant, skates right over. It is the most reliable revision tool there is.
5. *How much should I cut?* — Aim to lose ten to twenty percent from the draft you thought was done. Nearly every piece tightens and strengthens under that discipline.
6. *Should I pay for a professional editor before submitting?* — Not to magazines; the work should be ready on your own craft and a trusted reader or two. Paid editing matters more at book stage, and never confuse it with the scam of agent "reading fees."
7. *What's standard manuscript format?* — Readable 12-point font, double-spaced prose, your name and contact, page numbers, and the title — see the Shunn standard. Idiosyncratic formatting quietly signals inexperience.
8. *Is a typo really a dealbreaker?* — A typo in the first sentence often is, because the editor reasonably infers the rest is equally careless. Proofread the opening as if everything depends on it, because it can.
9. *How do I revise without losing the life of the first draft?* — Revise in the right order — structure, then sentences, then errors — and keep the original draft saved so you can restore anything you cut too aggressively. Cutting rarely kills life; it usually reveals it.
10. *I've revised twenty times and still hate it — what now?* — Put it in the drawer, work on something new, and return in a month with fresh eyes; sometimes a piece needs distance more than another pass, and sometimes it teaches you something for the next one.

## Session 6 — Rejection: The Real Curriculum

**At a Glance**
The most important emotional session of the course, and the one with the highest dropout-prevention value. We teach students to read rejections like an editor, to distinguish the form letter from the genuine invitation, and to build a relationship with rejection that lets a writing life survive its own arithmetic.

**Session Goals**
- Each student can distinguish a form rejection, a tiered/higher-form rejection, and a personal rejection.
- Each student understands the real acceptance rates and why mass rejection is the normal condition of a serious writer.
- Each student can extract the small signal hidden in certain rejections and act on it.
- Each student has a personal practice for handling rejection without quitting.
- Each student understands the legitimate uses and the scams around submission fees.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
Rejection is not an obstacle in the writing life; it is the medium the writing life swims in. The numbers make this unavoidable: a competitive magazine accepting one to two percent of submissions is, by definition, sending rejections to ninety-eight or ninety-nine of every hundred writers, most of whom wrote perfectly decent work. A writer who submits seriously for a decade will accumulate rejections in the hundreds or thousands. The writers who survive are not the ones who are rejected less — everyone is rejected at roughly the same brutal rate — but the ones who have learned to metabolize it. This is, bluntly, the real curriculum of a writing life, and a teacher who pretends otherwise is doing students a quiet cruelty.

What makes rejection bearable, and even useful, is that it is not monolithic. Editors deploy a graded vocabulary, and learning to read it is a genuine skill. The **form rejection** — "Thank you for sending us your work; unfortunately it's not right for us at this time" — is the overwhelming majority and carries essentially no information about quality; it means only "no," and frequently "no" decided by a tired first reader at volume. The **tiered or higher-form rejection** is a second-level form letter that a magazine sends to submissions that made it past the first cut — sometimes recognizable by slightly warmer or different wording ("we read this with real interest," "this made it to our final round"). The **personal rejection** is the gold: an actual human sentence, often signed, that comments on the work or invites more ("we'd love to see more from you," "this was very close"). A personal rejection is a small victory and an instruction: that magazine is now on your return list, and you send them your best work again, soon, referencing the encouragement.

The economics of rejection also include the fraught question of **submission fees**. The honest picture: roughly thirteen percent of magazines charge a fee, typically three dollars (often just the Submittable transaction fee) up to twenty or twenty-five dollars for venues that bundle a subscription or feedback ([Literary Hub, 2025](https://lithub.com/the-rise-of-the-submission-industrial-complex/)). A modest fee at a legitimate, well-known magazine is a defensible way they cover the cost of reading, and fee-free options always exist; no writer is ever obligated to pay to submit. But the territory shades quickly into exploitation: "tiered" fees that promise feedback for extra money, contests whose entry fees vastly exceed any plausible prize pool, and — the clearest line of all — literary agents who charge "reading fees," which are prohibited by the Association of American Literary Agents' Canon of Ethics and are a reliable marker of a scam ([Authors Guild](https://authorsguild.org/resource/an-authors-guide-to-agency-agreements/)). Teaching students to separate the legitimate small fee from the predatory one is part of teaching them to read rejection's whole economy.

The deepest content here is temperamental. Mr. Mulhern's own record — three hundred-plus placements and, by honest count, well over a thousand rejections — is the most powerful teaching instrument in the room. The point is not that he succeeded despite rejection but that the rejection *was* the path, the ordinary daily weather of the career that produced the books.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Lead with my own honest ratio: 300+ acceptances, well over 1,000 rejections. Rejection is not the enemy of the career; it IS the career's daily weather.
2. The math makes rejection inevitable: at 1–2% acceptance, 98–99 of every 100 writers get a no, most of them good writers. Mass rejection is normal, not a verdict.
3. Teach the three tiers of rejection: form, tiered/higher-form, personal. This vocabulary is a real, learnable skill.
4. The form rejection carries ZERO quality information. It means "no," often decided fast by a tired first reader. Do not read tea leaves into it.
5. The tiered rejection (slightly warmer wording, "final round," "real interest") means you got past the first cut. Log it; that magazine is warming to you.
6. The personal rejection is GOLD: a human sentence, "send more," "very close." That magazine goes on the return list; send your best again, soon, referencing it.
7. The return-list strategy: personal rejections are the warmest leads you will ever have. Most of my eventual acceptances came from magazines that first said a kind no.
8. Submission fees: ~13% of magazines charge; $3 (often just Submittable's transaction fee) to ~$20–25 with subscription/feedback. Legitimate in moderation; free options always exist.
9. The bright red line: agents NEVER charge reading fees (AAR Canon of Ethics). Any "agent" who does is a scam. Drill this.
10. Watch for predatory "feedback for a fee" tiers and contests whose fees dwarf the prize. Teach the smell test.
11. Practical rituals: keep work in the air (Session 4), celebrate the personal no, set a yearly rejection *target* (e.g., 100 rejections — you can't hit it without submitting hard).
12. The reframe that saves careers: a rejection is information about fit and timing, not a verdict on worth. Repeat until they believe it.

**Lecture Script**

I'm going to tell you a number I don't put in my bio. [pause] The bio says three hundred — three hundred-some publications in journals and anthologies. That's the number writers are allowed to say out loud. Here's the number we're not supposed to say: I have been rejected, over the course of my writing life, well over a thousand times. Probably closer to fifteen hundred; I genuinely lost count. [pause] And I want you to sit with the ratio for a second, because it is the most important thing I will teach you all term. For roughly every one yes, there were four or five noes. That is not the record of a failed writer. That is the record of a *working* writer. That is what the inside of a real writing life looks like. The rejection isn't the obstacle on the way to the career. The rejection *is* the career. It's the daily weather. [pause]

Let me show you why it has to be this way, because the math will set you free. A good magazine accepts one, maybe two percent of what's sent to it. Do the arithmetic from the other direction: that means for every hundred writers who submit, ninety-eight or ninety-nine get a rejection. And here's the part that matters — most of those ninety-eight wrote perfectly good work. The rejection is not sorting good from bad. There isn't room to publish all the good work. It's sorting a tiny amount of work the magazine has room for from a vast amount of work it doesn't, much of which is just as worthy. [pause] So when you get a no — and you will get hundreds of them — I need you to stop hearing it as "your work is bad." That is almost never what it means. What it almost always means is "we had room for two stories and we got nine hundred." That's it. That's the whole message.

But here's where it gets interesting, because not all noes are the same no, and learning to tell them apart is a real skill that I'm going to teach you tonight. [pause] There are three kinds of rejection, and they carry completely different amounts of information.

The first and most common is the *form rejection*. "Thank you for your submission; unfortunately it's not right for us at this time. We wish you luck placing it elsewhere." [pause] That letter carries zero information about the quality of your work. Zero. It was very likely a tired first reader at eleven at night clicking the form-reject button on submission number forty. Do not read the tea leaves. Do not analyze the wording. It means "no," and it means nothing else. Log it in your tracker and send the piece out again the same day. [pause]

The second kind is the *tiered* rejection — sometimes called a higher-form rejection. It's still a form letter, but it's a *different* form letter, the one a magazine sends to the work that made it past the first cut into a later round. You learn to recognize them: slightly warmer, slightly different — "we read this with real interest," "this made it to our final discussions." [pause] When you get one of those, something real happened. You got close. Log it. That magazine is warming to you.

And the third kind — the rarest, the one I want you to learn to treasure — is the *personal rejection*. An actual human being wrote you an actual sentence. "This was very close." "We'd love to see more from you." "The ending didn't quite land for us, but the voice is wonderful — please send again." [pause] That, my friends, is gold. That is a small victory disguised as a defeat. Because a busy editor took the time, out of nine hundred submissions, to single you out and *ask you to come back*. So you do something most writers fail to do: you put that magazine on a special list — your return list — and within a few months you send them your very best new work, and you say, in one line, "Thank you for your kind note on my earlier story; I hope you'll consider this one." [pause] I will tell you something true: a great many of my eventual acceptances came from magazines that first sent me a kind no. The personal rejection is the warmest lead you will ever get in this business. Most writers throw it in a drawer and grieve. You're going to act on it.

Now let me protect your wallet for a minute, because rejection has an economy and there are people who prey on it. [pause] About thirteen percent of magazines charge a fee to submit — usually three dollars, which is often just the cost the platform charges them, sometimes up to twenty or twenty-five if it comes with a subscription or feedback. A small fee at a legitimate magazine is defensible; reading costs them money. But fee-free magazines always exist, and you are never *obligated* to pay to submit. Be suspicious when the fee buys "feedback," and very suspicious of a contest whose entry fees, multiplied by the entrants, dwarf the prize. [pause] And here is the brightest red line in this entire course, so write it down: a literary *agent* never, ever charges you a reading fee. Never. It's against their professional code of ethics. Any "agent" who asks you for money up front to read your work is a scam, full stop, every time. We'll come back to that in Session 8, but I want it in your bones now. [pause]

Let me leave you with the reframe that has kept me writing for forty years. A rejection is information about *fit* and *timing*. It is not a verdict on your *worth*. Your worth is not on the table. It was never on the table. What's on the table is whether this particular magazine had room for this particular piece this particular month. That's all a no ever means. [pause] And I'll give you a strange piece of homework that real writers actually do: set a rejection *target* for the year. Aim to collect a hundred rejections. Because you cannot possibly collect a hundred rejections without submitting like a serious person — and somewhere inside those hundred noes will be the handful of yeses that build your life.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *What if my work is rejected by everyone?* — That's the normal early experience, not a verdict; serious writers collect rejections in the hundreds. Keep the work in the air, target the right tier and fit, and treat each no as information about room and timing, not worth.
2. *How do I tell a form rejection from a personal one?* — A form letter is generic and addressed to "Writer" with no comment on your piece; a personal one names your work or signs off with a real invitation like "send more." When unsure, Rejection Wiki helps decode a magazine's standard tiers.
3. *Should I reply to a rejection?* — Never to a form rejection. To a genuine personal one, a brief thank-you is gracious and optional — and the real reply is to send that magazine your best new work soon, referencing their encouragement.
4. *Is it bad luck or bad writing?* — At competitive venues it's overwhelmingly arithmetic: too much good work, too little room. Bad writing reveals itself in a total absence of any personal or tiered response over many strong submissions.
5. *How many times should I resubmit a rejected piece before retiring it?* — Keep a strong piece moving through many venues — often a dozen or more — before reconsidering; if it earns only flat form rejections everywhere, revisit the craft, not the luck.
6. *Are submission fees a scam?* — Not inherently; a small fee at a legitimate magazine can be a fair way to cover reading costs, and fee-free options always exist. Be wary of "feedback for a fee" and of contests whose total entry fees dwarf the prize.
7. *Do agents ever charge to read my manuscript?* — No, never — it's prohibited by the AAR Canon of Ethics and is a reliable sign of a scam. A legitimate agent earns only a commission on what they sell for you.
8. *Why do some rejections take a year?* — Volume and small volunteer staffs; many magazines are run by a handful of people reading thousands of submissions. Use the stated response time, query politely once past it, and keep other work circulating.
9. *Should I take a personal rejection's editorial advice?* — Weigh it seriously — a busy editor singled you out — but trust your own sense of the piece. The surest response is to send new work, whether or not you revise the rejected one.
10. *How do I keep going emotionally?* — Keep many pieces circulating so no single no dominates, celebrate the tiered and personal rejections as real progress, and set a yearly rejection target so that submitting hard is the goal you actually control.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
This is the session where you lose students if you're not careful — and save them if you are. The danger is the talented, sensitive writer for whom a few form rejections confirm a private verdict of worthlessness, and who quietly stops submitting. Counter it by normalizing the arithmetic relentlessly and by reframing the personal rejection as a win to be celebrated. A second difficulty is the cynic who concludes the whole system is rigged or corrupt; meet this with the editor's-eye truth that the gatekeeper is overworked and rooting for them, not conspiring against them. The recurring hard conversation appears here as the student who treats *every* rejection as proof the gatekeepers can't recognize genius (often the same student certain of *New Yorker* readiness); gently introduce the idea that a long run of flat form rejections, with no tiered or personal notes at all, is itself feedback worth heeding — about craft, not conspiracy.

## Session 7 — Book-Length Submissions: Novels & Story Collections

**At a Glance**
We move from the single piece to the book: how a novel, a story collection, a novella, or a memoir actually finds a publisher, what materials a book submission requires (query, synopsis, sample, proposal), and the very different paths for fiction versus collections.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands the materials a book submission requires and how they differ from a magazine submission.
- Each student can describe the two main paths to a book: agent-to-big-house, and direct-to-small-press.
- Each student understands why story collections and poetry are harder to sell than novels — and where they sell.
- Each student can draft the components of a book package (query, one-page synopsis, sample pages).
- Each student knows what "finished" means for a book and when to start submitting.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The leap from publishing pieces to publishing a book is the largest single step in a writer's career, and it changes nearly every rule learned so far. A magazine wants one finished piece; a book publisher is making a multi-year investment and buying not just a manuscript but, implicitly, a writer. The materials reflect this. Fiction is sold (with rare exceptions) on a *completed manuscript*: an editor or agent must be able to read the whole novel, because a debut novelist has not yet earned the right to sell on a partial. The submission package for an agented novel is typically a **query letter**, a **synopsis** (a one-to-two-page summary that, unlike a magazine cover letter, *does* reveal the whole plot including the ending), and **sample pages** (often the first ten pages or first chapter), with the full manuscript sent on request. Nonfiction and memoir follow a different logic and can be sold on a **book proposal** plus sample chapters, because the market viability and the author's platform matter as much as the prose.

The two principal paths diverge sharply, and which one a writer takes depends largely on what they've written. The **agent-to-major-house** path (Session 8) is the route for commercially ambitious fiction and most nonfiction with broad appeal; the agent submits to acquiring editors at the large houses, and advances, when they come, range from a few thousand dollars for quiet literary debuts to the rare six figures ([Barnett Ghostwriting, 2026](https://www.barnettghostwriting.com/blog/average-book-advance-for-first-time-authors/)). The **direct-to-small-press** path (Session 9) bypasses agents entirely: many independent and university presses accept unagented manuscripts, often through open reading periods or contests, and this is where the majority of literary fiction, virtually all poetry, most story collections, and much memoir actually get published.

The hard truth this session must deliver concerns *form*. The market has a clear hierarchy of saleability, and it has little to do with literary merit. The novel is the most saleable form. The **story collection** is notoriously hard to sell to a major house — the received industry wisdom, repeated by agents for decades, is that "collections don't sell," and most are placed instead through small presses and the first-book contests (the Flannery O'Connor Award, the Iowa Short Fiction Award, the Drue Heinz Prize), which exist substantially *because* the open market neglects the form. The **novella** is harder still and lives almost entirely with small presses and dedicated novella contests. **Poetry collections** are essentially never sold on the open trade market and are placed almost exclusively through contests and small-press open readings (Session 10). A writer needs to know the saleability of their chosen form before they spend two years querying the wrong door.

Mr. Mulhern's editorial experience is directly relevant: he has evaluated book-length manuscripts and can speak to what an acquiring editor weighs beyond the prose — the shape of the whole, the second-book question ("is there a career here?"), the comparison titles, and the cold commercial arithmetic that sits, often uncomfortably, beside the editorial love.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. The book is the biggest step and changes the rules: a publisher buys a multi-year investment and, implicitly, a writer's whole future, not one piece.
2. Fiction sells on a COMPLETED manuscript. A debut novelist cannot sell on a partial; finish the book before you query. This is non-negotiable.
3. The fiction package: query letter + synopsis + sample pages (first chapter / first 10 pp), full MS on request. Distinguish each from magazine materials.
4. The synopsis is NOT a teaser — it reveals the whole plot, including the ending. Editors need to know the book works. (We draft one in Session 8.)
5. Nonfiction/memoir can sell on a PROPOSAL + sample chapters because platform and market matter as much as prose. Memoir is a partial exception — often wants more finished pages.
6. Two paths: agent → big house (Session 8) for commercial reach; direct → small press (Session 9) for most literary work, poetry, collections, novellas.
7. The saleability hierarchy, honestly: novel (most saleable) > memoir > story collection (hard) > novella (harder) > poetry collection (essentially never on open market).
8. "Collections don't sell" is industry folk wisdom — mostly true at big houses. Collections live at small presses and first-book contests (O'Connor, Iowa, Drue Heinz).
9. Comp titles matter for books in a way they never did for magazines: "for readers of X and Y." Start collecting comps now.
10. Know your form's door before you spend two years knocking on the wrong one. A poet querying literary agents is wasting their life.
11. What "finished" means for a book: drafted, revised, read by trusted readers, professionally proofed, and — for fiction — complete. Then, and only then, query.
12. The acquiring editor's hidden questions: Does the whole hold together? Is there a second book? Who buys this? Can we position it? Teach them to anticipate these.

**Lecture Script**

When I was an editor, the single most painful letter I had to write was not the rejection of a bad book. [pause] It was the rejection of a *good* book that I could not figure out how to sell. And I want to start there tonight, because it is the truth about book publishing that no one tells beginning writers, and you need it before you spend two years of your life knocking on the wrong door.

I once read a story collection — unagented, came in over the transom — that was genuinely beautiful. Quiet, strange, perfectly made. I loved it. And I could not acquire it, and here is why: I knew, from experience, that I would walk it into the sales meeting and the sales people would ask me one question — "how many copies will this sell?" — and the honest answer for a debut story collection by an unknown writer was: not many. Story collections, the saying goes, don't sell. [pause] That saying is cruel and it's mostly true, at least at the big houses, and it had nothing to do with how good this book was. I wrote the writer a long, personal, anguished rejection. I told her the truth: this belongs at a small press, or it belongs in a first-book contest built exactly for work like this. I hope she listened, because that was the real advice. [pause]

So tonight is about the largest step you will ever take as a writer — from publishing *pieces* to publishing a *book* — and about how almost every rule we've learned changes when you take it. [pause] A magazine wants one finished piece. A book publisher is making a multi-year investment, and they are not just buying your manuscript. They are buying *you* — your second book, your third, the whole career they hope is sitting behind this one. That changes everything about how you approach them.

Let me start with the iron rule, because writers break it constantly and it costs them. [pause] If you are writing a novel, and it is your first, you sell it *finished*. The whole thing. Drafted, revised, read by trusted readers, proofed, complete. You do not query a novel you have not finished. A famous novelist can sell a book on three chapters and a promise; you cannot, yet, and querying a half-finished manuscript is the fastest way to get a no on a book that might have gotten a yes. Finish it. Then we talk. [pause]

Now — what does a book submission actually consist of? It's not a cover letter. For a novel going to an agent, it's three things. First, a *query letter* — and we're spending all of next week on that, because it's the hardest single page you'll ever write. Second, a *synopsis* — and I need you to understand how different this is from the magazine cover letter, where I forbade you to summarize your story. The synopsis does the *opposite*. It tells the whole plot, beginning to middle to end — *including the ending*. [pause] Why? Because the editor isn't deciding whether to be entertained. They're deciding whether the book *works* — whether the structure holds, whether the ending earns itself — and they can't know that if you coyly withhold it. So the synopsis spoils everything on purpose. Third, *sample pages* — usually your first chapter or first ten pages — with the full manuscript ready to send the instant they ask. [pause]

Memoir and nonfiction play by slightly different rules, and this surprises people. You can often sell nonfiction on a *proposal* — a document about the book and its market and why you're the person to write it — plus a sample chapter or two, before the book is finished. Because for nonfiction, the *idea* and the *author's platform* matter as much as the prose. Memoir is a halfway case: it's nonfiction, so a proposal helps, but because it lives or dies on the writing, editors usually want to see a lot more finished pages than they would for, say, a business book. [pause]

Here's the part I most want you to carry out of this room: there are two completely different doors, and which one you walk through depends almost entirely on *what you wrote*. [pause] Door one: agent, then big New York house. That's for commercially ambitious fiction and broad-appeal nonfiction. We cover it next week. Door two: straight to a small press or a contest, no agent at all. And here is the truth — *most* literary fiction, almost *all* poetry, most story collections, most novellas, and a great deal of memoir go through door two. The small press is not the loser's door. For most of the work in this room, it's the *correct* door, and we'll spend a whole session on it. [pause]

So before you spend two years querying anyone, ask yourself the one question that letter-to-the-beautiful-collection-writer taught me: what *form* did I write, and how saleable is that form? [pause] A novel is the most saleable thing you can write. A memoir, if it has a hook, can sell. A story collection is hard — it belongs at small presses and the great first-book contests, the Flannery O'Connor Award, the Iowa Short Fiction Award, the Drue Heinz. A novella is harder still. And a book of poems essentially does not sell on the open market at all — it's placed almost entirely through contests and small-press open readings. [pause] None of that is about how good your book is. It's about how the market is shaped. And a poet who spends two years querying literary agents is a tragedy I can prevent right now, tonight, by telling you: that is the wrong door. Your door is wonderful and we'll get to it. But it's a different door. Let's learn to tell them apart.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *Do I have to finish my novel before querying?* — Yes, absolutely, for a debut. Agents and editors expect a complete, revised manuscript because you haven't yet earned the track record to sell on a partial; an unfinished query is a near-automatic no.
2. *Why does the synopsis give away the ending when the cover letter couldn't summarize?* — Because the editor is judging whether the whole book *works* structurally, not whether to be entertained. They need to see that the ending earns itself, so the synopsis deliberately spoils everything.
3. *Is it true story collections don't sell?* — At the big houses, largely yes — it's decades-old industry wisdom. But collections thrive at small presses and in first-book contests like the Flannery O'Connor and Iowa awards, which exist precisely because the open market neglects the form.
4. *Can I sell my memoir before it's finished?* — Sometimes, on a proposal plus sample chapters — but because memoir lives on its prose, most agents want substantial finished pages or the whole manuscript. Treat it closer to fiction than to other nonfiction.
5. *What are comp titles and do I really need them?* — Comparable titles ("for readers of X and Y") tell an editor where your book sits on the shelf and who buys it. For books they matter enormously; start collecting recent, well-chosen comps now.
6. *How long should a novel / collection be?* — Literary novels commonly run 70,000–100,000 words; story collections roughly 40,000–80,000. Check each press's or contest's stated range; wildly off-length manuscripts get screened out.
7. *Do I need an agent for a story collection or poetry?* — Usually not; both go primarily to small presses and contests directly, no agent required. A poet querying literary agents is knocking on the wrong door entirely.
8. *Can previously published stories go in my collection?* — Yes, and prior journal publication is an asset — list it in your acknowledgments. The collection as a whole simply must not have been published before.
9. *What does an acquiring editor look for beyond good writing?* — Whether the whole holds together, whether there's a second book and a career, who the reader is, and how the house would position it. Anticipating those questions strengthens your package.
10. *How do I know which door — agent or small press — is mine?* — Largely by form and ambition: commercial fiction and broad nonfiction lean agent-and-big-house; literary fiction, poetry, collections, novellas, and quiet memoir lean small-press and contest. We map both doors over the next three sessions.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
The central difficulty is the writer who has chosen a hard-to-sell form and aimed it at the wrong door — the poet querying agents, the story-collection author expecting a big-house auction — and who experiences the market's neglect of their form as a neglect of their talent. Separate the two explicitly and often: the form's saleability is structural and impersonal; their work may be excellent and still belong at a small press. A second difficulty is the writer who queries an unfinished novel out of impatience; hold the iron rule kindly but firmly. The recurring hard conversation recurs as the student convinced their debut will sell at auction for six figures; without crushing the hope, share the honest distribution — most literary debuts that sell at all sell for low four figures at independent presses — and reframe the small-press path as a real and dignified destination, not a fallback.

**Workshop / Action Element** (90 minutes)
- (0–10) Instructor framing: the beautiful-collection rejection.
- (10–35) Lecture; the two doors; the saleability hierarchy; what each package contains.
- (35–60) Each student identifies their book's form, names its likely door (agent vs. small press/contest), and drafts a one-paragraph elevator pitch with two comp titles, using the samples as models.
- (60–80) Pair work: partners pressure-test each other's pitch ("what's the hook? who buys it? is the door right?").

Finish the book, then knock on the right door — and which door is right is decided by the form you wrote, not by how good it is.

## Session 8 — Literary Agents: When You Need One, When You Don't

**At a Glance**
The agent is the most mythologized figure in publishing and the most misunderstood. Tonight we strip the mystery: what agents actually do, the iron 15% rule, how to query one, how to spot a scam — and the liberating truth that most writers in this room may never need one at all.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands what a literary agent does, how they're paid, and the 15% standard.
- Each student can identify the bright-line markers of a scam agent (reading fees, upfront charges).
- Each student can write a one-page query letter and a one-page synopsis.
- Each student understands when an agent is necessary and when it isn't.
- Each student can build a targeted, researched agent list using free tools.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The literary agent emerged in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries as publishing professionalized, and today the agent is the gatekeeper's gatekeeper: most of the large trade houses will not read unagented submissions at all, which means that for the agent-to-big-house path, the agent is not optional — they are the door. What an agent does is concrete and valuable: they evaluate and often help edit the manuscript, they know which editors at which houses want what, they submit strategically, they negotiate the contract (advances, royalties, rights, the option and reversion clauses), they chase payments, and they manage subsidiary rights (foreign, film, audio). For a complex, commercially ambitious deal, a good agent earns their share many times over.

The pay structure is the single most important fact, and it is mercifully simple and standard: the agent takes **15% of domestic earnings** — advances, royalties, and most rights — and roughly **20% on foreign sales** where a sub-agent is involved ([Authors Guild](https://authorsguild.org/resource/an-authors-guide-to-agency-agreements/)). The agent is paid only when the writer is paid; they take their cut from the money they bring in. From this flows the brightest red line in publishing: **a legitimate agent never charges a reading fee or any upfront fee.** The Association of American Literary Agents' Canon of Ethics expressly prohibits members from charging clients or prospective clients to read or evaluate their work, and nearly all reputable non-member agents follow the same rule ([Writer's Digest](https://www.writersdigest.com/agents/are-fee-charging-agents-legit)). Any "agent" who asks for money to read, to edit, to "consider," or who steers a writer toward a paid editing service they're connected to, is a scam — full stop, every time.

The querying mechanics have standardized around free tools. **QueryTracker** lists thousands of agents with their interests and response data and tracks a writer's queries; **Publishers Marketplace** (paid) shows which agents have actually sold what, the single best signal of whether an agent is real and active; **Manuscript Wish List** (#MSWL) publishes agents' specific wants; and the **AALA membership directory** and **Writer Beware** screen out the predators. The query letter itself is a one-page document — hook, brief plot or premise, comp titles, short bio — and the synopsis is its companion (the full-plot, ending-included summary from Session 7). Querying is a numbers game with its own rejection arithmetic: a typical search involves querying dozens of agents over many months, with most queries drawing form rejections or silence.

The liberating counter-truth, which Mr. Mulhern can deliver with unusual authority because he has been on both sides, is that **most writers in a community room may never need an agent.** Agents are essential for commercial fiction and broad-appeal nonfiction headed to the big houses. They are largely *irrelevant* to the path most literary writers actually travel: poetry (no agents), story collections and novellas (small presses and contests, no agent required), and the great body of literary fiction and memoir published by independent and university presses that accept direct submissions. To chase an agent for a book that doesn't need one is to add a year of rejection to a journey that had a shorter, agent-free road all along.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Demystify first: an agent is a business partner, not a magic key or a literary judge. They do concrete things — sell, negotiate, manage rights.
2. For the big houses, the agent is mandatory: most major houses won't read unagented manuscripts. The agent IS the door there.
3. The 15% rule: domestic earnings 15%, foreign ~20% with a sub-agent. The agent is paid only when you are. Standard and non-negotiable.
4. THE BRIGHT RED LINE: a real agent NEVER charges a reading fee or any upfront fee. AAR Canon of Ethics prohibits it. Drill this until automatic.
5. Scam markers: reading fees, "editing" fees, mandatory paid editorial services, vague track record, no verifiable sales. Teach the smell test.
6. The single best legitimacy check: have they actually SOLD books recently? Publishers Marketplace shows real deals. No sales = not a working agent.
7. The query letter is ONE page: hook, brief premise, comps, short bio. Hardest page you'll write. We draft it tonight.
8. The synopsis (from Session 7): 1–2 pages, whole plot including ending. Draft it tonight too.
9. Querying is a numbers game: query in batches of 6–10, track in QueryTracker, expect mostly form rejections, refine as you go.
10. Personalize lightly and truthfully ("I'm querying you because you represent X"). No flattery, no mass-blast that's obviously a mass-blast.
11. THE LIBERATION: most writers here may never need an agent. Poetry, collections, novellas, much literary fiction/memoir go straight to small presses. Don't chase an agent you don't need.
12. If an offer comes: an agent is for life-of-a-book and often a career; vet them like a business partner (talk to their clients, confirm the contract has a clear termination clause).

**Lecture Script**

Let me tell you about the most dangerous letter I ever almost fell for, back when I was a young writer who didn't know better. [pause] It came from an "agency." It was warm, it was flattering, it said my work showed "tremendous promise" — and it said that before they could represent me, I'd need to have my manuscript "professionally evaluated," and they happened to know just the editorial service, and it would only cost a few hundred dollars. [pause] I was thrilled. I had an agent interested! And I came *this* close to sending the check. What stopped me was that I happened to mention it to an older writer, who looked at me the way you'd look at someone about to step off a curb into traffic, and said four words I'm going to give you tonight: *agents don't charge fees.* [pause] That's it. That's the whole lesson, and it would have saved me three hundred dollars and a piece of my dignity. So before we talk about what agents *are*, I'm going to tattoo on you what they are *not*. A real literary agent never, ever charges you money to read your work, to evaluate it, to edit it, or to "consider" it. Never. It's against their professional code of ethics. The moment money flows *from* you *to* an agent before they've sold anything, you are not talking to an agent. You're talking to a predator wearing an agent's clothes. [pause]

Good. Now that your wallet is safe, let me demystify the whole figure, because the agent is the most romanticized creature in publishing and the romance does you no good. [pause] An agent is not a magic key. An agent is not a literary judge who certifies you're a real writer. An agent is a *business partner* who does concrete, valuable things: they know which editor at which house is hungry for which kind of book, they submit your manuscript strategically, they negotiate your contract — the advance, the royalty rates, the rights, the clauses most writers don't even know to fight about — and they chase your money and manage your foreign and film rights. For a big, complicated deal, a good agent earns their keep ten times over. [pause]

How are they paid? Here's the beautiful, simple, standard answer: fifteen percent. [pause] Fifteen percent of what you earn domestically — your advance, your royalties — and about twenty percent on foreign deals where another agent abroad is involved. And — this is the elegant part — they only get paid when *you* get paid. They take their fifteen percent out of the money they bring in the door. That's why the reading fee is such a bright red flag: a real agent has no business taking your money before they've made you any. Their incentive is to sell your book. The scammer's incentive is to collect fees from hopeful writers and never sell anything at all. [pause]

So how do you find a real one and avoid the fake one? The single best test — write this down — is: *have they actually sold books recently?* A working agent has a track record of real deals at real houses, and you can look it up. There's a paid service, Publishers Marketplace, that shows you actual deals — who sold what to whom. If an "agent" has no verifiable sales, they are not a working agent, no matter how impressive the website. [pause] And there are free tools: QueryTracker lists thousands of agents and lets you track your queries; Manuscript Wish List shows you what specific agents are actively hungry for; and Writer Beware, run by the science fiction writers' organization, keeps a running list of the scammers. Use all of them.

Now let's talk about the document that gets you in the door: the query letter. [pause] It is one page. One. And it is, I promise you, the hardest single page you will ever write, because it has to do three things in under a page: hook the agent in the first line, sketch your premise without summarizing the whole plot, and tell them — briefly — who you are and what your book is like, with comparison titles. "For readers of Elizabeth Strout and Kent Haruf." That tells the agent in five words where your book sits on the shelf. We're going to draft one tonight, together, and then a synopsis to go with it — and remember, the synopsis is the *opposite* of coy; it tells the whole plot, ending and all. [pause]

Querying is a numbers game, like everything else in this life. You query in small batches — six to ten at a time — you track every one in QueryTracker, you expect mostly form rejections and silence, and you refine your letter as you learn. It's the rejection arithmetic from week six, all over again, just at the book level. [pause]

And now the thing I most want you to hear, the thing that having been an editor lets me say with a straight face. [pause] *Most of you may never need an agent at all.* I mean that. Agents are essential if you've written a commercial novel or a big-idea nonfiction book aimed at the New York houses. But if you've written poetry — there are no poetry agents, essentially. If you've written a story collection or a novella — those go to small presses and contests, no agent required. And a huge amount of literary fiction and quiet memoir is published by wonderful independent and university presses that take submissions *directly from you*, no agent in between. [pause] So I am giving you permission, tonight, to stop chasing the agent you've been told you need, if your book doesn't need one. To spend a year querying agents for a book of poems is a tragedy. There's a shorter road, and it doesn't run through this office at all. Next week, we walk it.

## Session 9 — Small Presses: Where Most Literary Writers Actually Live

**At a Glance**
The honest center of the course. We dismantle the prejudice that the small press is a consolation prize and show it for what it is: the natural and dignified home of literary fiction, poetry, collections, novellas, and quiet memoir — with its own real economics and its own great names.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands the role and economics of the independent, university, and small literary press.
- Each student can name and research real small presses appropriate to their genre.
- Each student understands how small presses acquire (open readings, contests, direct submission, no agent).
- Each student understands small-press contract terms and modest advances honestly.
- Each student can distinguish a reputable small press from a vanity operation — including how to evaluate Silver Current Press.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The small and independent press is not the minor leagues of literary publishing; for most of the work that matters, it is the major leagues. As the Big Five contracted their mid-list over three decades, the editorial mission of literary publishing migrated outward to a constellation of independent, nonprofit, and university presses — Graywolf, Coffee House, Tin House Books, Milkweed Editions, Sarabande, Copper Canyon, BOA Editions, Tupelo, Two Dollar Radio, Bellevue Literary Press, Deep Vellum, Catapult/Soft Skull, and dozens more — that now publish a large share of the most celebrated literary books in America, including National Book Award and Pulitzer winners. Graywolf alone has published Pulitzer and major-prize winners while remaining a nonprofit. These presses do what the big houses increasingly cannot: they publish the quiet, the formally adventurous, the regional, the translated, the slow-building career.

The economics are honest and modest, and students deserve the real numbers. Advances at small and independent presses for literary fiction commonly run **$1,000–$5,000**, often less; for poetry, frequently **$0** plus a small honorarium and author copies; for many nonprofit and university presses, the "advance" may be nominal and the value lies in the editorial care, the design, the distribution, and the prestige rather than the check ([Vox Ghostwriting, 2025](https://www.voxghostwriting.com/blog/average-book-advance-for-first-time-author/); [Barnett Ghostwriting, 2026](https://www.barnettghostwriting.com/blog/average-book-advance-for-first-time-authors/)). Royalty rates are broadly comparable to trade norms (often a touch higher on net at some indies), and crucially, **most small presses accept unagented submissions directly** — through open reading periods, standing submissions, or contests — which means the writer keeps the full earnings and skips the agent search entirely. Distribution varies and matters: the strongest indies distribute through Consortium, Publishers Group West, or university-press distributors and get real bookstore and library placement; weaker ones rely on print-on-demand and Amazon alone, which limits reach.

The essential discrimination this session must teach is **reputable small press vs. vanity press.** The bright line is money flow and selectivity. A *reputable* press — whatever its size — pays the author (even a token advance or just royalties and copies), selects work editorially (rejecting most submissions), and bears the production costs itself; the money flows *to* the author. A *vanity* or predatory operation charges the author to publish, accepts nearly everyone, and makes its money from writers rather than readers; the money flows *from* the author. This line is independent of size or polish. It is also the precise standard by which a student may, and should, evaluate **Silver Current Press**: it is Mr. Mulhern's own imprint, devoted to literary fiction, poetry, and the well-made book; like any reputable small press it should be judged by whether it pays its authors, selects editorially, and bears its own costs — and (per the course firewall) it is entirely off-limits to anyone enrolled. Teaching students to apply the same test to Silver Current that they'd apply to any press is both honest and self-protective.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Reframe hard and early: the small press is not a consolation prize. It is the natural home of literary fiction, poetry, collections, novellas, quiet memoir. Say it with conviction.
2. Name the great indies so they're real: Graywolf, Coffee House, Milkweed, Sarabande, Copper Canyon, BOA, Tupelo, Tin House Books, Two Dollar Radio, Bellevue Literary, Deep Vellum, Catapult/Soft Skull.
3. Indies win major prizes (Graywolf — Pulitzers, NBAs). This is not the minor leagues. Drive the point home.
4. Honest economics: literary fiction advances ~$1–5K at indies, often less; poetry often $0 + copies + honorarium. The value is editorial care, design, distribution, prestige.
5. Most small presses take UNAGENTED submissions directly — open readings, standing submissions, contests. No agent, full earnings, shorter road.
6. Distribution matters: strong indies use Consortium/PGW/university distributors → real bookstore + library reach. POD-and-Amazon-only limits reach. Teach them to check.
7. Contracts: broadly trade-comparable royalties, sometimes higher on net; watch the option clause, rights grants, and reversion. (Tie to glossary.)
8. THE BRIGHT LINE — reputable vs. vanity: money flows TO the author (reputable) vs. FROM the author (vanity). Selectivity + press bears production cost = legitimate.
9. Apply that test openly to Silver Current Press: judge my own imprint by the same standard — pays authors, selects editorially, bears costs. And reaffirm the firewall: off-limits to this class.
10. How to research a press: read their catalog, check distribution, look at their authors' other books, confirm they pay, read the contract terms if posted.
11. Reading fees at small presses: some legitimate nonprofits charge modest reading/contest fees ($3–$45, e.g., Tupelo's $45 prose fee) to defray costs — defensible, but always weigh against free open readings.
12. The dignity thread: a writer with three good books from good small presses has a complete, honorable career. Want that. Teach them to want that.

**Lecture Script**

I want to start tonight by saying something that the publishing world spent my whole career making writers ashamed of, and that I am going to un-shame, right now, in this room. [pause] If your book is published by a small press, you have not failed. You have not settled. You have not landed in the minor leagues. For the kind of writing most of us in this room actually do — literary fiction, poetry, story collections, novellas, the quiet memoir — the small press is not the consolation prize. It is the *correct destination.* It is where the work *lives.* [pause]

Let me make that concrete, because "small press" sounds small, and it isn't. When you read about a book of poems winning the Pulitzer, or a literary novel winning the National Book Award, look at the spine. More and more often it says Graywolf. Or Coffee House. Or Milkweed, or Sarabande, or Copper Canyon, or Tin House Books. [pause] Graywolf Press is a *nonprofit* in Minneapolis and it has published Pulitzer Prize winners. These are not vanity operations or hobby publishers. They are the beating heart of literary publishing in America, and they took over that role precisely *because* the big New York houses spent thirty years cutting the very kind of book they specialize in. When the mid-list contracted, the mission didn't die. It moved. It moved to the small press. [pause]

Now I have to give you the honest money, because I promised you the honest money on the first night and I'm not going to flinch now. [pause] A small press is probably not going to make you rich, and might not pay you much at all up front. A literary novel at an independent press commonly comes with an advance of one to five thousand dollars — sometimes less. A first book of poems? Often *no* advance at all: a small honorarium, a stack of author copies, and a beautifully made book with your name on it. [pause] And I want to reframe what you're actually being paid *with*, because it isn't only the check. You're being paid in editorial care — a real editor who reads your book closely and makes it better. You're being paid in design — a cover and an interior made by people who love books. You're being paid in distribution and in the quiet prestige of a press whose name means something to the few hundred readers who matter to you. For most literary writers, that's the real currency, and it's worth more than the small advance. [pause]

Here's the part that should make some of you breathe easier after last week. [pause] *Most small presses don't require an agent.* You submit to them *directly* — through an open reading period, or a standing submission window, or a contest. No agent in the middle. Which means you keep your full earnings, and you skip the whole year-long agent search we talked about last week. For a poet, a story-collection writer, a literary novelist — this is very often the *shorter* road, and it's the road that's actually open to you.

But — and now I have to teach you to protect yourself — there is a kind of operation that dresses up like a small press and is in fact a predator, and you must learn to tell them apart. The test is simple, and it's about which way the money flows. [pause] A *reputable* press, no matter how small, *pays you* — even if it's just royalties and copies — and it *selects*, meaning it rejects most of what it receives, and it pays the production costs *itself*. The money flows *to* the author. A *vanity* press does the opposite: it charges *you* to publish your book, it accepts almost everyone because its customers are writers not readers, and it makes its money *from* you. The money flows *from* the author. [pause] That line — which way does the money flow — is the whole test. It has nothing to do with how slick the website is. A slick vanity press is still a vanity press. A humble nonprofit that pays you in copies is still a real press.

And now I'm going to do something I want you to notice me doing, because it's the honest thing. [pause] I run a small press. It's called Silver Current Press; it publishes literary fiction and poetry and the well-made book. And I am telling you to apply that *exact same test* to *my* press that I just taught you to apply to any press. Does it pay its authors? Does it select editorially? Does it bear its own production costs? Judge Silver Current by the same standard as Graywolf and Tupelo and everyone else — no special pass because I'm standing in front of you. [pause] And — you remember the firewall from week one — Silver Current is completely off-limits to everyone in this room, during this course and for a year after. I will not publish you. I want you judging presses, including mine, with clear eyes, never wondering whether your teacher is also a salesman. That clarity is a gift I can give you precisely *because* I've drawn the line. [pause]

Let me leave you with the dignity, because that's the real subject of tonight. A writer who, over twenty years, publishes three good books with three good small presses — read carefully by a few thousand readers, reviewed in the places that review such books, respected by the writers they respect — that writer has a *complete* and *honorable* career. Not a failed shot at fame. A whole life in letters. I want you to learn to want *that*. Because that one is actually available to you, and it's better than the lottery you were taught to dream about.

**Sample Documents**

> **SAMPLE — How to Vet a Small Press (a checklist students can apply, including to Silver Current Press)**
>
> 1. **Which way does the money flow?** Do they PAY the author (advance and/or royalties and copies), or do they CHARGE the author to publish? Paying = legitimate. Charging = vanity. *(This is the whole test.)*
> 2. **Are they selective?** Do they reject most submissions, or accept nearly everyone? Selectivity signals a real editorial mission.
> 3. **Who bears production costs?** A real press pays for editing, design, and printing itself. If you're asked to pay or to "buy copies" as a condition, walk away.
> 4. **Distribution:** Do they distribute through Consortium, PGW, a university distributor, or only through Amazon/POD? Real distribution means bookstore and library reach.
> 5. **Catalog & authors:** Read their list. Are the books well-made? Where else have their authors published? Are the authors glad they signed?
> 6. **Contract terms:** Reasonable royalty, clear rights grant, a sane option clause, and a reversion clause that returns rights if the book goes out of print.
> 7. **Reputation:** Check Writer Beware, the authors' own accounts, and CLMP membership.
>
> *Applied to Silver Current Press:* judge it by 1–7 exactly as you would Graywolf or Tupelo — and note that, per this course's firewall, it does not consider work from anyone enrolled, during the course or for a year after.

>
> Dear Editors,
>
> I'm submitting my story collection *Tidewater* (68,000 words; twelve linked stories) to your open reading period. Eight of the stories have appeared in journals, including *The Cincinnati Review*, *Pithead Chapel*, and *The Galway Review* (full acknowledgments enclosed). The collection follows three generations of a Delaware estuary fishing family as the water rises; I believe it would sit comfortably alongside your editions of [name two of the press's actual titles].

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *Is publishing with a small press settling for less?* — No. The small press is the natural home of literary fiction, poetry, collections, and quiet memoir, and indies like Graywolf win Pulitzers and National Book Awards. For most literary work it's the correct destination, not a fallback.
2. *How much will a small press pay me?* — Honestly, modestly: roughly $1,000–$5,000 for literary fiction and often $0 plus copies and a small honorarium for poetry. The real value lies in editorial care, design, distribution, and prestige.
3. *Do I need an agent for a small press?* — Usually not; most small presses accept unagented submissions directly through open readings or contests. That means you keep your full earnings and skip the agent search.
4. *How do I tell a real small press from a vanity press?* — Follow the money: a real press pays you and bears production costs; a vanity press charges you to publish and accepts nearly everyone. That single test settles it, regardless of how polished they look.
5. *Is Silver Current Press a vanity press?* — Apply the same test you'd apply to any press: does it pay its authors, select editorially, and bear its own costs? Judge it exactly as you'd judge Graywolf — and note it's off-limits to this class by design, so the question stays academic for you.
6. *Why do some reputable presses charge a reading or contest fee?* — Many nonprofits charge a modest fee ($3–$45) to defray the genuine cost of reading; Tupelo's $45 prose fee is an example. That's defensible in moderation, but free open readings always exist too.
7. *Does distribution really matter?* — Yes; a press that distributes through Consortium, PGW, or a university distributor gets your book into stores and libraries, while an Amazon-and-POD-only operation limits your reach considerably.
8. *What contract terms should I watch?* — The royalty rate, the rights you're granting, the option clause (your next book), and especially the reversion clause that returns your rights if the book goes out of print. Have the Authors Guild or a knowledgeable friend review it.
9. *How do I find presses that fit my book?* — Read catalogs, note where writers like you publish, and use the CLMP and Poets & Writers directories. The best signal is a press whose existing list your book would sit comfortably beside.
10. *Will a small-press book be reviewed and taken seriously?* — The strong indies are reviewed in the places that review literary books and are taken very seriously by the readers and writers who matter to a literary career. Distribution and the press's reputation drive how far that reach extends.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
The deepest difficulty is the lingering shame — the student who has internalized that "real" books come from New York and who hears the small-press path as a demotion. Counter it with the prize-winning indie names and the dignity thread, repeated until the room reevaluates. The opposite difficulty is the student so eager to be published that they're vulnerable to a vanity press's flattering offer; the follow-the-money test is their armor, and you should drill it. A particular delicacy attends the Silver Current question: handle it with complete transparency, inviting students to apply the same standard to your press as to any other and reaffirming the firewall, so that your dual role never reads as a soft sell. The recurring hard conversation — the student who insists only a big house will do — is met by honoring the ambition while showing, with numbers, that the small press is where their *form* actually lives.

**Workshop / Action Element** (90 minutes)
- (0–10) Instructor framing: un-shaming the small press; the prize-winning indies.
- (10–35) Lecture; the honest economics; the follow-the-money test; the transparent Silver Current discussion.
- (35–60) Each student researches three real small presses that fit their genre, recording: what they publish, whether they take unagented work, how they acquire (open reading/contest), distribution, and whether they pay.
- (60–80) Vetting drill: present two anonymized "press offers" (one reputable indie, one vanity operation) and have the room apply the seven-point checklist.
- (80–90) Take-home: deepen one press into a real target — read three of its books' descriptions, note its next reading period, draft a direct submission note.

**One Thing to Remember**
The small press is the natural and honorable home of literary writing — and the whole test of any press, including your teacher's, is which way the money flows.

## Session 10 — Contests, Fellowships, Awards

**At a Glance**
Contests are the strange, important engine that publishes work the open market neglects — especially poetry and story collections — and the system that funds fellowships and first books. Tonight we teach how contests actually work, how to enter wisely, and how to tell the prestigious from the predatory.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands how literary contests and first-book prizes function, including the entry-fee model.
- Each student can name the major, legitimate first-book contests for their genre and their deadlines.
- Each student understands fellowships and grants (NEA, state arts councils, residencies' funding) and how they differ from contests.
- Each student can evaluate a contest's legitimacy (prize vs. fee math, judge, publisher, track record).
- Each student leaves with a short list of contests/fellowships matched to their work and a deadline calendar.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
For poetry and the short story collection, the contest is not a side path — it is the *main* path to a first book. The major first-book prizes exist precisely because the open trade market neglects these forms, and over decades they have become the recognized gateway. In poetry: the **Yale Series of Younger Poets** (the oldest annual literary award in the United States, founded 1919, publishing the winner through Yale University Press), the **Academy of American Poets First Book Award** ($5,000 plus publication by Graywolf and a six-week Italian residency, $35 entry fee, roughly September deadline), the **Walt Whitman Award**, the **National Poetry Series**, and the open first-book contests at presses like **Tupelo**, **BOA**, **Alice James**, and **Button Poetry** ($25 entry, $1,000 plus publication). In fiction: the **Flannery O'Connor Award for Short Fiction** (University of Georgia Press, ~April–May window, $1,000 plus publication), the **Iowa Short Fiction Award** (University of Iowa Press, July 1–August 31 window, for writers without a prior book-length work of fiction), and the **Drue Heinz Literature Prize** (University of Pittsburgh Press, May 1–June 30, no entry fee, requires some prior publication). These are legitimate, prestigious, and career-launching ([Poets.org First Book Award](https://poets.org/academy-american-poets/prizes/first-book-award); [Iowa Short Fiction Awards](https://fictionawards.writersworkshop.uiowa.edu/how-submit)).

The economic model must be taught plainly. Most contests charge an **entry fee** ($25–$35 is typical), and the fees, multiplied across hundreds or thousands of entrants, substantially fund the prize and the press's operations. This is not inherently corrupt — a $25 fee from 800 entrants funds a $1,000 prize, publication, and the reading labor — and at a legitimate, established contest it is a fair cost of an honest shot. But the model also breeds predators: "contests" whose fees vastly exceed any plausible prize, whose judges are anonymous or nonexistent, whose "winning" publication is really a disguised vanity deal. The legitimacy test mirrors Session 9: a real contest names a reputable judge, is run by or tied to a real publisher with a real catalog, has a verifiable history of winners who went on to careers, and publishes the winner without charging *them* anything further.

Fellowships and grants are a categorically different and better thing, because they pay the writer with no strings and no publication-as-prize: the **NEA Creative Writing Fellowship** awards **$50,000** to individual writers (alternating prose and poetry years, deadline typically March) — life-changing and intensely competitive; **state arts council** fellowships offer smaller but real grants; **Guggenheim Fellowships** support established artists; and many **residencies** (Session 13) carry funded or fee-waived components ([NEA FY26 Program Details](https://www.arts.gov/sites/default/files/FY26-CWF-Prose-Grant-Program-Details.pdf)). Unlike contests, fellowships don't take an entry fee in exchange for a shot at publication; they are merit grants. The writer's posture toward all of this should be strategic and unsentimental: enter the legitimate contests appropriate to your form, especially the no-fee ones; apply for fellowships you're eligible for; budget contest fees as a modest, deliberate annual line item; and never let a contest's outcome stand in for the verdict on your worth.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. For poetry and story collections, the contest is the MAIN road to a first book, not a side path. Built precisely because the open market neglects these forms.
2. Name the legitimate poetry first-book prizes: Yale Younger Poets (since 1919, Yale UP), Academy of American Poets First Book ($5K + Graywolf + Italy residency, $35), Walt Whitman Award, National Poetry Series, Tupelo/BOA/Alice James/Button.
3. Name the legitimate fiction-collection prizes: Flannery O'Connor (Georgia UP, ~Apr–May), Iowa Short Fiction (Iowa UP, Jul 1–Aug 31, no prior book), Drue Heinz (Pittsburgh UP, May 1–Jun 30, NO fee, needs prior pubs).
4. The entry-fee model, explained honestly: $25–35 typical; fees × entrants fund the prize and press. Not corrupt at legit contests; a fair cost of an honest shot.
5. The predator test (mirrors Session 9): real judge named? Real publisher with real catalog? Verifiable winners with careers? Winner charged nothing further? If not, walk.
6. Fellowships are categorically better — they PAY the writer, no publication-as-prize, no entry-for-a-shot. NEA Creative Writing Fellowship = $50,000 (alternating prose/poetry, ~March deadline).
7. State arts councils + Guggenheim (established artists) + funded residencies = the grant ecosystem. Apply where eligible.
8. Budget contest fees as a deliberate, modest annual line item. Prioritize no-fee contests (Drue Heinz) and high-value ones.
9. Read every contest's eligibility precisely — "no prior book-length work," page ranges, genre, simultaneous-entry rules. Disqualification on a technicality is heartbreaking and avoidable.
10. A contest loss tells you NOTHING about your worth — one judge, one year, one taste. Re-enter; winners often lost many times first.
11. Pushcart, Best American, Best Small Fictions, O. Henry: these are nomination-based anthology honors (magazines nominate you) — different from contests you enter. Explain the distinction.
12. Build a personal deadline calendar tonight (point to the back-matter calendar) matched to their form.

**Lecture Script**

I want to begin with the oldest literary prize in America, because its story tells you what a contest really is. [pause] The Yale Series of Younger Poets has been published every year since 1919. Yale University Press prints the winner's first book. More than a century of poets have come into the world through that one contest. And here is what I want you to notice: that prize exists because there was no *other* reliable way for a first book of poems to reach the world. There was no open market for it. There still isn't. [pause] So a publisher and a university built a *door* — a contest — specifically so that the work the market ignored could find its readers. That's what a literary contest fundamentally is. It's not a lottery and it's not a beauty pageant. For poetry and for the story collection, it is the *main road* to a first book. Not a detour. The road. [pause]

Let me name the real ones for you, because part of my job tonight is to make sure you spend your entry fees on the legitimate contests and not the predators. [pause] If you write poetry: the Yale Younger Poets, as I said. The Academy of American Poets First Book Award — five thousand dollars, publication by Graywolf, and, I'm not making this up, a six-week residency in a fifteenth-century castle in Italy; the entry fee is thirty-five dollars and the deadline is around September. The Walt Whitman Award. The National Poetry Series. And the open first-book contests at the good poetry presses — Tupelo, BOA, Alice James, Button Poetry. [pause] If you write short stories and have a collection: the Flannery O'Connor Award, published by the University of Georgia Press, reading roughly April into May. The Iowa Short Fiction Award — University of Iowa Press, open July and August, specifically for writers who haven't yet published a book of fiction. And the Drue Heinz Prize at Pittsburgh, which — write this down — charges *no entry fee at all*, though it asks that you've published some stories already. These are real, these are prestigious, and these *launch careers.* [pause]

Now I have to explain the money, because it's the part that confuses and sometimes angers writers, and you deserve to understand it clearly. [pause] Most contests charge an entry fee — twenty-five, thirty-five dollars is typical. And yes: those fees, multiplied by the hundreds or thousands of people who enter, are what *funds* the prize and keeps the little press running. Is that corrupt? No — not at a legitimate contest. Think about it honestly: a thirty-dollar fee from eight hundred entrants pays the thousand-dollar prize, pays to publish the winning book, and pays the human beings who read all eight hundred manuscripts. That's a fair cost for an honest shot at a real first book. [pause] But — and here's where you have to be sharp — the same model is irresistible to predators. So you apply the same test we used for vanity presses last week. Is a real, named judge involved? Is it run by a real publisher with a real catalog of books you can hold? Can you find past winners who went on to actual careers? And is the *winner* charged nothing further — no "publication package," no required purchase? If the answers are yes, it's legitimate. If a "contest" is anonymous, has no real publisher behind it, and the prize is suspiciously small next to the fees pouring in — walk away. [pause]

I want to draw a bright distinction now between contests and *fellowships*, because fellowships are a categorically better thing and most writers don't even try for them. [pause] A contest takes your fee and gives you a shot at publication. A fellowship simply *pays you* — no entry-for-a-shot, no publication-as-prize, just money to be a writer. The crown jewel is the National Endowment for the Arts Creative Writing Fellowship: *fifty thousand dollars*, given directly to individual writers, alternating between prose and poetry years, deadline usually in March. It's ferociously competitive, but it costs nothing to apply and it can change a life. Below it: your state arts council almost certainly offers fellowships — smaller, but real, and far less competitive than the NEA. The Guggenheim, for more established artists. And many residencies, which we'll cover in week thirteen, come with funding. [pause] Apply for the fellowships you're eligible for. The worst case is a no, and you already know what to do with those.

One more distinction, because people mix these up constantly. [pause] The Pushcart Prize, Best American Short Stories, the O. Henry Prize, Best Small Fictions — these are *not* contests you enter. These are *anthology honors* where the *magazines* that published you nominate your work. You don't apply; you get nominated, usually by the journal that ran your piece. Which is one more reason to build relationships with the magazines that publish you — they're the ones who put your name forward. [pause]

And I'll close with the same reframe I gave you in week six, because it applies double to contests. A contest loss tells you *nothing* about your worth. One judge. One year. One person's taste on one afternoon. The poet who wins the Yale prize this year was very likely rejected by it three years running. So you enter, and you lose, and you revise, and you enter again — the same persistence, the same long game, the same refusal to let a single no mean more than it does. Tonight we're going to build you a deadline calendar, matched to whatever form you write, so that entering becomes a habit instead of a heartbreak.

## Session 11 — The Author Platform: What Helps and What Is a Waste of Time

**At a Glance**
"Platform" is the most anxiety-producing word in a writer's vocabulary and the most misunderstood. Tonight we separate the platform that genuinely helps a literary writer from the social-media churn that mostly steals time from the writing — and we do it without contempt for either.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands what "platform" means and why it matters differently for fiction/poetry vs. nonfiction.
- Each student can distinguish the few platform activities that help from the many that mostly waste time.
- Each student understands the honest relationship between marketing and writing.
- Each student can decide, for their own goals, where (if anywhere) to invest platform energy.
- Each student is freed from platform guilt and equipped with a realistic, minimal plan.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
"Platform" entered the writer's vocabulary from the nonfiction world, where it has a precise and defensible meaning: an author's demonstrable ability to reach an audience that will buy the book — a column, a podcast, a professional standing, an email list, a following in a relevant field. For nonfiction and especially how-to, self-help, and topical books, platform is genuinely part of what a publisher buys; an expert with fifty thousand engaged newsletter subscribers can sell a proposal that identical prose from an unknown cannot. This is real, and students writing nonfiction should take it seriously.

For literary fiction and poetry, the picture is very different, and the honest version is rarely told because an entire economy of courses, consultants, and apps profits from telling writers the opposite. The truth: for literary work, platform helps modestly at the margins and is wildly overstated as a precondition. Editors at literary presses acquire on the quality of the manuscript, not on a TikTok following; a debut literary novelist with a beautiful book and two thousand Twitter followers is in essentially the same position as one with two hundred thousand. The data on author earnings is brutal and clarifying here: the great majority of literary books sell modestly regardless of their authors' social-media activity, and the hours a literary writer pours into platform-building are hours not spent on the only thing that reliably builds a literary career — the next book ([For The Writers, 2025](https://www.forthewriters.com/post/the-economic-realities-of-traditional-publishing-today)).

What *does* help a literary writer, in rough order: (1) **the work itself**, published steadily in journals and books — the genuine "platform" of a literary career is a body of work; (2) **a simple, professional author website** — a single page with a bio, a list of publications, and contact information, which costs little and lasts; (3) **genuine relationships** within the literary community — editors who've published you, writers you actually read and correspond with, the magazines that nominate you for Pushcarts; (4) **a modest email list or newsletter**, if and only if the writer enjoys it, as a direct line to the few hundred readers who care; and (5) **selective, pleasurable participation** in literary life — readings, a Substack one actually wants to write, conversations one would have anyway. What mostly *wastes* a literary writer's time: chasing follower counts, daily content churn, "engagement" metrics, paid ads for a poetry collection, and any activity undertaken from guilt or fear rather than genuine interest. The governing principle, which Mr. Mulhern can deliver with the authority of someone who built a real career without any of it, is that **marketing is not writing, and for literary work the writing is ninety-five percent of the job.** The platform anxiety is largely manufactured; the antidote is to write the next good thing.

## Session 12 — Self-Publishing & Indie: The Honest Truth

**At a Glance**
Self-publishing is neither the shameful last resort it was once branded nor the get-rich path the gurus sell. Tonight we give the genuinely honest version: when it's the right choice, when it isn't, what it actually costs and earns, and how to do it without being fleeced.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands the real spectrum from traditional to hybrid to self-publishing, and the money flow in each.
- Each student can identify when self-publishing is a sound choice and when it isn't, for literary work specifically.
- Each student understands the real costs (editing, design, ISBN) and real economics (royalty rates, sales reality).
- Each student can spot the predators: vanity presses dressed as "hybrid," overpriced packages, scam services.
- Each student can make an informed, unashamed decision about whether self-publishing fits their goals.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
Self-publishing has been transformed twice in twenty years: first by print-on-demand and Amazon's Kindle Direct Publishing (KDP), which removed the garage full of unsold books and made it possible for anyone to publish a professional-looking book for almost nothing; and second by the maturing of a genuine indie-author economy in which some writers — overwhelmingly in commercial genre fiction (romance, thriller, science fiction, LitRPG) — earn real, sometimes substantial, livings. The royalty math explains the appeal: where a traditional publisher pays roughly 10% of a hardcover's list price or 25% of net on ebooks, KDP pays up to **70%** on ebooks priced between $2.99 and $9.99 ([Barker Books, 2025](https://barkerbooks.com/royalties-from-books/)). For a prolific genre writer with a series and an engaged readership, that margin, multiplied by volume, is a business.

The honest qualifier — and the one literary writers most need — is that this success is concentrated almost entirely in genre fiction with series potential and active reader communities, and that it depends on the author becoming, in effect, a small publishing *business*: commissioning professional editing and cover design, managing metadata and categories, running ads, and producing books on a schedule. For literary fiction, poetry, and most memoir, the indie path rarely produces either the sales or the recognition that the small-press path offers, because literary readers, reviewers, prizes, and bookstores still largely route through traditional and small-press channels. A self-published literary novel is fully legitimate and occasionally finds its audience, but it forgoes the editorial imprimatur, the review coverage, the prize eligibility (many contests exclude self-published work), and the bookstore distribution that a small press provides. The instructor's own history is instructive and worth naming honestly: some of Mr. Mulhern's earlier titles appeared through CreateSpace/independent publishing, which is precisely this path — a legitimate route he can speak to from inside, neither romanticizing nor disowning it.

The essential protection this session must teach is the distinction among three things that are often deliberately blurred. **True self-publishing**: the author uses KDP, IngramSpark, or Draft2Digital, pays only for the services they choose (editing, design), keeps all rights and the lion's share of revenue, and the money flows *to* the author. **Hybrid publishing**: a legitimate hybrid asks the author to share costs but provides real editorial selection, professional production, and distribution, with clear contracts and the author retaining strong rights — a narrow, defensible category. **Vanity publishing dressed as "hybrid"**: the overwhelming majority of operations soliciting writers, which charge thousands of dollars for production an author could buy far more cheaply à la carte, accept nearly everyone, and make their money from authors rather than readers. The bright line, again, is selectivity and which way the money flows — and the scale of the charge: a legitimate freelance editor and cover designer might cost $1,500–$4,000 total; a "package" charging $8,000–$25,000 is almost always a vanity operation wearing a respectable costume.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Kill the old shame AND the new hype in the same breath: self-publishing is neither a disgrace nor a lottery ticket. It's a tool that fits some books and not others.
2. Two revolutions: print-on-demand + Amazon KDP (no more garage of books); and a real indie-author economy where some writers earn real livings.
3. The royalty appeal: KDP pays up to 70% on ebooks $2.99–$9.99, vs. ~10% hardcover / 25% net ebook traditional. Volume × margin = a business — for the right book.
4. THE BIG QUALIFIER: indie success is concentrated in GENRE fiction with series + reader communities. The author becomes a publishing BUSINESS (editing, covers, ads, schedule).
5. For LITERARY fiction, poetry, memoir: indie rarely beats the small press on sales OR recognition. Literary readers, reviews, prizes, bookstores still route through traditional/small-press channels.
6. Self-pub forgoes: editorial imprimatur, review coverage, prize eligibility (many contests bar self-pub work), bookstore distribution. Name these costs honestly.
7. Be transparent about my own CreateSpace/independent titles — a legitimate path I've used and can speak to from inside. Neither romanticize nor disown.
8. THREE things deliberately blurred: (a) true self-pub (KDP/IngramSpark/D2D — money flows TO author, keep rights); (b) legit hybrid (narrow, real selection + production, clear contract); (c) VANITY dressed as "hybrid" (most of them — charge thousands, accept everyone).
9. The cost reality: à la carte freelance editor + cover designer ≈ $1,500–$4,000 total. A "package" at $8K–$25K is almost always vanity in costume.
10. The bright line (again): selectivity + which way money flows + scale of the charge. Drill it.
11. If you self-publish, do it RIGHT: professional editing, professional cover, your own ISBN (not the platform's free one if you want to be the publisher of record), good metadata.
12. The unashamed decision: match the tool to the book and the goal. A family memoir for 40 relatives → self-pub is perfect. A literary novel seeking reviews and prizes → small press first.

[pause] There are three things that get *deliberately* blurred together, and you must learn to separate them. That's clean and legitimate. The second is *legitimate hybrid* publishing — a narrow, real category where you share costs but the press genuinely selects work, produces it professionally, distributes it, and gives you a fair contract. Those exist, but they're rare. [pause] And the third — the one that swallows most hopeful writers — is *vanity publishing wearing a hybrid costume.* These companies solicit you with flattery, accept essentially everyone, and charge you thousands — eight thousand, fifteen thousand, twenty-five thousand dollars — for production you could buy yourself, à la carte, for a fraction. [pause]

So here's the number that protects you. If you hire a good freelance editor and a good cover designer for a literary book, you might spend somewhere between fifteen hundred and four thousand dollars, total, and you'd own everything. If a company quotes you eight thousand, fifteen thousand, twenty-five thousand for a "package" — that is, almost always, a vanity operation in a respectable suit. The bright line is the same as last week, with one addition: Is it selective? Which way does the money flow? And now — *how big is the charge relative to what the services actually cost?* [pause]

So let me give you the honest, unashamed decision framework, and then we'll work through each of your books. [pause] Match the tool to the book and to your goal. If you've written a family memoir and what you want is forty beautiful copies for your relatives and to know it exists — self-publishing is *perfect*, do it, do it well, and feel proud. If you've written a literary novel and what you want is reviews, prizes, bookstore shelves, a literary reputation — try the small press first; that's the channel that delivers those things. And if you do choose to self-publish anything, do it *right*: professional editing, a professional cover, your own ISBN, good metadata. A self-published book done with care is a real book. A self-published book done cheaply looks exactly as cheap as it was, and that's the only thing that confirms the old shame. [pause] Let's match each of you to your tool.

5. *Is hybrid publishing legitimate?* — A narrow band is — real selection, real production, fair contracts, strong author rights. But most operations marketing themselves as "hybrid" are vanity presses charging far above cost; scrutinize selectivity, the fee's size, and which way the money flows.
6. *How do I spot a vanity press?* — It accepts nearly everyone, charges you thousands to publish, makes its money from authors not readers, and often pressures you to decide fast. A legitimate publisher pays you or, at most, a legitimate hybrid shares clearly-defined costs.
7. *Should I buy my own ISBN?* — If you want to be the publisher of record, yes (via Bowker in the US); the platform's free ISBN lists the platform as publisher. For a family memoir it may not matter; for a book you want to brand as your imprint, it does.
8. *Will bookstores carry my self-published book?* — Rarely through Amazon-only KDP, but IngramSpark provides the distribution channel bookstores and libraries order through. Stocking still depends on the store, and self-published literary titles face real resistance there.
9. *Can I self-publish now and go traditional later?* — Sometimes, but self-publishing a book usually ends its eligibility for debut deals and many first-book contests, and traditional houses rarely pick up a self-published title unless it sold remarkably. Decide deliberately, not as a fallback after rejections.
10. *Isn't self-publishing the fast way around all the rejection?* — It removes the gatekeeper, but it doesn't remove the hard part — producing an excellent book and finding its readers. It's a different job (becoming a small publisher), not an easier one, and for literary work it forgoes the recognition the small-press path provides.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
Two opposite difficulties meet here. Some students carry the old shame and need it lifted — self-publishing is a legitimate tool, and your own use of it is the proof. Others have absorbed the guru hype and expect a literary self-published book to earn a genre writer's income; deliver the honest, genre-specific economics. The gravest danger is the vanity predator: a discouraged writer, fresh off rejections, is exactly the target for a flattering five-figure "hybrid" offer, and you should arm them with the cost numbers and the follow-the-money test until refusing is reflexive. The recurring hard conversation appears as the student who, frustrated by gatekeepers, wants to self-publish a not-yet-ready literary manuscript to bypass the verdict; gently separate the legitimate uses of self-publishing from using it to escape the revision the work still needs.

**Workshop / Action Element** (90 minutes)
- (0–10) Instructor framing: killing the shame and the hype; the personal CreateSpace disclosure.
- (10–35) Lecture; the genre-vs-literary reality; the three blurred paths; the cost numbers.
- (35–55) Decision exercise: each student names their book and goal, then places it on the framework (traditional/small-press first vs. self-publish vs. neither-yet) and writes one sentence justifying the choice.
- (55–75) Predator drill: hand out the "hybrid" solicitation sample; the room marks every red flag and computes the à-la-carte cost of what's being sold.
- (75–90) For anyone choosing self-pub: draft the "done right" checklist for their book. Take-home: if self-publishing, get one freelance editor and one cover-designer quote; if not, return to the small-press/contest list from Sessions 9–10.

**Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor**
- Jane Friedman, *The Business of Being a Writer* (2nd ed., University of Chicago Press, 2024) — the clearest comparison of traditional, hybrid, and self-publishing economics.
- Guy Kawasaki & Shawn Welch, *APE: Author, Publisher, Entrepreneur* (Nononina Press, 2013) — a thorough, if optimistic, self-publishing how-to.
- Jane Friedman, "Hybrid Publishers: A Reality Check" and her ongoing posts on vanity operations: https://janefriedman.com/blog.
- Writer Beware (SFWA) — the standing archive of vanity/"hybrid" scams: https://writerbeware.blog.
- The Alliance of Independent Authors (ALLi), *Choosing a Self-Publishing Service* (annual guide): https://www.allianceindependentauthors.org.

**One Thing to Remember**
Self-publishing is a legitimate tool, not a disgrace and not a lottery — match it to the book and the goal, do it professionally, and remember the bright line: a real publisher pays you, a vanity press charges you.

## Session 13 — Conferences, Residencies, MFAs

**At a Glance**
The three institutions writers are told they "should" pursue — conferences, residencies, and the MFA — examined honestly: what each actually offers, what each costs, who genuinely benefits, and how to get the value without the debt or the disappointment.

**Session Goals**
- Each student understands what writing conferences (AWP, Bread Loaf, Sewanee) offer and what they don't.
- Each student understands residencies — funded vs. fee-based — and how to apply.
- Each student can weigh the MFA honestly: its real benefits, its real costs, and whether it's necessary (it isn't).
- Each student can decide which, if any, of these fits their goals and budget.
- Each student is freed from the belief that any of these is a prerequisite to publication.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The three institutions form the "professionalized" layer of the writing world, and each is genuinely valuable to *some* writers and genuinely oversold to *all* of them. The **MFA** — the Master of Fine Arts in creative writing — proliferated enormously in the late twentieth century; there are now hundreds of programs in the US. What an MFA actually buys is time (two to three years to write), teachers, a cohort and a network, and sometimes funding and teaching experience; what it does *not* buy is anything an editor requires. Many, perhaps most, of the writers in any literary magazine have no MFA. The honest case against it is financial: a fully funded MFA (one that pays tuition and a stipend) can be a gift, but an *unfunded* MFA — tens of thousands of dollars of debt for a degree with essentially no salary attached, in a field where median book income is in the low four figures — is a financial decision a teacher must help students see clearly. The rule of thumb the field's own honest voices repeat: pursue an MFA only if it's funded, or if you can truly afford it and want the experience for its own sake — never as a credential you believe you need to be published.

**Conferences** range from the enormous to the intimate. **AWP** — the Association of Writers & Writing Programs annual conference and bookfair — is the field's town square, drawing on the order of ten to twelve thousand attendees and a vast bookfair of presses and magazines; it is overwhelming, useful for immersion and for meeting editors at bookfair tables, and not in itself a path to publication ([San Antonio Report](https://sanantonioreport.org/12000-writers-to-descend-on-san-antonio-for-literary-super-bowl/)). The summer **workshop conferences** — **Bread Loaf** (Middlebury, the oldest and most famous, founded 1926), **Sewanee**, **Tin House**, **Napa Valley** — offer intensive workshops, faculty contact, and scholarships/fellowships that defray the substantial fees; their value is craft, community, and occasionally a meaningful connection, not a guaranteed door.

**Residencies** are, for many literary writers, the best value of the three because the strongest ones are *funded* — they give the writer time, space, and often room and board at no cost. **Yaddo** and **MacDowell** (the most prestigious American residencies, competitive, free to attend once admitted), the **Vermont Studio Center**, **Hedgebrook**, **Ucross**, and dozens of others offer uninterrupted weeks to work; some charge fees, others are fully funded, and many offer need-based aid. The honest counsel across all three institutions, which Mr. Mulhern can deliver as someone who built a career and was selected for a Writing Fellowship at Exeter College, University of Oxford without needing any of them as a credential, is that **none of these is a prerequisite to publication** — they are accelerants and pleasures for those who want and can afford them, not gates one must pass.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. Frame all three as "professionalized layer" — valuable to SOME, oversold to ALL. None is a prerequisite to publication. Lead with that liberation.
2. The MFA buys: time to write, teachers, cohort/network, sometimes funding + teaching experience. It does NOT buy anything an editor requires.
3. Most writers in any journal have no MFA. Say it. The degree is not a credential editors check.
4. THE MONEY RULE for MFAs: pursue ONLY if fully funded (tuition + stipend) OR you can genuinely afford it for the experience. Never go into debt for it as a "credential."
5. Unfunded MFA = tens of thousands in debt for a degree with no salary attached, in a field where median book income is low four figures. Make the math vivid.
6. AWP: the field's town square, ~10–12K attendees, huge bookfair. Useful for immersion + meeting editors at tables. NOT a path to publication by itself.
7. Summer conferences — Bread Loaf (since 1926), Sewanee, Tin House, Napa — intensive workshops, faculty contact, scholarships/fellowships defray fees. Value = craft + community, not a guaranteed door.
8. Residencies are often the BEST value: the strongest ones are FUNDED (free to attend). Yaddo, MacDowell (most prestigious), Vermont Studio Center, Hedgebrook, Ucross.
9. Residencies give the rarest gift: uninterrupted TIME. Apply to funded ones; many have need-based aid. A residency on your CV also reads well.
10. Scholarships/fellowships exist for nearly all conferences — never assume you must pay full freight. Apply for aid.
11. My own authority: built a career + an Exeter College Writing Fellowship at Oxford, without needing these as credentials. They're accelerants and pleasures, not gates.
12. Decision framework: what do you need — time (residency), teaching/cohort (MFA, if funded), immersion/contacts (conference)? Match the institution to the need and the budget.

**Lecture Script**

Tonight we're going to talk about the three things every writer is told they "should" do — get an MFA, go to conferences, win a residency — and I'm going to tell you the truth about all three, which is the same truth in three costumes: each one is genuinely wonderful for *some* writers, genuinely oversold to *everyone*, and *none* of them is a prerequisite to getting published. [pause] I can say that with a straight face because I built a whole writing life, and was selected for a Writing Fellowship at Exeter College, University of Oxford, without ever needing any of them as a *credential.* So let's take them one at a time and figure out which, if any, is right for *you.*

The big one first: the MFA. The Master of Fine Arts in creative writing. [pause] There are hundreds of these programs now, and the air around them is thick with the implication that a serious writer gets one. So let me tell you exactly what an MFA buys, honestly. It buys you *time* — two or three years where your main job is to write, which is an enormous gift. It buys you *teachers*, and a *cohort* — a group of fellow writers who become your readers and friends and sometimes your network for life. And sometimes it buys you *funding* and *teaching experience.* Those are real things. [pause] Now let me tell you what it does *not* buy: anything an editor requires. I'll say that again because it cuts against everything you've been told. No editor anywhere checks whether you have an MFA. Most of the writers in most of the magazines you admire don't have one. The degree is not a key, and the absence of it is not a lock. [pause]

So here is the rule, the money rule, and I want you to write it down because it can save you from a decision that ruins lives. [pause] Pursue an MFA *only* if it is fully funded — meaning they pay your tuition *and* give you a stipend to live on — *or* if you can genuinely afford it and you want the experience for its own sake. Never, ever go into debt for an MFA as a credential you think you need. Do the math out loud with me: an unfunded MFA can cost you forty, sixty, eighty thousand dollars in debt — for a degree that comes with *no salary attached*, in a field where the median writer earns from their books something in the low four figures a year. [pause] A funded MFA is a gift; take it if you want the time and the teachers. An unfunded one, taken out of the belief that you *need* it to be published, is a financial tragedy I'm trying to prevent right now, tonight.

Now, conferences. [pause] These run from the enormous to the intimate. The enormous one is AWP — the Association of Writers and Writing Programs conference — which is the closest thing our field has to a town square. Ten, twelve thousand writers descend on a city for a few days, and there's a vast bookfair where every literary magazine and small press has a table. It's overwhelming, it's exhausting, and it's genuinely useful — for immersing yourself in the field, for buying journals, for actually shaking the hand of the editor who publishes work like yours. But understand: going to AWP is not, by itself, a path to publication. It's immersion, not a door. [pause] Then there are the summer workshop conferences — Bread Loaf, the oldest and most storied, running since 1926 up in Vermont; Sewanee; Tin House; Napa Valley. These are intensive: you workshop your pages with a faculty writer, you live among other writers for a week or two, and — write this down — *they almost all offer scholarships and fellowships* that cover the substantial fees. Never assume you have to pay full freight. Apply for the aid. The value here is craft and community, with the occasional real connection — not a guaranteed yes. [pause]

And now the one I think is, for many of you, the best value of the three, and the one writers think about least: the residency. [pause] A residency gives you the single rarest and most precious thing in a writing life — *uninterrupted time.* Weeks, sometimes, in a quiet place, with nothing asked of you but to work. And here's the wonderful part: the *strongest* residencies are *funded.* Yaddo and MacDowell — the two most prestigious in America — are free to attend once you're admitted. Competitive, yes, but free. The Vermont Studio Center, Hedgebrook, Ucross, dozens of others — some charge, many are funded, many offer need-based aid. [pause] A residency gives you time *and* looks wonderful on your literary CV *and* puts you among working writers. If I could send each of you to exactly one of tonight's three institutions, for most of you it would be a funded residency, because time is the thing none of you have enough of.

So let me leave you with the decision framework, because the answer is different for each of you. [pause] Ask yourself what you actually *need.* If what you need is *time* — apply to funded residencies. If what you need is *teaching, a cohort, and you can get it funded* — consider the MFA, on the money rule. If what you need is *immersion and contacts* — go to a conference, and apply for the scholarship. [pause] And if what you need is to be told that you must do *any* of these before you're allowed to call yourself a real writer who can get published — then what you need is for me to tell you, one more time: you don't. These are accelerants and pleasures. They are not gates. The gate was never locked. You've been walking through it all term. Let's match each of you to the one that actually fits.

## Session 14 — Building a Submission Year

**At a Glance**
The synthesis session: we take everything learned and turn it into a *system* — a sustainable, repeatable annual rhythm of writing, revising, submitting, tracking, and entering, so that publication becomes the natural output of a process rather than the result of sporadic hope.

**Session Goals**
- Each student can design a realistic twelve-month submission calendar matched to their genre and life.
- Each student understands how to balance journal submissions, book/contest deadlines, and writing time.
- Each student can set quantitative targets (submissions out, pieces written) that they control.
- Each student understands the rhythm of the literary year (reading periods, contest deadlines).
- Each student leaves with a draft submission year on paper.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The difference between writers who publish and equally talented writers who don't is, more than anything else, the presence or absence of a *system* — a sustainable annual rhythm that keeps work being written, revised, and sent without depending on bursts of motivation. The literary year has a real shape, and a writer who learns it gains a quiet advantage. Most literary magazines have **reading periods** rather than year-round openness, commonly concentrated in the fall (roughly September–December) and late winter/spring (January–April), with many closing over the summer and around the December holidays. The major **book and contest deadlines** cluster predictably: poetry first-book prizes often in late summer and fall (the Academy of American Poets First Book Award closes around September 1; many press contests run August–November), the **Iowa Short Fiction Award** July 1–August 31, the **Flannery O'Connor Award** roughly April–May, the **Drue Heinz Prize** May 1–June 30, the **NEA Creative Writing Fellowship** around March, and Pushcart nominations made by magazines in the October–December window. A writer who maps these onto a calendar stops missing deadlines and stops scrambling.

The system's components are the tools and disciplines built across the course: the writing schedule (protected hours), the revision pipeline (the drawer, reading aloud, the checklist from Session 5), the submission engine (volume, simultaneous submission, the tracker from Session 4, keeping work "in the air" from Session 6), and the deadline calendar (contests and fellowships from Session 10, residencies from Session 13). The art is in *balance* — not letting submission admin devour writing time (Session 11's lesson applies), not letting perfectionism stall the pipeline, and setting targets on the inputs one controls rather than the outputs one doesn't. The healthiest targets are *process* targets: a number of new pieces drafted, a number of submissions sent, a number of contests entered, a number of rejections collected (the "100 rejections" frame from Session 6) — never "get published X times," which depends on gatekeepers and breeds despair.

The deeper teaching, and the one Mr. Mulhern can model from a forty-year practice, is that the submission year is how the *long quiet career* (the course's first recurring thread) is actually built — not in heroic sprints but in a sustainable rhythm repeated year after year, each year's body of work feeding the next year's books and credits. A writer who builds a good submission year and simply repeats it for a decade will, almost regardless of luck, accumulate a real and dignified career. The system is the secret. There is no other secret.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. The thesis: the system, not talent or luck, separates those who publish from those who don't. A sustainable annual rhythm beats bursts of motivation.
2. Teach the SHAPE of the literary year: most magazines have reading periods (heavy fall Sep–Dec, late-winter/spring Jan–Apr; many closed summer + December holidays).
3. Map the big recurring deadlines: NEA ~March; Drue Heinz May–Jun; Flannery O'Connor ~Apr–May; Iowa Jul–Aug; poetry first-book prizes late summer–fall (Academy First Book ~Sep 1); Pushcart noms Oct–Dec.
4. The four system components: writing schedule (protected hours) + revision pipeline (Session 5) + submission engine (Sessions 4 & 6) + deadline calendar (Sessions 10 & 13).
5. BALANCE is the art: don't let submission admin eat writing time (Session 11); don't let perfectionism stall the pipeline (Session 5). Protect the writing first.
6. Set PROCESS targets on inputs you control: # pieces drafted, # submissions sent, # contests entered, # rejections collected. NOT "get published X times" (you don't control that).
7. The "100 rejections" target (Session 6) operationalized: it forces a high submission volume, inside which the yeses hide.
8. A concrete monthly rhythm: a weekly "submission hour"; a monthly review of the tracker; quarterly contest/deadline check; an annual goal-setting.
9. Keep work in the air year-round: rejected piece goes back out same day; finished new piece enters the pipeline immediately.
10. Match the plan to the LIFE — a working parent's plan looks different from a retiree's. Realistic and sustainable beats ambitious and abandoned.
11. The long game: a good submission year, simply REPEATED for a decade, builds a career almost regardless of luck. The system IS the secret; there's no other.
12. Tonight they build a real draft submission year on paper — months, targets, deadlines, protected writing time.

**Lecture Script**

I'm going to tell you the secret tonight. [pause] After thirteen weeks of letters and trackers and agents and contests, after forty years of my own writing life, I'm going to tell you the single thing that most reliably separates the writers who get published from the writers who are just as talented and don't. And it isn't talent. And it isn't luck, though luck plays its part. It's a *system.* [pause] It's the unglamorous fact that the writers who publish have built a sustainable, repeatable, year-round *rhythm* — of writing, revising, submitting, tracking, entering — that keeps working whether or not they feel inspired, whether or not the last rejection stung, whether or not it's a good week. And tonight we're going to build you that system. We're going to take everything you've learned and turn it into a *submission year.* [pause]

Here's the first thing that gives you a quiet advantage over writers who don't know it: the literary year has a *shape.* [pause] It's not a flat, year-round field. Most literary magazines don't read all year — they have *reading periods.* And those periods cluster: heavily in the fall, roughly September through December, and again in late winter and spring, January through April. Many magazines *close* over the summer, and almost everything goes quiet around the December holidays. So if you learn that rhythm, you stop wasting submissions on closed magazines and you start front-loading your sending into the windows when editors are actually reading. [pause] And the big deadlines — the contests and fellowships — they cluster predictably too. The NEA fellowship around March. The Drue Heinz Prize in May and June. Flannery O'Connor in spring. The Iowa Short Fiction Award in July and August. The poetry first-book prizes in late summer and fall. Pushcart nominations made by magazines in the last months of the year. [pause] When you put all of those on a single calendar, you stop missing things, and you stop scrambling. The deadline stops being an ambush and becomes an appointment.

So let me give you the four parts of the machine, because you've already built all of them this term — we're just bolting them together tonight. [pause] Part one: a *writing schedule.* Protected hours, however few, that are sacred. Part two: the *revision pipeline* from week five — the drawer, reading aloud, the checklist — that turns drafts into ready work. Part three: the *submission engine* from weeks four and six — volume, simultaneous submission, the tracker, keeping work in the air. And part four: the *deadline calendar* — the contests, the fellowships, the residencies — from weeks ten and thirteen. Four parts. You own all four already. The system is just running them together, all year, in rhythm. [pause]

Now, the hardest part of the whole thing, and the one I most want to protect: *balance.* [pause] There is a failure mode where the submission machinery — the tracking, the researching, the formatting, the entering — quietly eats all your time, and you look up after a month and realize you've *administered* your writing career without actually *writing* anything. Remember week eleven: marketing is not writing, and admin is not writing either. The writing comes first. Protect the writing hours before you schedule anything else. [pause] And there's the opposite failure mode — the perfectionist who won't let anything into the pipeline until it's flawless, so the pipeline stays empty. Both of those are the system breaking. The system only works when work keeps *flowing* through it — written, revised, sent, tracked, repeat.

Here's the piece that will change how you feel about all of it. [pause] Set your targets on the things you *control.* You do not control whether you get published — that's the gatekeeper, the room available, the luck. So don't set "get published five times" as your goal, because you can do everything right and miss it and feel like a failure. Instead set *process* targets: I will draft this many new pieces. I will send this many submissions. I will enter this many contests. I will *collect a hundred rejections.* [pause] I love that last one, and I gave it to you in week six, and now I'm operationalizing it: you literally cannot collect a hundred rejections without submitting like a serious writer — and somewhere inside those hundred noes, the yeses are hiding. You aim at the rejections, which you control, and the acceptances arrive as a *byproduct.* That reframe alone has kept more writers going than any pep talk.

And the rhythm can be simple. [pause] A weekly *submission hour* — same time every week, when you send things out. A monthly look at your tracker — what's been out too long, what came back, what needs to go out again. A quarterly check of upcoming contest and reading-period deadlines. And once a year, you set your goals and look back at last year's. That's it. That's a professional writing life's entire administrative footprint. [pause]

Let me close with the long view, because it's the whole point of this course. [pause] A *good submission year, simply repeated for a decade,* builds a career — almost regardless of luck. That's not a motivational slogan; it's arithmetic. Ten years of steady writing, steady revision, steady submission at the right tier and fit, steady contest entries — it accumulates into a body of published work, into books, into a real and dignified life in letters. The system *is* the secret. There is no other secret. I've been looking for forty years, and that's the only one I ever found. [pause] So tonight we build you a year. A real one, on paper, matched to *your* life — your job, your family, your energy. Not the heroic year you wish you could do. The sustainable year you'll actually repeat. Let's build it.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *How many submissions a year is realistic?* — It depends on your life, but many serious writers send dozens to a few hundred pieces-to-venues a year. Set a number you can sustain, log it, and let volume make the acceptance math work.
2. *How do I balance submitting with actually writing?* — Protect the writing hours first and confine submission to a fixed weekly "submission hour," so admin never colonizes the creative time. Writing comes first; the system serves it, not the reverse.
3. *Should my goal be a number of publications?* — No; set process targets on what you control — pieces drafted, submissions sent, contests entered, rejections collected. Publications arrive as a byproduct, and tying your goals to them invites needless despair.
4. *When are the best times to submit?* — Front-load into the open reading periods, heaviest in the fall (September–December) and late winter/spring, and ease off in summer and around the December holidays when many magazines close.
5. *How do I keep track of so many deadlines?* — Build one deadline calendar — contests, fellowships, residencies, and reading-period openings — and check the next three months each quarter. The companion's Contest & Fellowship Calendar is a ready starting grid.
6. *What if I can only write a few hours a week?* — Then build a year around that reality; a sustainable small rhythm repeated for years beats an ambitious plan you abandon. Match the plan to your actual life, not an idealized one.
7. *How often should I resend a rejected piece?* — Ideally the same day it comes back, during your submission hour — keeping work "in the air" is the engine of the whole system. A rejected piece is simply between magazines.
8. *Should I write new work or submit more — which matters?* — Both, in balance; the new work feeds the pipeline and builds the body of work, while submitting circulates what's ready. A good year does both on a steady rhythm.
9. *How long until the system pays off?* — Often a year or two before the first acceptances cluster, and a decade to build a real body of work. The point is that a good submission year repeated reliably builds a career almost regardless of luck.
10. *What's the single most important habit?* — The protected writing time, closely followed by the weekly submission hour. Everything else is bookkeeping around those two appointments with yourself.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
The chief difficulty is sustainability: students design ambitious "heroic" years they abandon by March, then conclude they lack discipline rather than that the plan was unrealistic. Push every student toward a plan matched to their actual life — the working parent's year is not the retiree's — because the sustainable plan repeated for a decade is the one that works. A second difficulty is outcome-fixation: students who set publication-count goals and despair when gatekeepers don't cooperate; redirect firmly to process targets and the "100 rejections" frame. The recurring hard conversation appears as the student who wants the system to *guarantee* publication on a timeline; honor the hunger while holding the honest line — the system raises the odds and builds the career, but no system removes the gatekeeper or the luck. Watch for the admin-as-procrastination trap and protect the writing hours above all.

## Session 15 — Reading Together: Real Cover Letters, Real Queries, Real Rejections (Workshop)

**At a Glance**
The hands-on workshop session. We read and diagnose real documents together — cover letters, queries, synopses, rejections — first from the prepared anonymized samples, then from the students' own materials, building the diagnostic eye that turns this course's principles into instinct.

**Session Goals**
- Each student can diagnose the strengths and flaws of a cover letter, query, synopsis, and rejection at a glance.
- Each student receives constructive feedback on their own submission materials.
- Each student internalizes the course's principles by applying them to real examples.
- Each student leaves with a revised, workshop-tested set of submission documents.
- The cohort practices the generous, honest critique culture this course has modeled all term.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
This session is structurally different from the others: it is mostly workshop, and its "content" is the accumulated principles of the previous fourteen weeks applied in real time to real documents. Its pedagogical premise is sound and old: principles become instinct only through repeated application to concrete cases. A writer who has *heard* that a cover letter should not summarize the story will still write summaries; a writer who has diagnosed a dozen summary-laden letters as a group, named the flaw aloud, and then found it in their own draft, will stop. The workshop is where the course's knowledge migrates from the head to the hand.

The model to follow is the best of the writing-workshop tradition, stripped of its cruelty. The literary workshop has, at its worst, a reputation for performative harshness — the instructor or peers competing to wound. That tradition produces defensiveness, not growth, and it has no place in a community room of adult writers at varied stages. The better tradition — associated with the most humane teachers — is *diagnostic and generous*: name what works first and specifically, then name what doesn't work as a craft problem to be solved rather than a verdict to be delivered, and always with an actionable suggestion attached. The instructor's job is to model this and to protect it: to ensure the room critiques the *document*, never the person, and that every critique ends with a path forward.

Mr. Mulhern's dual authority makes this session uniquely valuable. He can read a student's query and say, with the standing of a former acquiring editor, "Here is exactly where I would have stopped reading, and here is why" — and then, with the standing of a writer who has submitted three hundred times, "and here is how I would fix it, because I have made this same mistake." The anonymized samples (some flawed, prepared in advance) provide a safe warm-up before students expose their own work, and the progression — sample diagnosis, then peer documents, then self-revision — lets the diagnostic muscle develop on low-stakes material before it's turned on one's own. The session also serves the cohort: it is where the generous-critique culture the course has modeled all term becomes a practiced skill the writers can carry into future workshops and writing groups.

**Key Teaching Points** (lecture notes to self)
1. This is mostly workshop — my talking is minimal. The principles of weeks 1–14 become instinct only through application to real cases.
2. Establish the critique culture FIRST and protect it all night: diagnose the DOCUMENT, never the person; name what works before what doesn't; every critique ends with an actionable fix.
3. No performative harshness. The cruel-workshop tradition produces defensiveness, not growth. Model the generous, honest, diagnostic alternative.
4. Use the prepared anonymized samples as a SAFE warm-up before anyone exposes their own work. Diagnose flawed samples as a group first.
5. The diagnostic checklist for a cover letter: real greeting? facts present? NO summary? honest unpadded bio? under 150 words? (Session 3)
6. For a query: hook in line one? premise without over-summary? real comps? appropriate length/word count? short honest bio? no bravado/ultimatum? (Session 8)
7. For a synopsis: whole plot including ending? structure visible? clear and not coy? (Sessions 7–8)
8. For a rejection: is it form / tiered / personal? what should the writer DO in response? (Session 6)
9. My unique move: "here's exactly where I'd have stopped reading, and why" (editor) + "here's how I'd fix it, I've made this mistake too" (writer). Use both hats openly.
10. Progression: sample diagnosis → peer documents → self-revision. Build the muscle on low stakes before turning it on one's own work.
11. Keep it moving and time-boxed; everyone's document gets attention; no one's gets dwelt on punitively.
12. End with each writer revising their OWN materials in light of what they diagnosed in others — the migration from head to hand.

Tonight I'm going to talk less than I have any other night, because tonight isn't for me. ## Session 16 — Capstone: Your Submission Plan

**At a Glance**
The final session brings the course full circle. Each student completes and presents a concrete, personalized submission plan — the written, executable strategy promised on the first night — and leaves not with hope but with a map and the next morning's first action.

**Session Goals**
- Each student completes a written, specific, twelve-month submission plan they could begin executing tomorrow.
- Each student revisits the twelve-month goal they wrote in Session 1 and reconciles it with what they now know.
- Each student identifies their immediate next three concrete actions.
- Each student understands the plan as a living document to be repeated and revised annually.

**Historical / Industry Context** (instructor background)
The capstone fulfills the contract set in Session 1: students leave not with hope but with a plan. The plan is the integration of everything — the genre-appropriate door (agent vs. small press vs. contest vs. self-publishing), the tiered list of journals and presses, the contest and fellowship deadlines, the submission-year calendar and process targets, the revision pipeline, the tracking system, and the minimal platform decisions. It is deliberately *personal*: a poet's plan, a memoirist's plan, and a commercial novelist's plan look entirely different, because the doors and the calendars differ by form. The pedagogical value of writing it down and presenting it aloud is well established — a plan committed to paper and spoken to witnesses is far more likely to be executed than a private intention.

The honest framing of this final night matters as much as its content. The course has promised, all term, not to over-promise, not to crush hope, and not to romanticize the work. The capstone must hold that balance to the end. The plan is not a guarantee of publication — no plan is — but it is a genuine, evidence-based raising of the odds and, more importantly, a structure for a sustainable writing *life* regardless of any individual outcome. The instructor's role is to help each student calibrate ambition to reality without extinguishing it: to ensure the plan has reach venues *and* realistic targets, deadlines *and* protected writing time, and a first concrete action small enough to actually take the next morning.

This is also where the course's deepest thread comes home. The capstone's emotional work is to send each writer out believing that this is both achievable and worth it — that the writing life, lived as a sustained practice rather than chased as a lottery, is available to every person in the room. Some of you wrote something modest and now know you can reach further; some wrote something grand and now know to build a ladder to it. [pause] If your goal changed, that is not failure. That is the entire point of the last sixteen weeks. You know things now you didn't know then, and a wiser goal is the best possible souvenir of this room.

So let me tell you what goes into your plan, because it's everything we've done, made *yours.* [pause] First: your *door* — and which door it is depends entirely on what you write. If you're a poet, your door is journals and first-book contests and small presses; there's no agent in your plan at all. If you're writing a commercial novel, your door is the agent. If you're writing literary fiction, or a story collection, or a memoir, your door is most likely the small press and the contest, with the agent as a maybe. *Your form chooses your door.* Second: your *tiered lists* — the journals and presses and contests that actually fit your work, a few reaches among many realistic targets. Third: your *calendar* — the submission year from last week, with the real deadlines for your genre marked. Fourth: your *process targets* — the numbers you control: pieces drafted, submissions sent, contests entered, rejections collected. Fifth: your *systems* — the revision pipeline, the tracker, the weekly submission hour. And sixth: your *platform decision* — which, for most of you, is "build the one-page website and otherwise go write." [pause] That's the plan. Six parts. You built every one of them this term. Tonight you assemble them into a single page that is *yours.*

I want to be honest with you on this last night exactly the way I was honest on the first, because the honesty is the whole reason this room is worth your time. [pause] This plan does not guarantee you will be published. No plan does. I cannot stand here and promise you a book, and any teacher who would is lying to you. What this plan *does* do is two things, both real. It genuinely raises your odds — measurably, significantly, by aiming the right work at the right door at the right time, again and again. And — this is the bigger one — it gives you a *sustainable writing life*, regardless of any single outcome. A life with a rhythm. A practice you can sustain. A way of being a writer that doesn't depend on the lottery coming in. [pause]

And here's the deepest thing I know, after forty years and three hundred-some publications and far more rejections than that — the thing I most want you to carry out of this room. [pause] *The plan, repeated, is the career.* That's it. That's the secret I've been circling all term. You take this plan, and you run it this year, and you revise it and run it again next year, and the year after, and for ten years — and almost regardless of luck, you will look up one day and find you have built the thing we talked about on the very first night: the long, quiet, dignified life in letters. A body of work. A few hundred readers who care. The second book better than the first. Not fame — we let go of fame in week one. Something better and more reliable than fame. A *writing life.* [pause] And it is available to every single person in this room. I mean that. Not to the lucky ones or the young ones or the credentialed ones. To the ones who build a plan and run it. To you.

One last honest word, and then we build. [pause] You remember the firewall from the first night — Silver Current Press will not publish anyone in this room, not now and not for a year. I want to tell you, on this last night, why I'm glad I drew that line. Because I would so much rather have spent sixteen weeks teaching you to publish yourselves *anywhere* — at any journal, any press, any contest in the whole wide field — than to have published one or two of you myself. I gave you the door instead of carrying you through it. That's the better gift. That's the one that lasts. [pause]

So. Take out a clean page. We're going to build your plan, and then each of you is going to stand up and tell this room — these people who've sat beside you for sixteen weeks — one sentence about what your next year looks like, and the *one thing you are going to do tomorrow morning.* Not next month. Tomorrow. One submission. One. Because the gap that swallows most writers is the gap between the last class and the first real act, and tonight, before you leave, we're going to bridge it. [pause] Let's build your writing life. Clean page. Start with your door.

**Anticipated Student Questions With Model Answers**
1. *What if I follow the whole plan and still don't get published this year?* — That's possible and not a failure; first acceptances often take a year or two of steady submission to cluster. The plan raises your odds and gives you a sustainable practice, and run for a decade it builds the career almost regardless of any single year.
2. *My Session-1 goal now seems wrong — should I feel bad?* — Not at all; a revised goal is the best souvenir of the course, evidence you now understand the doors and the odds. Update it to what you now know is wise and reachable.
3. *How often should I revise this plan?* — Treat it as a living document: run it for a year, review it against your process targets, and rebuild it each year as you learn. The plan repeated and refined is the career.
4. *What's the single most important part of the plan?* — The protected writing time and the next-morning action; everything else is structure around the writing and the willingness to actually begin. A plan unstarted is just a wish.
5. *Should my plan be ambitious or realistic?* — Both: include a few reach venues to honor the ambition and a realistic core that can actually produce acceptances, plus targets you can sustain for years rather than a heroic year you'll abandon.
6. *Can I really build a career without an agent or an MFA or fame?* — Yes; for most literary forms the small-press and contest path needs no agent, no degree, and no fame, and a plan run steadily for years builds a genuine body of work. That long quiet career is available to anyone who runs the plan.
7. *What if my form is the hard-to-sell kind (poetry, collection)?* — Then your plan simply routes through the doors built for it — contests and small presses — and skips the agent entirely. The path is real and dignified; it's just a different door, mapped in your plan.
8. *How do I keep going after the course ends?* — Run your weekly submission hour, keep work in the air, hold the next deadline as an appointment, and consider keeping the cohort as a writing group. The structure is what carries you when motivation flags.
9. *Will you reconsider publishing me through Silver Current once the year is up?* — The firewall keeps this course clean, and after a year you'd be judged exactly as any submitter would, by the same standards as any press. But the truer answer is that I'd rather you used this plan to get published anywhere in the whole field.
10. *What's the very first thing I should do?* — Tomorrow morning, send one real submission from your tiered list — small, specific, immediate. Bridging the gap between this last class and that first act is the whole point of tonight.

**Anticipated Difficulties**
The final session's difficulty is emotional closure: students invested for sixteen weeks may feel the gap between the plan's promise and the uncertainty of outcomes acutely, and some will want a guarantee you cannot honestly give. Hold the course's balance to the end — the plan raises odds and builds a life, but guarantees nothing — while sending each writer out genuinely believing the writing life is achievable and worth it. A second difficulty is the plan that's all ambition or all caution; ensure each has both reach and realism, deadlines and protected writing time. The recurring hard conversation may make its final appearance here — the student whose plan still aims only at the summit; honor the dream, insist gently on the ladder of realistic targets beneath it, and make sure their next-morning action is something they can actually succeed at. End every individual exchange, as all term, on the path forward and the next small act.

## Instructor Glossary

A working vocabulary for the submitting writer. These are the words students will meet in submission guidelines, contracts, rejection letters, and conference hallways. Define them plainly; the jargon is a gate, and our job is to open it.

**Simultaneous submission.** Sending the same piece to more than one market at the same time. Standard and accepted practice for most journals today, because response times are long; the writer's obligation is to withdraw the piece everywhere else the moment it's accepted somewhere. Always check each market's policy first — a minority still forbid it.

**Simsub.** The everyday shorthand for "simultaneous submission." You'll see it in guidelines ("Simsubs OK; please withdraw promptly") and in writers' forums. Knowing the slang signals you're a working submitter, not a tourist.

**Multiple submission.** Sending more than one separate piece to the *same* market at once — for example, three different flash pieces in one packet. Distinct from simultaneous submission. Some journals welcome it, many cap it ("up to 3 poems"), and a few forbid it; the guidelines always say.

**Slush pile.** The accumulated stack of unsolicited submissions waiting to be read at a journal, press, or agency. Your work begins life here. It is not an insult — most accepted work, and many careers, climb out of the slush. First readers and interns often triage it before an editor ever sees it.

**Query letter.** The one-page business letter by which a writer pitches a novel or nonfiction project to a literary agent or editor. It introduces the book, the writer, and the hook, and asks permission to send more. The single most important document a novelist learns to write; getting it wrong closes the door before the manuscript is read.

**Synopsis.** A compressed prose summary of a book's entire plot, including the ending — typically one to two pages, present tense, third person. Agents request it alongside or after the query. Writers hate it because it flattens art into machinery, but it proves the story holds together and the ending lands.

**Comp title.** A "comparable" or "competitive" title — a recently published book the writer names to show where their own book sits on the shelf and who buys it ("for readers of X and Y"). Used in queries and pitches to orient an agent or editor commercially. Choose comps that are successful but not mega-bestsellers, and recent (within three to five years).

**The gatekeeper.** The agent, editor, first reader, or contest judge who stands between the writer and publication. The course's recurring point: the gatekeeper is overworked but rooting for you — they *want* to find the good one. They are a reader to be persuaded, not an enemy to be defeated.

**Reading fee.** A charge to submit work for consideration. For journals and contests, a modest reading fee (a few dollars to ~$20) is now common and legitimate. For *literary agents*, a reading fee is a red flag: the Association of American Literary Agents' Canon of Ethics prohibits members from charging them, and any "agent" who does is almost certainly a scam ([Authors Guild](https://authorsguild.org/resource/an-authors-guide-to-agency-agreements/)).

**Response time.** How long a market takes to reply, from days to many months (six months is unremarkable; a year happens). Published in guidelines and tracked on databases like Duotrope and Chill Subs. Knowing typical response times is what lets a writer keep many submissions in the air without anxiety.

**Form rejection.** A standardized, impersonal "no" sent to most submitters — "Thank you for sending; this isn't right for us." Carries no information beyond the decision. The overwhelming majority of rejections are form rejections; reading meaning into them is a beginner's mistake.

**Personal rejection.** A "no" that includes a specific, human comment — a line of praise, a reason, an invitation to submit again. A meaningful encouragement and a sign the work is close; experienced writers celebrate a warm personal rejection almost as a near-win.

**Tiered rejection.** The practice, at some journals, of sending slightly different form rejections depending on how far a piece advanced ("we read this with interest" vs. the baseline note). Sites like Rejection Wiki decode the tiers. Useful intelligence, but not worth obsessing over.

**The auction.** When multiple publishers bid against one another for a book an agent is shopping, driving up the advance. Rare and reserved for hotly wanted projects; the stuff of publishing news, not the typical debut's experience. Students should know it exists and know it won't describe most careers.

**The preempt.** A pre-emptive offer a publisher makes to take a book *off the table* before an auction can happen, usually a strong offer with a short deadline. Like the auction, real but uncommon. Naming it demystifies the deal-making students read about.

**Advance.** Money a publisher pays the author up front against future royalties — "an advance against royalties." The author earns no further royalty until the advance "earns out." For debut literary fiction at small and independent presses, advances commonly run from roughly \$1,000 to \$5,000; poetry collections often carry no advance at all, only author copies ([Barnett Ghostwriting](https://www.barnettghostwriting.com/blog/average-book-advance-for-first-time-authors/)).

**Royalty rate.** The percentage of a book's price (or net receipts) the author earns per copy sold. Traditional trade rates run roughly 10–15% of list on hardcover, about 7.5% on trade paperback, and around 25% of net on ebooks ([Barker Books](https://barkerbooks.com/royalties-from-books/)). Self-publishing platforms like Amazon KDP can pay up to 70% on ebooks priced \$2.99–\$9.99, a very different math.

**The option clause.** A contract provision giving the publisher first look at the author's *next* book. Standard in trade contracts; negotiable in its details (the publisher should have a limited window, not an indefinite lock). Worth understanding before signing, ideally with an agent or a literary attorney.

**The kill fee.** A partial payment a magazine or publication owes a writer if it commissions a piece and then declines to run it. Common in journalism and commercial nonfiction; rarer in literary work. Teaches writers that commissioned ≠ guaranteed, and that terms should be on paper.

**Advance against royalties.** See **Advance** — the full name makes the mechanism clear: it is a loan against money you may earn, not a bonus on top of royalties. If the book never earns it back, you generally keep the advance but see no further money.

**The trim size.** The physical dimensions of the finished book's pages (e.g., 5.5″ × 8.5″ for many literary paperbacks). A production detail authors encounter in the design stage; useful to recognize so a first-time author isn't bluffed by jargon in a production conversation.

**ISBN.** International Standard Book Number — the unique identifier that lets booksellers and libraries order and track a specific edition. Each format (hardcover, paperback, ebook) needs its own. Traditional publishers supply them; self-publishers buy them (in the U.S., from Bowker) or accept a platform-issued one.

**Library of Congress Control Number (LCCN).** A unique number the Library of Congress assigns to a catalog record, helping libraries acquire and shelve a book. Traditional publishers handle it; independent publishers can apply through the LC's pre-assignment program. A mark of a book entering the library system.

**Distribution.** The machinery that physically and digitally gets books from publisher to bookstores, libraries, and readers — wholesalers, distributors, and sales reps. The single biggest practical advantage a traditional or well-distributed indie press offers over self-publishing, because bookstores order through distributors they already work with.

**Returns.** The trade practice by which bookstores may send unsold copies back to the publisher for credit. It means a "sale" to a store isn't final, and it shapes print runs and an author's real earnings. A sobering reality of how few copies "sold" can later come back.

**Remainders.** Unsold copies a publisher sells off cheaply (you've seen the bargain tables) once a title stops moving at full price. The author typically earns little or nothing on remaindered copies. A normal, unglamorous end-stage of most print runs.

**Midlist.** The broad middle of a publisher's list — competently selling books that are neither bestsellers nor failures. Most literary books live here. The term often carries a wistful edge ("midlist author"), but the midlist is where most real, sustained careers actually happen.

**The comp.** Shorthand for **comp title** (see above), also used as a verb: "What do you comp it to?" Knowing your comps is part of speaking the industry's language fluently.

**AWP.** The Association of Writers & Writing Programs, and especially its annual conference and bookfair — the largest literary conference in North America, drawing roughly 10,000–12,000 writers, teachers, editors, and presses ([San Antonio Report](https://sanantonioreport.org/12000-writers-to-descend-on-san-antonio-for-literary-super-bowl/)). The field's great annual gathering; overwhelming and invaluable.

**MFA.** Master of Fine Arts in creative writing — the standard terminal degree for the field, offered in residential and low-residency forms. Useful for craft, community, and teaching credentials, but emphatically *not* required to publish. The course's stance: a fine path, not a gate.

**Residency.** A funded or low-cost stay (a week to months) at a colony or center — MacDowell, Yaddo, and many others — giving writers uninterrupted time and community. Competitive, prestigious, and life-changing for some; one of many supports, not a prerequisite.

**Fellowship.** A grant of money or time awarded to support a writer's work, often by merit and competition — the NEA Creative Writing Fellowship, for instance, awards \$50,000 and alternates between prose and poetry years ([NEA](https://www.arts.gov/sites/default/files/FY26-CWF-Prose-Grant-Program-Details.pdf)). Fellowships fund the writing life; teach students to track and apply for the ones they're eligible for.

**Prize.** An award, usually carrying money and prestige, for a published or submitted work — from small journal prizes to major national awards. A line on a résumé and sometimes a check; never the point of the writing, but a real and useful form of recognition and support.

**Contest.** A competition writers *enter*, typically by submitting work and (often) paying a fee, to win publication and/or money — the dominant path to a first book in poetry and short fiction. Many first-book contests exist precisely because the market won't otherwise easily publish debut collections; understand the fee economics before entering widely.

**Chapbook.** A short book, usually 20–40 pages, common in poetry and occasionally flash fiction. A respected, accessible first or interim publication, often produced by small presses through their own contests. A real way to put a tangible book in the world without a full-length manuscript.

**Full-length collection.** A complete book of poems (typically 48–100+ pages) or stories — the standard "first book" most poetry and story contests award. Assembling one — sequencing, cutting, finding the throughline — is its own craft, distinct from writing the individual pieces.

**Work-for-hire.** Writing done under a contract in which the hiring party, not the writer, owns the copyright and is legally the "author" — common in ghostwriting, packaged books, and corporate work. It pays, sometimes well, but the writer surrenders ownership and credit. A legitimate way to earn from writing, clearly distinct from authoring one's own work.


## Recommended Reading List on the Writing Life and Publishing

Two pages of real, verifiable books on the practice and business of the writing life. Organized by what a student needs at each stage. Annotations are honest — some of these books are kind, some are bracing, and a writer's library should hold both. Library copies are fine; none of these needs to be bought new.

### On the Daily Practice and the Inner Life of the Writer

- **Anne Lamott, *Bird by Bird: Some Instructions on Writing and Life*** (Pantheon, 1994). The warmest and most quoted book on writing; "shitty first drafts" and "bird by bird" entered the language for good reason. Read for permission and persistence.
- **Annie Dillard, *The Writing Life*** (Harper & Row, 1989). Short, luminous, severe. Not a how-to but a meditation on the cost and devotion of the work. Read for tone, not strategy.
- **Stephen King, *On Writing: A Memoir of the Craft*** (Scribner, 2000). Half memoir, half practical craft, fully generous. The single best gift for a beginner who thinks talent is the whole story; King's gospel is work.
- **Ralph Keyes, *The Courage to Write: How Writers Transcend Fear*** (Henry Holt, 1995). On the fear that stops writers and how the published ones write scared anyway. Pairs with the course's stance that rejection is the curriculum.
- **Dorothea Brande, *Becoming a Writer*** (1934; reissued Tarcher). Nearly a century old and still the sanest book on building the writing habit and getting out of one's own way.
- **Verlyn Klinkenborg, *Several Short Sentences About Writing*** (Knopf, 2012). A bracing corrective at the sentence level for writers ready to take craft seriously.

### On Craft for the Submitting Writer

- **Francine Prose, *Reading Like a Writer*** (HarperCollins, 2006). How to read the masters as an apprentice rather than a fan. The book that turns reading into training.
- **Janet Burroway, *Writing Fiction: A Guide to Narrative Craft*** (multiple editions, University of Chicago Press). The standard workshop text; thorough, clear, and full of examples. The reference shelf's anchor for fiction.
- **Mary Oliver, *A Poetry Handbook*** (Harcourt Brace, 1994). A slim, kind, rigorous introduction to the formal life of the poem — sound, line, meter — from a master.
- **Ted Kooser, *The Poetry Home Repair Manual*** (University of Nebraska Press, 2005). Plainspoken, practical advice on writing and revising poems from a former U.S. Poet Laureate who began as a working insurance man — proof of the long quiet career.
- **William Zinsser, *On Writing Well*** (Harper, 7th ed. 2006). The classic on nonfiction clarity; indispensable for memoirists and essayists.

### On the Business and the Career

- **Jane Friedman, *The Business of Being a Writer*** (2nd ed., University of Chicago Press, 2024). The most current, comprehensive, and trustworthy map of how publishing actually works and how writers actually earn. The course's single most important business reference.
- **Betsy Lerner, *The Forest for the Trees: An Editor's Advice to Writers*** (rev. ed., Riverhead, 2010). The view from the editor's desk — what editors see, want, and fear. Wise, funny, humane; the book to read to understand the gatekeeper.
- **Carolyn See, *Making a Literary Life: Advice for Writers and Other Dreamers*** (Random House, 2002). On building a sustained, dignified writing life through small daily acts and "charming notes." The course's closing vision in book form.
- **Eric Maisel, *A Writer's Space*** (Adams Media, 2008). On building the physical and mental room the work requires; useful for writers with full lives and little time.
- **Manjula Martin, ed., *Scratch: Writers, Money, and the Art of Making a Living*** (Simon & Schuster, 2017). Honest essays and interviews about how working writers actually pay the bills. Antidote to the myth of the lucrative debut.
- **Priya Parker, *The Art of Gathering*** (Riverhead, 2018). Not a writing book, but the best guide to running the readings, workshops, and launches a writing life eventually asks of you.

### On Submission, Agents, and the Market

- **Jeff Herman, *Jeff Herman's Guide to Book Publishers, Editors & Literary Agents*** (updated regularly). A working directory of who acquires what; pair it with free online databases. Confirm everything against current listings.
- **Noah Lukeman, *The First Five Pages: A Writer's Guide to Staying Out of the Rejection Pile*** (Fireside, 2000). From a working agent: the concrete reasons manuscripts get rejected fast, and how to fix them.
- **Katharine Sands, ed., *Making the Perfect Pitch: How to Catch a Literary Agent's Eye*** (Writer's Digest, 2004). Agents on what makes a query and pitch work. Read several such voices; the consensus is the lesson.

### A Note on Currency

Business and market books age quickly. Friedman's *The Business of Being a Writer* and the free online resources in the session lists (Poets & Writers, CLMP, Writer Beware, the Authors Guild) should be checked for current figures before any number is quoted to students or acted upon. The craft and practice books above age slowly or not at all.


## Journal List for Adult Writers

A one-page starting grid of literary journals, organized by tier and genre, with brief notes on what each takes. Tiers describe *reach and competitiveness*, not worth — an emerging journal that loves your work and puts it in front of real readers is worth more than a silent "reach" rejection. The honest counsel for students: send to a few reach venues to honor the ambition, build a core of well-matched journals where acceptance is realistic, and use emerging venues to get work into the world and into print. Always confirm current reading periods and guidelines before submitting; most journals post them clearly and many use Submittable.

### Reach Tier (highly competitive, national/international visibility)

- **The New Yorker** — Fiction and poetry; famously selective, agented or slush, very long odds. The benchmark students invoke; teach realistic expectations here especially.
- **Poetry** (Poetry Foundation) — The flagship American poetry magazine; open submissions, pays well, extremely competitive.
- **The Sewanee Review** — The oldest continuously published U.S. literary quarterly; fiction, poetry, essays; a genuine reach venue with open reading periods.
- **Ploughshares** — Guest-edited issues; fiction, poetry, nonfiction; prestigious, competitive, reads seasonally.
- **The Kenyon Review**, **The Paris Review**, **AGNI**, **Tin House** (online), **A Public Space** — All reach-tier; literary fiction, poetry, essays; long odds and long response times.

### Core Tier (respected, realistic with strong, well-matched work)

- **Rattle** — Poetry; large readership, accessible aesthetic, runs a well-known poetry prize; one of the friendliest "real" poetry venues for serious newcomers.
- **The Galway Review** — Poetry and prose; international, welcoming to a wide range of voices (a venue the instructor knows from his own publication history).
- **The Threepenny Review**, **The Missouri Review**, **Crazyhorse / swamp pink**, **Prairie Schooner**, **The Cincinnati Review** — Established quarterlies taking fiction, poetry, and nonfiction; competitive but reachable with polished, well-targeted work.
- **SmokeLong Quarterly**, **Wigleaf** — Flash fiction specialists; excellent core targets for very short prose.
- **Brevity** — The leading home for the flash nonfiction essay (750 words or fewer); a core target every memoirist and essayist should know.

### Emerging Tier (open, welcoming, real publication and readership)

- **Impspired** — Poetry, prose, art; broad, generous, international (in the instructor's own publication history).
- **Sparks of Calliope** — Formal and metrical poetry especially; welcoming to traditional craft (instructor-published).
- **Vita Brevis Press** — Poetry with a thematic, life-affirming bent; accessible and community-minded (instructor-published).
- **Academy of the Heart and Mind** — Poetry, prose, essays; open and encouraging to developing writers (instructor-published).
- **Fragmented Voices** — Poetry and prose; small-press energy, themed calls (instructor-published).
- **CC&D / Scars Publications** — Long-running, prolific outlet for poetry and prose; very open (instructor-published).

### How to Read This List

Genre routing matters more than tier. A flash-fiction writer should look first to SmokeLong, Wigleaf, and Brevity (for nonfiction) regardless of tier; a formal poet to Sparks of Calliope and Rattle; a literary realist to the core quarterlies. Use the free database Chill Subs (https://www.chillsubs.com) and Duotrope (https://duotrope.com, ~\$50/yr) to filter by genre, length, fee, response time, and acceptance rate, and to find the dozens of strong venues no one-page list can hold. Build each student's personal grid from these tools; this list is only the seed.


## Small Press List

A one-page list of reputable independent and small presses, organized by genre, with brief notes on what each publishes and how it accepts work. These are presses that pay attention to literary value, treat authors well, and provide real distribution — the dignified path the course champions. Submission terms vary: some open directly to unagented writers, some only through contests, some only by agent or invitation. Always confirm current open reading periods and guidelines at each press's site, and check any press against Writer Beware (https://writerbeware.blog) and the CLMP directory (https://www.clmp.org) before submitting.

### Literary Fiction (novels and beyond)

- **Graywolf Press** (Minneapolis) — One of the most respected nonprofit literary publishers in the U.S.; fiction, nonfiction, poetry. Largely agented but a genuine standard-bearer; the press behind major literary debuts.
- **Coffee House Press** (Minneapolis) — Adventurous literary fiction, poetry, and essays; periodic open or query-based reading windows.
- **Two Dollar Radio** (Columbus) — Bold, independent fiction with a strong identity; family-run; reads from unagented writers in announced windows.
- **Deep Vellum** (Dallas) — Literary fiction and translation; mission-driven and prolific; open to a range of voices.
- **Catapult / Soft Skull Press** — Literary fiction and nonfiction (Soft Skull with a countercultural edge); largely agented, watch for windows.
- **Bellevue Literary Press** (New York) — Literary fiction and nonfiction at the intersection of arts and sciences; small, prestigious (publisher of Paul Harding's Pulitzer-winning *Tinkers*).

### Poetry (full-length collections and chapbooks)

- **Copper Canyon Press** (Port Townsend) — One of the premier poetry publishers in the country; open reading periods and contests.
- **BOA Editions** (Rochester) — Distinguished nonprofit poetry press; runs contests (e.g., the A. Poulin, Jr. Poetry Prize for a first book) and open periods.
- **Alice James Books** (Farmington, ME) — Poet-cooperative press; respected first-book prizes and a strong list.
- **Tupelo Press** (North Adams, MA) — Poetry and select prose; multiple contests and open reading periods (note reading fees; see the contest calendar).
- **Button Poetry** (Minneapolis) — Contemporary and spoken-word-adjacent poetry; runs a well-known first-book contest with publication and a cash prize.

### Novella and Short Forms

- **Two Dollar Radio** — Also a champion of the novella and the short, intense book (its "Two Dollar Radio Moves" line and compact novels).
- **Melville House** (Brooklyn) — Home of "The Art of the Novella" series; literary fiction and nonfiction.
- **Tin House Books** (Portland) — Literary fiction and nonfiction including shorter forms; selective, periodic windows and workshop pipelines.

### Story Collections

- **Sarabande Books** (Louisville) — Fiction (including collections), poetry, and essays; runs the Mary McCarthy Prize in Short Fiction.
- **Milkweed Editions** (Minneapolis) — Literary fiction, poetry, and nonfiction with an ecological and humane bent; contests and open periods.
- **University presses with story-collection prizes** — Iowa (Iowa Short Fiction Award), Georgia (Flannery O'Connor Award), and Pittsburgh (Drue Heinz Literature Prize) are the classic contest routes to a first collection (see the contest calendar).

### Memoir and Creative Nonfiction

- **Graywolf Press** and **Coffee House Press** — Both publish distinguished literary memoir and essay.
- **Milkweed Editions** — Nature, place, and personal essay especially.
- **Catapult** — Literary memoir and essay collections (largely agented).
- **University presses** — Many run creative-nonfiction and essay prizes (e.g., the AWP Award Series' Sue William Silverman Prize for nonfiction); a strong route for memoir without an agent.

### A Note on Vetting

The presence of distribution, real editing, no author-paid fees beyond modest contest entry, and a verifiable backlist are the marks of a legitimate small press. Be alert: any "press" that asks the *author* to pay for publication, or that bundles "marketing packages," is a vanity operation, not a publisher. Writer Beware and CLMP are the student's two essential vetting tools; teach them to use both before sending a manuscript anywhere.


## Contest and Fellowship Calendar

A one-page calendar of major first-book contests and fellowships, organized roughly by deadline through the year, with brief notes. **Every date, fee, and prize below changes — confirm at the source before entering.** For most poetry and short-fiction writers, contests are the primary path to a first book, so a student's annual plan should anchor to a handful of these deadlines as fixed appointments. Track them all in Poets & Writers' free Grants & Awards calendar (https://www.pw.org/grants).

### Winter / Spring

- **NEA Creative Writing Fellowship** — Deadline ~early-to-mid March; award \$50,000; alternates between prose and poetry years, so confirm which genre is open in a given cycle. Among the most significant fellowships in American letters ([NEA program details](https://www.arts.gov/sites/default/files/FY26-CWF-Prose-Grant-Program-Details.pdf)).
- **Flannery O'Connor Award for Short Fiction** (University of Georgia Press) — Reading period ~April–May; publication by UGA Press plus a cash award. A classic route to a first or second story collection.

### Late Spring / Early Summer

- **Drue Heinz Literature Prize** (University of Pittsburgh Press) — Reading period ~May 1–June 30; **no entry fee**; award plus publication. Open to writers who have published a book of fiction or a minimum number of stories/novellas in literary journals — so it rewards the writer who has built a journal record first ([Pitt Press]).
- **Sarabande Mary McCarthy Prize in Short Fiction** and companion poetry prizes — Typically open in this window; confirm dates; publication plus cash.

### Summer

- **Iowa Short Fiction Award / John Simmons Short Fiction Award** (University of Iowa Press) — Submission window ~July 1–August 31; open to writers who have **not** previously published a book of fiction; publication by Iowa Press. One of the most prestigious routes for a debut collection ([Iowa Writers' Workshop](https://fictionawards.writersworkshop.uiowa.edu/how-submit)).

### Late Summer / Fall

- **Academy of American Poets First Book Award** — Deadline ~September 1; \$35 entry fee; award of \$5,000, publication by Graywolf Press, and a six-week residency in Italy. For a poet's first book; a flagship first-book prize ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/academy-american-poets/prizes/first-book-award)).
- **Yale Series of Younger Poets** — The oldest annual literary award in the United States (since 1919); publication by Yale University Press for a first book by a poet under forty. Deadline typically in the fall/winter window; confirm yearly.
- **Tupelo Press contests** (e.g., the Helena Whitehill / Berkshire Prize and prose awards) — Several open in fall; note reading fees (around \$45 for prose). Read the fee economics with students before they enter widely ([Tupelo Press]).

### Rolling / Nomination-Based (not entered on a calendar)

- **Button Poetry First Book Contest** — \$25 entry fee; award around \$1,000 plus publication; confirm the annual window. A strong contemporary-poetry route.
- **The Pushcart Prize** — **Not entered by writers.** Magazines and small presses nominate published work (nomination window ~October 1–December 1). The way to "enter" is to publish work editors love enough to nominate ([Pushcart Prize](https://www.pushcartprize.org/nominations)). Teach this distinction explicitly — students routinely misunderstand it.

### Residencies (rolling deadlines, worth tracking)

- **MacDowell** and **Yaddo** — The two most prestigious U.S. artist residencies; competitive, multiple deadlines per year, no cost to attend once admitted. Aspirational but real; many working writers apply for years before admission.

### How to Use This Calendar

Have each student choose three to five deadlines that fit their genre and stage, enter them in their personal calendar as fixed appointments, and budget the entry fees honestly for the year. The discipline is the point: a contest deadline is one of the few external forces that reliably gets a manuscript finished and out the door. Confirm every figure at the source — these programs revise dates, fees, and amounts annually.
