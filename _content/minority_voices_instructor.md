## Preface — How to Use This Book

Dear Mr. Mulhern,

This companion is built to sit on the desk beside you in the hour before each class, and to be read more slowly in the days before that. It is not a script you must follow, and it is no substitute for your own reading of the primary texts, which remain the only indispensable preparation. It is, rather, a working scaffold: a place where the biography, the history, the close-reading, and the likely shape of the room have already been laid out, so that your attention in the week of teaching can go where it belongs — into the texts and into the people in front of you.

Each session mirrors the others in structure: an "At a Glance" orientation, biographical sketches long enough to teach from, historical and cultural context specific to the racial and political moment of the work, the readings with freely available sources where they exist, a lecture script in your voice for the opening ten to fifteen minutes, close-reading notes on specific passages, discussion questions with model answers, a note on anticipated difficulties, further reading for you, and one line worth memorizing. Use what helps; cross out what doesn't. The lecture scripts in particular are meant to be spoken, revised, abandoned mid-sentence if the room takes you somewhere better.

A word on tone, because it matters more in this course than in almost any other you teach. You will most likely be standing in a largely white room — adult learners, curious, generous, in many cases meeting this literature for the first time as literature rather than as history lesson or headline. You are not yourself a member of most of the communities whose writers you are teaching, and that fact should sit lightly and honestly on your shoulder the whole term. It is not a disqualification; you have spent a lifetime reading carefully and teaching well, and careful reading is the qualification that matters. But it asks a particular humility. Your job in the lecture scripts is not to speak *for* Frederick Douglass or Zora Neale Hurston or Joy Harjo. It is to introduce them, to set their work in its moment, to ask good questions, and then to get out of the way so that the writing — and the room — can do the work. When in doubt, quote the writer rather than paraphrase the writer's experience. Let Douglass say what slavery felt like. You say what the sentence is doing.

There are two temptations to resist, and they pull in opposite directions. The first is to flatten this material into universal humanism — "these are stories about the human condition" — which is true at a level so general that it quietly erases the specific histories of slavery, removal, exclusion, and migration that are the whole point of the course. The second is to overcorrect into a hushed reverence that treats every text as too sacred to analyze, which kills discussion, patronizes the writers, and turns artists into monuments. The path between is to treat this as what it is: a body of great American literature, particular in its histories and its griefs and its forms, fully available to careful readers willing to learn its idioms. You can be warm and exacting at once. Assume intelligence. Explain the historical term and move on.

Several sessions carry historical violence directly into the room — the slave narratives of Douglass and Jacobs, the lynching at the center of Toomer's "Blood-Burning Moon," the Indian boarding schools in Zitkala-Ša, the catastrophe folded into Morrison's *Beloved*. Handle these with extra weight. Build in silence and allow it; do not rush to make the room feel better. When a reading contains the racial vocabulary of its period — the word "Negro" in a 1903 text, the dialect Dunbar wrote in, a slur quoted inside a passage — name the convention plainly *before* you read, decide in advance whether you will speak the word or signal it, and do not let a flinch become a lesson in shame. There are notes on each of these moments in the sessions where they fall.

On what to do when the room goes quiet: wait. Count to ten in your head. The silence after a hard passage is not a failure of the discussion; it is the discussion. On what to do when a resident says something the room hears as offensive: slow down, stay in the room, and treat it as a teaching moment rather than a verdict on a person. Ask the speaker what they meant; ask the room what they heard; return, always, to the text on the page. You are not a referee assigning fault. You are a reader helping other readers think. And on the deepest matter of all — how to honor these writers without making them into representatives of their groups: keep returning to craft. A poem is not a press release from a community. Dunbar speaks for Dunbar; Cisneros speaks for Cisneros. The honor we owe them is the honor of reading them as artists who made particular choices, not as delegates sent to explain a people to us.

With respect, and with confidence in your hands,

*— The Companion*

## Recurring Threads Across the Course

It will help you, and your students, to name a small number of threads at the outset and then pull on them deliberately week after week. The pleasure of a course like this — the thing that turns sixteen separate writers into a single conversation in the students' minds — comes from recognition: *we have seen this before, and now it has changed.* Below are five threads worth tracking aloud. You need not raise all five every week, but you should know where each runs.

**Double consciousness.** This is the oldest analytic thread in the course, and it is best to hand the class the phrase early, in Du Bois's own words from Session 3 — "this sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others." Du Bois named a condition that runs underneath nearly every writer here: the experience of holding two pictures of oneself at once, the self one knows from the inside and the self the dominant culture insists upon from the outside. Watch how it migrates and changes form. Dunbar's "mask" is double consciousness made into a single image. Hurston's "I am not tragically colored" is a writer wrestling the concept to the ground and refusing it. Kingston's "No Name Woman" is double consciousness across a generation and an ocean. Ask the students, late in the term, whether double consciousness is only a burden, or whether — as several of these writers insist — it is also a form of second sight, a way of seeing the country more clearly than the country sees itself.

**The politics of audience.** Almost every text in this course was written with acute awareness of *who was likely to be reading.* This is not a side issue; it is often the central craft problem the writer is solving. Douglass and Jacobs wrote to persuade skeptical white Northerners and shaped every sentence accordingly. Washington's Atlanta address is pitched to a white audience that held real power over Black lives; Du Bois writes, in part, to Black readers and refuses to flatter the white ones. Then comes the great pivot: Hurston, Baldwin, and finally Morrison — who said plainly that she did not write *Beloved* to explain Black life to white readers — claim the freedom *not* to address the dominant gaze at all. Keep asking, every week: who is this writer imagining on the other side of the page? The answer reorganizes everything.

**The recovery of voices the canon tried not to hear.** The standard story of American literature — Puritans to Franklin to Emerson to Whitman to Twain to Hemingway — is not false, but it is radically incomplete, and the gap is not an accident. Many of the writers in this course were celebrated in their own moment and then dropped; Hurston died in obscurity and was recovered by Alice Walker in the 1970s, and her case is the rule, not the exception. Press the students on what "recovery" means. We are not doing remediation, filling gaps in a deficient education. We are reading some of the best writing America has produced, writing that was systematically under-celebrated for reasons that had nothing to do with its quality. That distinction is worth repeating until it lands.

**The long argument with the dominant American narrative.** America tells itself a story — of liberty, of the self-made man, of the welcoming shore, of the frontier. Nearly every writer here is in a sustained argument with that story, and the argument takes a hundred forms: Douglass turning the Fourth of July into an indictment; Hughes answering Whitman's "I Hear America Singing" with "I, too, am America"; Bulosan keeping faith with an America that has beaten him; Cisneros writing the house the American Dream promised and did not deliver. The argument is rarely simple rejection. More often it is the harder thing — a claim *on* America, an insistence that the country live up to its own words. Track whether each writer is rejecting the national story, mourning it, or demanding entry to it.

**Should minority literature be taught as minority literature at all?** This is the live, contested question your course quietly stages, and you should not pretend to a settled answer. To group these writers by the category "minority" is to risk the very thing the course warns against — reducing artists to representatives, reading sociology where we should read craft. And yet to scatter them into a "general" American literature that has historically excluded them is to risk erasing the specific histories that shaped the work. There is no clean resolution, and the tension is productive. Raise it openly, perhaps in Session 1 and again at the capstone. By the end, the students should see that the category is a scaffold, not a cage — useful for assembling a conversation that the canon refused to have, and meant to be questioned even as we use it.

Carry these five threads lightly but persistently. They are the rope that ties the sixteen weeks together.

## Session 1 — Welcome & Frederick Douglass: *Narrative of the Life*

### At a Glance
We open the course with the foundational text of the American slave narrative: the book in which a man legally defined as property seizes the pen and writes himself into personhood. The session establishes the course's central premise — that to tell your own story, in your own voice, against a world that denies you both, is itself an act of self-possession — and gives students a shared vocabulary (witness, literacy, audience, double consciousness to come) for the weeks ahead. The aim is to read Douglass not as a historical artifact but as the formidable rhetorician and prose artist he was at twenty-seven.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Frederick Douglass was born Frederick Augustus Washington Bailey, in February 1818, on the Eastern Shore of Maryland, into slavery ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Frederick Douglass"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Frederick-Douglass)). The exact date he never knew — a deprivation he makes the very first wound of the *Narrative*, and one worth dwelling on with students, because the not-knowing is itself the argument. To be denied your own birthday is to be denied a place in human time; the enslaver kept that knowledge as he kept everything else. Douglass was separated from his mother, Harriet Bailey, in infancy, as was customary, and saw her only a handful of times before her death; he believed, though could not prove, that his father was a white man, very possibly his enslaver. This uncertainty about his own origins — mother withheld, father unnameable, birthday unknown — is the ground from which his lifelong insistence on self-definition grows.

As a boy he was sent to Baltimore to the household of Hugh and Sophia Auld, and it is here that the central drama of the *Narrative* unfolds. Sophia Auld, at first, began to teach him the alphabet — until her husband forbade it, declaring, in words Douglass never forgot, that learning would "spoil" a slave and "unfit him to be a slave." Douglass recognized in that prohibition a confession: that literacy and bondage were incompatible, that the slaveholder kept the enslaved in ignorance precisely because knowledge was a path to freedom. He resolved, from that moment, to learn to read by any means — trading bread to poor white boys in the street for lessons, copying letters from timber in the shipyard. The discovery that learning to read would also teach him the full horror of his condition — that it was, as he writes, "a curse rather than a blessing" — is one of the great paradoxes in American letters, and it is the engine of the chapters you will assign.

In 1838, at about twenty, Douglass escaped to the North by disguising himself as a free Black sailor and traveling by train and boat to New York and then to New Bedford, Massachusetts. (He withheld the details of his escape in the 1845 *Narrative*, lest he endanger those who helped him and close the route to others — a discretion worth noting as evidence of his strategic mind.) He took the name Douglass, married Anna Murray, a free Black woman who had helped finance his escape, and entered the abolitionist movement, where William Lloyd Garrison and the Massachusetts Anti-Slavery Society recognized in him an orator of staggering power. He was, in fact, *too* powerful: audiences began to doubt that so eloquent a man could ever have been a slave. The *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave* (1845) was written in part to answer that doubt — to supply names, places, and dates so specific that no one could call him a fraud. That it also endangered him, by identifying him to his legal owner, drove him to England for two years, where British supporters purchased his freedom.

Douglass became the most photographed American of the nineteenth century and one of its greatest public men. He founded and edited the abolitionist newspaper *The North Star*, broke painfully with Garrison over strategy and over the Constitution (Douglass came to argue it was an anti-slavery document), advised Abraham Lincoln, recruited Black soldiers for the Union, and after emancipation held federal office and campaigned for Black suffrage and women's rights. He revised and expanded his autobiography twice more — *My Bondage and My Freedom* (1855) and *Life and Times of Frederick Douglass* (1881, revised 1892) — each version reflecting a freer man with more room to tell. He died in 1895, hours after attending a women's rights meeting. His Fourth of July address of 1852 — "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" — remains, with the *Narrative*, one of the supreme texts of American oratory.

For our purposes, the thing to keep before the students is craft. Douglass is not valuable because he suffered; many suffered. He is valuable because he transformed what he suffered into prose of immense control — restrained where a lesser writer would weep, concrete where a lesser writer would generalize, structured so that the famous turn ("You have seen how a man was made a slave; you shall see how a slave was made a man") lands with the force of revelation. He wrote the *Narrative* at twenty-seven. It is worth saying that aloud in the room.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The *Narrative* appears in 1845, sixteen years before the Civil War, in the white heat of the abolitionist movement. To read it you must give students the institution it describes: American chattel slavery, in which roughly four million human beings would be held as property by 1860, their bondage heritable through the mother, their families breakable at the auction block, their literacy criminalized by law in much of the South. Maryland, where Douglass was enslaved, was a border state; its proximity to free territory shapes the geography of escape that runs through the book.

The slave narrative was, by the 1840s, an established and politically potent genre — first-person accounts of bondage and escape, published and promoted by abolitionists as evidence in the national argument over slavery. Douglass's was the most influential of them, selling in the tens of thousands. Students should understand the genre's double bind, because it shapes every page: the formerly enslaved author had to prove both the truth of the atrocities (against white readers' disbelief) and his own full humanity and reliability (against the racist assumption that a Black man could not author such a book). Hence the apparatus of authentication — the prefaces by Garrison and Wendell Phillips, white men vouching for the Black author's veracity. Have students notice that a Black man's life story in 1845 had to be introduced by white sponsors to be believed. That fact is part of the text.

The deeper context is the relationship between literacy and power. Southern law forbade teaching the enslaved to read for a reason Douglass understood better than the lawmakers: a literate enslaved population was an ungovernable one. The *Narrative* is, among other things, an argument that the written word is freedom's instrument — which makes the act of writing the book the completion of the escape that the book describes.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Chapter 1.** Douglass introduces himself: born in Tuckahoe, Maryland, of an enslaved mother, Harriet Bailey, and an unknown white father (rumored to be his enslaver). He does not know his age — a deprivation he names as slavery's first injury. He is separated from his mother in infancy and sees her only a few times at night before she dies; he is not permitted to attend her illness, death, or burial. The chapter ends with the chapter's central atrocity: the child Douglass witnesses the brutal whipping of his Aunt Hester by the overseer, Captain Anthony — "the blood-stained gate, the entrance to the hell of slavery."

**Chapter 6.** Douglass is sent to Baltimore to the household of Hugh and Sophia Auld. Sophia, new to slaveholding and initially kind, begins teaching him the alphabet. Hugh Auld discovers it and forbids it, declaring that learning would "spoil" a slave and unfit him for bondage. Douglass overhears this and undergoes a revelation: the slaveholder's fear of literacy reveals literacy as "the pathway from slavery to freedom." He resolves to learn to read at any cost. The chapter also observes how slavery corrupts Sophia, hardening her once-kind nature.

**Chapter 7.** Over roughly seven years in Baltimore, Douglass teaches himself to read — trading bread to poor white boys for lessons, copying letters in the shipyard, reading *The Columbian Orator* and its arguments against slavery. But reading deepens his torment: it reveals the full horror of his condition without offering escape, until he "would at times feel that learning to read had been a curse rather than a blessing." He learns the word "abolition" and resolves, eventually, to escape. The excerpt ends with Douglass possessed of knowledge but not yet of freedom.

The [Documenting the American South](https://docsouth.unc.edu/neh/douglass/douglass.html) edition at UNC offers a clean scholarly text.
- **Recommended for your own preparation:** the 1852 oration "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" — freely available via [the Frederick Douglass Heritage site](https://www.frederick-douglass-heritage.org/) and many archives. Read it before class even if you do not assign it; it sharpens your sense of his voice.

### Lecture Script

Good evening, and welcome. We are going to spend sixteen weeks together with a body of American writing that, for most of our lives, was kept at the edges of the story — and I want to begin at the very root of it, with a man who was not supposed to be able to write at all. [pause]

I'd like to tell you the first thing this book tells us. Frederick Douglass did not know his own birthday. He tells us that on the first page — that white children knew their ages and he did not, that he had no more right to that knowledge than a horse has. Sit with that for a second. Before slavery takes your labor, before it takes your body, it has already taken something stranger and more total: your place in time. You don't know how old you are. You can't say, "I was born on such a day." [pause] And the whole book that follows is, in a way, this man taking back, one piece at a time, what was stolen — beginning with the right to say *I*. [pause]

Here is the scene I want you to watch for tonight. Douglass is a boy in Baltimore, and his enslaver's wife begins, kindly, to teach him his letters. And her husband stops her. He says — and Douglass quotes him, because he never forgot it — that learning would "spoil" the boy, would "unfit him to be a slave." And the remarkable thing is what the boy hears in that. He hears a confession. He realizes that the slaveholder is afraid of the alphabet — that the thing they are guarding so carefully must be the very thing that leads out. "From that moment," he writes, "I understood the pathway from slavery to freedom." A grown man, by accident, has handed a child the key. [pause]

Now I want to be careful and honest about who wrote this book, because it matters for how we read it. Douglass wrote the *Narrative* in 1845. He was twenty-seven years old. He had escaped slavery only seven years before. And he was already one of the most powerful speakers in America — so powerful that white audiences had begun to whisper that no real ex-slave could possibly be so eloquent, that he must be a fraud. So part of why this book exists, part of why it's so full of names and places and exact dates, is that he is proving he is who he says he is. He is writing against a wall of disbelief. Keep that in mind every time he gives you a specific name. He's not just remembering. He's testifying. [pause]

And here is the thing I most want you to take from tonight, because it sets the tone for the whole course. We are not reading Frederick Douglass because he suffered. A great many people suffered, and suffering by itself does not make literature. We are reading him because of what he *did* with it — because of the control in these sentences, the restraint, the way he can describe the most appalling cruelty in level, exact prose and let the facts do the work. There is a line near the middle of the book, the hinge of the whole thing: "You have seen how a man was made a slave; you shall see how a slave was made a man." Listen to the symmetry in that. That is a writer. That is craft of the highest order. [pause]

So tonight, let's read him as he would want to be read — not with pity, which he did not ask for, but with attention, which he earned. Open to Chapter One. Let's begin where he begins: with a boy who does not know how old he is.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "I have no accurate knowledge of my age, never having seen any authentic record containing it" (Chapter 1).** What to bring out: the opening wound is epistemological, not physical. Slavery is introduced as a theft of *knowledge* — of self, origin, and time. Note the cool legal diction ("authentic record"); Douglass meets the dehumanization with the very rationality it denies him. Ask: why open here, and not with a whipping?

**2. The whipping of Aunt Hester (Chapter 1).** Douglass calls it "the blood-stained gate, the entrance to the hell of slavery, through which I was about to pass." What to notice: he was a child witness; the horror enters through a child's eyes. Note his restraint — he does not editorialize the cruelty so much as *report* it, trusting the reader. This is a place to slow the room down (see Anticipated Difficulties). The passage is also gendered: the first violence Douglass witnesses is against a woman, a thread Jacobs will take up next week.

**3. Hugh Auld forbids the reading lessons (Chapter 6).** "If you teach that nigger... how to read, there would be no keeping him." Here the period racial vocabulary appears inside quoted speech. What to bring out: Douglass deliberately preserves the slaveholder's word to indict the slaveholder — the ugliness belongs to Auld, not to Douglass. Analytically, this is the book's turning point: the boy converts a prohibition into a program. Literacy is reframed, in a single paragraph, from privilege to weapon.

**4. "It had given me a view of my wretched condition, without the remedy... I would at times feel that learning to read had been a curse rather than a blessing" (Chapter 7).** The great paradox. What to notice: knowledge is double-edged — it frees the mind before it can free the body, and that gap is agony. This is, in embryo, the double consciousness Du Bois will name in Session 3. Ask students whether they have ever learned something they could not un-know.

**5. The fight with Covey and "how a slave was made a man" (Chapter 10, for context).** Though Chapter 10 may fall outside the assigned pages, the famous formulation depends on it. What to bring out: Douglass locates his rebirth not in escape but in an act of physical self-defense. Note the chiasmus — "a man was made a slave… a slave was made a man" — and how the sentence's perfect balance enacts a reversal of fortune. Worth reading aloud even if unassigned.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does Douglass open with his unknown birthday rather than with physical cruelty?**
   *Model answer:* Because he wants to show that slavery's first theft is of identity and personhood, not merely of comfort or safety. Not knowing one's age severs a person from human time and family. It is a quieter, deeper outrage than the whip, and it frames everything that follows.

2. **The *Narrative* was written to persuade a skeptical white Northern audience. How does that purpose shape the prose?**
   *Model answer:* It accounts for the abundance of specific names, places, and dates — evidence against the charge of fraud — and for the controlled, unsentimental tone, which forestalls the accusation of exaggeration. Douglass writes to be believed by readers who did not want to believe him.

3. **What does Douglass mean by calling literacy "a curse rather than a blessing"?**
   *Model answer:* Reading reveals the full injustice of his condition before it can deliver him from it, so knowledge arrives as torment as well as power. The mind is freed while the body remains enslaved, and that gap between awareness and remedy is its own suffering.

4. **Why preserve the slaveholder's exact, ugly words on the page?**
   *Model answer:* Because the cruelty belongs to the speaker, and quoting it precisely indicts him more effectively than any paraphrase. Douglass lets Hugh Auld convict himself, and in doing so turns the master's prohibition into the slave's strategy.

5. **The book is introduced by white abolitionists who vouch for its truth. What does that framing tell us?**
   *Model answer:* It reveals that in 1845 a Black man's account of his own life could not stand on its own authority; it required white validation to be believed. The apparatus of authentication is itself a record of the racism the book opposes.

6. **Douglass withholds the method of his escape. Why, and what does the choice reveal?**
   *Model answer:* He withholds it to protect those who helped him and to keep the route open for others still enslaved. The omission reveals a strategic, responsible mind weighing the safety of a community against the completeness of his own story.

7. **Where in these chapters does Douglass show emotion through scene rather than statement? What is the effect?**
   *Model answer:* In the whipping of Aunt Hester, witnessed silently by the child, the horror is conveyed through observed detail rather than declared feeling. The restraint forces readers to supply the emotion themselves, which makes it land harder than any lament.

8. **What is the relationship between literacy and freedom in this text? Is it as simple as Douglass suggests?**
   *Model answer:* Douglass presents literacy as the pathway from slavery to freedom, and for him it largely was. But the text also complicates the equation: reading first deepens his anguish, and freedom finally requires the body's flight, not only the mind's awakening. Literacy is necessary but not sufficient.

9. **How does Douglass establish his own humanity for a readership that doubted it?**
   *Model answer:* Through the quality of the prose itself — its reason, structure, and restraint — which performs the very personhood slavery denied. The book is an argument made as much by *how* it is written as by what it says.

10. **The *Narrative* was written when Douglass was twenty-seven. How should that change our reading?**
    *Model answer:* It underscores how much he had already mastered, and how recent and raw the experiences were. The composure of the prose is the achievement of a very young man writing about wounds only seven years closed, which is astonishing rather than merely admirable.

### Anticipated Difficulties

This is the course's first encounter with historical violence and with the racial vocabulary of an older text, so set the terms now and they will serve you all term. Before reading the Aunt Hester passage or the Hugh Auld quotation aloud, tell the room plainly what is coming and why the language is on the page — that Douglass preserves the slaveholder's word to indict the slaveholder. Decide in advance whether you will voice the slur in quoted passages or signal it; either choice is defensible, but make it deliberately and tell the room your practice. When the violence lands, let silence sit; do not rush to interpretation. A largely white room may freeze, unsure whether it has the standing to discuss a Black man's account of slavery — reassure them that careful, humble reading is exactly what is wanted, and that no one is being asked to perform either guilt or expertise. Keep returning to Douglass as artist, not victim; it steadies the room and honors him.

## Session 2 — Harriet Jacobs: *Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl*

### At a Glance
The first slave narrative published by a Black woman in the United States, and a book that occupies different ground than Douglass: more intimate, more bodily, focused on sexuality, motherhood, and the particular terrors slavery held for women. The session deepens the genre established in Session 1 while complicating it — Jacobs writes to a Northern white female reader she does not fully trust, asks for solidarity rather than pity, and makes formal choices (a pseudonym, a sentimental frame) that are strategies of survival, not failures of nerve. The goal is for students to feel the difference a woman's standpoint makes, and to see Jacobs as the shrewd literary tactician she was.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Harriet Ann Jacobs was born into slavery around 1813 in Edenton, North Carolina ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Harriet Jacobs"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Harriet-Jacobs); [Documenting the American South, UNC](https://docsouth.unc.edu/fpn/jacobs/bio.html)). Her early childhood was, by her own account, unusually sheltered: she did not understand that she was a slave until she was six, when her mother died and she was taken into the household of a mistress who taught her to read, sew, and spell — a kindness Jacobs never forgot and which makes the betrayals that follow sharper. When that mistress died, the eleven-year-old Harriet was bequeathed, by will, to a young niece, and so passed effectively into the control of the niece's father, Dr. James Norcom — the man she would render in her book under the name "Dr. Flint."

The central ordeal of Jacobs's young womanhood, and of her narrative, was Norcom's relentless sexual pursuit. From her early teens he subjected her to harassment, threats, and psychological siege, while his jealous wife treated her as a rival. To escape Norcom — and, crucially, to assert some fragment of her own will in choosing — Jacobs entered a relationship with a white neighbor, Samuel Tredwell Sawyer (the "Mr. Sands" of the book), with whom she had two children, Joseph and Louisa Matilda. She describes this decision in the book with extraordinary candor and anguish, anticipating her white female reader's judgment and refusing to accept it: "I know I did wrong," she writes, and then insists that the woman who has never lived under such conditions cannot be her judge. This is one of the boldest passages in nineteenth-century American writing, and it is the heart of why the book matters.

When Norcom threatened to send her children to a plantation to break her, Jacobs made the decision the book is famous for: she went into hiding. For nearly seven years she concealed herself in a tiny crawlspace above a storeroom in her free grandmother's house — a space about nine feet long, seven wide, and three high at its peak, where she could not stand, where the heat and cold and insects were torments, and from which, through a small hole she bored, she could watch her own children play in the yard without their knowing she was there. She called it her "loophole of retreat." She remained there until 1842, when she finally escaped north by boat. Have students hold the physical fact of that crawlspace; it is one of the most extraordinary acts of endurance in American letters, and it was undertaken so that she could remain near her children rather than flee without them.

In the North, Jacobs found work, reunited eventually with her children, and became connected to abolitionist and reform circles, including the writer and editor Lydia Maria Child, who would serve as her editor. She remained legally a fugitive — Norcom and later his daughter pursued her even in the North under the Fugitive Slave Act — until friends purchased her freedom in 1852, an outcome that humiliated her even as it freed her, because it confirmed that she had been, all along, a thing that could be bought. Encouraged by abolitionist friends, and writing at night while employed as a domestic servant, she composed her story over several years. *Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl, Written by Herself* was published in 1861, under the pseudonym "Linda Brent," with an introduction by Child vouching for its authenticity.

For more than a century the book's authorship was doubted; some scholars assumed Child had written it, or that it was fiction. It was the meticulous archival work of the scholar Jean Fagan Yellin, beginning in the 1980s, that authenticated Jacobs's authorship beyond dispute, matching names, places, and dated letters, and restored *Incidents* to its rightful place as a major American autobiography. That recovery is itself worth telling students: a woman wrote one of the period's essential books, and the literary establishment spent a century assuming she could not have. After the Civil War, Jacobs worked in relief efforts among freedpeople in the South and in Washington. She died in Washington, D.C., in 1897. She is now understood as the indispensable counterpart to Douglass — the woman's witness to a system whose cruelties fell on women in ways Douglass's account could not fully reach.

### Historical & Cultural Context

*Incidents* shares the abolitionist context of Douglass's *Narrative* but speaks from a standpoint the male narratives largely omit: the sexual vulnerability of enslaved women and the impossibility, under slavery, of the domestic virtues that nineteenth-century America prized in women. This is the crux. The dominant culture of Jacobs's white readership held up an ideal of "true womanhood" — piety, purity, domesticity, submission. Slavery made every one of those impossible for an enslaved woman: she could not protect her own chastity, could not keep her own children, could not command her own home. Jacobs's genius is to expose the cruelty of judging enslaved women by a standard the system forcibly denied them.

The law is essential background. Under the principle *partus sequitur ventrem*, a child's enslaved or free status followed the mother's, which meant that the children an enslaved woman bore — including children fathered by white men, often by force — were born into slavery and counted as her enslaver's property. This legal fact turned enslaved women's bodies into instruments of capital and made sexual exploitation not an aberration but a structural feature of the system. The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850, which compelled Northern states to return escapees and criminalized those who aided them, is the reason Jacobs remained in danger and legally a fugitive even after reaching the North.

Finally, the genre context: Jacobs is writing within and against the conventions of the sentimental novel, the dominant form of women's popular fiction in her era, with its direct address to the "dear reader," its appeals to feeling, and its domestic plots. She borrows its tools to reach her audience while bending them toward a truth sentimental fiction never told. Students who recognize the sentimental frame will read her formal choices as strategy rather than naïveté.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Chapter 1, "Childhood."** Jacobs (as "Linda Brent") recounts a sheltered early childhood; she does not realize she is a slave until age six, when her mother dies. Her first mistress teaches her to read, sew, and spell, and Jacobs loves her — but when the mistress dies, she wills the eleven-year-old Harriet not to freedom but to a young niece, placing her effectively under the niece's father, Dr. Flint (the fictional name for Dr. James Norcom). Her free, respected grandmother ("Aunt Marthy") is established as a moral anchor.

**Chapter 5, "The Trials of Girlhood."** As Linda enters adolescence, Dr. Flint begins a campaign of sexual harassment — whispering "foul words," threatening, surveilling. His jealous wife treats Linda as a rival. Jacobs writes around the sexual content with deliberate indirection, addressing the reader's "delicacy" while making the siege unmistakable. She names the special horror slavery holds for women.

**Chapter 10, "A Perilous Passage in the Slave Girl's Life."** To resist Flint and exert some sliver of will, Linda enters a relationship with a white neighbor, Mr. Sands (Samuel Sawyer), and bears his child. She confesses the decision with anguish — "I know I did wrong" — and then refuses the reader's right to judge a woman who never faced her conditions. This is the book's moral climax.

**Chapter 21, "The Loophole of Retreat."** When Flint threatens her children, Linda hides in a crawlspace above her grandmother's storeroom — roughly nine by seven feet, three feet high — where she cannot stand. Through a bored peephole she watches her own children play, unseen, enduring heat, cold, and vermin. She remains there nearly seven years before escaping north.

### Lecture Script

Last week we met Frederick Douglass, and tonight we meet the woman who stands beside him at the foundation of this whole literature — Harriet Jacobs. And I want to start by saying why we need both of them, why Douglass alone is not enough. [pause]

Douglass wrote a great book. But there were things his book could not reach, because there were cruelties of slavery that fell on women in particular — and Jacobs wrote those. Her book is more intimate than Douglass's. It is about the body, about sexuality, about motherhood. It is about a young woman pursued for years by the man who owned her, and about the impossible choices that left her. And it was the first time an American Black woman told that story, in print, in her own words. [pause]

Here is the thing I most want you to carry tonight — a single physical fact. To escape her enslaver, Harriet Jacobs hid. Not for a night, not for a week. For nearly seven years she hid in a crawlspace above a storeroom in her grandmother's house. The space was about nine feet long, seven feet wide, and three feet high at the tallest point. She could not stand up in it. Seven years. And she chose it — she chose that coffin-sized space over running north — because from a little hole she bored in the wall, she could see her own children playing in the yard. She could watch them grow and they did not know she was there. [pause] She called it her "loophole of retreat." Hold that image tonight. We will come back to it.

Now I have to talk to you honestly about how this book speaks, because it speaks differently than Douglass. Jacobs wrote under a false name — "Linda Brent" — and she addressed her book to a particular reader: a respectable white woman of the North. And she does not entirely trust that reader. She knows that reader has been raised to judge a woman's worth by her purity, her virtue, her chastity. And so when Jacobs comes to the hardest part of her story — the relationship she entered with a white man, the children she bore — she does something extraordinary. She says, in effect: I know what you think of me. I know your rules. And I am telling you that you have no right to apply them to me, because you have never lived where I lived. [pause] "I know I did wrong," she writes — and then she takes the judgment out of the reader's hands. That is one of the bravest moves I know of in nineteenth-century American writing.

One more thing, and it tells you something about how this literature has been treated. For more than a hundred years, people did not believe Harriet Jacobs wrote this book. They thought her white editor wrote it, or that it was a novel. It took a scholar named Jean Fagan Yellin, working in the archives in the 1980s, digging up letters and records, to prove what should never have been in doubt — that Harriet Jacobs wrote her own life. [pause] We will see that pattern again and again in this course: a writer doing extraordinary work, and a literary world that could not, or would not, believe it. Tonight, let's believe her. Let's read her closely, the way she earned. Open to Chapter One.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "I was born a slave; but I never knew it till six years of happy childhood had passed away" (Chapter 1).** What to bring out: the sheltered opening is a deliberate structural choice — Jacobs gives us innocence so that its loss will register. Compare to Douglass's opening: he never had such innocence to lose, while Jacobs's whole book turns on a fall *from* a protected childhood. The contrast is gendered and class-inflected (her grandmother was a respected free woman).

**2. "The Trials of Girlhood" (Chapter 5).** Jacobs describes, with great care and necessary indirection, the onset of Norcom's sexual harassment. What to notice: the *reticence* is itself meaningful — she writes around the unspeakable, trusting her reader to understand, partly to protect the reader's "delicacy" and partly because the sentimental conventions she's using cannot name such things directly. Discuss how silence and circumlocution can carry more force than explicitness.

**3. "I know I did wrong... but I will not try to screen myself behind the plea of compulsion" — and the address to the reader (Chapter 10).** The book's moral and rhetorical climax. What to bring out: Jacobs simultaneously confesses and refuses to be judged, performing humility while seizing authority. Note the direct address: "Pity me, and pardon me, O virtuous reader!" — which can read as supplication but functions as a challenge to the reader's right to judge. Ask: is she asking for forgiveness, or denying that forgiveness is the reader's to give?

**4. "The Loophole of Retreat" (Chapter 21).** The crawlspace. What to notice: the brutal physical specificity (dimensions, heat, vermin, the bored peephole) and the bitter irony of "retreat" — a word of leisure applied to a torment chosen as the lesser horror. What to draw out: this is freedom redefined under slavery, where the only available autonomy is the choice of *which* suffering. The peephole — seeing her children, unseen — is a devastating image of motherhood under bondage.

**5. "Slavery is terrible for men; but it is far more terrible for women."** What to bring out: Jacobs's explicit thesis. Press students on the claim — what specifically does she point to? (Sexual exploitation; the breaking of mothers from children; the impossibility of protecting one's family or one's own body.) This is the line that justifies the whole book's existence alongside Douglass's.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does Jacobs write under a pseudonym and address a Northern white woman? How does that shape what she says and omits?**
   *Model answer:* The pseudonym protected her and others still in danger, and the imagined white female reader is the audience she most needed to move toward solidarity. The framing makes her careful, indirect about sexual matters, and constantly aware of the reader's likely judgment — which she both anticipates and resists.

2. **How does Jacobs's narrative voice differ from Douglass's, and what explains the difference?**
   *Model answer:* Jacobs is more intimate, more bodily, more openly emotional, and more focused on family and sexuality, where Douglass is restrained and oratorical. The difference reflects both their distinct experiences of slavery as woman and man, and the different audiences and conventions — sentimental versus oratorical — each was working within.

3. **What does the seven years in the crawlspace reveal about the choices available to enslaved mothers?**
   *Model answer:* It reveals that her only autonomy was the choice between terrible options — that to stay near and protect her children she had to entomb herself for years. The episode shows that motherhood under slavery offered no good path, only the selection of the least unbearable one.

4. **Examine the line "Pity me, and pardon me, O virtuous reader." Is Jacobs submitting to the reader's judgment or challenging it?**
   *Model answer:* Both at once, strategically. She performs the humility her audience expects while simultaneously insisting that a woman who never faced her conditions has no standing to judge her. The apparent plea is in fact a redistribution of moral authority.

5. **How does Jacobs use — and subvert — the conventions of the sentimental novel?**
   *Model answer:* She adopts its direct address, its appeals to feeling, and its domestic concerns to reach a readership trained on that form. But she fills the form with truths sentimental fiction never told, using familiar tools to deliver unfamiliar and unwelcome content.

6. **Evaluate her claim that slavery is "far more terrible for women." What is she pointing to?**
   *Model answer:* She points to sexual exploitation, the legal fact that her children were born enslaved through her, and the systematic destruction of motherhood and family. Her case is strong precisely because it names harms the male narratives could not, though students may also note that comparing suffering is itself fraught.

7. **What is the significance of Jacobs's literacy and her grandmother's free status?**
   *Model answer:* Both complicate any single picture of slavery — Jacobs had a protected early childhood and learned to read, and her grandmother was a respected free woman with a house. These details show the range of conditions under slavery and give Jacobs a base of support and self-regard that shapes her sense of injustice.

8. **Why was Jacobs's authorship doubted for over a century, and what does that tell us?**
   *Model answer:* It was doubted because a literary establishment found it hard to credit that a formerly enslaved Black woman could have written so accomplished a book, assuming her white editor was responsible. The long disbelief is itself evidence of the racism and sexism the book confronts, and Yellin's authentication is a model of literary recovery.

9. **How does Jacobs ask for solidarity rather than pity, and does she succeed?**
   *Model answer:* She presents herself not as a passive victim but as an intelligent agent making impossible choices, and she addresses the reader as a fellow woman and potential ally rather than a benefactor. Whether she "succeeds" is worth debating, but her refusal to be merely pitied is consistent and deliberate.

10. **Set Jacobs beside Douglass. What does reading them together give us that either alone cannot?**
    *Model answer:* Together they show that slavery was not one experience but many, shaped decisively by gender. Douglass gives the public, oratorical drama of self-making; Jacobs gives the intimate, bodily, familial cost. The two are complementary witnesses, and the foundation of the literature is incomplete without both.

### Anticipated Difficulties

This session handles sexual violence, and you should signal that at the end of the previous week so no one is ambushed. Jacobs's own reticence is your ally: she writes around these matters with deliberate indirection, and you can follow her lead, naming what the text is describing without graphic elaboration. Some students — especially in a largely white room — may find the direct addresses to the "virtuous reader" uncomfortable, feeling implicated; let that discomfort be productive rather than something to smooth over, and redirect to the rhetorical question of what Jacobs is *doing* to that reader. As with Douglass, resist pity as the dominant register; Jacobs explicitly refuses it. If the room goes quiet after the crawlspace passage, let it. The silence is appropriate to what she endured, and you need not fill it.

## Session 3 — Two Visions: Booker T. Washington vs. W.E.B. Du Bois

### At a Glance
Two of the most influential Black intellectuals in American history offered competing answers to the same question: how should Black Americans survive and thrive in a white-dominated society after the failure of Reconstruction? Washington counseled accommodation, vocational uplift, and patience; Du Bois demanded civil and political rights now, and named the inner experience of being Black in America with a phrase that will run through the rest of the course — "double consciousness." This session puts their arguments in direct conversation and gives students the analytic vocabulary they will use for the next thirteen weeks. The goal is to let the debate breathe as a real disagreement between serious men, not a contest with a predetermined winner.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Booker T. Washington (1856–1915).** Washington was born into slavery in 1856 on a Virginia farm and was a child of nine at emancipation ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Booker T. Washington"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Booker-T-Washington)). His autobiography *Up from Slavery* (1901) narrates a relentless self-betterment: laboring in salt furnaces and coal mines as a boy, walking and riding hundreds of miles to reach the Hampton Institute in Virginia, where he absorbed its philosophy of industrial education and Christian discipline. In 1881 he was chosen to lead a new normal school for Black students in Tuskegee, Alabama, and over the next three decades he built the Tuskegee Institute into the most famous Black educational institution in the country and himself into the most powerful Black man in America — adviser to presidents, controller of philanthropic purse strings, and, through a network of allies, a dominant force in Black politics. His public philosophy, crystallized in the 1895 Atlanta Exposition Address that you will assign, urged Black Americans to focus on economic self-sufficiency and vocational skill while, for the present, accepting social separation and deferring agitation for political and civil rights — "cast down your bucket where you are." To white audiences this was reassuring; to a rising generation of Black intellectuals it was capitulation. Washington's private conduct was more complex than his public accommodationism — he quietly funded legal challenges to segregation — but his public posture defined an era and made him, for better and worse, the gatekeeper of Black advancement at the turn of the century.

**W.E.B. Du Bois (1868–1963).** William Edward Burghardt Du Bois was born free in Great Barrington, Massachusetts, in 1868, three years after the Civil War, into a small Black community in a largely white New England town ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "W.E.B. Du Bois"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/W-E-B-Du-Bois)). His path was the opposite of Washington's: Fisk University in Nashville (where he first encountered the Jim Crow South), then Harvard, where in 1895 he became the first Black American to earn a Ph.D., and study in Germany. He was a scholar of formidable range — sociologist, historian, novelist, editor, activist — and his 1903 *The Souls of Black Folk*, a book of essays you will draw from, is one of the foundational texts of American letters. In it he openly challenged Washington, arguing that accommodation surrendered the very political and civil rights on which any lasting progress depended, and insisting on higher education for a "Talented Tenth" of Black leadership. Du Bois helped found the NAACP in 1909 and edited its magazine, *The Crisis*, for decades, shaping Black political thought across the first half of the twentieth century. His thinking grew increasingly radical over a very long life; disillusioned with American racism, he joined the Communist Party, renounced his citizenship, and moved to Ghana, where he died in 1963 — the day before the March on Washington, a coincidence Martin Luther King Jr. noted from the podium. To students, present Du Bois as the intellectual fountainhead of much that follows: the man who gave the tradition the words "double consciousness," "the veil," and "the color line," and who insisted that the problem of the twentieth century would be "the problem of the color line."

### Historical & Cultural Context

The Washington–Du Bois debate is unintelligible without the catastrophe that frames it: the collapse of Reconstruction. After the Civil War, a brief period (roughly 1865–1877) saw Black men vote, hold office, and exercise citizenship under federal protection. Then, with the withdrawal of federal troops from the South in 1877, that protection vanished. By the 1890s and into the early 1900s — exactly when Washington and Du Bois were writing — Southern states had erected the full apparatus of Jim Crow: legal segregation, sanctioned by the Supreme Court's *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) and its "separate but equal" doctrine; the systematic disenfranchisement of Black voters through poll taxes, literacy tests, and grandfather clauses; and a wave of lynching and racial terror that reached its peak in these decades. This is the world into which both men spoke: not slavery, but its grim successor, a regime designed to return Black Americans to subordination by other means.

Their disagreement, then, was strategic and urgent, a genuine argument about survival under terror. Washington's Atlanta address of 1895 was delivered to a largely white audience at a regional exposition, and its accommodationist tone must be read against the lynching epidemic and the political climate of the moment — a moment in which open Black demands for equality could be answered with violence. Du Bois's 1903 challenge came eight years later, from a Northern-born, European-educated scholar with less to fear from a Southern mob and a different theory of what Black progress required. Students should grasp that both men were responding to the same emergency with different calculations of risk and possibility. The debate also carries a class and regional dimension: Washington, the formerly enslaved Southerner championing the rural masses and the dignity of labor; Du Bois, the free-born New England intellectual championing higher education and the leadership class. The question they argued — accommodation versus agitation, economic uplift versus political rights — would recur, in new forms, through the Harlem Renaissance, the Civil Rights Movement, and beyond.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Booker T. Washington, *Up from Slavery*, Chapter 14, "The Atlanta Exposition Address" (1895).** Washington recounts being invited to speak at the Cotton States and International Exposition in Atlanta — the first time a Black man had been asked to address such a Southern audience. He reproduces the speech. Its central image is a ship lost at sea, dying of thirst, signaled by a friendly vessel to "cast down your bucket where you are" — and finding the water around it fresh. Washington applies the parable to both races: Black Southerners should "cast down" their buckets in agriculture, mechanics, domestic service, and commerce rather than seeking political agitation or migration; white Southerners should "cast down" theirs among the loyal Black laborers already present rather than seeking immigrant labor. He offers the era's most famous metaphor of accommodation: "In all things that are purely social we can be as separate as the fingers, yet one as the hand in all things essential to mutual progress." He counsels patience on civil rights and emphasizes economic self-help. The chapter records the speech's rapturous white reception.

**W.E.B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk*, Chapter 1, "Of Our Spiritual Strivings" (1903).** Du Bois opens by naming the unspoken question white people put to him: "How does it feel to be a problem?" He recalls the childhood moment a white girl refused his visiting card and the veil descended. He introduces his central concept: the American Negro lives with "double consciousness," "this sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others," a "twoness" of being both American and Negro. He sketches the history of Black striving since emancipation — the disappointments of the vote, of education, of freedom that did not deliver equality — and frames the spiritual task ahead as the merging of the two selves into a "better and truer self" without losing either. The essay establishes "the veil" and "the color line" as governing metaphors for the book.

### Lecture Script

Tonight we do something a little different. We're going to put two men in the same room and let them argue — two of the most important Black thinkers in American history, who disagreed, profoundly, about the most urgent question of their time. And I want to say at the outset: this is a real argument between serious people, and I am not going to tell you who won, because I genuinely don't think it's that simple. [pause]

Here's the question they were both trying to answer. It's around 1900. Slavery has been over for a generation. But Reconstruction — that brief window when Black men voted and held office — has collapsed. In its place, the South has built Jim Crow: legal segregation, Black men stripped of the vote, and a wave of lynching that is at its absolute peak in these very years. So the question is not abstract. It is: how do Black Americans survive — and more than survive — under a regime designed to crush them? [pause]

Booker T. Washington had one answer. He was born into slavery, built the Tuskegee Institute up from nothing, and became the most powerful Black man in America. And in 1895, in Atlanta, before a mostly white audience, he gave a speech. He told a parable — a ship dying of thirst at sea, told to "cast down your bucket where you are," and finding fresh water all around it. His message: focus on work, on skills, on economic self-sufficiency. Build wealth and dignity through labor. And for now — for now — set aside the fight for social and political equality. "In all things purely social," he said, "we can be as separate as the fingers, yet one as the hand in all things essential to mutual progress." The white South loved it. [pause]

Eight years later, a very different man answered him. W.E.B. Du Bois was born free in Massachusetts, educated at Fisk and Harvard and in Germany — the first Black American to earn a Harvard Ph.D. And in *The Souls of Black Folk*, he said, in effect: Washington is asking us to give up the very things — the vote, civil rights, higher learning — without which none of the rest can last. You cannot build a people on labor alone while they are stripped of citizenship. [pause]

But Du Bois did something more than argue policy. He gave us a phrase I want you to carry for the rest of this course — for all thirteen weeks that follow. He called it "double consciousness." Listen: "this sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others, of measuring one's soul by the tape of a world that looks on in amused contempt and pity." [pause] He's describing what it feels like to be Black in America — to always carry two pictures of yourself: who you know you are, and who the country insists you are. He called the thing between them "the veil." And here is what's remarkable — he opens the book not with an answer but with a question that white America was always silently asking him: "How does it feel to be a problem?" [pause]

So tonight, hold both men in your mind at once. Washington, the formerly enslaved Southerner, speaking carefully in a world where the wrong word could get a man killed. Du Bois, the free Northerner, with the education and the relative safety to demand more. Were they enemies? Or were they two halves of a single, impossible situation? Let's read them and find out. Start with Washington's bucket. Then we'll let Du Bois answer.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "Cast down your bucket where you are" (Washington).** What to bring out: the parable's brilliance and its trap. The image dignifies labor and counsels patience, but "where you are" accepts the existing order — including segregation — as the starting point. Ask students to notice how a metaphor can do political work: the bucket reassures white listeners that Black ambition will stay in its place.

**2. "In all things that are purely social we can be as separate as the fingers, yet one as the hand" (Washington).** The most quoted and most contested sentence. What to notice: the hand/fingers image makes segregation sound natural and cooperative. Du Bois and his successors heard in it a surrender of the principle of equality. Press the class: what does "purely social" conceal? (In practice, "social" separation underwrote political and economic subordination.)

**3. "How does it feel to be a problem?" (Du Bois).** What to bring out: Du Bois opens by ventriloquizing the white gaze — the question is one he is *asked*, in a hundred unspoken ways. The rhetorical move places the reader inside the experience of being objectified. The optional student prompt builds on this; the lecture should land it gently.

**4. "Two souls, two thoughts, two unreconciled strivings; two warring ideals in one dark body" (Du Bois).** The definition of double consciousness. What to notice: the relentless doubling of the syntax enacts the split it describes. Note that Du Bois does not present the twoness only as affliction — he speaks of "second-sight," a gift of clearer vision. Draw out both valences; they will recur in Dunbar's mask and Hurston's refusals.

**5. The two visions side by side.** Set Washington's "cast down your bucket" against Du Bois's insistence that the Negro must not be made to "bleach his Negro soul in a flood of white Americanism." What to bring out: the deepest difference is over what must *not* be surrendered. Washington defers rights for security; Du Bois holds that some things — selfhood, citizenship — cannot be deferred without being lost.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **State Washington's central claim and Du Bois's counter-claim, each in one sentence. Which is harder to argue against?**
   *Model answer:* Washington: Black Americans should build economic self-sufficiency and defer the fight for social and political equality. Du Bois: deferring civil and political rights surrenders the foundation on which any lasting progress depends. Which is harder to refute depends on one's read of the era's dangers — Washington's caution looks prudent under terror, Du Bois's principle looks vindicated by history.

2. **Du Bois introduces "double consciousness." Restate it in your own words. Does one need to be Black to experience something like it?**
   *Model answer:* It is the experience of seeing oneself through the contemptuous eyes of a dominant culture while also holding one's own self-knowledge — a permanent inner twoness. Analogues exist for many marginalized people, but Du Bois roots it in the specific condition of Black Americans behind "the veil," and the specificity should not be dissolved into generality too quickly.

3. **How does the intended audience shape each man's argument and tone?**
   *Model answer:* Washington addresses a white Southern audience with power over Black lives, so he reassures and accommodates. Du Bois writes substantially to Black readers and to Northern reformers, so he can name grievances and demand rights openly. Audience is the key to the difference in register.

4. **Both men use the word "freedom." How would each define it?**
   *Model answer:* For Washington, freedom is largely economic — the dignity and security of skilled, self-sufficient labor. For Du Bois, freedom is also political and spiritual — the vote, civil equality, higher learning, and the right to an undivided self. The same word names two different goals.

5. **Read against the lynching epidemic of the 1890s, does Washington's caution look like cowardice or wisdom?**
   *Model answer:* It can be read as a survival strategy under genuine threat of violence, which is not the same as cowardice. But it can also be read as accepting a subordination that emboldened the very forces threatening Black people. The text supports an argument on either side, which is why the debate endures.

6. **What does Du Bois mean by "second-sight," and how does it complicate the idea that double consciousness is only a burden?**
   *Model answer:* Second-sight is the clearer vision that comes from seeing the society both from inside and from its margins — the outsider's penetrating view of the whole. It reframes double consciousness as partly a gift, a source of insight unavailable to those who have never been made to look at themselves through others' eyes.

7. **Why might Washington's private actions (quietly funding anti-segregation lawsuits) differ from his public posture?**
   *Model answer:* Because his public accommodationism was itself a strategy for surviving and accumulating influence in a hostile environment, which he then used to act covertly. The gap suggests his real views were more militant than his rhetoric, and that the rhetoric was, in part, protective coloration.

8. **"How does it feel to be a problem?" Why open a book this way?**
   *Model answer:* It immediately places the reader inside the objectifying gaze Du Bois is analyzing, turning a person into a "problem" before our eyes. The opening enacts the very dehumanization the book sets out to overturn, and it implicates the reader in the question.

9. **This debate took place around 1903. How much of it is still alive?**
   *Model answer:* Its terms recur whenever Black advancement is debated — economic uplift versus political power, working within systems versus demanding their transformation, respectability versus confrontation. Students will recognize versions of the argument in contemporary politics, which is part of the point.

10. **Is it fair to teach these two as opponents, or were they arguing past each other?**
    *Model answer:* They genuinely disagreed about strategy and priorities, so "opponents" is fair. But they also shared the goal of Black flourishing and faced the same terror, so framing them only as enemies flattens a complex relationship. The richest reading holds both the real conflict and the shared predicament.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Both texts use the word "Negro" throughout, as the standard and dignified term of their period — Du Bois uses it with deliberate pride. Name this once at the start: explain that "Negro" was the respectful term of the era, that we read it as a period usage, and that we neither flinch from it on the page nor import it into our own present-day speech. Settle that and the room can relax into the texts. A subtler difficulty is the temptation to turn the debate into a morality play with Du Bois as hero and Washington as sellout; resist it, because it flattens both men and lets the room off the hard thinking. Hold Washington's caution inside its context of lethal violence. Finally, "double consciousness" is the course's master concept; budget time to define it slowly and let students test it, because everything downstream depends on it landing here.

## Session 4 — The Mask and the Voice: Paul Laurence Dunbar

### At a Glance
Dunbar was the first African American poet to win sustained national recognition, and his career is a parable of the politics of audience. White readers and publishers wanted his dialect verse — Black speech rendered for a white market that often heard it as quaint or comic — and Dunbar, who needed the income and the fame, gave it to them, even as he resented being confined to it and longed to be read for his poems in formal English. This session reads four poems that show his full range and the bind he lived inside: the anguished lyric "Sympathy," the harrowing anti-lynching ballad "The Haunted Oak," the affirming dialect celebration "When Malindy Sings," and the elegy "Frederick Douglass." The goal is to let students feel both the mastery and the trap, and to take the dialect poems seriously as art rather than as embarrassment.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Paul Laurence Dunbar was born in 1872 in Dayton, Ohio, to parents who had both been enslaved in Kentucky before the Civil War ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Paul Laurence Dunbar"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Paul-Laurence-Dunbar); [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/paul-laurence-dunbar)). His mother, Matilda, who had been freed by emancipation, encouraged his reading and recited to him the songs and stories of the plantation world she had known; his father, Joshua, had escaped slavery and served in the Union Army. The tales and cadences his mother carried out of bondage would later feed the dialect poems, which is worth stressing — the dialect was not a costume Dunbar invented but a living inheritance, however much the literary marketplace then exploited it.

Dunbar was the only Black student in his Dayton high school class, where a classmate named Orville Wright (later of the airplane) was a friend; he edited the school paper and was elected class poet. Brilliant and ambitious, he nonetheless graduated into a job market that, for a Black man in 1891, offered him work as an elevator operator. He wrote poems between floors. He self-published his first collection, *Oak and Ivy* (1893), selling copies to his elevator passengers, and a second, *Majors and Minors* (1895), whose title encodes the divide that would define him — the "majors," his serious poems in standard English, and the "minors," the dialect verse. When the powerful white critic William Dean Howells reviewed *Majors and Minors* enthusiastically in *Harper's Weekly*, praising above all the dialect poems, Dunbar's national career was launched — and his cage was built. Howells's preface to the subsequent volume *Lyrics of Lowly Life* (1896) made Dunbar famous and simultaneously fixed his reputation as a "dialect poet," to Dunbar's lasting frustration. He is reported to have lamented that Howells had "done me irrevocable harm in the dictum he laid down regarding my dialect verse."

For roughly a decade Dunbar was the most celebrated Black writer in America. He published prolifically — poetry, short stories, novels (including *The Sport of the Gods*, 1902), and song lyrics — and read his work to audiences in the United States and on a triumphant tour of England. He worked for a time at the Library of Congress. In 1898 he married the writer Alice Ruth Moore (later Alice Dunbar-Nelson, herself an important figure); the marriage was turbulent and ended in separation. His most famous poem, "We Wear the Mask" — which many of your residents will already know, and which we will leave largely to one side this evening precisely because it is so widely taught — distills in fourteen lines the central theme of his serious work: the discipline of concealment, the smiling face worn over suffering. That mask is also a fair emblem of his career, in which he performed for a white audience a version of Black life it found palatable while privately chafing at the performance.

His life was short and shadowed. He contracted tuberculosis, was prescribed alcohol and whiskey as treatments in the manner of the day, and struggled with both the disease and the drinking. He died in Dayton in 1906, at thirty-three. His reputation suffered afterward, partly because later generations — including some figures of the Harlem Renaissance who arrived just a decade or two later — were embarrassed by the dialect verse and its associations with minstrelsy and the plantation tradition. But a fuller reading has restored him: scholars now read the dialect poems as a complex, sometimes subversive negotiation with a hostile market, and the standard-English lyrics as among the finest of their era. Have students hold both truths — the genuine beauty of the work and the painful conditions of its production. Dunbar is the course's clearest case study in the politics of audience, the thread we will pull all term.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Dunbar wrote in the nadir of American race relations — the same Jim Crow decades that frame the Washington–Du Bois debate of Session 3, and indeed Dunbar's elegy "Frederick Douglass" (Douglass died in 1895) sits squarely in that political moment. This was the era of legalized segregation after *Plessy* (1896), of mass disenfranchisement, and of an epidemic of lynching — the precise context of "The Haunted Oak," a poem spoken by the very tree from which a man is hanged. To read Dunbar's anti-lynching poem as topical journalism in verse is not wrong; lynching was a daily American reality, often a public spectacle, and Black writers who addressed it did so at real risk.

The literary marketplace Dunbar entered was shaped by the "plantation tradition" — a popular white literature (Joel Chandler Harris's Uncle Remus, Thomas Nelson Page's nostalgic Old South) that rendered Black characters in dialect for white amusement and often for explicitly nostalgic, pro-Southern ends. Dunbar's dialect poems were marketed into that tradition and read by many white readers through its lens, which is the source of the later embarrassment. But Dunbar's relationship to dialect was far more serious than minstrelsy: he drew on authentic African American speech and, in poems like "When Malindy Sings," used the vernacular to assert the superiority of an unschooled Black artistry over trained white performance — a quiet act of cultural defiance inside an apparently genial poem. Students should understand that the same period that lynched Black men also consumed Black dialect as entertainment, and that Dunbar was navigating both facts at once. The tension between what white audiences wanted (charming dialect) and what Dunbar wanted (to be read as a poet, in any register) is the historical engine of his work.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Sympathy" (1899).** Three stanzas. Stanza one: the speaker declares he knows what the caged bird feels — evoking the free world outside the bars (sun on slopes, wind in grass, the river, the opening bud, the bird's perfume) that the captive cannot reach. Stanza two: he knows why the caged bird beats its wing against the bars until it bleeds and returns to its perch — not for song but from longing for freedom. Stanza three, the famous turn: he knows why the caged bird *sings* — its song is not joy but prayer and plea, "a prayer that he sends from his heart's deep core," and the refrain becomes "I know why the caged bird sings!" The bird is a transparent figure for the imprisoned Black self under Jim Crow; the song is suffering misread as contentment.

**"The Haunted Oak" (1900).** A ballad in which an oak tree narrates. A man is taken by a lynch mob — falsely accused, the poem makes clear, "and ne'er a word spoke he" of guilt — and hanged from the tree's bough. The tree describes the mob (including, pointedly, a judge and a doctor among the "masked" men), the victim's innocence and terror, and the night of the killing. Cursed by the act, the bough on which the man died withers and the tree is haunted ever after, bending and shrinking under the memory. The horror is delivered through the tree's unwilling complicity and permanent grief.

**"When Malindy Sings" (1895).** A dialect poem in which the speaker tells "Miss Lucy" to put away her written music and "books," because no trained, schooled singing can compare to the natural, soul-deep singing of Malindy. Malindy's voice silences the mockingbird, moves listeners to tears, conjures old spirituals ("Swing Low, Sweet Chariot"), and seems to open heaven itself. The poem inverts the racial hierarchy of its day: the unschooled Black singer's art is presented as vastly superior to cultivated white performance.

**"Frederick Douglass" (1895, elegy).** A formal elegy mourning Douglass's death. Dunbar laments that Douglass has died at a moment when Black America most needs him — " instead of a calm, the wind blows from the storm" — praises Douglass as a towering voice and leader, a "brave one," likens him to a beacon and a tower, and closes by promising that his name and example will endure. The poem registers the political darkness of the 1890s and the loss of the era's great Black voice.

### Lecture Script

Tonight we meet a man caught in a trap — a trap made, in part, out of his own success. Paul Laurence Dunbar was the first Black poet in America to become truly famous, nationally famous, and I want to tell you the price of that fame, because it is one of the clearest examples in this whole course of something we'll keep coming back to: the politics of audience — the question of who a writer is allowed to write for, and what they're allowed to say. [pause]

Dunbar was born in Dayton, Ohio, in 1872. His parents had both been enslaved in Kentucky. His mother carried out of slavery the songs and the speech of that world, and she gave them to her son. He was brilliant — the only Black student in his high school class, the class poet, a friend of young Orville Wright. And when he graduated, in 1891, the only job America would give a gifted Black man was running an elevator. So he wrote poems between floors, and sold his first book to his passengers. [pause]

Then a powerful white critic discovered him and praised him to the skies — and praised, above all, his poems written in Black dialect. And here's the trap. White America wanted Dunbar's dialect poems. They found them charming. And Dunbar wrote them, beautifully, because they made him famous and they paid — but he ached to be read for his serious poems in formal English, and he felt that the praise for his dialect had, in his own words, done him "irrevocable harm." He spent his short life — he died at thirty-three — inside that contradiction. [pause]

We are going to read four poems tonight, and I've chosen them to show you his whole range. Now, many of you already know his most famous poem, "We Wear the Mask" — and it is wonderful, but precisely because you may know it, we're going to set it aside tonight and read four others that deserve the same attention. [pause]

First, "Sympathy." This is the poem with the line you *do* know, even if you don't know you know it: "I know why the caged bird sings." Maya Angelou took that line for the title of her memoir, sixty years later. Listen to what Dunbar is doing: the caged bird beats its wings bloody against the bars, and when it finally sings, the song is not joy — it's a prayer, a plea. He is telling us that what the world hears as a happy Black song may be the sound of suffering. [pause]

Second, the hardest poem of the night: "The Haunted Oak." This is an anti-lynching poem, and Dunbar does something extraordinary with it — he gives the poem to the *tree*. The oak itself speaks, describing how an innocent man was hanged from its branch by a masked mob that included a judge and a doctor, and how the branch withered and the tree has been haunted ever since. I'll have more to say before we read it; I want us to slow down for this one. [pause]

Third, to show you the other side of dialect — "When Malindy Sings." Here the dialect is not a cage; it's a celebration. The speaker tells a white woman to put away her sheet music, because no trained singing on earth can touch the natural, God-given voice of Malindy. Inside a poem that white audiences found charming, Dunbar quietly turns the whole racial hierarchy upside down. [pause]

And last, his elegy for Frederick Douglass — the man we began this course with — who died in 1895. Dunbar mourns him as the great voice silenced just when the storm is rising. It closes a circle for us: the witness of Session One, mourned by the poet of Session Four. Let's read. We'll begin with the caged bird.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "Sympathy" — the refrain "I know why the caged bird sings!"** What to bring out: the poem withholds the word "sings" until the final stanza, building from feeling (stanza 1) to violent struggle (the bloodied wing, stanza 2) to song-as-prayer (stanza 3). The crucial reversal: song is not contentment but supplication. Connect to double consciousness — the bird's song is *misread* by the world outside the cage, exactly as Black expression was misread. Note the Angelou inheritance as a live example of how a line travels.

**2. "Sympathy" — "It is not a carol of joy or glee, / But a prayer."** What to notice: Dunbar explicitly corrects a misreading inside the poem, instructing us how to hear. This is a poet acutely aware that his audience may mistake his meaning — which is the whole drama of his career. The self-interpreting move is itself a comment on the politics of audience.

**3. "The Haunted Oak" — the speaking tree and the masked "judge" and "doctor."** What to bring out: the genius of the displaced narrator. By voicing the tree, Dunbar makes an unwilling witness of nature itself and avoids the impossible position of speaking *as* the lynched man. Note the indictment of respectability — the mob contains the town's pillars, judge and doctor, behind masks. The withering bough literalizes a curse: the violence poisons the land. This is where the racial vocabulary and historical terror are most acute; handle with care (see Anticipated Difficulties).

**4. "When Malindy Sings" — "G'way an' quit dat noise, Miss Lucy."** What to notice: the dialect is doing assertive cultural work, not comic work. The poem ranks the untrained Black voice above the trained white one, and summons the spirituals ("Swing Low, Sweet Chariot") as the height of art. Discuss whether reading the dialect aloud honors or risks caricaturing it — and let the room decide how to read it (see Anticipated Difficulties). The point: dialect can be power, not only constraint.

**5. "Frederick Douglass" — "And Ethiopia, with bosom torn, / Laments the passing of her noblest born."** What to bring out: the high formal diction (the opposite register from "Malindy") and the elegiac convention of the nation in mourning. Note how Dunbar places Douglass in a storm — the political darkness of the 1890s — making the elegy also a protest against the present. Tie back explicitly to Session 1: this is the poet of the tradition mourning its founder.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **In "Sympathy," why does Dunbar withhold the word "sings" until the last stanza?**
   *Model answer:* The delay forces us to experience the bird's longing and bloody struggle before we reach its song, so that when the song arrives we already know it is not joyful. The structure trains us to hear the singing correctly — as a prayer wrung from pain, not a carol of contentment.

2. **The poem insists the song is "a prayer," not "a carol of joy." Why does Dunbar feel he must correct the reader inside the poem?**
   *Model answer:* Because he knew his audience was prone to mishear Black expression as cheerful or contented. The self-correction reflects his lifelong awareness that white readers might take the surface for the truth, so he names the truth explicitly rather than trust them to find it.

3. **Why does Dunbar give "The Haunted Oak" to the tree rather than to the victim or a human witness?**
   *Model answer:* The tree is an innocent, unwilling participant whose testimony cannot be dismissed as bias, and voicing it spares Dunbar the impossible task of speaking as the murdered man. The device also implicates nature and place in the crime, suggesting the violence poisons the whole land.

4. **What is the significance of the judge and the doctor among the masked mob?**
   *Model answer:* Their presence shows that lynching was not the work of social outcasts but of the community's respected leaders. Dunbar indicts respectable society itself, exposing the gap between Jim Crow's pretense of law and order and its actual savagery.

5. **How does "When Malindy Sings" use dialect differently than the plantation tradition expected?**
   *Model answer:* Instead of rendering Black speech for white amusement, the poem uses the vernacular to assert that untrained Black artistry surpasses cultivated white performance. The dialect becomes a vehicle of pride and a quiet inversion of the racial hierarchy, not a comic turn.

6. **Is dialect in Dunbar a limitation or a form of power? Can it be both?**
   *Model answer:* It is both, depending on the poem and the reader. Marketed into the plantation tradition it was a cage; deployed in "When Malindy Sings" it is a source of authentic power and cultural pride. Dunbar lived inside that doubleness rather than resolving it.

7. **How does the elegy "Frederick Douglass" connect to where we began this course?**
   *Model answer:* It mourns the very man whose narrative opened the course, closing a circle from witness to poet. It also reads Douglass's death as a loss arriving precisely when the political storm of the 1890s most demanded his voice, making the elegy a protest as well as a tribute.

8. **What do these four poems together tell us about the "trap" of Dunbar's career?**
   *Model answer:* They show a poet equally capable of high formal elegy, searing protest, and affirming vernacular, yet remembered mainly for charming dialect. The range proves how badly the marketplace narrowed him, and why he resented being fixed as a "dialect poet."

9. **Maya Angelou borrowed "I know why the caged bird sings" for her memoir. What does that inheritance suggest?**
   *Model answer:* It suggests Dunbar's image was potent and durable enough to anchor a major work two generations later, and that the tradition carries its own lines forward. The borrowing also reframes Dunbar as a foundational voice rather than a dated one.

10. **Why were later Black writers sometimes embarrassed by Dunbar, and is the embarrassment fair?**
    *Model answer:* The dialect verse's association with minstrelsy and the plantation tradition made some Harlem Renaissance figures uneasy. The embarrassment is understandable but not entirely fair, since it overlooks both the subversive uses of dialect and the power of his formal lyrics — a fuller reading restores him.

### Anticipated Difficulties

This is the session where the course's hardest classroom problems converge, so prepare deliberately. **Dialect:** decide in advance how "When Malindy Sings" will be read aloud. Reading dialect can feel to a white reader like risking caricature; one good option is to invite a volunteer, to read it yourself plainly and without performance, or to read it silently and discuss. Name the dilemma openly and let the room choose — the honesty defuses the discomfort. **Lynching:** "The Haunted Oak" depicts a lynching, and you should flag it before reading, let silence follow, and not rush to "move on." **Racial vocabulary:** these public-domain texts and their period contexts contain the era's language; reaffirm the practice you set in Session 3 — period usage is read as period usage, never imported into our own speech. **White students discussing Black suffering:** reassure the room that careful, humble reading is exactly the work, that no one need perform guilt, and that the question on the table is always what the poem is *doing*. When in doubt, slow down and breathe.

## Session 5 — The Harlem Renaissance I: Langston Hughes & Claude McKay

### At a Glance
The Great Migration carried hundreds of thousands of Black Americans out of the Jim Crow South into the cities of the North, and in the 1920s Harlem became a world capital of Black art, music, and letters. This session opens the Renaissance with two of its most electric and politically charged voices: Langston Hughes, who fused the blues and the spoken vernacular into a new American poetry and claimed America in Whitman's own breath, and Claude McKay, the Jamaican-born radical whose sonnets turned a European form into a weapon. We deliberately set aside Hughes's most-anthologized pieces in favor of the deeper, angrier, more formally adventurous work. The goal is to hear the Renaissance not as a polite cultural flowering but as a body of writing that argued, mourned, danced, and demanded.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Langston Hughes (1901–1967).** James Mercer Langston Hughes was born in Joplin, Missouri, in 1901, and raised largely by his grandmother in Lawrence, Kansas, after his parents separated ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Langston Hughes"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Langston-Hughes); [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/langston-hughes)). His grandmother's first husband had died in John Brown's raid at Harpers Ferry, linking the boy to the long history of Black resistance. He began writing poetry in high school in Cleveland, and at seventeen, on a train crossing the Mississippi to visit his estranged father in Mexico, wrote "The Negro Speaks of Rivers," which Du Bois published in *The Crisis* in 1921. Hughes briefly attended Columbia, then worked as a mess boy on a freighter to Africa and as a cook and busboy in Paris and Washington, all the while writing. His debut collection, *The Weary Blues* (1926), announced a poet determined to bring the rhythms of jazz and the blues — the actual music of Black working life — into literature, against the wishes of those who thought such material undignified. His 1926 essay "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain" became the Renaissance's aesthetic manifesto: a declaration that Black artists would write out of their own experience without apology or shame. Across a long career he wrote poetry, fiction, plays, essays, autobiography, children's books, and newspaper columns featuring the everyman character Jesse B. Semple ("Simple"). His politics were radical, especially in the 1930s, and he was later called before Senator McCarthy's committee. He became the most widely beloved Black poet in American history, the "poet laureate of Harlem," and died in New York in 1967. Stress to students that Hughes deliberately wrote *for* and *about* ordinary Black people in their own idiom — a democratic, vernacular project that some Black critics of his own day found insufficiently genteel.

**Claude McKay (1889–1948).** Festus Claudius McKay was born in 1889 in rural Jamaica, then a British colony, and grew up among a proud, landowning peasantry ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Claude McKay"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Claude-McKay); [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/claude-mckay)). He published two books of dialect verse in Jamaica before emigrating to the United States in 1912, where the ferocity of American racism — far harsher and more codified than what he had known — shocked and radicalized him. He studied briefly at Tuskegee and Kansas State, then moved to New York, working menial jobs and writing. McKay became a committed political radical, associated with socialism and communism; he traveled to the Soviet Union, lived in Europe and North Africa for years, and was in many ways an internationalist rather than strictly an American writer. His landmark poems appeared just before and at the dawn of the Renaissance: "If We Must Die," written in response to the anti-Black violence of the 1919 "Red Summer," and the poems of *Harlem Shadows* (1922), often considered the book that opened the Harlem Renaissance. McKay's signature was the paradox of his form: he poured rage, defiance, and exile into the strict, "respectable," European sonnet, weaponizing the master's form. He also wrote fiction, including the bestselling novel *Home to Harlem* (1928). Late in life he converted to Catholicism and his radicalism cooled. He died in Chicago in 1948. For students, McKay is the course's first clearly *diasporic* voice — a Caribbean immigrant who became central to African American letters while never quite belonging fully to any single national tradition, and whose work holds love and rage for America in the same breath.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The Harlem Renaissance (roughly 1918–1937) was made possible by the Great Migration: between about 1916 and 1970, some six million Black Americans left the rural South for the urban North and West, fleeing Jim Crow, lynching, and the collapse of Southern agricultural economies, and seeking industrial work and relative freedom. Harlem, a neighborhood of Manhattan, became the symbolic capital of this transformed Black America — densely populated, culturally electric, home to writers, musicians, painters, and intellectuals. The movement was named and theorized in part by Alain Locke, whose 1925 anthology *The New Negro* gave it a banner: the "New Negro" was assertive, modern, race-proud, and unwilling to accept the old subordinations.

Students must understand the central tension of the Renaissance, because it is the politics-of-audience thread in its sharpest form. Much of the movement's funding, publication, and publicity flowed through white patrons, publishers, and curiosity-seekers who came uptown to consume Black culture — the so-called "Negro vogue." This created a genuine dilemma: Black artists gained unprecedented platforms, but often before a white audience with its own appetites and stereotypes. Hughes's "racial mountain" essay is a direct response to this bind, insisting on the right to depict Black life truthfully, including its working-class and blues-saturated forms, rather than producing a sanitized, respectable image for white approval or Black middle-class comfort.

The period was also intensely political. The "Red Summer" of 1919 saw white mobs attack Black communities in dozens of cities; McKay's "If We Must Die" responds directly to that terror. The 1920s coincided with the rise of Marcus Garvey's mass Black-nationalist movement and with a surge of radical and socialist politics among Black intellectuals, McKay among them. By the time the Great Depression arrived in 1929, the white patronage that had fueled the "vogue" dried up, and the Renaissance as a discrete movement faded — but it had permanently changed American literature, establishing Black writing at the center of the national culture and proving, against the whole weight of the canon, that the vernacular of Black America was the stuff of major art.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Hughes, "The Negro Speaks of Rivers" (1921).** A short free-verse poem in which the speaker claims ancient kinship with the great rivers of human civilization: "I've known rivers ancient as the world." He has bathed in the Euphrates at dawn, built his hut near the Congo, looked upon the Nile and raised the pyramids, and heard the Mississippi's singing when Abraham Lincoln went to New Orleans. The refrain — "My soul has grown deep like the rivers" — links the Black self to the deep time of human history and to the long memory of a people. Written when Hughes was seventeen.

**Hughes, "The Weary Blues" (1925).** The poem describes a Black pianist playing the blues in a Harlem club on Lenox Avenue late into the night. Hughes embeds the actual lyrics of the man's song ("I got the Weary Blues / And I can't be satisfied") inside the poem, fusing literary verse with the blues form. The musician pours his sorrow into the music, then goes home and sleeps "like a rock or a man that's dead." The poem enacts the blues as both lament and survival.

**Hughes, "Let America Be America Again" (1936).** A long poem in which the speaker invokes the American dream of liberty and equality — and then, in parenthetical asides, confesses that America "never was America to me." He catalogues those for whom the dream failed: the poor white, the Negro, the Native American, the immigrant, the exploited worker. The bitter refrain insists "America never was America to me," yet the poem turns at the end toward a fierce hope that America *must* and *will* yet be made into the land it promised: "America will be!"

**Hughes, "I look at the world" (written 1930, published 2009).** A brief poem in which a Black speaker looks at the world "from awakening eyes in a black face," sees the "fenced-off narrow space / Assigned to me," recognizes the "silly walls / oppression builds," and declares they "will have to go." He then looks at his own body and hands and resolves that those hands "can make / The world that's in my mind," calling on "comrades" to find the road forward. A compact poem of awakening and resolve.

**McKay, "If We Must Die" (1919).** A Shakespearean sonnet exhorting the oppressed not to die "like hogs / Hunted and penned" but to meet death "nobly," fighting back so that "the monsters we defy" must respect even their dying. The closing couplet: "Like men we'll face the murderous, cowardly pack, / Pressed to the wall, dying, but fighting back!"

**McKay, "America" (1921).** A sonnet that holds love and hatred together: America "feeds me bread of bitterness" and "sinks into my throat her tiger's tooth," yet the speaker confesses "I love this cultured hell that tests my youth." He stands within her walls without "terror, malice, not a word of jeer," yet foresees, "darkly," her "might and granite wonders" sinking in time like "priceless treasures sinking in the sand."

**McKay, "The Tropics in New York" (1922).** The speaker sees tropical fruit — bananas, ginger-root, tangerines — displayed in a New York shop window, and is overcome by memory of his Jamaican home: "hungry for the old, familiar ways," his eyes grow dim, and he "turned aside and bowed my head and wept." A compact lyric of immigrant homesickness and exile.

### Lecture Script

Tonight Harlem. Tonight the 1920s — the decade when Black America moved north in its millions and made, in a few square miles of upper Manhattan, one of the great explosions of art in this country's history. We call it the Harlem Renaissance, and over the next two weeks we'll meet its writers. But I want to warn you against a gentle misunderstanding of that word "renaissance." This was not a polite tea party of culture. It was loud, political, sometimes furious, set to the sound of jazz and the blues — and it was happening in a country that, in the same years, was still lynching Black men. Hold both of those at once. [pause]

Two poets tonight. The first you may think you know: Langston Hughes. He's beloved, he's on the school walls, and precisely because some of his poems are so famous, I've chosen four that are a little deeper, a little angrier, a little stranger. Here's something to know about him. He wrote his first great poem at seventeen, on a train crossing the Mississippi, and it begins "I've known rivers." A teenager, claiming kinship with the Euphrates and the Nile and the Congo and the Mississippi — claiming that the Black soul is as old and deep as human civilization itself. That's "The Negro Speaks of Rivers," and we'll start there. [pause]

Then I want you to hear what Hughes did that was genuinely new. He took the blues — the actual music of poor Black America, the music some respectable people thought was beneath literature — and he put it on the page. "The Weary Blues" has a man playing piano in a Harlem club, and Hughes writes the man's song right into the poem. He's saying: this music *is* poetry. This is one of the bravest artistic decisions of the century. [pause]

And then the political Hughes. In "Let America Be America Again," he does something I find almost unbearable in its honesty. He recites the American dream — land of liberty, land of opportunity — and then, in whispered asides in parentheses, he says: it never was America to me. And he names everyone the dream failed: the Black man, the poor white, the Native, the immigrant. And yet — and this is the thing — he does not end in despair. He ends by insisting that America *will* be, someday, the thing it promised. He's arguing with his country. He's making a claim on it. That's our thread all term: the long argument with the American story. [pause]

Our second poet came from somewhere else entirely. Claude McKay was born in Jamaica, under British rule, and when he came to America in 1912 the sheer brutality of American racism shocked him — and radicalized him. And here's what makes McKay extraordinary as a craftsman. When he wanted to write his most defiant poem — "If We Must Die," written after white mobs attacked Black neighborhoods in 1919 — he chose the sonnet. The most refined, most European, most "respectable" form there is. A Black man in 1919 writing about dying with dignity, in the form of Shakespeare. Why? We'll talk about it. [pause]

And notice, in his poem "America," that he does not simply hate this country. He writes, "I love this cultured hell that tests my youth." Love and rage, in a single line. McKay is also our first immigrant voice — listen, in "The Tropics in New York," to a grown man brought to tears by fruit in a shop window because it smells of the Jamaica he lost. Let's read. Begin with the rivers.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. Hughes, "The Negro Speaks of Rivers" — "My soul has grown deep like the rivers."** What to bring out: the poem claims deep time. By placing the Black speaker at the Euphrates, the Congo, the Nile, and the Mississippi, Hughes locates Black identity at the origins of civilization, not at the margins of American history. Note the single American river — the Mississippi, "when Abe Lincoln went down to New Orleans" — quietly folding emancipation into the ancient sequence. The repetition and the long lines give the poem the slow weight of a river itself.

**2. Hughes, "The Weary Blues" — the embedded song lyric.** What to notice: Hughes literally sets blues lyrics ("I got the Weary Blues / And I can't be satisfied") inside the literary poem, collapsing the boundary between "high" art and the music of the Black working class. The frame (the poet listening) and the song (the musician's voice) create two registers. Draw out the ending — the man sleeps "like a rock or a man that's dead" — and ask whether the blues here is despair, release, or survival.

**3. Hughes, "Let America Be America Again" — the parenthetical asides.** What to bring out: the poem's structure stages a debate within itself. The public voice recites the patriotic dream; the parenthetical voice ("America never was America to me") corrects it from below. This is double consciousness rendered as poetic form. Note the final turn toward "America will be!" — not rejection of the national promise but a demand that it be honored.

**4. McKay, "If We Must Die" — the sonnet form.** What to notice: the strict Shakespearean sonnet (three quatrains and a couplet) channels fury into discipline. The choice is the meaning: by mastering the "respectable" European form, McKay refuses the racist premise that Black expression is formless or primitive, and he dignifies the call to resist. The famous couplet — "dying, but fighting back!" — lands with the full force of the form's closure. (Note: the poem is universal enough that Winston Churchill is said to have quoted it during WWII — a striking afterlife worth mentioning.)

**5. McKay, "America" — "I love this cultured hell that tests my youth."** What to bring out: the oxymoron ("cultured hell") and the unresolved holding of love and rage. The poem neither rejects America nor forgives it; it stands "within her walls" undaunted while foreseeing her eventual decline "like priceless treasures sinking in the sand." Ask whether the contradiction is a failure of resolve or the truest possible response to a country that both nourishes and wounds.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **"The Negro Speaks of Rivers" claims kinship with ancient rivers. What is Hughes claiming through them, and why does it matter that he wrote it at seventeen?**
   *Model answer:* He claims that Black identity is as ancient and deep as human civilization itself, rooted in the Euphrates, Nile, and Congo as much as the Mississippi. That a seventeen-year-old reached for such vast historical authority shows both precocious craft and the urgency of asserting Black dignity against a culture that denied Black people any deep past.

2. **Why did Hughes put actual blues lyrics inside "The Weary Blues"? What did that decision risk and achieve?**
   *Model answer:* He insisted that the blues — the music of poor Black America — was itself major art worthy of literature. It risked the disapproval of respectable readers who thought such material undignified, but it achieved a genuinely new American poetry rooted in living Black culture rather than borrowed European models.

3. **How does the structure of "Let America Be America Again" — public lines and parenthetical asides — create its meaning?**
   *Model answer:* The public voice recites the patriotic dream while the parenthetical voice undercuts it with the truth that the dream excluded many. The two-track form enacts double consciousness and stages an argument within the poem, so that patriotism and indictment coexist on the page.

4. **Does "Let America Be America Again" reject America or lay claim to it?**
   *Model answer:* It lays claim to it. Hughes exposes how the country betrayed its promise, but ends insisting "America will be" — demanding that the nation become what it said it was. The poem is an argument for inclusion, not a renunciation.

5. **Why might a Black poet in 1919 choose the sonnet — the most "respectable" European form — for "If We Must Die"?**
   *Model answer:* The form's discipline dignifies the call to resist and refutes the racist assumption that Black expression is primitive or formless. Mastering the master's form is itself an act of defiance, and the sonnet's tight structure concentrates the poem's fury into an unforgettable closing blow.

6. **In "America," is the contradiction between love and rage resolved? Should it be?**
   *Model answer:* It is not resolved, and arguably should not be. McKay's refusal to choose captures the honest condition of a person nourished and wounded by the same country. The unresolved tension is more truthful than any neat reconciliation.

7. **"I look at the world" moves from seeing to making. Trace that movement.**
   *Model answer:* The speaker first sees the "fenced-off narrow space" assigned to him, then judges the "silly walls" oppression builds and declares they must fall, then looks at his own hands and resolves to build the world in his mind. The poem advances from perception to verdict to action, ending in collective motion with "comrades."

8. **McKay is the course's first clearly diasporic and immigrant voice. How does that shape "The Tropics in New York"?**
   *Model answer:* The poem renders the immigrant's split consciousness — physically in New York, emotionally in lost Jamaica — through the sensory trigger of tropical fruit. The grown man's tears dramatize exile as an ongoing condition, not a single departure, and connect McKay to the later immigrant writers in the course.

9. **What was the "Negro vogue," and how does it complicate our reading of Renaissance poetry?**
   *Model answer:* It was the 1920s white fascination with Black culture that brought patronage and platforms but also a consuming, often stereotyping gaze. It means these poems were often produced for and mediated by a white audience, making the politics of audience inseparable from the work's form and content.

10. **Hughes and McKay were contemporaries with very different temperaments. What do we gain by reading them together?**
    *Model answer:* Hughes gives the vernacular, musical, democratic strain of the Renaissance; McKay gives the formally classical, internationalist, openly enraged strain. Together they show the movement's range and prevent us from reducing the Renaissance to a single mood or style.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty here is the gap between residents' likely image of Hughes — gentle, inspirational, school-poster Hughes — and the angrier, more radical poet on the page. Use that gap: it is genuinely surprising, and the surprise teaches. "Let America Be America Again" and "I look at the world" carry explicit class and even socialist politics; present them as part of the historical record without turning the room into a referendum on present-day politics. The word "Negro" appears in titles and texts as the dignified period term; reaffirm the practice from earlier sessions. "If We Must Die" treats violent resistance; let students sit with the moral complexity rather than resolving it quickly. Finally, the blues lyrics in "The Weary Blues" and the dialect inflections invite the same care about reading aloud that you established with Dunbar — offer the room a choice, and read without performance.

## Session 6 — The Harlem Renaissance II: Zora Neale Hurston & Jean Toomer

### At a Glance
The other interior of the Renaissance: not the manifestos and the politics, but the experimental, joyful, and formally daring writing that trusted Black Southern life and language as the wellspring of art. Zora Neale Hurston — anthropologist, novelist, essayist, and one of the great prose stylists in American literature — wrote a personal essay so exuberant and so contrarian that it scandalized some of her peers. Jean Toomer's *Cane* shattered the boundaries between poetry, prose, and drama to capture a vanishing rural South. The session asks what it meant to write Black life *for its own sake*, in its own idiom, and to refuse the obligation to be either tragic or uplifting. The goal is to show students the Renaissance's range — and to introduce two writers whose later silences are themselves part of the story.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Zora Neale Hurston (1891–1960).** Hurston was born in 1891 in Notasulga, Alabama, but raised in Eatonville, Florida — one of the first incorporated all-Black towns in the United States, a fact central to her entire sensibility ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Zora Neale Hurston"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zora-Neale-Hurston); [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/zora-neale-hurston)). (She often gave her birth year as 1901 or later, shaving a decade off her age, a small self-invention worth noting.) Growing up in a town governed entirely by Black people, where she never absorbed the assumption of Black inferiority, shaped the confident, unbowed voice that distinguishes her from nearly every contemporary. After her mother's death and a scattered, interrupted youth, she made her way to Howard University and then to Barnard College, where she studied anthropology under Franz Boas, the founder of American cultural anthropology. This training is essential: Hurston went back into the rural South and the Caribbean as a trained folklorist, collecting the stories, songs, sermons, and speech of Black communities, and her literary work is saturated with that fieldwork. She published the essay "How It Feels to Be Colored Me" (1928), the folklore collection *Mules and Men* (1935), and her masterpiece, the novel *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937), written in a few weeks in Haiti. Hurston was a vivid, controversial figure — politically idiosyncratic, often at odds with male contemporaries like Richard Wright, who attacked *Their Eyes* for not being a protest novel. Her insistence on depicting Black life as full, joyful, and self-sufficient rather than primarily as a reaction to white oppression put her at odds with the era's dominant protest aesthetic. She fell out of fashion, worked her last years as a maid, and died in poverty in Florida in 1960, buried in an unmarked grave. In 1973 the novelist Alice Walker located that grave, placed a marker on it ("A Genius of the South"), and wrote the essay that launched Hurston's recovery. She is now read as a foundational figure. Her case is the course's clearest single example of "the recovery of voices the canon tried not to hear."

**Jean Toomer (1894–1967).** Nathan Eugene "Jean" Toomer was born in 1894 in Washington, D.C., into a prominent mixed-race family; his grandfather, P.B.S. Pinchback, had been a Reconstruction-era governor of Louisiana ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Jean Toomer"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Toomer); [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/jean-toomer)). Light-skinned and ambivalent about racial categories his whole life, Toomer resisted being classified as a "Negro writer" and at times declined to identify with any single race, calling himself simply "American" — a stance that makes him a complicated figure in a course organized around minority voices, and worth presenting honestly as such. In 1921 he spent a few months as the head of a Black school in rural Sparta, Georgia, and the encounter with the Deep South — its beauty, its violence, its spirituals, its vanishing folk culture — overwhelmed him and produced *Cane* (1923), a genre-defying book of interwoven sketches, stories, poems, and a closing drama. *Cane* is now regarded as one of the masterpieces of American modernism and a cornerstone of the Renaissance, though it sold poorly at the time. After it, Toomer largely withdrew from Black literary life, became a follower of the mystic Gurdjieff, married white women in an era when that was scandalous, and spent decades pursuing spiritual rather than literary aims. He wrote a great deal more but published little of lasting note; *Cane* remains his monument. He died in 1967. For students, Toomer poses a productive problem: a writer who produced a central text of Black modernism and then spent his life resisting the racial identity that text is read through. His ambivalence is not a flaw to be excused but a subject to be examined — a real instance of the course's question about whether and how literature should be sorted by the author's race.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Hurston and Toomer write inside the same Harlem Renaissance as Hughes and McKay, but they point its energy in a different direction: toward the rural South and the folk culture the Great Migration was leaving behind. This is a crucial counter-current. While the Renaissance was an urban, Northern phenomenon, much of its deepest material came from the South — the spirituals, the blues, the folktales, the church, the speech — that migrants carried north in memory. Both writers were, in different ways, salvage artists, preserving a folk culture they sensed was vanishing under modernity and migration. Hurston's anthropological training made this explicit; Toomer's *Cane* is shot through with elegy for a Georgia he experienced as already passing.

The intellectual debate that frames this session is the argument over what Black art was *for*. One camp, associated with Du Bois and later with Richard Wright's protest fiction, held that Black literature should advance the race's cause and answer white racism, presenting dignified or politically useful images. Another camp — Hurston is its boldest exponent — insisted that Black life was worth depicting in its fullness, including its joy, humor, sensuality, and ordinary self-sufficiency, without constant reference to white people or white oppression. "How It Feels to Be Colored Me" is a deliberate provocation in this debate: Hurston refuses the posture of the tragic victim ("I am not tragically colored") at a moment when that refusal could be read as a betrayal of the cause. Students should grasp that her exuberance was a *political* and aesthetic position, not naïveté — a claim about the right of Black writers to depict Black life on its own terms, which is the politics-of-audience thread in yet another form.

Finally, both writers were experimenting with modernist form at the same moment as Eliot, Pound, and Stein. *Cane*'s fragmentation, its mixing of genres, its impressionism, place it squarely within international modernism while drawing on distinctly African American materials (spirituals, the rural Southern landscape, the trauma of lynching). To teach Hurston and Toomer is to show students that Black writers were not following modernism from a distance but helping to make it.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Hurston, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me" (1928).** A short autobiographical essay. Hurston recalls a happy childhood in all-Black Eatonville, Florida, where she greeted passing white tourists from her porch and felt herself simply "everybody's Zora," not yet "colored." She "became colored" at thirteen, when she was sent to school in Jacksonville and was made aware of race. She rejects self-pity: "I am not tragically colored. There is no great sorrow dammed up in my soul," and declares she does not "belong to the sobbing school of Negrohood." In a famous passage she sits in a jazz club, the New World Cabaret, and feels the music return her to a primal, ecstatic self while her white companion remains unmoved — then she "creep[s] back slowly to the veneer we call civilization." She closes with the image of all people as "brown bags" of miscellaneous contents leaning against a wall — different on the outside, jumbled and similar within.

**Toomer, *Cane* (1923), "Blood-Burning Moon."** A short story in three sections set in rural Georgia. Louisa, a young Black woman, is involved with two men: Tom Burwell, a Black field worker who loves her, and Bob Stone, the white son of the family Louisa works for, who comes to her secretly. A full red moon rises ominously over the factory and the town; the workers sing uneasily. Bob Stone, jealous and possessive, goes looking for Louisa; he and Tom confront each other, and in the fight Tom kills Bob with a knife. A white mob forms, hunts Tom down, and burns him alive at the stake in an old factory. The story ends with Louisa, dazed, the full "blood-burning moon" hanging over the horror. The recurring song refrain ("Red nigger moon. Sinner!") threads through the sections; the lynching is rendered with terrible lyric compression.

### Lecture Script

Last week the Renaissance argued and demanded and set itself to jazz. Tonight we meet its other side — two writers who pointed all that energy not at the city and the protest but at the rural South, at the folk culture that the great migration north was leaving behind. And we meet two of the most interesting personalities in all of American literature. [pause]

Zora Neale Hurston grew up in Eatonville, Florida — one of the first all-Black towns in America. Think about what that means. She grew up in a place run entirely by Black people: the mayor, the storekeeper, the preacher, all Black. And so she never absorbed, the way so many did, the lie of her own inferiority. She sat on her porch as a girl and watched white tourists drive through and thought of them as the passing show — *she* was the one at home. That confidence is the key to everything she wrote. [pause]

She trained as an anthropologist — a real one, under the great Franz Boas at Barnard — and she went back into the South and the Caribbean and collected the stories and songs and sermons of Black folk life, and she poured all of it into her writing. And in 1928 she published a little essay that scandalized some of her peers. It's called "How It Feels to Be Colored Me," and in it she writes: "I am not tragically colored. There is no great sorrow dammed up in my soul." [pause] Now — in 1928, with everything we have already studied in this course, you can imagine how that landed. Some of her fellow Black intellectuals thought it was a betrayal. How dare she not be sorrowful? But I want you to read it more carefully than that. Hurston is making a claim — a fierce one — about the right to depict Black life as full, and joyful, and complete in itself, and not only as a wound made by white people. That is a political act disguised as a happy essay. [pause]

I have to tell you how her story ends, because it is the clearest example in this whole course of something we keep returning to — the way the canon dropped these writers and had to be made to pick them up again. Hurston went out of fashion. She died in 1960 in poverty, working as a maid, and was buried in an unmarked grave. And in 1973, a young writer named Alice Walker went down to Florida, found that grave in the weeds, and put a stone on it that reads "A Genius of the South." And then she wrote an essay that brought Hurston back to the world. She had to be *recovered*. Hold that. [pause]

Our second writer is stranger and harder to place. Jean Toomer wrote one extraordinary book — *Cane*, in 1923 — and then spent the rest of his life refusing to be called a Negro writer at all. He was light-skinned, of mixed race, and he insisted he was simply "American." I'm not going to smooth that over for you; it's a real complication, and we'll talk about it honestly. But the book he made out of a few months teaching in rural Georgia is one of the masterpieces of American modernism — poetry and prose and even a play, all mixed together, trying to catch the soul of a South that was already disappearing. [pause]

The story we read, "Blood-Burning Moon," is beautiful and it is brutal — it ends in a lynching, rendered in some of the most compressed, lyric prose you'll ever read. I'll say more before we read it. For now, open to Hurston. Let's start with her joy, because we will need it. Then we'll go down to Georgia under that red moon.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. Hurston, "I am not tragically colored. There is no great sorrow dammed up in my soul."** What to bring out: the deliberate provocation. Hurston refuses the role of victim at a moment when refusing it was controversial. Note the wit and swagger of the prose. Draw out that this is a *position* in the era's debate over Black art, not mere personal cheerfulness — a claim about who Black writing is for.

**2. Hurston, "I remember the very day that I became colored."** What to notice: race here is something that *happens* to her at thirteen, upon leaving Eatonville — not a fact of birth but an event of social positioning. This is double consciousness given a precise autobiographical date. Discuss how the all-Black town delayed and reshaped her racial self-awareness.

**3. Hurston, the jazz club: "I dance wildly inside myself... Then I creep back slowly to the veneer we call civilization."** What to bring out: the passage stages a temporary escape from imposed identity into ecstatic feeling, then a deliberate return to "veneer." The white companion sits unmoved — Hurston pointedly reverses the usual hierarchy, depicting white restraint as a limitation. Ask what "veneer" implies about the pressures of assimilation.

**4. Toomer, the recurring song — "Red nigger moon. Sinner!"** Here the racial slur appears inside a folk refrain Toomer threads through the story. What to bring out before reading: the word is part of a chanted song that tolls like a bell of doom over the action; Toomer uses the community's own idiom and the era's brutal language to summon dread. Decide your reading practice in advance (see Anticipated Difficulties). Analytically, the refrain binds the three sections and turns the moon into an omen of the violence to come.

**5. Toomer, the lynching of Tom Burwell.** What to notice: Toomer renders the burning with terrible compression and lyric distance — beauty and atrocity in the same sentences. What to draw out: the modernist technique (fragmentation, imagery, refrain) does not soften the horror but estranges it, forcing the reader to feel it freshly. This is the session's hardest passage; let silence follow it.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Hurston writes "I am not tragically colored." Is this defiance, denial, or something more complicated?**
   *Model answer:* It is primarily defiance — a deliberate refusal of the victim role and an assertion that Black life is full and joyful in itself. Read in context, it is a position in the debate over Black art, claiming the right to depict that life on its own terms rather than only as a response to white oppression. Whether it underplays real suffering is a fair question, but it is far from simple denial.

2. **What is the significance of Hurston having grown up in all-Black Eatonville?**
   *Model answer:* It meant she absorbed no assumption of Black inferiority and saw Black people running every institution, which produced her unusual confidence and her sense that race was something that happened *to* her later. The town is the source of the self-possessed voice that sets her apart from her contemporaries.

3. **Analyze the jazz-club passage. What is Hurston saying about Black expression and the pressure to assimilate?**
   *Model answer:* She presents music as access to a deep, ecstatic self that "civilization" — the white-coded "veneer" — normally suppresses. By depicting her white companion as unmoved, she reverses the racial hierarchy, framing white restraint as impoverishment. The passage suggests assimilation costs something vital.

4. **How does *Cane* mix genres, and what does the mixing achieve in "Blood-Burning Moon"?**
   *Model answer:* Toomer fuses lyric prose, poetic imagery, and the recurring folk song into a fragmented, impressionistic whole. In "Blood-Burning Moon," that technique builds dread through the chanted refrain and renders the lynching with an estranging beauty that makes the horror land harder rather than softer.

5. **What is the function of the recurring moon and its song refrain?**
   *Model answer:* The blood-red moon and the tolling refrain bind the three sections and turn the natural world into an omen, foretelling violence. The song is the community's own voice, lending the doom a folk-ritual inevitability and embedding the story in a living oral culture.

6. **Hurston and Toomer wrote in the same movement but in opposite registers. What does that range tell us about the Renaissance?**
   *Model answer:* It shows the Renaissance was not a single style or mood but a wide field — Hurston exuberant, first-person, and affirming; Toomer dark, fragmented, and elegiac. The contrast guards against reducing the movement to either protest or celebration, revealing its genuine artistic diversity.

7. **Why was Hurston forgotten, and what does her recovery by Alice Walker tell us about literary history?**
   *Model answer:* Her refusal of the protest aesthetic, conflicts with powerful male contemporaries, and the general neglect of Black women writers pushed her out of print and into poverty. Walker's recovery shows that the canon is curated and contingent, and that major artists can be lost and found according to shifting values rather than merit.

8. **Toomer resisted being called a "Negro writer." How should we hold that fact while reading *Cane* in this course?**
   *Model answer:* Honestly and without resolving it. His ambivalence is a real instance of the course's question about sorting literature by race, and *Cane* is plainly rooted in Black Southern material even as its author declined the label. We can read the book as central to Black modernism while taking his resistance seriously as a subject rather than a verdict.

9. **How do both writers function as "salvage artists" of a vanishing folk culture?**
   *Model answer:* Both preserve the spirituals, speech, and rural Southern life that the Great Migration was leaving behind — Hurston through trained folklore collection, Toomer through elegiac modernist sketches. Their work is partly an act of cultural memory, holding onto a world they sensed was disappearing.

10. **In what sense are Hurston and Toomer modernists, not just Renaissance figures?**
    *Model answer:* They experimented with form at the same moment as Eliot, Pound, and Stein — Hurston with vernacular and voice, Toomer with fragmentation and genre-mixing — while drawing on African American materials. They were not imitating modernism from the margins but helping to invent it.

### Anticipated Difficulties

This session pairs Hurston's joy with Toomer's lynching, and the tonal whiplash is intentional — but prepare the room for it. Flag the violence of "Blood-Burning Moon" before reading. The hardest specific problem is the racial slur embedded in Toomer's folk-song refrain: decide in advance whether you will voice it, signal it, or have the room read silently, and tell them your practice and why the word is on the page (it is the community's own brutal idiom, tolling like a bell of doom). Some students may find Hurston's cheerfulness suspect or even irritating given what they have read in earlier weeks; treat that reaction as a discussion question rather than a problem, since Hurston *meant* to provoke it. Toomer's racial ambivalence may unsettle a room expecting clear identity politics; hold it open rather than explaining it away. After the lynching passage, let silence sit.

## Session 7 — Native American Voices I: Sarah Winnemucca & Zitkala-Ša

### At a Glance
Two Native women, a generation apart, who learned the colonizer's language and turned it back on the colonizer — using English to bear witness against what was being done to their peoples. Sarah Winnemucca wrote the first known autobiography by a Native American woman, an activist's plea on behalf of the Northern Paiutes; Zitkala-Ša wrote one of the earliest first-person accounts, from the inside, of the federal Indian boarding school system designed to erase Native identity. The session opens the course's attention to a literature of survival, dispossession, and cultural translation — and to the agonizing paradox of writing one's resistance in the language of those who sought one's destruction. The goal is to read these women as strategic authors and witnesses, not as ethnographic specimens.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Sarah Winnemucca (Thocmetony) (c. 1844–1891).** Sarah Winnemucca was born around 1844 near Humboldt Lake in present-day Nevada, a member of the Northern Paiute people; her name in Paiute, Thocmetony, means "shell flower" ([*Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Sarah Winnemucca"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sarah-Winnemucca); [National Women's History Museum](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/sarah-winnemucca)). She was the granddaughter of a Paiute leader and daughter of a chief, and she came of age precisely as white settlement overran Paiute lands in the 1840s and 1850s. Multilingual — she learned English, Spanish, and several Native languages — she became an interpreter and cultural intermediary between her people and the U.S. Army and Indian agents, a role that placed her in the impossible middle ground of the colonized go-between. She witnessed the broken treaties, the corrupt Indian agents who stole the rations meant for her people, the forced removals, and the violence of the era's "Indian wars." Convinced that the Paiutes' survival depended on appealing directly to white Americans of conscience, she became a public lecturer, touring Eastern cities to plead the Paiute cause, and in 1883 published *Life Among the Piutes: Their Wrongs and Claims* — the first known autobiography written in English by a Native American woman. The book is at once memoir, ethnography of Paiute life and customs, and political indictment. She gathered thousands of signatures on a petition to Congress; some reforms were promised and largely betrayed. Disillusioned, exhausted, and attacked by enemies who smeared her character, she eventually returned west and founded a school for Native children that taught in their own language, before dying in 1891. Present her to students as a pioneering activist-author working the genre of witness under brutal constraints — writing to persuade the very public whose government was destroying her people.

**Zitkala-Ša (Gertrude Simmons Bonnin) (1876–1938).** Zitkala-Ša — the name means "Red Bird" in Lakota — was born in 1876 on the Yankton Indian Reservation in South Dakota, a member of the Yankton Dakota (Sioux); her later Anglicized and married name was Gertrude Simmons Bonnin ([National Park Service, "Zitkala-Ša"](https://www.nps.gov/people/zitkala-sa.htm); [National Women's History Museum](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/zitkala-sa)). At eight years old she left the reservation — lured, she later wrote, by missionaries' promises of a land of red apples — to attend White's Manual Labor Institute, a Quaker boarding school in Indiana. The experience of the boarding school, where her long braids were forcibly cut and Native languages and customs were punished, became the searing material of her autobiographical essays. She was a gifted student and musician, attended Earlham College, taught at the Carlisle Indian Industrial School (the model boarding school, whose founder coined the phrase "Kill the Indian... and save the man"), and studied violin. Between 1900 and 1902 she published a series of autobiographical essays in the *Atlantic Monthly* — including "Impressions of an Indian Childhood" and "The School Days of an Indian Girl" — among the first literary works written in English by a Native American author drawing directly on personal experience rather than dictated to a white editor. She also collaborated on the first Native American opera, *The Sun Dance Opera*. Increasingly an activist, she co-founded the National Council of American Indians in 1926 and campaigned tirelessly for Native citizenship, suffrage, education, and the preservation of Native cultures until her death in 1938; she is buried at Arlington National Cemetery. For students, Zitkala-Ša is the indispensable inside witness to the boarding-school assault on Native identity — a writer who used the English she was forced to learn to indict the system that forced it.

### Historical & Cultural Context

These two writers bracket the most devastating decades of U.S. policy toward Native peoples. By the time Winnemucca was writing in the 1880s, the wars of conquest in the West were ending in the near-total dispossession of Native nations: treaties made and broken, peoples forcibly removed from ancestral lands onto reservations, the buffalo economy destroyed, and rampant corruption in the federal Indian agencies that controlled rations and survival. *Life Among the Piutes* documents this from the inside — the starvation, the broken promises, the specific named agents who stole what was due her people. Winnemucca writes as an advocate to a white readership she is trying to move to outrage and reform.

Zitkala-Ša writes from inside the next phase: forced assimilation. With armed conquest largely complete, federal policy turned to erasing Native identity through education. The Indian boarding school system, exemplified by the Carlisle Indian Industrial School (founded 1879) and its motto — Richard Henry Pratt's notorious "Kill the Indian in him, and save the man" — removed Native children, often far from home, cut their hair, forbade their languages, replaced their names and clothing, and sought to sever them from their cultures entirely. The policy was deliberate cultural destruction carried out in the name of benevolence, and its traumas echo across Native communities to this day. "The School Days of an Indian Girl" is one of the earliest first-person literary testimonies of what this felt like to a child subjected to it.

The deep paradox students must sit with is linguistic and cultural. Both women wrote in English — the language of the people destroying their world — and both served, in different ways, as *translators* of their cultures for outside audiences. This is the bind of the colonized intellectual: to be heard at all, they had to use the conqueror's tongue and the conqueror's literary forms (autobiography, the magazine essay), which inevitably shaped and constrained what could be said. Yet that same act was a powerful seizure of voice: they made the master's language carry testimony against the master. The Dawes Act (1887), which broke up communal Native landholdings and accelerated dispossession, and the long denial of citizenship to Native peoples (not granted to all until 1924) form the legal backdrop.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Sarah Winnemucca, *Life Among the Piutes: Their Wrongs and Claims* (1883), opening chapter ("First Meeting of Piutes and Whites").** Winnemucca opens with her own birth and lineage — granddaughter of the Paiute leader the whites called Captain Truckee, daughter of a chief — and recounts the Paiutes' first contacts with white settlers around the time of her early childhood. She describes her grandfather's initial belief that the whites were long-lost "white brothers" to be welcomed, and the people's fear and confusion as settlers arrived. She narrates a terrifying childhood episode: hearing that whites were killing Paiutes and even rumored to eat them, her mother buried Sarah and a cousin in the ground up to their faces, covering them with sage to hide them as a settler party passed. She describes Paiute customs, kinship, and the dignity of her people, weaving ethnography into memoir. The chapter establishes both the humanity of the Paiutes and the dawning catastrophe of white encroachment, framing the book's purpose: to plead the Paiute cause to a white audience.

**Zitkala-Ša, "The School Days of an Indian Girl" (1900).** A sequence of autobiographical vignettes. The eight-year-old narrator is lured east to boarding school by promises of red apples and a wonderful land. On arrival she is overwhelmed by the alien, regimented institution — strange clothes, clattering shoes, a tugging discipline. The central, famous episode is the forced cutting of her hair: told that her long braids will be cut (a mark, in her culture, of mourning or of cowards/captives), she resists, hides under a bed, is dragged out, tied to a chair, and her braids are shorn while she cries for her mother. "I lost my spirit," she writes. Later vignettes recount the harshness and small cruelties of school life, the deaths of classmates, her growing alienation from both the white world and, painfully, from her own mother and people on her returns home. The excerpt records the systematic stripping of a Native child's identity and the lasting wound it leaves.

### The Text(s) for This Session

- Sarah Winnemucca (Hopkins), *Life Among the Piutes: Their Wrongs and Claims* (1883) — Chapter 1, opening pages (approximately 10 pages). **Freely available:** [Internet Archive](https://digital.library.upenn.edu/women/winnemucca/piutes/piutes.html) — public domain.
- Zitkala-Ša, "The School Days of an Indian Girl" (1900) (approximately 15 pages). **Freely available:** [American Literature](https://digital.library.upenn.edu/women/zitkala-sa/stories/school.html) — public domain. The original *Atlantic Monthly* texts are also freely available via [the Atlantic's archive](https://www.theatlantic.com/) and Wikisource.

### Lecture Script

Tonight we turn, for the first time, to Native American writing — and to two women who did something almost unbearable in its difficulty. They took the language of the people who were destroying their world, and they used it to testify against that destruction. They wrote in English, the conqueror's tongue, because it was the only way to be heard at all. Hold that paradox tonight. It will run through everything we read. [pause]

The first is Sarah Winnemucca. Her people are the Northern Paiutes, of what is now Nevada, and her name in Paiute means "shell flower." She was born around 1844, just as white settlers were beginning to flood across Paiute land, and she grew up in the middle — literally in the middle, as an interpreter between her people and the U.S. Army, trusted fully by neither side. In 1883 she wrote a book called *Life Among the Piutes*, and it is, as far as we know, the first autobiography ever written in English by a Native American woman. [pause]

I want to give you one image from her childhood, and I want you to sit with it. When Sarah was very small, word came that white men were killing Paiutes — and there were rumors, terrifying rumors, that they were eating them. And so her mother did what a mother does. She buried her children in the ground — buried Sarah and another child up to their faces, and covered them with sage brush, and left them there, hidden, while a party of white settlers passed by. A small child, lying in the earth, told to be silent, listening to the footsteps. [pause] That is where this literature begins. Not in a library. In the dirt, hiding to stay alive.

Our second writer is from the next generation, and from a different nation — the Yankton Dakota. Her name was Zitkala-Ša, "Red Bird." And she is our witness to one of the most deliberate campaigns of cultural destruction in American history: the Indian boarding schools. [pause] Here is what those schools were. With the wars mostly over, the U.S. government decided the way to deal with Native peoples was to take their children — sometimes by persuasion, sometimes by force — ship them far from home, cut their hair, forbid them to speak their languages, change their names and their clothes, and erase, as completely as possible, everything that made them who they were. The man who founded the model school said the goal out loud: "Kill the Indian," he said, "and save the man." [pause]

Zitkala-Ša was eight years old when she was taken east — lured, she tells us, by a promise of a land full of red apples. And the scene at the center of her account, the one we will read aloud, is the cutting of her hair. In her culture, shorn hair was a mark of mourning, or of cowards and captives. She fought. She hid under a bed. They dragged her out, tied her into a chair, and cut off her braids while she cried for her mother. "I lost my spirit," she writes. Four words. [pause]

I am going to ask us to slow down tonight, and to let some silences be long. These are hard pages. But notice, as we read, what these women *do* with the conqueror's language — how they take English and bend it into a weapon of witness. That is not weakness. That is one of the bravest things a writer can do. Let's begin with Sarah, and that child in the earth.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. Winnemucca, the burial in the sage.** What to bring out: the scene fuses a child's terror with an indictment of the settlers whose violence (and the cannibalism rumor) made such hiding necessary. Note Winnemucca's restraint — she reports the event plainly and lets it accuse. The image of a child buried alive to survive is the book's emotional foundation and a counter to any narrative of benign settlement.

**2. Winnemucca, the grandfather's welcome of the "white brothers."** What to notice: she records her grandfather's initial belief that whites were long-lost kin to be welcomed — a generosity the settlers betrayed. What to draw out: the structure is tragic irony; Paiute hospitality is repaid with dispossession. This complicates the settler myth of Native hostility by showing the welcome came first.

**3. Winnemucca's double role as interpreter.** What to bring out: throughout, she occupies the painful middle ground — trusted fully by neither side, using English to mediate and to plead. Discuss the cost and the power of the cultural translator, and connect to the session's central paradox: she must use the conqueror's language to defend her people.

**4. Zitkala-Ša, the cutting of the braids.** The session's central passage; read it aloud. What to notice: the layered meanings of hair — in her culture short hair signified mourning, cowardice, or captivity, so the cutting is not cosmetic but a symbolic killing. Note the bodily resistance (hiding, being tied) and the terrible economy of "I lost my spirit." What to draw out: the school takes far more than hair; it takes selfhood, language, and belonging in a single act.

**5. Zitkala-Ša, the alienation on returning home.** What to bring out: the cruelest cost of assimilation — the child returns to the reservation no longer fully at home there, estranged even from her mother, while also never accepted by the white world. This is double consciousness in a specifically Native, colonial key: belonging nowhere, made foreign to herself. Connect forward to Kingston (Session 12) and the later immigrant writers.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Both women wrote in English, the language of those destroying their peoples. Is there a tension in that? Is there also a power?**
   *Model answer:* The tension is acute: the conqueror's language and literary forms inevitably shaped and limited what could be said, and writing in English meant addressing the very public complicit in the destruction. But it is also a seizure of power — they made that language carry testimony against the conquest, reaching the audience that needed to hear it.

2. **Why is the cutting of Zitkala-Ša's braids so devastating? What is being cut along with the hair?**
   *Model answer:* In her culture short hair marked mourning, cowardice, or captivity, so the cutting is a symbolic killing, not grooming. Along with the hair go her language, her name, her clothing, and her belonging — the school's aim was to sever her from her entire identity, which is why she writes "I lost my spirit."

3. **How does Winnemucca's purpose as an activist shape her voice and what she must do that Zitkala-Ša does not?**
   *Model answer:* Writing to move a white readership to political reform, Winnemucca must explain Paiute customs, document specific wrongs and named agents, and plead a case — blending ethnography, memoir, and indictment. Zitkala-Ša, writing literary autobiography for a magazine, can stay closer to personal experience and interior feeling without building a legal-political brief.

4. **What does the burial-in-the-sage episode reveal about the "settlement" of the West?**
   *Model answer:* It reframes settlement as terror from the Native point of view — a child hidden in the earth to escape killers. It punctures any romance of peaceful pioneering by showing the violence and dread that white expansion brought to the people already living there.

5. **What does it mean that Winnemucca's grandfather first welcomed the whites as "brothers"?**
   *Model answer:* It establishes that the Paiutes met the newcomers with hospitality and even kinship before that welcome was betrayed by dispossession. The detail overturns the settler myth of inherent Native hostility and casts the resulting tragedy as a betrayal of trust.

6. **Both writers are "translators" of their cultures for outside audiences. What is gained and lost in that act?**
   *Model answer:* Gained: their peoples' humanity, customs, and suffering reach an audience that might otherwise never see them, and a record survives. Lost: the framing for outsiders can flatten or exoticize, and the conqueror's forms cannot fully hold a Native worldview, so something is inevitably distorted in translation.

7. **What was the explicit goal of the Indian boarding schools, and how does Zitkala-Ša's account expose it?**
   *Model answer:* The goal was forced assimilation — to "kill the Indian and save the man" by erasing language, dress, names, and culture. Her first-person account exposes the benevolent rhetoric as a campaign of cultural destruction by showing its effect on a single terrified child.

8. **How does Zitkala-Ša's estrangement from her own mother and people on her return deepen the tragedy?**
   *Model answer:* It shows the assimilation machine succeeded in unmooring her from her origins without granting her acceptance in the white world, leaving her belonging nowhere. The deepest wound is not just what was taken at school but the home she can no longer fully return to.

9. **These are early works of Native literature in English. How should that shape our expectations as readers?**
   *Model answer:* We should read them as pioneering acts of self-representation against enormous constraint, not measure them against later, freer Native writing. Their use of autobiography and the essay reflects the only forms available for being heard, and their achievement is testifying at all.

10. **How do these texts connect to the course's larger thread about double consciousness?**
    *Model answer:* Both writers live and write at the seam between two worlds, seeing themselves through the colonizer's eyes while holding their own knowledge — the Native version of Du Bois's twoness. Zitkala-Ša's belonging-nowhere and Winnemucca's go-between role are colonial inflections of the same divided consciousness that runs through the course.

### Anticipated Difficulties

These are among the hardest pages in the course emotionally, and the boarding-school material in particular carries trauma that remains live in Native communities today. Flag it in advance, read the haircut scene slowly, and let the silence after it be long — this is a place to slow down and breathe. The period texts use "Indian," "Piute/Paiute," and other terms of their day; explain that "Indian" was and remains contested, that we use it here as the period and sometimes still-used term while preferring specific nation names (Northern Paiute, Yankton Dakota) where we can. Guard against the ethnographic trap — the temptation to read these women as informants or specimens rather than as authors making strategic choices; keep returning them to the status of writers. A largely non-Native room may feel it lacks standing; reassure them that careful, humble witness is precisely the work asked of a reader here.

## Session 8 — Asian American Voices I: Sui Sin Far & Carlos Bulosan

### At a Glance
Two writers who insisted on the humanity of Asian immigrants in an America that had made them, by law, unwelcome. Edith Maude Eaton — writing as Sui Sin Far — was the first person of Chinese heritage to publish sympathetic fiction about Chinese Americans in the mainstream press, in the era of the Chinese Exclusion Act. Carlos Bulosan, a Filipino migrant laborer, wrote one of the great American memoirs of poverty, racism, and stubborn hope. The session opens the course's attention to Asian American literature, to the immigration regimes that criminalized whole peoples, and to the painful question of what it means to love a country that does not love you back. The goal is to read these writers against the specific legal machinery — exclusion, anti-miscegenation, labor exploitation — that shaped their lives.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Sui Sin Far (Edith Maude Eaton) (1865–1914).** Edith Maude Eaton was born in 1865 in Macclesfield, England, the eldest daughter of an English father and a Chinese mother — making her, in the racial taxonomy of her day, "Eurasian," a person who belonged fully to neither world ([Oxford Bibliographies, "Edith Maude Eaton"](https://academic.oup.com/reference/62335/reference-article/554025282); [*Encyclopaedia Britannica*]). The family emigrated, settling eventually in Montreal, where Eaton grew up amid genteel poverty in a household of fourteen children. Light-skinned enough that she could have "passed" as white, she made the deliberate choice — central to her significance — to claim her Chinese heritage publicly and to write on behalf of Chinese immigrants, adopting the pen name Sui Sin Far (a Cantonese name associated with the narcissus or water lily). She began as a journalist in Montreal in the late 1880s and, suffering from poor health her whole life, moved to the United States around 1898, living and working on the West Coast (San Francisco, Seattle) and later in Boston, supporting herself with secretarial work while writing. Her autobiographical essay "Leaves from the Mental Portfolio of an Eurasian" (1909) is a landmark meditation on mixed-race identity; her story "In the Land of the Free" (1909) and her collection *Mrs. Spring Fragrance* (1912) — the first book of fiction by a writer of Chinese descent published in the United States — depicted Chinese Americans as complex, dignified human beings at a moment when popular culture rendered them only as sinister caricatures or comic figures. (Her younger sister Winnifred took the opposite path, inventing a Japanese persona, "Onoto Watanna," because Japanese identity was then more fashionable — a striking sibling contrast worth mentioning.) Sui Sin Far died in Montreal in 1914, at forty-eight. Long neglected, she has been recovered as the foundational figure of Asian American literature. Present her to students as a writer who used her in-between position not as a deficit but as a vantage point, and who wrote sympathetic Chinese American lives as a deliberate act of resistance against the Exclusion era's dehumanizing propaganda.

**Carlos Bulosan (1913–1956).** Carlos Bulosan was born in 1913 in Binalonan, in the Pangasinan province of the Philippines, then a U.S. colony, into a poor farming family ([Academy of American Poets, "Carlos Bulosan"](https://poets.org/poet/carlos-bulosan)). In 1930, at about seventeen, he emigrated to the United States, joining the wave of young Filipino men recruited as cheap migrant labor for the farms and canneries of the American West. What he found was brutal: backbreaking stoop labor following the harvests up and down the Pacific coast, vicious anti-Filipino racism (including mob violence and the "anti-miscegenation" laws that forbade Filipino men from marrying white women), grinding poverty, and chronic illness — he spent years in hospitals with tuberculosis, where he read voraciously and educated himself. Out of this he became a writer and a labor organizer, deeply involved in the multiethnic farmworker and cannery union movements. His best-known book, *America Is in the Heart* (1946), is a semi-autobiographical narrative — part memoir, part collective portrait of the Filipino migrant experience — that moves from his childhood in the Philippines through the violence and exploitation of migrant life in America to a stubborn, hard-won faith in America's democratic promise. The title is bitterly double-edged: written by a man America beat and excluded, it insists nonetheless that "America" is an ideal carried in the heart rather than a place that has kept its promises. Bulosan's reputation faded in the McCarthy era (his radical labor politics made him a target), and he died in poverty in Seattle in 1956 at forty-two. Recovered in the 1970s alongside the broader Asian American literary movement, he is now read as the essential chronicler of the Filipino American experience. For students, Bulosan embodies the course's "long argument with the American narrative" — a man who keeps faith with American ideals precisely while documenting America's failure to honor them.

### Historical & Cultural Context

This session is unintelligible without the legal architecture of Asian exclusion. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 was the first major U.S. law to bar a specific nationality from immigration and naturalization; it suspended Chinese labor immigration, denied Chinese immigrants the right to become citizens, and was repeatedly renewed and broadened, remaining substantially in force until 1943. It created the world of Sui Sin Far's fiction: a Chinese American community living under suspicion and surveillance, families separated by immigration law, and a popular culture saturated with the "Yellow Peril" stereotype. "In the Land of the Free" dramatizes exactly this machinery — the bureaucratic seizure of a child under immigration regulations — and the title's irony is the whole point. Students should grasp that Sui Sin Far's simple act of portraying Chinese people as ordinary, loving, complex human beings was, in this climate, a radical intervention.

Bulosan's context is the next chapter of Asian exclusion and the colonial relationship between the United States and the Philippines. The U.S. had seized the Philippines from Spain in 1898 and ruled it as a colony; Filipinos were U.S. "nationals" (not citizens) and could travel to the mainland, which made them a convenient source of cheap agricultural labor in the 1920s and 1930s as Chinese and Japanese immigration was choked off. Filipino migrant laborers — overwhelmingly young men — faced savage discrimination: exclusion from many jobs and neighborhoods, anti-miscegenation laws barring marriage to white women, vigilante violence (the Watsonville riots of 1930 are emblematic), and the desperate conditions of follow-the-harvest farm labor during the Great Depression. *America Is in the Heart* documents this world from the inside. The cruel irony at the book's heart — that Filipinos were taught to revere American democracy in colonial schools and then met its violent betrayal on American soil — is the engine of Bulosan's complex patriotism. Both writers, then, force the question the whole course returns to: what is owed to, and by, a nation that legally defines certain people as undesirable?

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Sui Sin Far, "In the Land of the Free" (1909).** A Chinese merchant's wife, Lae Choo, returns by ship to San Francisco with her infant son, born during a visit to China, to rejoin her husband Hom Hing. At the dock, American immigration officials inform the parents that the child has no documentation proving his right to enter the country and must be detained. The agonized parents are forced to surrender their baby to the authorities "temporarily" while they obtain papers. Months pass; bureaucratic delay drags on; the parents hire a glib white lawyer, James Clancy, who extracts their savings and Lae Choo's jewelry in fees while the case crawls. When the child is finally released — nearly a year later — and the mother rushes to reclaim him, the boy no longer knows her: raised in the mission nursery, he shrinks from this stranger, clinging to the white missionary woman and crying, calling for "Fader" in English. The story ends on the mother's devastation. What has been stolen is not only the months but the bond itself; the "land of the free" has made a Chinese mother a stranger to her own child.

**Carlos Bulosan, *America Is in the Heart* (1946), excerpt from Part III (ch. 20–21).** In the assigned excerpt, the narrator (Carlos/"Allos") is deep in the migrant-labor world of the American West — moving between farm fields, canneries, and skid-row hotels, among fellow Filipino laborers. He witnesses and endures the era's anti-Filipino violence and humiliation: beatings, exclusion, the constant threat from white vigilantes and police, the degradation of the labor camps. He is drawn toward labor organizing and toward books, educating himself amid illness and squalor. The passages hold in tension the brutality of his American experience and his persistent, almost stubborn idealism — the conviction that the democratic America he was promised must somehow be real and attainable, even as the actual America brutalizes him. The famous formulation of the book's faith — that America is "in the hearts of men that died for freedom," an ideal rather than a fact — anchors the excerpt's emotional logic: doom and hope held together.

### Lecture Script

Tonight we open a literature we have not yet touched in this course — Asian American writing — and we begin in a hard place: with an America that did not merely discriminate against the people we'll read tonight, but that wrote laws specifically to keep them out, to deny them citizenship, to forbid them to marry, to declare them, in the language of the time, undesirable. Keep that legal machinery in mind tonight, because it is not background. It is the plot. [pause]

Our first writer published under the name Sui Sin Far — a Chinese name, meaning roughly the narcissus flower. But she was born Edith Eaton, in England, in 1865, to an English father and a Chinese mother. She was what her era cruelly called "Eurasian" — half of each, fully accepted by neither. And here is the choice that makes her important. She was light-skinned. She could have lived her whole life as a white Englishwoman, and no one would have known. Her own sister did something like the reverse — invented a Japanese identity, because that was more fashionable, and built a career on it. But Edith Eaton chose to claim her Chinese heritage, publicly, and to spend her career writing Chinese immigrants as what they were: ordinary, loving, complicated human beings. [pause] That does not sound radical to us. In 1909, with the Chinese Exclusion Act in full force and the popular press full of sinister "Yellow Peril" caricatures, it was a genuinely radical act.

The story we read, "In the Land of the Free" — listen to that title, and listen to the bitterness in it. A Chinese mother comes back to San Francisco from China with her baby boy. And at the dock, the immigration officials say the baby has no papers proving he is allowed in. So they take him. "Temporarily," they say. And the months drag on, and a smooth white lawyer takes the family's savings and the mother's jewelry in fees, and finally — nearly a year later — they get the boy back. And he does not know his mother. He has been raised by strangers. He cries and pulls away from her and reaches for the white woman from the mission. [pause] That is what the land of the free has done. It has made a mother a stranger to her own child. Sit with that ending tonight.

Our second writer takes us a generation later, to the farm fields of the American West. Carlos Bulosan came from the Philippines — which, you should know, the United States had taken as a colony in 1898. So Filipinos were taught in colonial schools to revere American democracy, and then, when young men like Bulosan came to America for work, they met its other face: brutal labor in the fields, beatings, exclusion, laws forbidding them to marry white women, mobs. [pause] Bulosan lived all of it — the stoop labor following the harvest, years in hospitals with tuberculosis, where he read every book he could reach and made himself a writer.

And then he wrote a book with one of the most remarkable titles in American literature: *America Is in the Heart*. Think about what it took to write that title. A man whom America beat and excluded and nearly killed — insisting that America is real, that it lives as an ideal in the heart, even if the actual country has betrayed it at every turn. Is that irony? Is it faith? Is it both? That's our question tonight, and it's the great question of this whole course: the long argument between these writers and the American story. Let's read. We'll start at the dock in San Francisco.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. Sui Sin Far, the seizure of the child at the dock.** What to bring out: the cold bureaucratic language of the officials against the parents' incomprehension and terror. Note how Sui Sin Far dramatizes the *machinery* of exclusion — not a villain, but a process, papers and procedures, which is more chilling. The baby is reduced to a case lacking documentation.

**2. Sui Sin Far, the lawyer James Clancy.** What to notice: the white lawyer extracts the family's money and the mother's jewelry while the case stalls, embodying how the legal system preyed on the very people it disenfranchised. What to draw out: the contrast between Lae Choo's desperate love and Clancy's transactional indifference exposes the moral emptiness behind the "free" country's procedures.

**3. Sui Sin Far, the final non-recognition: the child reaches for the white missionary and away from his mother.** The story's devastating climax. What to bring out: the deepest theft is the bond itself; the months of separation have made mother and child strangers. The boy's English cry and his clinging to the white woman literalize forced assimilation at its most intimate. Let this land; do not rush past it.

**4. Bulosan, the scenes of anti-Filipino violence.** What to notice: the matter-of-fact accumulation of beatings, exclusions, and humiliations, rendered without melodrama. What to draw out: the structural nature of the brutality — it is not bad luck but a system (vigilantes, police, laws) arrayed against Filipino laborers. Connect to the legal context (anti-miscegenation, the Watsonville violence).

**5. Bulosan, the assertion of faith in America amid the doom.** What to bring out: the central paradox — "it was a land of doom for me, but I also came to know that I was in America, and I must find a way." Discuss whether Bulosan's idealism is naïve, strategic, or a hard-won refusal to let America's failures define America's promise. This is the politics-of-audience and the argument-with-America threads fused.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **In "In the Land of the Free," what exactly has been taken from the family beyond the months of separation?**
   *Model answer:* The bond between mother and child itself. The boy, raised by strangers during the long detention, no longer recognizes his mother and recoils from her. The state has not merely delayed a reunion but destroyed the relationship, stealing the very intimacy that made them a family.

2. **How does the story's title function?**
   *Model answer:* It is bitterly ironic. The "land of the free" is precisely the place that imprisons an infant over paperwork and bankrupts his parents through a predatory legal process. The title indicts the gap between America's self-image and its treatment of Chinese immigrants.

3. **How does Sui Sin Far's "in-between" Eurasian position shape what she writes about?**
   *Model answer:* Belonging fully to neither white nor Chinese worlds gave her a vantage point from which to see and critique racial boundaries, and a personal stake in challenging them. Rather than treat her mixed identity as a deficit, she made it the source of her sympathy for those caught between worlds and her insistence on shared humanity.

4. **What was radical about simply depicting Chinese Americans as ordinary, complex human beings in 1909?**
   *Model answer:* In an era of the Chinese Exclusion Act and "Yellow Peril" caricature, popular culture portrayed Chinese people only as sinister or comic. Rendering them as loving parents and full human beings directly contradicted the propaganda that justified their legal exclusion, making ordinary representation an act of resistance.

5. **Bulosan titled his book *America Is in the Heart* despite documenting America's cruelty to Filipinos. Irony, faith, or both?**
   *Model answer:* Both at once. The title is ironic given the violence he records, but it also expresses a genuine conviction that America's democratic ideal is real and worth claiming even when the actual nation betrays it. The doubleness is the book's central truth, not a contradiction to be resolved.

6. **What does the U.S. colonial relationship with the Philippines add to our reading of Bulosan?**
   *Model answer:* It explains the cruelty of his disillusionment: Filipinos were taught in colonial schools to revere American democracy, then met its violent betrayal as laborers on the mainland. Bulosan's idealism is the inheritance of that colonial education, and his suffering is its broken promise.

7. **How do the two writers each use the American Dream — critique it, mourn it, cling to it?**
   *Model answer:* Sui Sin Far exposes its hollowness through the irony of the "free" country destroying a family. Bulosan does all three at once — critiquing America's failures, mourning the dream's betrayal, and still clinging to its promise. Neither simply rejects the dream; both wrestle with it.

8. **What does it mean that federal law had made the people in these stories, in effect, illegal or undesirable?**
   *Model answer:* It means their humanity was contradicted by the state itself, which defined them as unfit for citizenship or unwelcome entirely. The literature must therefore argue for a recognition the law denied, making the simple assertion of full personhood inherently political.

9. **Compare Sui Sin Far's restrained, ironic fiction with Bulosan's raw, accumulative memoir. How does form fit purpose?**
   *Model answer:* Sui Sin Far's controlled irony suits a quiet subversion aimed at a genteel magazine readership, letting understatement expose injustice. Bulosan's sprawling, semi-autobiographical accumulation suits the testimony of a collective experience, conveying the relentless grind of migrant life through sheer mass of incident.

10. **How do these writers extend the course's threads from the earlier sessions?**
    *Model answer:* They carry forward the politics of audience (writing humanity into a hostile readership), the long argument with the American narrative (claiming or indicting the national promise), and double consciousness (the in-between self of the Eurasian writer and the colonized migrant). Asian American exclusion becomes a new chapter in patterns the course has already traced through Black and Native writing.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The legal history is unfamiliar to most readers and essential; budget a few minutes to lay out the Chinese Exclusion Act and the colonial status of the Philippines plainly, because the stories do not make sense without them. "In the Land of the Free" can produce a quiet devastation at its ending; let it. Bulosan's excerpt contains racial violence and slurs of the period directed at Filipinos; flag it and reaffirm your reading practice. A largely white room may not know that Asian Americans faced legally codified exclusion comparable in its machinery to other forms of American racism; present this as historical fact, not competition among injuries. Watch, too, for the opposite error — the "model minority" assumption that Asian immigration was a smooth success story; these texts are the corrective, and naming the stereotype (which we define in the glossary) helps the room see why.

## Session 9 — Mid-Century Black Voices: Baldwin & Brooks

### At a Glance

This session pairs the essay and the lyric at mid-century: James Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son" and three poems by Gwendolyn Brooks. Both writers came of age in a segregated America that was beginning, violently, to crack — and both made forms equal to the pressure. Baldwin remakes the English sentence; Brooks remakes what can fit into fourteen lines.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**James Baldwin (1924–1987).** Baldwin was born in Harlem on August 2, 1924, the eldest of nine children, and raised in poverty by a mother and a stepfather, David Baldwin, a storefront preacher whose rage and rigidity shadowed his son's childhood ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/james-baldwin)). As a teenager Baldwin himself preached in Pentecostal churches — an experience whose cadences never left his prose. He left the church and, after the death of his stepfather in 1943, left for Greenwich Village, then in 1948 for Paris, where he wrote much of his early work in self-imposed exile, seeking distance from the American racial order and room to be a Black, gay writer ([National Museum of African American History and Culture](https://nmaahc.si.edu/explore/stories/introduction-james-baldwin)). His first novel, *Go Tell It on the Mountain* (1953), drew on his Harlem boyhood and the Pentecostal world. But it was the essay that made him a national conscience. *Notes of a Native Son* (1955), the collection from which our reading is drawn, established a voice — at once analytical and intimate, prophetic and self-implicating — that would define American letters for a generation. *The Fire Next Time* (1963) became one of the central texts of the civil rights years. Baldwin returned often to the United States to lecture and march, and his later essays, novels, and the documentary record of his debates and interviews made him one of the most recognizable public intellectuals of the century. He died in Saint-Paul-de-Vence, France, in 1987. What to keep in front of residents: Baldwin chose the essay because, as he said, fiction let him off the hook. The essay required him to stand in the open and be accountable for every sentence — and his sentences, with their long suspensions and sudden reversals, enact a mind refusing easy conclusions about a country he loved and indicted in the same breath.

**Gwendolyn Brooks (1917–2000).** Brooks was born in Topeka, Kansas, on June 7, 1917, and raised on the South Side of Chicago, the Bronzeville that would become her great subject ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/gwendolyn-brooks)). She published her first poem at thirteen and was a disciplined, prolific young poet long before recognition came. Her first collection, *A Street in Bronzeville* (1945), drew on the lives of her South Side neighbors. Her second, *Annie Allen* (1949), won the 1950 Pulitzer Prize for Poetry, making Brooks the first African American to win a Pulitzer in any category ([Library of America](https://www.loa.org/news-and-views/1629-globalgwen-initiative-celebrates-gwendolyn-brooks-and-a-literary-milestone/)). *The Bean Eaters* (1960), from which two of our poems come, marked a turn toward more direct social engagement. In 1967, after attending the Fisk University Black Writers' Conference and encountering the energy of the younger Black Arts generation, Brooks underwent a deliberate transformation — moving to Black-owned presses, writing more explicitly for and to Black readers, and embracing the Black Aesthetic the course names in its glossary. She served as Poet Laureate of Illinois from 1968 until her death and as Consultant in Poetry to the Library of Congress (the position later called Poet Laureate). She died in Chicago in 2000. For our purposes: Brooks is a master of compression. "We Real Cool" is twenty-four words long. Hold up that economy against Baldwin's expansive paragraphs and you have two complete and opposite answers to the same question — how do you make a form carry the weight of Black life in mid-century America?

### Historical & Cultural Context

Baldwin and Brooks wrote out of the Great Migration's second generation — the children and grandchildren of those who had left the rural South for Chicago, New York, and Detroit, only to find the North's own machinery of segregation waiting: restrictive covenants, redlining, the kitchenette apartments carved out of single units to extract maximum rent from Black tenants who had nowhere else to go. "Notes of a Native Son" is set partly against the Harlem riot of 1943, which erupted the day of Baldwin's father's funeral — the private and the political colliding in a single August day ([National Museum of African American History and Culture](https://nmaahc.si.edu/explore/stories/introduction-james-baldwin)). Brooks's Bronzeville is the Chicago end of the same migration. Her kitchenette poems document a specific architecture of constraint — the dream measured against "onion fumes" and "yesterday's garbage ripening in the hall." Both writers worked in the years between the Harlem Renaissance and the Black Arts Movement, a period sometimes treated as a lull but in fact the time when the literature found its mid-century maturity: less the manifesto, more the achieved work. The civil rights movement was gathering as they wrote; *Brown v. Board of Education* came in 1954, the year before *Notes of a Native Son*. Place them, for residents, as the bridge generation — heirs to Hughes and Hurston, elders to the writers of the 1960s.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Notes of a Native Son" (1955), first half (through the funeral).** Baldwin's title essay braids three strands. The first is the death of his stepfather, David Baldwin, who died of tuberculosis (and mental illness) on July 29, 1943, the same day Baldwin's youngest sibling was born and, hours later, the day Harlem erupted in riot. The second is Baldwin's reckoning with his father — a bitter, paranoid, deeply religious man whom Baldwin had hated and feared, and whom he comes to understand only after his death, recognizing in himself the same chronic rage. The third is Baldwin's own coming-of-age in racism: he recounts working in defense plants in New Jersey, being refused service repeatedly, and finally hurling a water glass at a waitress in a restaurant before fleeing, terrified by the murderousness he found in himself. The assigned first half moves toward the funeral on Baldwin's nineteenth birthday, where, looking at his father's face, he begins the lifelong labor of understanding — that the bitterness which destroyed his father is a poison every Black American must learn to carry without being consumed.

**"We Real Cool" (1960).** Subtitled "The Pool Players. Seven at the Golden Shovel." Eight short lines spoken in the collective voice of seven young men playing pool instead of being in school. Each sentence is three words; the rhyming word falls at the line's end before "We" enacts a defiant swing into the next line. They left school, lurk late, strike straight, sing sin, thin gin, "Jazz June." The final line lands without a trailing "We": "We / Die soon." The poem's swagger and its fatalism are the same breath.

**"The Bean Eaters" (1960).** An old married couple, "mostly Good," eats their dinner of beans on plain chipware in a rented back room. The poem catalogs the modest remnants of a long life — "twinklings and twinges," "beads and receipts and dolls and cloths, tobacco crumbs, vases and fringes." Brooks confers dignity on the overlooked through sheer attention to their small surviving things.

**"kitchenette building" (1945).** The poem asks whether a "dream" can survive in a kitchenette apartment, where survival itself consumes the day. The speaker wonders whether a dream could "send up through onion fumes / Its white and violet, fight with fried potatoes / And yesterday's garbage ripening in the hall." It ends with the deflating practicality of needing to get into the lukewarm shared bathroom before the water cools — the dream displaced by the next tenant's claim on the tub.

### Lecture Script

Today we put a great essayist next to a great poet, and we let them argue about form. [pause] Both of these writers are working in the 1940s and '50s, in the years between the Harlem Renaissance you met two weeks ago and the movement poetry we'll read next time. People sometimes call this a quiet period in Black literature. I want to suggest the opposite — that this is when the work grows up.

Let's start with Baldwin. [pause] He wrote novels, but he said something that I find unforgettable: that fiction let him off the hook. In a novel he could hide behind a character. In an essay he had to stand in the open, in his own voice, and be answerable for every sentence. And so the essay became his great form. Listen to what he does in "Notes of a Native Son." His father dies. On the same day, his sister is born. Hours later, Harlem explodes into a riot. Baldwin takes those three events — a death, a birth, a riot — and he refuses to let them stay separate. [pause] He insists that the private grief and the public rage are the same weather.

Now I want to be careful here, because this is not my story to tell. [pause] What I can do — what we all can do — is read closely what Baldwin himself says. He writes about discovering a murderous rage in his own chest — the night he threw a glass at a waitress and ran. He is not describing someone else's anger from a safe distance. He is implicating himself. He calls it a poison, and he says every Black American has to learn to carry it without being eaten alive by it. That is the labor of the essay: not to resolve the rage, but to look at it without flinching.

[pause] Now turn to Brooks, because she does the opposite thing with the same material. Baldwin needs long, branching sentences. Brooks needs almost no words at all. "We Real Cool" is twenty-four words long. Count them. Seven young men at a pool hall, and Brooks puts the word "We" at the end of each line, so that every time you reach it you have to swing down into the next line — the rhythm itself is restless, defiant, alive. And then the last line drops the "We." [pause] "We / Die soon." The swagger and the death sentence are the same breath. She said she imagined these were boys who would not live long, and she wanted you to feel the bravado and the doom together.

Look at "kitchenette building." [pause] Here is the kitchenette — the single apartment chopped into pieces, the shared bathroom, the onion fumes, yesterday's garbage in the hall. And Brooks asks a real question: can a dream survive in a space like this? Not as metaphor — as a practical matter. The poem ends not with an answer but with someone needing to get into the lukewarm bathwater before it goes cold. The dream loses to the tub. [pause]

So here is what I want you to carry out of today. Two writers, the same world — Northern, urban, supposedly free, actually penned in by redlines and covenants — and two completely opposite forms. Baldwin's expansion, Brooks's compression. Both of them true. [pause] When someone tells you there is a right way to write about hard things, remember this pairing. There is the long way and the short way, and a master can break your heart with either one.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**Baldwin's three-event braid.** The structural heart of the essay's first half is the coincidence of the death, the birth, and the riot. Have residents notice that Baldwin does not editorialize the connection — he reports the three facts and lets their simultaneity do the argumentative work. This is the essay's central technique: meaning through juxtaposition rather than assertion.

**The water glass.** Baldwin's account of throwing a glass at a waitress and fleeing is the essay's moral pivot. The key move is that he turns the analysis on himself; the racism he has absorbed has produced a violence that frightens him. Ask: what does it cost a writer to make himself, rather than his oppressors, the case study?

**"We Real Cool" — the line breaks.** Read the poem twice aloud, once pausing at line ends and once running through. The placement of "We" at line ends, and its absence in the final line, is the whole poem. The grammar enacts both the collective bravado and its sudden silencing.

**"kitchenette" — the cluster of nouns.** "onion fumes," "fried potatoes," "yesterday's garbage ripening in the hall." Brooks builds the dream's obstacle out of smell. Note how "ripening" turns rot into a grotesque flowering — the same verb you would use for fruit.

**"The Bean Eaters" — the inventory.** The closing list ("beads and receipts and dolls and cloths, tobacco crumbs") is an act of attention as dignity. The poem's argument is that a life of small surviving things is worth the poem's full notice.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Baldwin describes his father's funeral on the day of the Harlem riot. What is he saying about the relationship between personal grief and political rage?** *Model answer:* He refuses to separate them. The private loss and the public uprising occur on the same day, and Baldwin treats that coincidence as a truth rather than an accident — grief and rage are the same inheritance, and the essay's form binds them together.

2. **"We Real Cool" has seven words per sentence and a jazz-like syncopation. Brooks said she imagined young men who would not live long. Does knowing that change how you hear it?** *Model answer:* It usually does. On first reading the poem sounds like swagger; with Brooks's intention in mind, the bravado becomes elegy. The dropped "We" before "Die soon" reveals that the silencing was built into the rhythm all along.

3. **"kitchenette building" asks whether a dream can survive in a space this cramped. How do the physical details answer the question?** *Model answer:* They answer it pessimistically. The dream is outcompeted by onion fumes, fried potatoes, and ripening garbage, and the poem ends with the practical urgency of the shared bathwater — the material conditions crowd the dream out before it can bloom.

4. **Baldwin wrote essays because he said fiction let him off the hook. What did he mean? What can an essay do that a novel cannot?** *Model answer:* In fiction he could displace himself onto characters; the essay forced him to speak in his own accountable voice. The essay can implicate its author directly, which is exactly what Baldwin wanted — to examine his own rage rather than assign it to an invented figure.

5. **Both writers depict Black life in Northern cities — survival in supposedly free America. What do they find there?** *Model answer:* They find a subtler but real confinement: segregation enforced by economics and architecture rather than law alone, the kitchenette and the redline standing in for the plantation's fence. The North's promise of freedom is shown to be conditional.

6. **How does Brooks's compression compare to Baldwin's expansion as a way of carrying weight?** *Model answer:* Both succeed by opposite means. Baldwin accumulates qualifications until a sentence holds contradiction; Brooks strips away until a single dropped word ("We") carries the death. Form is shown to be a choice, not a constraint.

7. **Why might Brooks have moved, after 1967, toward Black-owned presses and a more explicitly Black readership?** *Model answer:* The Black Arts Movement reframed the question of audience the course raised in its recurring threads; Brooks chose to write to her community rather than past it, aligning her distribution with her aesthetic commitments.

8. **What does the inventory of objects in "The Bean Eaters" accomplish?** *Model answer:* It dignifies an overlooked couple by giving their small possessions the poem's full attention, asserting that an ordinary survived life merits the same care a poem would give to grandeur.

9. **Where does Baldwin locate hope, if anywhere, in the funeral passage?** *Model answer:* Not in resolution but in understanding — the recognition, achieved only after his father's death, that comprehending the poison is the precondition for not being destroyed by it. Hope lies in the labor of seeing clearly.

10. **What do you gain by reading the essayist and the poet together rather than separately?** *Model answer:* You see that the period's literary achievement was not a single style but a range, and that compression and expansion are equally capable of bearing historical weight. The pairing dramatizes form as the writer's deliberate instrument.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Baldwin's prose is demanding; some residents will need permission to read a sentence twice. Model that openly — read one of his long sentences aloud and unpack its turns, so re-reading looks like competence, not failure. The funeral passage and the water-glass scene contain the period's racial vocabulary and real violence; flag it before you read and reaffirm the reading practice the course has used since Session 1. Because Baldwin implicates his own rage, a white room can be tempted to either over-identify ("we all feel that way") or distance into pity; steer toward the text's specific claims rather than universalized emotion. With "We Real Cool," resist the urge to explain the poem to death — read it, let the dropped "We" sit, and allow a silence before anyone speaks.

## Session 10 — The Civil Rights Era: King & Lorde

### At a Glance

Two kinds of witness from the movement years: the prophetic public argument and the lyric private testimony. Martin Luther King Jr.'s "Letter from Birmingham Jail" is one of the great works of American prose. Audre Lorde's poems extend the era's idea of justice in directions King's public rhetoric did not always reach. Read together, they map the breadth of what the movement was.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Martin Luther King Jr. (1929–1968).** King was born in Atlanta on January 15, 1929, the son and grandson of Baptist ministers, and was ordained himself before completing a doctorate in systematic theology at Boston University in 1955 ([Stanford King Institute](https://kinginstitute.stanford.edu/martin-luther-king-jr-biography)). That same year, as a young pastor in Montgomery, Alabama, he was thrust into leadership of the bus boycott that followed Rosa Parks's arrest, and he emerged as the foremost spokesman of the nonviolent civil rights movement. He helped found the Southern Christian Leadership Conference in 1957 and led campaigns in Albany, Birmingham, and Selma. In April 1963, jailed during the Birmingham desegregation campaign, he wrote — in the margins of a newspaper and on scraps smuggled out by his lawyers — an open letter responding to eight white Alabama clergymen who had called the demonstrations "unwise and untimely" ([Stanford King Institute](https://kinginstitute.stanford.edu/letter-birmingham-jail)). That August he delivered the "I Have a Dream" address at the March on Washington; the following year he received the Nobel Peace Prize. He was assassinated in Memphis on April 4, 1968. For our purposes the emphasis is literary as much as historical: the "Letter" is a masterclass in rhetorical structure — its appeals to Augustine and Aquinas, its patient anatomy of "just" and "unjust" laws, its turn from defense to indictment — and residents should meet it as a made thing, an argument constructed with the care of a sermon and the logic of a brief.

**Audre Lorde (1934–1992).** Lorde was born in New York City on February 18, 1934, to immigrant parents from the Caribbean island of Grenada and Barbados, and grew up in Harlem ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/audre-lorde)). She described herself, in a famous self-naming, as "black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet" — a deliberate refusal to be reduced to any single category, and a model of the intersectional thinking the course names in its glossary ([National Women's History Museum](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/audre-lorde)). She earned a master's in library science and worked as a librarian while writing the poems that would make her one of the central voices of late-twentieth-century American literature. Her collections include *The First Cities* (1968), *Coal* (1976), and *The Black Unicorn* (1978); her prose includes *The Cancer Journals* (1980) and *Sister Outsider* (1984), essays that became foundational to Black feminist thought. She insisted that poetry was "not a luxury" but a vital necessity — a way of giving name and form to feelings that precede the ideas built upon them. She died of cancer in 1992 in St. Croix, having taken the African name Gamba Adisa. Place her, for residents, as the writer who pressed the movement's logic of justice further than its public face usually went — toward gender, sexuality, and the interior life — and who insisted that survival itself was a political act.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The "Letter from Birmingham Jail" belongs to a precise moment: April 1963, the Birmingham campaign of marches, boycotts, and sit-ins designed to break the segregation of one of the most rigidly segregated cities in America. King was arrested on April 12; the letter answers a public statement by eight white clergymen urging Black residents to abandon the streets for the courts and to wait. King's reply turns "wait" into the letter's central word — and into an indictment ([Stanford King Institute](https://kinginstitute.stanford.edu/letter-birmingham-jail)). Within weeks the campaign's images — police dogs and fire hoses turned on children — would help force federal action and shape the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Lorde wrote in and after these same years, but from a different vantage. The movement's public leadership was largely male and clerical; Lorde spoke from the margins it sometimes neglected — as a woman, a lesbian, a daughter of immigrants. Her work belongs to the broadening of "civil rights" into the overlapping liberation movements of the late 1960s and 1970s, and to the emergence of a Black feminist tradition that asked the movement to widen its definition of who needed to be free. Reading the prophet and the lyric witness together lets residents see the era not as one voice but as a chorus with internal arguments — exactly the polyphony the course's recurring threads describe.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Letter from Birmingham Jail" (1963), first half.** King opens by addressing the eight clergymen who called his actions "unwise and untimely," establishing his standing — he is in Birmingham because injustice is here, and "injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere." He explains the four steps of a nonviolent campaign (collection of facts, negotiation, self-purification, direct action) and argues that Birmingham's leaders left no path but direct action. He then confronts the word "wait," cataloging in one long, accumulating sentence the daily humiliations of segregation — lynching, police brutality, a daughter asking why she cannot go to the amusement park advertised on television, the constant explaining to children why they are treated as less. He distinguishes just from unjust laws, invoking Augustine ("an unjust law is no law at all") and Aquinas (a just law squares with moral law; an unjust law degrades human personality), and argues that one has a moral duty to disobey unjust laws openly and with willingness to accept the penalty. The assigned first half closes as King turns toward his disappointment — first with the white moderate who prefers "order" to justice, the theme that will dominate the letter's second half.

**"Coal" (1968/1976).** The poem's speaker declares "I is the total black, being spoken / from the earth's inside." Coal becomes a figure for the Black self and for language born under pressure — like the diamond that coal can become. Words are imagined as living things: some "stapled," some open like a diamond catching light. The poem ends with the speaker claiming voice and visibility: "I am black because I come from the earth's inside / take my word for jewel in your open light." It is a poem about the value and the danger of naming oneself.

**"A Litany for Survival" (1978).** Addressed to "those of us who live at the shoreline" — those who live on the edges, never meant to be safe — the poem catalogs the ways the marginalized live in fear: afraid when the sun rises, afraid when it sets, afraid to speak and afraid in silence. Its turn comes in the final lines: "so it is better to speak / remembering / we were never meant to survive." The recognition that survival was never the plan becomes the ground for speech — defiance built directly on despair.

### Lecture Script

Today is the day the course has been building toward in one sense, because today we read what may be the single greatest piece of American persuasive prose, and we set it next to a poet who took the movement's idea of justice and pushed it further than the movement's public voice usually went. [pause]

Let's begin with the King letter, and let's begin with how it was made. April 1963. King is in jail in Birmingham. Eight white clergymen — not segregationists, mind you, but moderates, men who said they agreed with the goal — have published a statement calling the demonstrations "unwise and untimely," telling Black citizens to be patient and use the courts. King answers them in the margins of a newspaper and on scraps of paper smuggled out by his lawyers. [pause] I want you to hold that physical fact in your mind: this masterpiece of structure was composed in a cell, without a library, from memory.

Now watch what he does. He does not begin by shouting. He begins by establishing his right to be there — "injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere." Then he lays out the four steps of a nonviolent campaign, calm as a logician. And then he reaches the word "wait." [pause] And here the temperature changes. King writes one enormous sentence — it runs for most of a page — cataloging what "wait" has meant: the lynchings, the brutality, and then, quietly, a father trying to explain to his small daughter why she cannot go to the amusement park she just saw advertised on television. [pause] He builds and builds and ends the sentence with the simple statement that there comes a time when patience runs out. The form is the argument. The sentence makes you feel the waiting.

I want to be careful here, as I have been all term. This is not my history. [pause] What I can do is show you how the letter is built, because that craft belongs to all of us as readers. Notice that King reaches for Augustine and Aquinas — for the deep Western tradition the clergymen claimed as their own — and uses it to define a just and an unjust law. He is not arguing outside their framework. He is standing inside it and turning it on them. That is rhetorical mastery.

Now turn to Lorde, because she asks a question King's public voice did not always ask: who gets left out of "we"? [pause] Lorde named herself — "black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet" — and refused to drop any of those words. In "A Litany for Survival" she writes for "those of us who live at the shoreline," the people standing on the constant edges of decision, never meant to be safe. And the poem ends with one of the hardest, truest lines in American poetry: "we were never meant to survive." [pause] Now — is that defeat? Read it again. It is the opposite. Because if you were never meant to survive, then survival is not owed and silence buys you nothing. "So it is better to speak." The despair becomes the ground for the courage.

So here is the pairing. [pause] King, the prophet, building a public argument so well-made it changed federal law. Lorde, the lyric witness, insisting that justice has to reach the people the public movement overlooked. They did not always share political ground. Read them together and you hear the era as it actually was — not one voice, but a chorus arguing about how wide the circle of freedom has to be. [pause]

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**The "wait" sentence.** King's single long sentence cataloging the meaning of "wait" is the letter's emotional engine. Have residents track its grammar: it is one sentence because the experience it describes allows no pause. The amusement-park detail — the daughter's question — is placed near the climax precisely because it is the smallest and most domestic of the injuries.

**Just and unjust laws.** King's definitions ("an unjust law is no law at all") borrow Augustine and Aquinas. The rhetorical point is that he argues within the moral tradition his opponents claim, denying them the high ground rather than abandoning it.

**The white moderate (transition into the second half).** The assigned reading turns toward King's "disappointment" with the white moderate "more devoted to order than to justice." Flag this as the letter's hinge — and, gently, as a mirror a contemporary room might hold up to itself.

**Lorde, "Coal" — "I is the total black."** The deliberate ungrammaticality ("I is") fuses the self with the substance. Coal under pressure becomes diamond; the self under pressure becomes speech. Note the closing imperative: "take my word for jewel."

**Lorde, "A Litany" — the final turn.** The poem spends most of its length on fear, in anaphora ("when the sun rises... when the sun sets... when we are loved... when we are alone"). The single word "so" pivots the whole poem: better to speak. Read the last three lines slowly and stop.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **King wrote in response to white clergymen who called his protests "unwise and untimely." Who is the real audience of the letter?** *Model answer:* Nominally the eight clergymen, but King is plainly writing past them to the national public and to history. The clergymen are the occasion; the readership King actually addresses is everyone who counsels patience to the oppressed.

2. **King distinguishes just from unjust laws using Aquinas and Augustine. Why reach for those authorities?** *Model answer:* He grounds civil disobedience in the very moral and religious tradition his opponents claim, so that breaking the law appears not as lawlessness but as fidelity to a higher law. The strategy denies the moderates their assumed moral high ground.

3. **Lorde writes of "those of us who live at the shoreline." Who is she addressing, and how does her "we" differ from King's?** *Model answer:* She addresses the multiply marginalized — those excluded even within liberation movements. Her "we" is more pointedly intersectional than King's public "we," reaching toward women, lesbians, and others the era's mainstream leadership often overlooked.

4. **"A Litany for Survival" ends "we were never meant to survive." Defeat or defiance?** *Model answer:* Defiance. The recognition that survival was never intended removes the incentive for silence — if speaking and not speaking are equally unsafe, one might as well speak. The despairing premise yields a courageous conclusion.

5. **King and Lorde did not always share political ground. What do you gain by reading them together?** *Model answer:* You see the movement as a chorus with internal arguments rather than a single doctrine. King's prophetic public address and Lorde's lyric attention to the overlooked together define the era's true breadth.

6. **Why does King's "wait" sentence run so long?** *Model answer:* Its length enacts its content — the unbearable accumulation of deferred justice. A clipped sentence could not make the reader feel the duration of waiting; the grammar is the argument.

7. **What does Lorde mean by calling poetry "not a luxury"?** *Model answer:* She means poetry gives form to feelings that precede and generate ideas, making it a tool of survival and change rather than ornament. For the marginalized, naming one's experience is a precondition of acting on it.

8. **How does "Coal" use a natural substance to think about language and selfhood?** *Model answer:* Coal — black, formed under pressure, capable of becoming diamond — figures both the Black self and words forged under duress. The poem claims that what is made under pressure can refract light, turning condition into value.

9. **King writes about the disappointment of the white moderate. Why might that be the letter's most uncomfortable passage for a contemporary reader?** *Model answer:* Because it targets not open enemies but sympathetic bystanders who prefer order to justice — a position many comfortable readers can recognize in themselves. The discomfort is the point.

10. **Both writers ask what "survival" requires. How do their answers differ?** *Model answer:* King frames survival as collective liberation achieved through disciplined, public, lawful-in-spirit disobedience. Lorde frames survival as the daily, interior courage of the unprotected to speak at all. One is strategic and communal; the other is existential and personal.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The King letter is long and dense; budget time and read the "wait" sentence aloud rather than assigning it cold. The amusement-park passage and King's catalog of violence are painful and contain the era's realities plainly; flag before reading. A largely white room may find the "white moderate" passage personally implicating — that is appropriate, but guide residents to sit with the discomfort rather than rushing to defend or distance. With Lorde, the temptation is to read "we were never meant to survive" as bleak resignation; slow down and let the room arrive at the turn themselves. Be ready, too, for the reverse error — treating King as a safe, sanitized icon; the letter is radical, and the room should feel its edge. As always, let silence sit after the hardest lines.

## Session 11 — Latinx Voices: Cisneros & Baca

### At a Glance

Sandra Cisneros's *The House on Mango Street* and two poems by Jimmy Santiago Baca bring the course into the Latinx tradition. Cisneros invented a form — neither novel nor story collection nor memoir — exactly fitted to a girl's coming-of-age between two languages. Baca, who learned to read and write in prison, made a poetry of love, the body, and the senses.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Sandra Cisneros (b. 1954).** Cisneros was born in Chicago on December 20, 1954, the only daughter among seven children of a Mexican father and a Mexican-American mother ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/sandra-cisneros)). Her childhood moved between Chicago and Mexico City as the family followed her father's homesickness back and forth, an unrootedness that shaped her sense of never quite belonging to one place — the in-between condition the course has traced in writer after writer. She studied at Loyola University Chicago and then at the prestigious Iowa Writers' Workshop, where, she has said, the gap between her working-class Mexican-American experience and the assumptions of her largely white, privileged classmates pushed her to write deliberately from what they did not have — to claim her difference as her subject ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sandra-Cisneros)). The result was *The House on Mango Street* (1984), a book of brief, lyrical vignettes narrated by a girl named Esperanza Cordero growing up in a poor Chicago Latino neighborhood. It became one of the most widely taught works of contemporary American literature, selling millions of copies and entering classrooms at every level. Cisneros has also published the story collection *Woman Hollering Creek* (1991), the poetry collection *Loose Woman* (1994), and the novel *Caramelo* (2002). She received the National Medal of Arts in 2015. For our purposes: Cisneros's great achievement is the double register of *Mango Street* — a voice that sounds like a child and means like an adult, simple sentences carrying complex freight. The book's form, she has said, was a deliberate refusal of the conventional novel, a way of making something that could be read by the people she grew up among.

**Jimmy Santiago Baca (b. 1952).** Baca was born in Santa Fe, New Mexico, on January 5, 1952, of Chicano and Apache descent, and had one of the most extraordinary paths to literature of any American poet ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/jimmy-santiago-baca)). Abandoned by his parents at two, he lived with a grandparent and then in an orphanage, ran away at eleven, and was living on the streets as a teenager. At twenty-one he was convicted on drug charges and sent to a maximum-security prison in Arizona, where he spent years, some of it in isolation. It was there, illiterate when he entered, that Baca taught himself to read and write — trading and stealing books, copying poems, discovering language as both survival and freedom ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jimmy-Santiago-Baca)). His early poems, written in prison, were sent out and eventually published; *Immigrants in Our Own Land* (1979) appeared the year he was released. His memoir *A Place to Stand* (2001) tells the prison story and won the International Prize. Baca has since devoted much of his life to literacy work with prisoners, at-risk youth, and others on the margins. For the room, his biography is itself a text about the course's deepest theme — that the recovery of voice is sometimes literal, the difference between silence and speech, and that literature is not a luxury but, as Lorde said last week, a means of survival.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The Latinx literary tradition the course enters here is not new — Mexican-American writing predates the United States' acquisition of the Southwest in 1848 — but it reached a flowering in the wake of the Chicano Movement of the 1960s and '70s, which, alongside the civil rights and feminist movements, asserted Mexican-American cultural pride, labor rights (the farmworkers' organizing under César Chávez and Dolores Huerta), and a literature of bilingual, bicultural experience. Cisneros and Baca both write out of that ferment, though in different keys: Cisneros from the urban Midwest and the Iowa Writers' Workshop, Baca from the rural Southwest and the prison. Their work raises questions the course has met before — the politics of audience (for whom does a bilingual writer write?), assimilation and its costs, and the in-between self — but inflects them with the specific textures of Latinx life: the Spanish that surfaces in English sentences, the Catholic iconography, the food and the family and the neighborhood. Note for residents that "Latinx" is a contemporary umbrella term covering enormously varied national origins (Mexican, Puerto Rican, Cuban, Dominican, Central and South American) and that Cisneros and Baca represent one strand — the Mexican-American or Chicano — not the whole. Their arrival in the early 1980s, alongside Kingston, Lorde, Harjo, and Erdrich, marks the moment the course's recurring threads converge: a great simultaneous surfacing of voices long present and long unheard.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"The House on Mango Street" (vignette).** The opening vignette establishes the book's situation: Esperanza's family has moved often, and they finally own a house on Mango Street — but it is not the house they dreamed of. It is small, red, with crumbling bricks and a tight stair, nothing like the house with a garden and real stairs her parents promised. A nun from her school once made her feel ashamed of where she lived, pointing and asking "You live *there*?" Esperanza resolves that this is not the house she will keep; she wants "a house all my own," a real house she can point to. The vignette sets the book's engine: the gap between the place she is and the place — and self — she intends to claim.

**"My Name."** Esperanza meditates on her name, which means "hope" in English but in Spanish carries "too many letters" and "sadness" and "waiting." She was named after her great-grandmother, a wild woman tamed by marriage who spent her life looking sadly out a window — a woman Esperanza refuses to inherit. "I have inherited her name, but I don't want to inherit her place by the window." She imagines renaming herself "Zeze the X" — something new, something that is hers. The vignette is about female inheritance, the weight of a name, and the will to self-creation.

**"A Rice Sandwich."** Esperanza begs her mother to write a note so she can eat lunch in the school canteen with the "special" kids, rather than going home. After much pleading her mother relents and packs a rice sandwich (the family cannot afford lunch meat). At the canteen a stern nun humiliates Esperanza — making her point out her house through the window, doubting she lives far enough away — until Esperanza cries. She eats her cold rice sandwich alone, the longed-for privilege turned to shame. The vignette captures class wounding and the small daily costs of poverty.

**"Hips."** Esperanza and her friends jump rope and talk about hips — what they are for, why they arrive, what they mean. The girls trade theories (hips are for dancing, for holding a baby, for becoming a woman) and improvise rhymes. The vignette marks the threshold of adolescence and the body's new, ambiguous significance, mixing childhood play with the dawning awareness of womanhood.

**"I Am Offering This Poem."** Baca's poem is a love poem in which the poem itself is the gift offered. The speaker says he has nothing material to give — "I have nothing else to give you" — but offers the poem as one offers a coat against cold, a pot of corn, a scarf, a lantern in a dark night. The refrain "Keep it, treasure this" frames language and love as warmth and sustenance. The poem closes with the image of love as the only durable wealth the poor and the imprisoned possess.

**"Green Chile."** The poem contrasts the speaker's own preference for mild green chile with his grandmother's passionate love of the fierce red. He describes her ritual preparation of red chile — her hands, her care, the sensuous, almost erotic devotion of an old woman to the food of her homeland. The chile becomes a vessel of cultural memory, heritage, and the body's loyalty to place; the grandmother's love for the red chile is her love for her origins, transmitted through the meal.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**"My Name" — the window.** "I have inherited her name, but I don't want to inherit her place by the window." The image of the window — the great-grandmother looking sadly out — becomes the emblem of female confinement Esperanza refuses. **"The House on Mango Street" — "You live *there*?"** The nun's question, and the pointing finger, is the wound that organizes the whole book — the externalized shame that fuels Esperanza's resolve to claim "a house all my own." **"A Rice Sandwich" — the cold sandwich.** The longed-for privilege turns to humiliation; the sandwich she begged for is eaten alone, in tears. The reversal — desire to shame — is Cisneros's method for rendering class without naming it abstractly.

**Baca, "I Am Offering This Poem" — the refrain.** "Keep it, treasure this." The poem accumulates domestic, sustaining images (coat, corn, lantern) to argue that language is warmth. The man who has nothing offers the one thing poverty cannot take.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Cisneros named her narrator Esperanza — "hope." 2. **"My Name" is about a name and also about everything else. 3. **The book sounds like a girl but means like an adult. The double register lets painful truths arrive disarmingly, carried by a voice the reader trusts and underestimates.

4. **Baca's "I Am Offering This Poem" is a love poem and a statement about what language is for. What is he saying about poetry's purpose?** *Model answer:* He frames the poem as a gift of warmth and sustenance for someone who has no material wealth to give. Poetry's purpose, in this view, is to provide what poverty cannot — durable love made of language.

5. **"Green Chile" uses food, memory, and the body. How does a sensory image carry something larger?** *Model answer:* The grandmother's devotion to the fierce red chile becomes devotion to her homeland and heritage, transmitted bodily through the meal. The concrete image carries cultural memory more vividly than any abstract statement of belonging could.

6. **How does Baca's biography illuminate the course's theme of recovering voice?** *Model answer:* Baca learned to read and write in prison, so for him the recovery of voice was literal rather than figurative. His path makes concrete the course's recurring claim that access to language is access to selfhood and freedom.

7. **Why might Cisneros have chosen the vignette form rather than a conventional novel?** *Model answer:* The short, lyrical pieces mirror a child's episodic perception and remain accessible to readers like those she grew up among. The form is itself a statement about audience — a refusal of the conventional novel's gatekeeping.

8. **What does "A Rice Sandwich" reveal about class without naming it directly?** *Model answer:* The family's inability to afford lunch meat, the begging for a small privilege, and the resulting humiliation render poverty through concrete incident. Cisneros lets a child's disappointment carry a social truth the narrator never has to state.

9. **How do Cisneros and Baca each handle the presence of Spanish within English?** *Model answer:* Both let Spanish and bicultural reference surface naturally — in names, foods, and cadences — without translation or apology. The bilingual texture asserts that the in-between self is whole, not deficient.

10. **What does it mean that Cisneros, Baca, Kingston, Lorde, Harjo, and Erdrich were all publishing in roughly the same years?** *Model answer:* It marks the convergence the course's recurring threads anticipated — a great simultaneous surfacing of voices long present and long unheard. The 1980s become the moment the counter-canon becomes undeniable.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Some residents will treat *Mango Street* as "easy" because it reads simply; gently complicate that by slowing down on a single vignette and counting what the plain sentences carry. The Spanish words may prompt requests for translation — provide them lightly, but make the point that the bilingual texture is meaningful, not an obstacle. Baca's biography can tip into voyeurism if mishandled; present the prison story as the ground of his literacy and his art, not as spectacle, and keep the focus on the poems. As with every session, the term "Latinx" itself may draw questions; acknowledge the debates around the word briefly and move on — the point is the literature. Let the room sit with "A Rice Sandwich"; its small cruelty lands hard for anyone who has felt singled out by poverty.

## Session 12 — Asian American Voices II: Kingston & Li-Young Lee

### At a Glance

Maxine Hong Kingston's *The Woman Warrior* and two poems by Li-Young Lee carry the course deeper into Asian American literature. Kingston invented a genre between memoir, myth, and novel that claims the right to tell stories you only half-know. Lee writes some of the most formally beautiful and personally devastating poems in contemporary American verse — grief rendered in small domestic images.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Maxine Hong Kingston (b. 1940).** Kingston was born Maxine Ting Ting Hong in Stockton, California, on October 27, 1940, the eldest of six American-born children of Chinese immigrants who ran a laundry ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/maxine-hong-kingston)). Her parents had been educated in China — her mother trained as a midwife — and the family home was full of "talk-story," the Cantonese tradition of oral narrative through which her mother transmitted myth, family history, warning, and grief, often without marking where fact ended and legend began. That blurring became Kingston's great subject and method. She graduated from the University of California, Berkeley, and taught school before publishing *The Woman Warrior: Memoirs of a Girlhood Among Ghosts* (1976), the book from which "No Name Woman" is drawn ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maxine-Hong-Kingston)). It won the National Book Critics Circle Award for nonfiction and became one of the most widely taught — and most debated — works of contemporary American literature, in part because of its insistence on mixing remembered fact, inherited story, and frank imagination. Its companion volume, *China Men* (1980), won the National Book Award. Kingston has also written the novel *Tripmaster Monkey* (1989) and later works of memoir and peace activism. She received the National Medal of Arts. For the room: Kingston's innovation is precisely her refusal to separate what she knows from what she imagines — and "No Name Woman" opens by dramatizing that refusal, beginning with a secret her mother forbids her to repeat, which Kingston then repeats to the world.

**Li-Young Lee (b. 1957).** Lee was born in Jakarta, Indonesia, on August 19, 1957, to Chinese parents living in political exile, and his family history is one of the most remarkable of any American poet ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/li-young-lee)). His maternal great-grandfather had been the first president of the Republic of China; his father had served as a personal physician to Mao Zedong before the family fell out of favor ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Li-Young-Lee)). Fleeing anti-Chinese sentiment in Indonesia, the family endured a period in which Lee's father was imprisoned, then traveled through Hong Kong, Macau, and Japan before reaching the United States in 1964, where the father became a Presbyterian minister in a small Pennsylvania town. That father — learned, devout, exacting, and tender — is the central presence in Lee's poetry. His first collection, *Rose* (1986), from which both our poems come, contains his most beloved work, including "The Gift" and "Eating Alone"; *The City in Which I Love You* (1990) won the Lamont Poetry Prize. His memoir *The Winged Seed* (1995) tells the family's flight. For our purposes: Lee is the course's great poet of domestic grief — the splinter pulled from a child's hand, the meal eaten alone — and of the father, in whom the personal and the historical, the tender and the lost, are fused.

### Historical & Cultural Context

This session extends the Asian American story the course began with Sui Sin Far and Bulosan, but moves it into the post-1965 era. The Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965 ended the national-origins quota system that had effectively barred most Asian immigration since the exclusion laws, opening the way for a new and far larger wave of Asian immigrants and refugees — and for the generation of writers, born here or arriving as children, who would define contemporary Asian American literature. Kingston, American-born, writes out of the older Chinese-American community of California; Lee, a refugee child, writes out of the displacements of the mid-century Chinese diaspora. The term "Asian American" itself was coined in the late 1960s by activists who insisted that the many distinct Asian-origin communities shared a political situation in America — a deliberate act of coalition the course can name as part of the same impulse that produced "Chicano," "Black Arts," and the other self-namings of the era. Both writers also engage the model-minority stereotype the course defines in its glossary: the flattening myth that Asian Americans are uniformly successful, uncomplaining, and assimilated. Kingston's haunted, conflicted girlhood and Lee's grief-saturated poems are, among other things, refusals of that flattening — insistences that these lives have interiors, ghosts, and losses the stereotype erases.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"No Name Woman" (opening of *The Woman Warrior*, 1976).** The chapter begins with Kingston's mother issuing a command: "You must not tell anyone what I am about to tell you." She then tells the story of Kingston's father's sister in China — an aunt the family has erased. While the aunt's husband was away in America, she became pregnant by another man (or was raped — the story does not say). On the night she gave birth, the villagers, enraged at the disgrace, raided the family farm: they slaughtered animals, smeared blood, destroyed property, and screamed. The aunt gave birth in a pigsty and, the next morning, drowned herself and the newborn in the family well. The family's punishment was to never speak of her — to deny she had ever been born. Kingston's mother tells the story as a warning to the newly menstruating Kingston about the dangers of female sexuality. But Kingston, in the assigned opening, takes the forbidden story and begins to imagine her aunt's inner life — inventing possible versions (the aunt as victim, as romantic, as quiet rebel) — and in so doing both betrays her mother's command and restores to the "no name woman" the human interior her family denied her.

**"Eating Alone" (1986).** The speaker, working in his autumn garden, pulls the last onions and recalls a moment when he thought he saw his dead father stooping among the pears — but it was only the wind, only a shovel leaning. He prepares a simple meal alone — green onions, sesame oil, rice — and the poem ends with the quiet, devastating line that there is "nothing so sweet" as the food, "and my loneliness." Grief is rendered entirely through the domestic ritual of cooking and eating; the father's absence inhabits the ordinary garden and kitchen.

**"The Gift" (1986).** The speaker recalls his father removing a metal splinter from his palm when he was a boy — the father's low voice, his tenderness, the way the story he told distracted the child from pain and fear. As an adult, the speaker removes a splinter from his wife's hand and recognizes that what his father gave him was not just relief from a splinter but a way of loving — tenderness passed from hand to hand across generations. The "gift" of the title is this inherited capacity for gentle care; the poem ends with the grown speaker kissing his father's hand in memory.

### Lecture Script

Today we go deeper into Asian American writing, into the years after 1965, when the immigration laws finally changed and a new generation of writers — some born here, some arriving as children — began to define this literature. [pause] And we begin with a book that did something genuinely new with form.

*The Woman Warrior.* Listen to how it opens. Kingston's mother says to her: "You must not tell anyone what I am about to tell you." [pause] And then Kingston tells us — and the whole world. Right there, in the first sentence, is the book's daring. It is built on a betrayal. Her mother tells her a secret precisely so that she will keep it, and Kingston breaks the silence on the first page.

What is the secret? An aunt in China — her father's sister — who became pregnant while her husband was away, was attacked by the enraged village on the night she gave birth, and drowned herself and her baby in the family well. [pause] And the family's punishment was to erase her: to never speak of her, to deny she was ever born. The "no name woman." Kingston's mother tells the story as a warning — this is what happens to a woman who steps wrong.

But watch what Kingston does, because this is the heart of today. [pause] She takes this forbidden, half-known story and she begins to imagine into it. She invents possible versions of the aunt. Maybe she was raped and had no choice. Maybe she fell in love. Maybe she was a quiet rebel who wanted one thing of her own. Kingston does not know — she cannot know — and she refuses to pretend she knows. Instead she does something I find very moving: she gives the no-name woman back her interior. The family erased her; Kingston imagines her into personhood again. [pause]

Now, this troubled some readers. Is it memoir if she's making things up? Should a memoir separate fact from imagination? I want you to sit with that question rather than answer it too fast, because Kingston's whole point is that for a girl raised on "talk-story" — on her mother's oral tales that never marked where history ended and legend began — the truth of a life and the imagining of a life cannot be cleanly separated. [pause] The imagining is how she knows her aunt at all.

Now Li-Young Lee, and a complete change of scale. [pause] Where Kingston gives us a village and a well, Lee gives us a kitchen. His father is the great presence in his poems — a learned, exacting, tender man who had been, of all things, a physician to Mao before the family fled into exile. But Lee almost never tells you that history directly. Instead he gives you a splinter.

"The Gift." [pause] The speaker remembers being a boy, and his father pulling a metal splinter from his palm — the low voice, the story the father told to distract him from the pain. And then, grown, the speaker pulls a splinter from his own wife's hand and realizes: what his father gave him was not just relief from a splinter. It was a way of loving. Tenderness, handed from one generation to the next, hand to hand. [pause] And in "Eating Alone," the father is dead, and the speaker thinks he sees him stooping in the garden — but it is only the wind. He cooks a simple meal and eats it alone, and the poem ends: there is nothing so sweet as the food, and his loneliness. [pause]

So here is the pairing. Kingston, telling the forbidden story to restore a woman to personhood. Lee, telling the smallest domestic story — a splinter, an onion — to hold the largest grief. Both of them refusing the flattening stereotype that says these lives are simple, successful, untroubled. [pause] These lives have ghosts. These lives have wells and gardens full of the dead. That is what the literature is for. [pause]

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**The forbidden opening.** "You must not tell anyone what I am about to tell you." Have residents notice that the book's first act is to break this command. The betrayal is structural, not incidental — Kingston founds her art on the refusal of imposed silence, the course's deepest theme.

**The invented aunt.** Kingston offers several incompatible versions of the aunt's story (victim, romantic, rebel) without choosing. The technique restores interiority to an erased woman precisely by refusing a single authorized account — multiplicity as a form of respect.

**Lee, "The Gift" — the transferred tenderness.** The adult speaker removing his wife's splinter mirrors the father removing his. The poem's argument is that what is inherited is not the splinter but the manner of love. Trace how the two scenes are laid over each other.

**Lee, "Eating Alone" — the false vision.** The figure thought to be the father turns out to be wind and a leaning shovel. The deflation is the grief: the dead are everywhere and nowhere in the ordinary garden.

**Lee, "Eating Alone" — the final line.** "nothing so sweet" as the food "and my loneliness." The yoking of sweetness and loneliness in one phrase is the poem's quiet devastation; the grief is not bitter but tender.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **The book opens with the mother's command not to tell — and Kingston tells. What is she doing with that betrayal? Is it a betrayal?** *Model answer:* She founds her art on breaking an imposed silence, which is both a betrayal of her mother's command and an act of restoration toward the erased aunt. The course's theme reframes it: refusing enforced silence is not betrayal but witness.

2. **Kingston mixes what she knows, imagines, and was told, without always distinguishing them. Does that trouble you? Should memoir separate fact from imagination?** *Model answer:* Reasonable readers differ, and that is the point. Kingston argues that a life known only through "talk-story" cannot be cleanly divided into fact and legend, so imagining becomes a legitimate mode of knowing rather than a violation of memoir.

3. **"The Gift" is about a father removing a splinter. What is the gift of the title?** *Model answer:* The gift is not relief from the splinter but the inherited capacity for tenderness — a way of loving passed hand to hand. The adult speaker realizes this only when he repeats his father's gesture with his wife.

4. **"Eating Alone" ends with his father appearing among the onions. How does grief work in small domestic images?** *Model answer:* The father is glimpsed and then revealed to be only wind, so the loss inhabits an ordinary garden and meal. Grief is rendered as the dead person's presence in and absence from the smallest daily acts.

5. **Both Kingston and Lee are American writers with deep ties to Chinese history. How do they negotiate the hyphen in "Chinese-American"?** *Model answer:* Neither resolves the hyphen; both inhabit it. Kingston makes the gap between her mother's China and her American girlhood her subject, while Lee carries the diaspora's losses into American kitchens — the in-between is not a problem to solve but the place they write from.

6. **Why does Kingston offer multiple versions of the aunt's story rather than one?** *Model answer:* The multiplicity restores the aunt's interiority by refusing a single authorized account, honoring the unknowability of an erased life. It also models the course's value of polyphony — many possible truths held at once.

7. **How do these works refuse the "model minority" stereotype?** *Model answer:* They insist that these lives contain ghosts, shame, grief, and conflict rather than uncomplicated success. The stereotype flattens; Kingston and Lee restore depth, which is itself a quiet act of resistance.

8. **What role does the father play in Lee's poetry?** *Model answer:* The father is the fused site of the personal and the historical — learned, exacting, tender, and lost — through whom Lee approaches both private grief and the family's exile. The history enters obliquely, through gesture and image rather than statement.

9. **What does "talk-story" contribute to Kingston's method?** *Model answer:* It is the oral tradition that blurs history and legend, and it licenses Kingston's blending of fact and imagination. Her form is the written extension of her mother's inherited way of telling.

10. **What is the effect of placing Kingston's village and Lee's kitchen side by side?** *Model answer:* It shows the range of Asian American storytelling — epic erasure and intimate domestic grief — and demonstrates that the largest matters can be carried at any scale. ## Session 13 — Native American Voices II: Harjo & Erdrich

### At a Glance

Two poems by Joy Harjo — the first Native American U.S. Poet Laureate — and the opening chapter of Louise Erdrich's *Love Medicine* represent the full maturity of contemporary Native American literature: formally sophisticated, spiritually grounded, and unsparing about history. This session returns to the course's first Native session (Winnemucca and Zitkala-Ša) and shows how far the tradition has traveled.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Joy Harjo (b. 1951).** Harjo was born in Tulsa, Oklahoma, on May 9, 1951, and is an enrolled member of the Muscogee (Creek) Nation ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/joy-harjo)). She came of age during the activism of the American Indian Movement and the broader Native cultural resurgence of the 1970s, studied at the Institute of American Indian Arts and the University of New Mexico, and earned an MFA from the Iowa Writers' Workshop. Her poetry — collected in volumes including *She Had Some Horses* (1983), from which both our poems come, *In Mad Love and War* (1990), and *An American Sunrise* (2019) — fuses Muscogee story and ceremony, the landscapes of Oklahoma and the Southwest, jazz, and a deep historical consciousness of removal and survival ([poets.org](https://poets.org/poet/joy-harjo)). She is also a saxophonist and performer who often sets her poems to music. In 2019 she was named the 23rd Poet Laureate of the United States — the first Native American to hold the office — and served three terms. For our purposes: Harjo's signature is the incantatory, ceremonial use of repetition. "Remember" is built as a series of imperatives; "She Had Some Horses" turns a single repeated line into a vast catalog. Both draw on the oral and ritual traditions the course first met in the Native session — but now fully fused with contemporary American poetic form.

**Louise Erdrich (b. 1954).** Erdrich was born in Little Falls, Minnesota, on June 7, 1954, and grew up in Wahpeton, North Dakota, the daughter of a German-American father and a mother of Ojibwe (Chippewa) and French descent; she is an enrolled member of the Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/louise-erdrich)). Her grandparents had been tribal leaders, and the reservation world of her heritage became the setting for her major fiction. She was among the first class of women admitted to Dartmouth College, where she met the anthropologist Michael Dorris, later her husband and collaborator; she earned an MA at Johns Hopkins ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louise-Erdrich)). Her first novel, *Love Medicine* (1984), from which our reading is drawn, won the National Book Critics Circle Award and launched an interconnected series of novels that follow several Ojibwe and mixed families across generations on and around a North Dakota reservation — a sustained, polyphonic chronicle often compared to Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha. Her later novels include *The Round House* (2012), which won the National Book Award, and *The Night Watchman* (2020), which won the Pulitzer Prize. She owns Birchbark Books, an independent bookstore in Minneapolis. For the room: Erdrich's method is polyphony — *Love Medicine* is told through many narrators across time — and "The World's Greatest Fishermen" opens that chorus, beginning not with explanation but with a death and a family already in motion.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Harjo and Erdrich belong to what scholars call the Native American Renaissance — the flowering of Native literature that began with N. Scott Momaday's Pulitzer-winning *House Made of Dawn* (1968) and accelerated through the 1970s and '80s with writers including Leslie Marmon Silko, James Welch, and Simon Ortiz. This literary surge ran parallel to a political one: the American Indian Movement, the 1969–71 occupation of Alcatraz, the 1973 standoff at Wounded Knee, and a broad reassertion of tribal sovereignty and cultural pride. The history these writers carry is the one the course met in Session 6 — removal, the reservation system, the boarding schools that tried to erase Native languages and identities, the long federal assault on tribal life. But where Winnemucca and Zitkala-Ša wrote as witnesses pleading to a white readership in the only forms then available to them, Harjo and Erdrich write from a position of cultural confidence, addressing Native and non-Native readers alike and drawing openly on tribal story, ceremony, and worldview as literary resources rather than as material to be explained or defended. Their work also reframes "memory" as the course's recurring threads predict: for Native writers, as Harjo has said, remembering is itself an act of resistance against a history designed to make Native people disappear. Place this session, for residents, as the answer to Session 6 — the same survival, two generations on, no longer pleading but singing.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Remember" (1983).** The poem is a litany of imperatives — "Remember" repeated as the opening of nearly every clause. The speaker instructs the reader to remember the sky and the stars at the moment of birth, the moon, the sun, one's mother and father, the earth whose skin we are, the plants and animals who are alive and have their own stories, the wind and its voice, language itself as it "came from this." It builds toward the culminating instruction: "Remember you are all people and all people are you. / Remember you are this universe and this universe is you." The poem fuses self, kin, nature, and cosmos into a single web, using the ceremonial form of repeated command to bind them.

**"She Had Some Horses" (1983).** The poem uses the refrain "She had some horses" to generate a long, paradoxical catalog. The horses are contradictory and plural: horses of sand and of red clay, horses who were skins of ocean water, horses who licked razor blades, horses who were afraid and horses who were not, horses who lied and horses who told the truth, horses who waited for destruction and horses who waited for resurrection. The horses figure the fractured, multiple aspects of a self (often read as a Native woman's self) — beauty and damage, fear and courage held together. The poem's closing turn reveals that "these were the same horses" — that the contradictory selves are one, loved and hated at once.

**"The World's Greatest Fishermen" (opening chapter of *Love Medicine*, 1984).** The chapter opens on the death of June Kashpaw, a Chippewa woman, on the snowy plains of North Dakota on the eve of Easter. June, worn down by years of hard living, accepts a drink and a sexual encounter from a stranger (a mud engineer) in Williston; afterward, instead of going home, she walks out into a sudden spring snowstorm and freezes to death, "walking home" across the fields. The narrative then shifts to the extended Kashpaw and Lamartine family gathering weeks later, told in part by June's niece Albertine, who returns to the reservation and finds the family already quarreling, grieving, and reckoning with June's death and the complicated loves and resentments among them. The chapter introduces, in fragments, the web of relations the rest of the novel will unfold, and establishes Erdrich's polyphonic, time-shifting method: we begin in the middle of a family's long story, with a death that ripples outward.

### Lecture Script

Two weeks of this course ago — well, Session 6 — we met the first Native voices, Sarah Winnemucca and Zitkala-Ša, writing in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. [pause] I want you to remember how they had to write: as witnesses, pleading their people's case to a white readership, in the only literary forms then available to them. Today we meet the answer to that session, two generations on. Joy Harjo and Louise Erdrich do not plead. They sing, and they tell, from a position of complete cultural confidence. [pause]

Start with Harjo. She is, as I mentioned, the first Native American Poet Laureate of the United States. And her great instrument is repetition — but not decorative repetition. Ceremonial repetition. Listen to "Remember." [pause] It is built almost entirely out of one repeated command: "Remember." Remember the sky you were born under. Remember the moon. Remember your mother. Remember the earth whose skin you are. And it builds and builds until it reaches: "Remember you are all people and all people are you." [pause] That repeated imperative is not a poetic trick. It is the form of ceremony, of prayer, of the oral tradition — the form that binds a community together by saying the words together. Harjo takes that ancient form and makes a contemporary American poem out of it.

And here is something Harjo has said that I want you to carry. For Native people, she says, the act of remembering is an act of resistance. [pause] Think about why. There was a centuries-long effort — removal, the reservation, the boarding schools that took the children and forbade the languages — designed precisely to make Native people forget who they were, and so to disappear. Against that, simply to remember — to say the sky, the moon, the mother, the language — is to refuse to vanish. [pause]

Now "She Had Some Horses." This one is stranger and I love it. The refrain is "She had some horses," and Harjo runs an enormous catalog through it. Horses of sand and red clay. Horses who were the skins of ocean water. Horses who licked razor blades. Horses who were afraid, horses who were not. Horses who told the truth, horses who lied. [pause] What are the horses? Most readers take them as the contradictory aspects of a single self — a Native woman's self, beautiful and damaged, brave and terrified, all at once. And then comes the turn at the end: "these were the same horses." The contradictions are one being. The self she loved and the self she hated were never two. [pause]

Now turn to Erdrich, and to a different power: the power of the chorus. [pause] *Love Medicine* is told by many narrators across many years, a whole reservation world built up voice by voice — people compare it to Faulkner. And look at how the first chapter begins. Not with explanation. Not with "here is the reservation, here are the rules." It begins with a death. A woman named June Kashpaw, worn down by a hard life, in Williston, North Dakota, on the day before Easter. She takes a drink and a stranger, and then instead of going home she walks out into a sudden spring snowstorm — and she freezes, "walking home" across the fields. [pause] And then the chapter pulls back to the family weeks later, already grieving and quarreling, told partly by June's niece Albertine. We are dropped into the middle of a long story, and we have to find our footing the way you do when you walk into a family you don't yet know. That is deliberate. Erdrich trusts you to keep up. [pause]

So here is the day. Harjo's ceremony and Erdrich's chorus — repetition that binds, and polyphony that builds a world. Two Native writers, in the same decade, no longer explaining themselves to anyone, simply making literature of the highest order out of their own traditions. [pause] That is what survival sounds like when it has had two more generations to find its voice. [pause]

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**Harjo, "Remember" — the imperative.** Every clause begins with the command "Remember." Have residents feel how the repetition functions as ceremony rather than emphasis — it enacts communal prayer. The widening scope (sky, kin, earth, language, cosmos) culminates in the dissolution of the boundary between self and all things.

**Harjo, "Remember" — "you are all people and all people are you."** The closing fusion is the poem's argument: memory binds the individual into the whole web of life. Tie this to Harjo's claim that for Native people remembering is resistance.

**Harjo, "She Had Some Horses" — the contradictory catalog.** The horses are paired in opposites (afraid/not afraid, lied/told the truth). The refrain holds the contradictions in suspension until the final revelation that "these were the same horses." Note how the form refuses to resolve the self into a single thing.

**Erdrich, the opening death.** June "walking home" into the snow is the novel's originating event. Note that Erdrich gives us interiority and tenderness for June even as she dies — the death is not sensationalized but quietly devastating, and it sets the whole family chronicle in motion.

**Erdrich, the shift to the family.** The pivot from June's solitary death to the crowded, quarreling family gathering establishes the polyphonic method. The reader is not oriented from outside but dropped inside — a deliberate demand of trust.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **"Remember" is built as a series of instructions. Who is being told to remember, and why does the imperative suit the content?** *Model answer:* The reader — and, by extension, a community — is instructed to remember origin, kin, earth, and language. The imperative suits the content because it reproduces the form of ceremony and prayer, binding the listener into the web the poem describes.

2. **"She Had Some Horses" repeats its title line as refrain and variation. What are the horses? Are they the same in every stanza?** *Model answer:* The horses figure the contradictory aspects of a single self — fearful and brave, lying and truthful, damaged and beautiful. They are not the same until the poem's final turn reveals that "these were the same horses," collapsing the contradictions into one being.

3. **What does Erdrich achieve in a short opening chapter that a history book cannot?** *Model answer:* She renders the reservation world from inside, through a particular death and a particular family's grief and friction, so that history is felt as lived relation rather than summarized as fact. The chorus of voices conveys a community's texture no chronicle could.

4. **Harjo has said that for Native people remembering is an act of resistance. What does that mean, and does it apply to other communities?** *Model answer:* Because removal, reservations, and boarding schools were designed to erase Native identity, the act of remembering refuses that erasure and asserts continued existence. The principle extends to any community whose history was suppressed — recovery of memory is recovery of self, a course-long thread.

5. **Harjo and Erdrich were writing in the 1980s, alongside Kingston, Cisneros, and Lorde. What does that simultaneity mean?** *Model answer:* It marks the convergence the course has traced — many long-unheard traditions reaching full literary maturity at once. The 1980s become the decade the counter-canon becomes undeniable across communities.

6. **How does this session answer Session 6 (Winnemucca and Zitkala-Ša)?** *Model answer:* The earlier writers pleaded to a white readership in borrowed forms; Harjo and Erdrich write from cultural confidence, drawing on tribal story and ceremony as literary resources without explanation or apology. The survival is the same; the stance has changed from witness to mastery.

7. **Why might Erdrich begin with a death rather than an introduction?** *Model answer:* The death of June ripples through the family and generates the novel's web of relations, so beginning there starts the story in motion rather than in exposition. It also demands the reader's trust, refusing to orient them from outside the community.

8. **How does Harjo's use of repetition differ from mere emphasis?** *Model answer:* Her repetition is ceremonial — it reproduces the structure of prayer and oral ritual, performing communal binding rather than underscoring a point. The form carries cultural meaning beyond rhetorical force.

9. **What is the effect of the contradictions in "She Had Some Horses" being finally named "the same horses"?** *Model answer:* It insists that a self is not required to be consistent to be whole, and that what one loves and what one hates in oneself may be identical. The resolution honors complexity rather than erasing it.

10. **What do Harjo's ceremony and Erdrich's polyphony share as approaches to community?** *Model answer:* Both build the collective out of the literary form itself — repetition that binds a congregation, and many voices that compose a world. Each makes community a structural fact of the writing, not just a theme.

### Anticipated Difficulties

June's death — involving alcohol, a casual sexual encounter, and freezing in the snow — risks activating stereotypes about Native people and drinking; address this head-on by noting Erdrich's tender, interior treatment of June and the historical conditions behind such losses, and by insisting the room read her as a full person, not an emblem. Non-Native residents may want to "decode" the ceremonial elements of Harjo's poems as exotic; redirect to the universal human work the forms perform (memory, prayer, the divided self). The polyphonic opening of *Love Medicine* disorients some readers; reassure the room that the confusion is intended and that footing comes with patience. As in Session 6, the boarding-school and removal history is essential context; lay it out plainly. Let the final line of "She Had Some Horses" sit before discussion.

## Session 14 — Contemporary Black Writers: Morrison & Tracy K. Smith

### At a Glance

Toni Morrison — the only American-born Nobel Laureate in Literature since 1938 — and Tracy K. Smith, a former U.S. Poet Laureate, anchor the course's turn to the contemporary. We read the opening three pages of *Beloved* and two poems by Smith that move between the domestic and the cosmic. Together they ask what language owes to grief, and what the difference is between a ghost and a memory.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Toni Morrison (1931–2019).** Morrison was born Chloe Ardelia Wofford in Lorain, Ohio, on February 18, 1931, into a working-class Black family steeped in folklore, music, and storytelling ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/toni-morrison)). She studied at Howard University and Cornell, taught, and then spent years as a senior editor at Random House — where she championed Black writers and shaped the publishing landscape — before her own fiction made her one of the towering figures of world literature ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Toni-Morrison)). Her novels include *The Bluest Eye* (1970), *Sula* (1973), *Song of Solomon* (1977), and *Beloved* (1987), which won the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction and was later cited prominently when she received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1993 — the first Black woman, and the first American-born writer since 1938, to win it. *Beloved* was inspired by the historical case of Margaret Garner, an enslaved woman who, having escaped, killed her own child rather than see her returned to slavery. Morrison famously said she did not write for or explain herself to white readers; she wrote from inside Black life, on its own terms, for its own people. Her prose fuses the cadences of the spirituals and the sermon with a modernist density and a willingness to render the unspeakable. She died in 2019. For our purposes: *Beloved* opens not with orientation but with disorientation — "124 was spiteful" — and the first three pages are a masterclass in how a writer can drop a reader inside trauma's logic, where a house holds a grudge and a ghost is simply a fact of the household.

**Tracy K. Smith (b. 1972).** Smith was born in Falmouth, Massachusetts, on April 16, 1972, and raised in Fairfield, California, the youngest of five children; her father was an engineer who worked on the Hubble Space Telescope, a detail that surfaces in her cosmic imagery ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/tracy-k-smith)). She studied at Harvard, where she was part of the Dark Room Collective of Black writers, and earned an MFA at Columbia. Her collections include *The Body's Question* (2003), *Duende* (2007), from which our first poem comes, and *Life on Mars* (2011), which won the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry and from which "The Weather in Space" comes ([poets.org](https://poets.org/poet/tracy-k-smith)). *Life on Mars*, written partly in elegy for her father, uses outer space — David Bowie, science fiction, the Hubble — as a vocabulary for grief, distance, and the divine. She served as the 22nd Poet Laureate of the United States from 2017 to 2019, traveling especially to rural communities to bring poetry to readers far from literary centers. She has also written a memoir, *Ordinary Light* (2015). For the room: Smith is the course's poet of scale — she moves from a kitchen to the cosmos in a few lines, and asks whether God might be "an astronaut / Orbiting very slowly," distant and possibly indifferent. Pair her ease of scale with Morrison's compression of trauma into a house number.

### Historical & Cultural Context

This session brings the course's long engagement with Black American writing to the present — from Douglass's nineteenth-century slave narrative to a twenty-first-century Pulitzer poet. *Beloved* (1987) is itself a work of historical recovery: Morrison turned to the era of slavery and its immediate aftermath (the novel is set in 1873 Cincinnati, with the trauma of slavery time pressing through) to write what she called a story for "the sixty million and more" — the uncounted dead of the Middle Passage and bondage, to whom the book is dedicated. It belongs to a wave of late-twentieth-century literature reckoning anew with slavery's unfinished aftermath, and to Morrison's larger project of writing Black interiority without reference to a white gaze. Smith writes a generation later, in a moment when a Black woman could be Poet Laureate of the United States and win the Pulitzer for poems about her father and the Hubble telescope — a measure of how far the institutional landscape had shifted, even as the griefs the poems address remain intimate and universal. Both writers engage the course's recurring thread about audience: Morrison's deliberate refusal to explain Black life to outsiders, and Smith's confident movement across registers — domestic, cosmic, theological — without asking permission. Place them, for residents, as the contemporary culmination of the Black literary tradition the course has followed from Session 1, and as proof that the question "what does language owe to grief?" has no expiration date.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Opening three pages of *Beloved* (1987).** The novel opens: "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom." 124 is the address of a house on the edge of Cincinnati, haunted by the ghost of a dead baby. In these first pages we learn that the house's inhabitants are Sethe, a formerly enslaved woman, and her surviving daughter Denver; Sethe's two sons, Howard and Buglar, have already fled the house, driven off by the ghost's rages (a shattered mirror, tiny handprints in a cake). Baby Suggs, Sethe's mother-in-law, has died. The ghost is the spirit of Sethe's baby daughter, whose throat Sethe cut years earlier rather than let her be taken back into slavery, and whose tombstone reads only "Beloved" — the single word Sethe could pay for (with ten minutes of sex with the engraver). The opening establishes the haunting as ordinary fact of the household, introduces Sethe and Denver's isolated life, and sets the stage for the arrival of Paul D, a man from Sethe's enslaved past at the plantation ironically named Sweet Home, whose appearance will disturb the fragile equilibrium. The three pages drop the reader inside trauma's logic, where grief is literal enough to break furniture.

**"Duende" (2007).** The poem takes its title from the Spanish/Flamenco term for the dark, possessed spirit of deep artistic feeling — the soul that art summons in the presence of death. Smith's poem moves associatively through images of song, the body, exile, and political violence (it includes a section evoking the disappeared and the silenced under authoritarian regimes), asking what it is in art that comes alive only at the edge of suffering. The poem connects the ecstatic and the mournful: duende is the force that makes a singer's voice break with truth, the spirit that "wants to die" and yet sings. It holds private feeling and public atrocity in the same frame.

**"The Weather in Space" (2011).** The opening poem of *Life on Mars*, it asks: "Is God being or pure force? The wind / Or what commands it?" It moves through images of the body, water, weather, and the cosmos, meditating on whether the divine is present or merely a distant motion. It closes on the indelible image of God as "an astronaut / Orbiting very slowly" — a deity reconceived as remote, mechanical, possibly indifferent, circling the human world at an unreachable distance. The poem sets the elegiac, space-haunted tone of the collection written in the wake of her father's death.

### Lecture Script

We have come a long way. [pause] We began this course with Frederick Douglass, born into slavery, teaching himself the forbidden alphabet. Today we read a novel about slavery written by the only American-born Nobel laureate in literature since 1938 — and a poet who was Poet Laureate of the United States and won the Pulitzer for poems about her father and the Hubble telescope. The distance between those two points is the distance this course has traveled.

Let's begin with Morrison, and with the first sentence of *Beloved*. [pause] "124 was spiteful." Three words. And notice — Morrison does not tell you that 124 is a house. She does not orient you. She drops you inside a world where a house has a personality, where a house can be spiteful, "full of a baby's venom." You are disoriented, and that is exactly the point. [pause] You are being placed inside the logic of trauma, where the grief is so total that it has soaked into the walls.

What is haunting the house? The ghost of a dead baby — and over the course of the novel we learn the unbearable thing, which I will tell you now because it is the engine of the book: Sethe, who had escaped slavery, killed her own infant daughter rather than let the child be taken back into bondage. The tombstone says only one word — "Beloved" — because that is the one word Sethe could afford to have carved. [pause] And the ghost is not a metaphor in this house. The sons have run away because of it. There are handprints in the cake. The haunting is simply a fact of the household, the way a draft is a fact, or grief is a fact.

Now I want to tell you something Morrison said, because it bears directly on a thread we've followed all term. [pause] She said she never explained Sethe's choice to white readers, because she did not write the book for white readers. She wrote it from inside Black life, on its own terms. Think about what a radical decision that is for a writer in America — to decide who your book is for, and to refuse to translate yourself for anyone else. We saw the seed of that question all the way back in Dunbar, in the mask, in the politics of audience. Morrison answers it with total confidence. [pause]

Now Tracy K. Smith, and a complete shift of scale. [pause] Smith's father was an engineer who worked on the Hubble Space Telescope, and when he died she wrote a book — *Life on Mars* — that reaches for outer space as a language for grief. Listen to how "The Weather in Space" ends. She has been asking whether God is "being or pure force," and she lands on this: "God is an astronaut / Orbiting very slowly." [pause] What kind of God is that? A God who is up there, circling, mechanical, unreachably far — present, maybe, but at a distance that feels a great deal like absence. That is grief reaching all the way to the edge of the cosmos and finding it cold.

And in "Duende," she reaches the other direction — into the body, into song, into political violence and the disappeared — to ask what it is in art that only comes alive at the edge of suffering. Duende is that dark spirit, the thing that makes a singer's voice break with truth. [pause]

So here is the day, and here is the question that holds Morrison and Smith together: what does language owe to grief? [pause] Morrison compresses a mother's unbearable act into a house number. Smith expands a daughter's loss into the orbit of the cosmos. Compression and expansion again — we've seen that before, with Baldwin and Brooks. And one more question for you to carry: what is the difference between a ghost and a memory? Morrison's ghost breaks the furniture. Smith's father orbits very slowly. [pause] Hold those two against each other, and you have today. [pause]

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**"124 was spiteful."** The opening's refusal to orient is the technique. Morrison grants the house a temperament before revealing it is a house, placing the reader inside trauma's logic. Have residents notice how much of the horror is conveyed by treating the supernatural as mundane.

**The single word "Beloved."** The tombstone bears one word because that is all Sethe could pay for — and the price (ten minutes with the engraver) compounds the violation. The book's title is thus an economy of grief: love measured against what slavery permitted a mother to afford.

**The fleeing sons and the handprints.** The domestic evidence of the haunting (the shattered mirror, the handprints in the cake, the boys driven off) renders the ghost as household fact. The effect is to make grief literal and physical, not figurative.

**Smith, "The Weather in Space" — the closing image.** "God is an astronaut / Orbiting very slowly." Examine the theology: divinity recast as remote, mechanical motion. Ask whether the image offers comfort or its withdrawal — the ambiguity is the point.

**Smith, "Duende" — the yoking of song and atrocity.** The poem holds ecstatic art and political violence in one frame, locating duende at the seam where beauty and suffering meet. Trace how Smith modulates between the intimate and the historical without a seam showing.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Morrison begins with a house number — "124 was spiteful." What does she accomplish in the first three pages, and why is the opening so disorienting?** *Model answer:* She places the reader inside trauma's logic, where grief saturates a house until it has a temperament. The disorientation mirrors the characters' condition and refuses to ease the reader in, insisting that this world be entered on its own terms.

2. **Morrison said she never explained Sethe's choice to white readers because she did not write for them. What does it mean for a novelist to decide who their audience is?** *Model answer:* It is an assertion of creative sovereignty — the refusal to translate or justify Black experience for outsiders. It answers the course's audience thread (Dunbar's mask) with full confidence, writing from inside a community rather than performing for one outside it.

3. **"Duende" moves through the Challenger-era cosmos, suicide, love, and political violence. How does Smith hold all of that in one poem?** *Model answer:* She locates duende — the dark spirit art summons at the edge of death — as the common thread, so disparate images cohere around the question of what comes alive in suffering. The associative movement is held together by a single emotional logic rather than narrative.

4. **"The Weather in Space" ends "God is an astronaut / Orbiting very slowly." What kind of God is this?** *Model answer:* A remote, mechanical deity, present but unreachably distant — divinity reimagined as cold orbit. The image registers grief's experience of a God who does not intervene, leaving comfort and abandonment ambiguously fused.

5. **Both writers are interested in what haunts us. What is the difference between a ghost and a memory?** *Model answer:* In Morrison the ghost is literal and active — it breaks mirrors and drives off the living; in Smith the dead orbit at a distance, present only as absence. The pairing suggests a ghost is a grief that still acts upon the world, while a memory is one held at a remove.

6. **How does the single word "Beloved" function as the novel's title and moral center?** *Model answer:* It is the only word Sethe could afford to carve, so the title measures love against the economy slavery imposed. It names both the murdered child and the impossible love that drove the act.

7. **How does this session complete the arc that began with Douglass?** *Model answer:* The course moves from a man teaching himself the forbidden alphabet to a Nobel laureate and a Poet Laureate writing on their own terms. The distance measures both the persistence of the griefs and the transformation of who gets to author the record.

8. **How does Smith use her father's profession in her cosmic imagery?** *Model answer:* Her father worked on the Hubble telescope, so the instruments of looking into deep space become her vocabulary for looking into loss. The personal biography grounds the cosmic scale in intimate grief.

9. **Compare Morrison's compression with Smith's expansion as responses to grief.** *Model answer:* Morrison concentrates an unbearable act into a house number and a single carved word; Smith dilates a private death into the orbit of the cosmos. As with Baldwin and Brooks, the pairing shows opposite scales serving the same emotional truth.

10. **What does it mean that a Black woman could be U.S. Poet Laureate and Pulitzer winner in Smith's generation?** *Model answer:* It marks a transformed institutional landscape relative to the course's earlier writers, even as the griefs the poems address remain intimate and unresolved. The change is in access and authority, not in the depth of the human material.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The premise of *Beloved* — a mother killing her child to save her from slavery — is among the most harrowing in American literature; state it plainly and with gravity before reading, and give the room a moment. Morrison's deliberate disorientation frustrates some readers who want to be oriented; reassure them the confusion is intended and will resolve. A white room may feel implicitly addressed by Morrison's statement that she did not write for white readers; let that sit honestly rather than smoothing it over — it is one of the course's most clarifying moments about audience. Smith's cosmic register can read as cold or abstract; ground it in the biographical fact of her father's death and his Hubble work. As always, let silence follow the first page of *Beloved*; do not rush to interpret.

## Session 15 — Immigrant Voices: Lahiri & Ocean Vuong

### At a Glance

Jhumpa Lahiri's "A Temporary Matter" and two works by Ocean Vuong bring the course to the contemporary immigrant and refugee experience. Lahiri writes the Indian-American family with a surgeon's precision and a deep tenderness; Vuong — Vietnamese-American, queer, a refugee's child — reinvents what American English can say. Both write the person who belongs fully to neither place.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Jhumpa Lahiri (b. 1967).** Lahiri was born Nilanjana Sudeshna Lahiri in London on July 11, 1967, to Bengali Indian immigrant parents, and was raised in Kingston, Rhode Island, where her father worked as a university librarian ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/jhumpa-lahiri)). Growing up between her parents' Bengali world and American life — visiting Calcutta for long stretches, never quite belonging wholly to either place — gave her the central subject of her early fiction: the immigrant family and the second generation negotiating two cultures. She earned multiple graduate degrees from Boston University, including a PhD in Renaissance studies, before her debut story collection *Interpreter of Maladies* (1999) — which opens with "A Temporary Matter" — won the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction, a rare honor for a first book and for a short-story collection ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jhumpa-Lahiri)). Her novel *The Namesake* (2003) was widely read and adapted to film; later collections and novels followed, and in a striking late turn she began writing in Italian, having moved to Rome, and translating her own work. For our purposes: Lahiri is a master of restraint. Her sentences are plain and exact; her devastations are quiet. "A Temporary Matter" turns a series of scheduled power outages into the occasion for a marriage's unraveling, and the precision of her prose is what makes the pain bearable to read.

**Ocean Vuong (b. 1988).** Vuong was born in Ho Chi Minh City (Saigon), Vietnam, on October 14, 1988, and spent part of his early childhood in a refugee camp in the Philippines before his family — led by his mother and grandmother, who had survived the war — resettled in Hartford, Connecticut, when he was two ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/ocean-vuong)). He has said he was the first in his family to learn to read fluently, and did not read proficiently in English until around age eleven; his mother, who worked in a nail salon, was illiterate, and the household's language was a hard-won, improvised English ([poets.org](https://poets.org/poet/ocean-vuong)). Out of that history Vuong made a poetry of startling originality. His debut collection *Night Sky with Exit Wounds* (2016), which contains "Someday I'll Love Ocean Vuong," won the T. S. Eliot Prize; his novel *On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous* (2019), written as a letter from a son to his illiterate mother, was a best-seller and finalist for major awards. He received a MacArthur Fellowship in 2019. His work braids the Vietnam War's long aftermath, queer desire, family love and violence, and the immigrant's remade language. For the room: Vuong is the course's living proof of the recovery of voice — a refugee child, late to English, who became one of the most celebrated poets of his generation — and his work asks what can be built from a language one was not born holding.

### Historical & Cultural Context

This session gathers two strands of the post-1965 immigration the course has been tracking. Lahiri's parents were part of the wave of educated South Asian professionals who came to the United States after the 1965 immigration reforms opened the door; her fiction documents the interior life of that immigration — the comfortable but dislocated middle-class family, the second generation fluent in America and haunted by an ancestral elsewhere. Vuong's family came as refugees in the aftermath of the Vietnam War, part of the Southeast Asian refugee resettlement of the late 1970s and 1980s; his work carries the war's violence and the refugee's particular dislocation, compounded by poverty, trauma, and a household without English. Together they show that "immigrant literature" is not one experience but many — the professional and the refugee, the comfortable and the desperate, the prose of restraint and the poetry of rupture. Both writers engage the course's recurring threads directly: the in-between self that belongs fully to neither place, and the question the course has asked since Sui Sin Far — is that in-between a condition of loss, or does it create something a person rooted in one place cannot have? Lahiri tends to render it as quiet loss; Vuong insists it can be generative, even gorgeous. Place this session, for residents, as the contemporary face of the immigrant story the course has followed from the exclusion era to now.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"A Temporary Matter" (1999).** Shukumar and Shoba are a young Indian-American couple in Boston whose marriage has frozen in grief: their baby was stillborn six months earlier (Shoba was at the hospital alone; Shukumar was away at a conference), and since then they have drifted into silence, avoiding each other in the house. A notice arrives that the electricity will be cut for one hour each evening for five days while a line is repaired. In the enforced dark, they begin to eat dinner together by candlelight and to play a game: each night, each tells the other something they have never told before — small confessions at first (a forgotten anniversary lie, a torn-out magazine photo, a drunken evening). The darkness loosens their tongues, and for a few nights it seems intimacy might be returning. On the final night, Shoba asks Shukumar to leave the lights on; she has a confession of her own — she has signed a lease on an apartment and is leaving him. In retaliation, or in grief, Shukumar tells her the one thing he had withheld: that he had arrived at the hospital in time to hold their dead son before the cremation, a fact he had let her believe he never knew. The story ends with the couple weeping together in the dark "for the things they now knew." The confessions, which seemed to draw them closer, were a preparation for parting.

**"On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous" (2019), excerpt.** Vuong's novel takes the form of a letter from a young man called Little Dog to his mother, Rose, who cannot read English. The excerpt renders the family's life in Hartford — the mother's labor in a nail salon, her love braided with the violence of her own war trauma, the grandmother Lan's fractured memories of Vietnam, and Little Dog's coming-of-age as a queer Vietnamese-American boy. The prose moves associatively, lyrically, between past and present, war and tenderness. The title phrase names the book's governing idea: that life is fleeting and beautiful precisely because it does not last — "briefly gorgeous." The letter is an act of love to a mother who can never read it, which is itself the book's central paradox of language and belonging.

**"Someday I'll Love Ocean Vuong" (2016).** The poem (its title borrowed from a Frank O'Hara line) is addressed by the speaker to himself, in the future tense of tenderness: "Ocean, don't be afraid." It moves through images of the body, fear, family violence, desire, and survival, repeatedly returning to gestures of self-comfort and self-forgiveness. The speaker tells himself to get up, to love his life, to recognize that "the most beautiful part of your body is wherever your mother's shadow falls." It ends with the speaker reaching the future where self-love is possible — a poem that is at once a survival instruction and a love letter to the self one might become.

### Lecture Script

We are nearly at the end, and today we read the contemporary immigrant story in two of its very different keys. [pause] I want to begin by insisting on that word — different. "Immigrant literature" is not one thing. Lahiri's family came as educated professionals after the 1965 immigration reforms. Vuong's came as refugees from the wreckage of the Vietnam War. The comfortable and the desperate. The prose of restraint and the poetry of rupture. Hold both, and don't let either stand for the whole.

Lahiri first. [pause] "A Temporary Matter" is, on its surface, almost nothing: a young Indian-American couple in Boston, and the power company is going to cut the electricity for one hour each evening for five days. That's the whole external event. But underneath it is the real situation — their baby was stillborn six months ago, and since then they have gone silent, two people moving through the same house like ghosts. [pause] And then the dark does something. In the candlelight, they start a game: each night, each tells the other something they have never confessed. Small things at first. And it seems — it really seems — like the dark is bringing them back to each other.

I want you to watch Lahiri's restraint here, because it is the whole art. [pause] Her sentences are plain. She never raises her voice. She never tells you how to feel. And that restraint is exactly what makes the ending bearable — because the ending is devastating. On the last night, Shoba asks him to leave the lights on. She has something to confess too: she has signed a lease on her own apartment. She is leaving. [pause] And Shukumar, in his grief, tells her the one thing he had kept back — that he had reached the hospital in time to hold their dead son, a thing he had let her believe he never did. And the story ends with the two of them weeping in the dark "for the things they now knew." [pause] The confessions that seemed to bring them together were a preparation for parting. That's the knife, and Lahiri slides it in without ever raising her voice.

Now Vuong, and everything changes — the register, the temperature, the language itself. [pause] Vuong was born in Saigon, spent time in a refugee camp, grew up in Hartford in a household where his mother, who worked in a nail salon, could not read English. He has said he did not read proficiently in English until he was about eleven. And out of that — out of a language he was not born holding — he made some of the most original poetry of his generation. [pause] Think about what that means against everything this course has said about the recovery of voice. Douglass and the forbidden alphabet. Baca learning to read in prison. And now Vuong, a refugee child, late to English, who became a celebrated poet. The recovery of voice is not history. It is happening now.

Look at "Someday I'll Love Ocean Vuong." [pause] It is a poem he addresses to himself — to his own future self, in the tense of tenderness. "Ocean, don't be afraid." It moves through fear, family violence, desire, survival, and it keeps returning to a gesture of self-comfort, self-forgiveness. And there is this line I cannot forget: "the most beautiful part of your body is wherever your mother's shadow falls." [pause] What is it to write a love letter to the self you have not yet become? Is that hope? I think it is something braver than hope. It is an instruction for survival.

And his novel, *On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous*, is written as a letter from a son to his mother — who cannot read it. [pause] Sit with that. A book-length act of love addressed to someone who can never receive it. That paradox — language reaching for someone it cannot reach — is, I think, the deepest thing this course has to say about the immigrant's in-between place. [pause]

So here is the question I leave you with today, the one we've asked since Sui Sin Far. The person who belongs fully to neither place — is that only loss? Lahiri tends to say: yes, quietly, it is loss. Vuong insists: no — it can be generative, even gorgeous, briefly gorgeous. [pause] You will have to decide for yourselves. That's the assignment of a lifetime, not a week. [pause]

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**The power outage as device.** The scheduled hour of darkness is Lahiri's enabling device — a mundane utility notice that creates the condition for confession. Have residents notice how she lets a literal, external event carry the entire emotional structure without symbolic heavy-handedness.

**The confession game's reversal.** The nightly confessions feel like reconciliation but turn out to be preparation for separation. The structural irony — intimacy as the road to parting — is the story's design; trace how Lahiri plants the reversal in plain sight.

**Lahiri's withheld grief — the held son.** Shukumar's final confession (that he held their dead son) is the story's deepest wound, withheld until it can only wound. Note that Lahiri never editorializes the cruelty; the plain report is the devastation.

**Vuong, "the most beautiful part of your body is wherever your mother's shadow falls."** The line fuses tenderness and the mother's troubling presence (love braided with violence). Examine how Vuong refuses to separate love from harm — the shadow is both shelter and threat.

**Vuong, the letter to an unreadable reader.** *On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous* is addressed to a mother who cannot read English. Discuss the paradox: language as an act of love that cannot reach its object — the immigrant's in-between rendered as a formal condition of the book.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **"A Temporary Matter" is about a dead baby, a failing marriage, and the cruelty intimacy makes possible. How does Lahiri make it bearable to read?** *Model answer:* Through restraint — plain, exact sentences that never raise their voice or instruct the reader's feeling. The understatement creates distance enough to bear the grief while making the final wound land all the harder.

2. **The power outage creates a space for honesty. Why do darkness and disruption sometimes allow people to say what they cannot say in ordinary life?** *Model answer:* Darkness removes the gaze and suspends ordinary routine, lowering the defenses that daily life maintains. The interruption of normalcy creates a liminal space where confession feels possible and consequences feel temporarily suspended.

3. **Vuong did not read proficiently in English until around eleven. What do you make of the language he has made from that history?** *Model answer:* He treats English as something built rather than inherited, which yields startling, unexpected constructions and a heightened attention to the word. His relationship to the language echoes the course's theme that the recovery of voice can be literal and hard-won.

4. **"Someday I'll Love Ocean Vuong" is addressed to his future self. What does it mean to write a love letter to your own future self?** *Model answer:* It is both hope and survival instruction — a way of speaking tenderness toward a self not yet able to receive it. The future tense holds open the possibility of self-forgiveness that the present cannot yet manage.

5. **Lahiri and Vuong both write the person who belongs fully to neither place. Is that loss, or does it create something a rooted person cannot have?** *Model answer:* The course leaves this genuinely open. Lahiri tends to render the in-between as quiet loss; Vuong insists it can be generative and beautiful. The pairing asks residents to weigh dislocation as deprivation against dislocation as a distinct and valuable vantage.

6. **How do the two writers represent the diversity within "immigrant literature"?** *Model answer:* Lahiri depicts educated professional immigration and its comfortable dislocation; Vuong depicts refugee resettlement marked by war trauma and poverty. Together they refute the idea of a single immigrant experience, spanning class, circumstance, and register.

7. **What is the effect of *On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous* being addressed to a mother who cannot read it?** *Model answer:* It dramatizes the immigrant's in-between as a formal condition — language reaching for someone it cannot reach. The unbridgeable gap between son and mother becomes the book's central, productive paradox.

8. **How does the title phrase "briefly gorgeous" frame Vuong's vision?** *Model answer:* It asserts that beauty and life are precious because they are fleeting, not in spite of it. The phrase reframes loss and impermanence as the very conditions of value.

9. **Compare Lahiri's and Vuong's prose styles as responses to dislocation.** *Model answer:* Lahiri's restraint enacts the muffled, controlled grief of the assimilated middle class; Vuong's associative, rupturing lyric enacts the fragmentation of war and refugee memory. Each style is fitted to the experience it carries.

10. **What does this session say about the course's long thread of recovering voice?** *Model answer:* Vuong's path — refugee child, late to English, celebrated poet — shows the recovery of voice as a living, present reality rather than a historical one. It links Douglass's forbidden alphabet and Baca's prison literacy to the contemporary moment.

### Anticipated Difficulties

## Session 16 — Capstone: Your Voice, Your Choice

### At a Glance

The final session is not a lecture but a facilitation: residents bring one piece of writing by any underrepresented American voice — chosen freely, from any source — and read a passage aloud, saying why it matters to them. There is no grade and no critique. This guide adapts the usual session format because there is no single author to introduce; instead it gives the instructor a plan for holding a room while it speaks for itself.

### Context & Purpose of the Session

After fifteen weeks of reading writers who fought — sometimes across centuries, sometimes literally — to be heard, the final session hands the room over to the residents. The pedagogical logic is deliberate and worth naming to yourself before you walk in. Every session of this course has, in one way or another, been about the recovery of voice: Douglass and the forbidden alphabet, Hurston and the recovered grave, Baca learning to read in prison, Vuong's hard-won English. The capstone completes that arc by asking each resident to exercise a voice of their own — to choose, to read aloud, and to say why. The act of choosing a text is itself an act of literary judgment, the skill the course has been quietly building. The act of reading aloud is an act of taking up space with one's own attention and care. And the act of saying "this matters to me, and here is why" is the small, brave thing the whole course has modeled: speaking honestly about what moves you, without performance.

This is also, frankly, a session about community in a particular setting. This course is taught not in a university classroom but in the community room of a building where people from many backgrounds live side by side. The capstone is the moment when the literature the instructor brought in meets the lives the residents brought in — when the residents become, briefly, the curators rather than the audience. Your job is not to teach today. It is to hold the space: to make it safe, to keep it warm, to ensure everyone who wants to speak can, and to protect the silences. The hardest discipline of this session, for a professor of English, is restraint — to resist the reflex to analyze, correct, or improve what residents share. Today you listen.

A note on the instructor's own voice, consistent with the whole companion: you are not a member of most of the communities whose literatures the room may bring forward, and the capstone makes that especially clear, because residents may share writing from traditions far closer to their own lives than to yours. The posture is the one you have held all term — respectful host, not authority on others' experience. Your warmth is welcome; your interpretation, today, is not needed.

### Summary of What the Session Does

The session has a simple, repeatable shape. **Before class:** each resident chooses one piece of writing — a poem, a short story excerpt, a paragraph of memoir, a song lyric — by any writer from an underrepresented American voice. It need not be from the syllabus; it can be from the resident's own community, family history, or a tradition they feel connected to. They bring it ready to read a short passage aloud and to say, briefly, why they chose it. **During class:** the group goes around the room. Each person reads their passage and offers one thing about why it matters to them. There is no commentary and no criticism — only listening. **At the end:** the group spends a few minutes reflecting on the course as a whole — what surprised them, what stayed with them, what they want to read next. The session converts a semester of receptive reading into a single hour of expressive sharing, and it closes the community's experience of the course on the residents' own terms rather than the instructor's.

### The Material for This Session

There is no assigned text. Residents supply the texts. Practically, you should:

- Remind residents the week before, and again by note if you can, to bring something — and reassure them it can be very short (even a few lines) and from anywhere.
- Bring a few spare copies of short, accessible pieces from the syllabus (a Brooks poem, a Harjo poem, a Hughes poem freely available at the [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/) or [poets.org](https://poets.org/)) so that anyone who arrives empty-handed can still take part without embarrassment.
- Have a printed copy of the course's full reading list (the syllabus's "All Course Readings" section) on hand, both as a memory aid and to seed the closing "what should we read next?" conversation.

### Facilitation Script

I want to start today by not starting — by which I mean, today I am going to talk less than I have all term, and you are going to talk more. [pause] For fifteen weeks I have brought writers into this room. Today the room belongs to you.

Here is what we are going to do, and here is what we are not going to do. [pause] Each of you brought something — a poem, a few lines of a story, a piece of a memoir, a song lyric — by a writer from a voice that has had to fight to be heard. It did not have to be from our list. It could be from your own family, your own community, a tradition you feel close to. We are going to go around the room. Each person reads their passage — even just a few lines is plenty — and says one thing about why they chose it. And then we listen. [pause] That's the whole rule. No commentary. No criticism. No one is going to analyze your choice or improve your reading. We are just going to listen to each other. That is rarer than it sounds, and today we are going to do it on purpose.

If you did not bring something, that is completely fine. I have a few short pieces here you can borrow, and you are just as welcome to read one of those. [pause] There is no grade today. There is no right answer. There is no performance. This is a sharing.

I'll say one thing before we begin, about why we are doing this. [pause] Every single week of this course has been, underneath the particular writer, about one thing: a voice that someone tried to silence, finding a way to be heard. Douglass had to steal the alphabet. Hurston was buried in an unmarked grave until another writer went looking for her. Baca learned to read in a prison cell. Vuong did not read English well until he was eleven, and he became one of the great poets of his generation. [pause] So when you read your few lines aloud today, you are doing the small version of the thing this whole course has been about. You are using your voice to say: this matters, and here is why. That's it. That's the whole semester, in one sentence.

[pause] So let's begin. Who would like to go first? And if no one wants to go first — that's all right, I'll wait. The silence is fine. We've gotten good at silence this term.

[As residents share, the facilitator's job is to thank each person simply — "thank you" — and to resist any urge to interpret, connect, or praise the choice in literary terms. Keep the rhythm gentle. If a resident's piece touches something painful, let the room hold it; do not rush to fill the quiet.]

[After everyone who wishes to has shared:] Thank you — all of you. [pause] Before we close, I want to ask you a few questions about the whole course, and again, there are no wrong answers. Which writer surprised you most? Is there something on our list you would have replaced or added? Has reading these writers changed how you think about American literature — or about America? What is the most important thing you carry out of this room? And — this one matters to me — what should we read next?

[pause] I'll end with this, and then the floor is yours again. It has been my privilege to read these texts with you. The writers in this course were not assigned to this building's community room by accident. They belong here — in a building where people from many backgrounds live side by side — more than they belong in almost any classroom I have taught in. Thank you for reading with me. [pause]

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

These are the closing, whole-course questions. Because the session is reflective, the "model answers" below are not correct responses but examples of the *kind* of thinking to encourage and gently model if the room is quiet.

1. **Which writer from this course surprised you most? Why?** *Model answer:* A strong response names a specific writer and a specific reason rooted in the text — e.g., surprise that Dunbar's dialect poems were a deliberate, double-edged craft rather than quaint, or that Brooks could carry a death sentence in twenty-four words. Encourage specificity over generality.

2. **Is there a text you would add to or replace if you were designing the course?** *Model answer:* A thoughtful answer treats the syllabus as a set of deliberate choices with trade-offs — recognizing that any list omits someone, and proposing an addition with a reason (a community underrepresented, a form not yet seen). The exercise builds the curatorial judgment the course has cultivated.

3. **Has reading these writers changed how you think about American literature — or about America?** *Model answer:* The richest answers connect the literary to the civic — seeing the counter-canon not as an appendix to American literature but as central to it, and seeing American identity as itself a contested, many-voiced thing. Personal, honest answers matter more than sweeping ones.

4. **What is the most important thing you carry out of this room?** *Model answer:* Answers will vary widely and personally; the facilitator's task is to receive each without ranking it. A resident who says "that I can read a hard poem twice and not feel stupid" has carried out something as real as one who names double consciousness.

5. **What should we read next?** *Model answer:* Treat this as a genuine reading list in the making. Capture the suggestions on the board or paper; they are both a gift to the group and useful intelligence for the instructor designing a future iteration.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The central difficulty is the instructor's own restraint: a trained close reader will feel the pull to respond critically to what residents share, and that pull must be resisted — today is listening, not teaching. Some residents will be nervous about reading aloud; normalize brevity ("even a few lines is plenty") and offer the borrowed pieces so no one is exposed by arriving empty-handed. Silence will happen, especially at the start and after emotional shares; do not rush to fill it — the course has built the room's tolerance for silence, and this is where it pays off. Be prepared for a resident to share something raw or personal; hold it with a simple thank-you rather than analysis, and let the group's quiet do the work. Finally, watch the clock gently so that everyone who wishes to speak can, and so the closing reflection is not crowded out — but never cut someone short mid-share. If the room is large, you may need to invite (not require) people to keep passages brief.

## Instructor Glossary

These definitions are pitched for the instructor — fuller and more analytically loaded than the versions in the student syllabus. Use them to clarify your own framing before class and to answer the sharper questions a resident may raise. Several of these terms also appear, in simpler form, in the student materials.

**Double consciousness.** W. E. B. Du Bois's term, from *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903), for the experience of "always looking at one's self through the eyes of others," of feeling one's "two-ness — an American, a Negro." It names the inner division produced by living inside a society that defines you as a problem. The course uses it as a master key, and you will find it useful far beyond Du Bois — in Dunbar's mask, Sui Sin Far's Eurasian self, the immigrant's hyphen.

**The slave narrative.** The autobiographical genre, flourishing roughly 1830–1865, in which formerly enslaved people (Douglass, Jacobs, Equiano before them) told their own stories, typically tracing the journey from bondage to literacy to freedom. The narratives were both literature and political weapon, written for a white Northern readership and often framed by white authenticating documents. Note the genre's characteristic tension between the truth the narrator wishes to tell and the conventions the abolitionist market demanded.

**The spirituals.** The sacred folk songs created by enslaved African Americans, fusing Christian scripture with African musical traditions and coded meanings (the "sorrow songs" Du Bois wrote about). They are the deep substratum beneath much later Black literature — Hughes's blues poems, Brooks's cadences, Morrison's prose all draw on their rhythms and their double-voiced messaging.

**Dialect poetry.** Verse written in a phonetically rendered vernacular speech rather than standard literary English. In the Black tradition it carries a fraught history: Dunbar's dialect poems won him fame but also trapped him in white readers' expectations, even as they preserved and dignified Black speech. Distinguish, for the room, between dialect used to demean (the minstrel tradition) and dialect used to honor (Dunbar, Hughes, Hurston's recorded folk speech).

**Vernacular.** The everyday spoken language of a community, as opposed to formal literary or standard language. The "vernacular tradition" in African American literature — its sermons, songs, toasts, folktales, and idioms — is treated by scholars such as Henry Louis Gates Jr. as the wellspring of the written tradition. To teach the vernacular is to insist that ordinary speech is a literary resource, not a deficiency.

**Signifying.** In Gates's formulation (*The Signifying Monkey*, 1988), the African American rhetorical practice of indirect, figurative, double-voiced speech — saying one thing to mean another, repeating with a critical difference. It is the engine of much Black literary play and critique, and it descends from the coded communication of the enslaved. Useful for explaining how a text can mean two things to two audiences at once.

**The Harlem Renaissance.** The flowering of African American literature, art, music, and intellectual life centered in Harlem in the 1920s and early 1930s. Its figures include Hughes, McKay, Hurston, Toomer, Countee Cullen, and the critic-impresarios Alain Locke and W. E. B. Du Bois. Stress to yourself that it was not monolithic: it contained sharp internal arguments about audience, respectability, and the proper subjects of Black art.

**The New Negro.** The term, popularized by Alain Locke's 1925 anthology *The New Negro*, for the assertive, self-defining Black identity of the post–World War I generation — proud, modern, urban, and unwilling to accept the old subordinate roles. It is the self-naming that underwrote the Harlem Renaissance, and a forerunner of the later self-namings ("Black Arts," "Chicano," "Asian American").

**The Black Arts Movement.** The artistic and literary movement of the mid-1960s and 1970s, closely tied to Black Power, which called for a militant, community-centered Black art addressed to Black audiences and serving Black liberation. Associated with Amiri Baraka, and decisive for Gwendolyn Brooks's 1967 turn. It reframed the audience question the course tracks: write to your people, not past them.

**The Black Aesthetic.** The theory, advanced by Black Arts critics such as Addison Gayle Jr. (*The Black Aesthetic*, 1971), that Black art should be judged by standards rooted in Black culture and experience rather than by Eurocentric criteria. It is the critical complement to the Black Arts Movement and a direct challenge to the idea of a single, universal (implicitly white) literary standard.

**Code-switching.** The practice of shifting between languages, dialects, or registers depending on social context — between, say, vernacular and standard English, or English and Spanish. For minority writers it is both a lived survival skill and a literary technique; it dramatizes the in-between self and the negotiation of multiple audiences. Distinguish the sociolinguistic sense from the looser metaphorical one.

**Diaspora.** The dispersion of a people from an original homeland across other regions, and the communities so formed. The African diaspora, the Chinese diaspora, the South Asian diaspora all figure in the course. The concept lets you talk about identity that is rooted in a place one may never have seen, and about the cultural memory carried across generations and oceans.

**The diasporic.** The adjectival and analytic mode that attends to diaspora — to displacement, cultural memory, the relationship between homeland and host land, and the identities forged in between. A diasporic reading of a text (Lee, Vuong, McKay) asks how the elsewhere persists inside the here.

**Assimilation.** The process by which a minority or immigrant group takes on the dominant culture's language, customs, and values, often with the loss of its own. The course treats assimilation as neither simply good nor simply bad but as a site of cost and negotiation — what is gained in belonging, what is lost in becoming. Set it against its alternatives: pluralism, separatism, the maintenance of the hyphen.

**The model minority.** The stereotype, applied especially to Asian Americans since the mid-twentieth century, that a minority group has achieved success through hard work and quiet conformity, and is therefore "good." The course names it as a flattening myth that erases internal diversity, conceals real hardship, and is often used to discipline other minority groups. Kingston and Lee's interior, haunted, grieving works are among its correctives.

**The canon.** The body of literary works treated by an institution or culture as the most important and worth preserving, studying, and teaching. The traditional American and Western canons were overwhelmingly white and male. Much of this course is an argument with the canon — about who was left out, why, and what changes when they are read.

**The counter-canon.** The body of works assembled in deliberate opposition to or supplement of the established canon — here, the tradition of minority American writing the course foregrounds. The term keeps in view that this is not merely "more" literature but a corrective and a challenge to what "the canon" claimed to be complete.

**Polyphony.** Literally "many voices"; in literary criticism (after Bakhtin), the quality of a text that holds multiple distinct voices or consciousnesses in genuine dialogue, without subordinating them to a single authorial view. Erdrich's multi-narrator novels are the course's clearest example; the concept also describes the course's own design — many voices, not one.

**Witness.** The act, and the literary stance, of testifying to suffering or injustice so that it is recorded and not denied. From the slave narratives through King and Lorde to contemporary work, the literature of witness insists on bearing accurate, unflinching testimony. Distinguish witness (telling what happened) from protest (demanding change), though they often coincide.

**Ethnography.** The descriptive study of a culture from within, as practiced by anthropologists. It matters here because Zora Neale Hurston trained as an anthropologist under Franz Boas and brought ethnographic methods — collecting folklore, recording speech — into her fiction. The term raises a productive tension: when does writing about one's own community become studying it, and who is the writing for?

**The bildungsroman.** The novel (or narrative) of formation — the story of a protagonist's growth from youth to maturity, education, and self-understanding. *The House on Mango Street* and *The Woman Warrior* are minority inflections of the form, in which coming of age is complicated by race, gender, language, and the in-between. Note how these works often strain or rewrite the classic European form.

**The immigrant novel.** The narrative centered on the experience of migration and its aftermath — arrival, adjustment, generational conflict, the negotiation between old country and new. Lahiri's fiction is a refined contemporary instance. The category overlaps with but is not identical to the diasporic: it foregrounds the act and consequences of migration itself.

**The postcolonial.** The condition, and the critical framework, that follows formal colonial rule — attending to the lasting cultural, psychological, and economic effects of colonization and to the literatures produced in its wake. It bears on Bulosan (the colonized Philippines), McKay (colonial Jamaica), and the broader question of writing in the colonizer's language. Use it to connect American minority literature to global anticolonial writing.

**Race vs. ethnicity.** A distinction worth keeping precise: *race* refers to socially constructed categories based on perceived physical characteristics and tied to systems of power and hierarchy; *ethnicity* refers to shared culture, ancestry, language, and tradition. The two overlap but are not the same — one can share an ethnicity across races, or a race across ethnicities — and conflating them muddies discussion. The course treats both as constructed and consequential.

**Intersectionality.** The framework, named by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw (1989), for understanding how multiple categories of identity and systems of oppression — race, gender, class, sexuality — intersect and compound rather than operating separately. Audre Lorde's self-naming ("black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet") is the course's lived embodiment of it. Use it to resist single-axis readings of any writer's situation.

## Notes for Future Iterations

A few honest observations for the next time through, recorded while the design is fresh.

The course leans heavily on the African American tradition in its first half and then opens outward; that weighting is defensible — the slave narrative and the Harlem Renaissance are foundational to everything after — but a future iteration might interleave a Native or Latinx voice earlier to signal the breadth sooner. The copyright wall is real and uneven: the public-domain nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century material (Sessions 1–8) links freely, while the contemporary sessions (9, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15) depend on library loans and printed excerpts. Build in the reminder, weeks ahead, that residents will need the Free Library for the modern texts, and keep clean printed excerpts of every copyrighted reading on hand — assume some residents will arrive without the book.

The text replacements made this term (steering Dunbar and Hughes away from their most-anthologized poems, to avoid overlap with the building's "Art of Telling" salon) worked well and arguably improved the course: "Sympathy" and "The Haunted Oak" show a darker, angrier Dunbar than "We Wear the Mask" alone, and "Let America Be America Again" gives a more political Hughes than the school-anthology staples. Keep that approach; it rewards the room with the less-trodden poem.

Pacing: the densest sessions are King's "Letter" (10) and the Morrison opening (14); both reward a slower hand and more silence. The capstone (16) consistently does more than its modest design suggests — protect it from being shortened. Finally, watch the room's appetite for historical context; some residents want more of it, some want to get to the text. The "Historical & Cultural Context" and "Summary of the Work(s)" sections in this companion exist precisely so the instructor can meter that on the fly — give more context to a room that craves it, move faster for a room that wants the words on the page.
