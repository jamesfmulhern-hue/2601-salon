There’s a moment in every workshop I’ve ever taught when the room leans forward—everyone eager to be helpful, eager to *fix*—and I find myself wanting to protect the draft the way you’d protect a small animal in a bright kitchen. Not from criticism, exactly. From the hurry.

Students hurry because they love the story they’re trying to tell. That’s the kindness beneath the rush: they want to get you to the good part, the revelation, the flare. But the trouble is that the good part is often made of what they skip—the soft tissue between events, the pressure that builds when a sentence refuses to sprint.

I’ve been thinking about how hard it is to teach that pause. You can’t simply say, “Slow down.” That turns the pause into a moral instruction, and nobody writes better because they’ve been scolded into patience. The pause is not a speed limit; it’s a kind of attention.

Sometimes I’ll take a student’s paragraph and read it aloud, leaving a little more air than the punctuation asks for. A comma becomes a porch. A period becomes a door you actually close—hand on the knob, the click. The room changes when they hear it. They realize the sentence is not only meaning; it’s breath. And breath has consequences.

What students fear, I think, is that if they pause, the reader will leave. But in the reading life—my own, at least—it’s often the opposite. I stay because the writer trusts me with silence. I stay because the unsaid thing is given weight. The story isn’t merely a sequence of outcomes; it’s an atmosphere. And atmosphere requires room.

So lately my best teaching move has been modest: I ask them to circle one sentence they like, then underline the word that carries the most pressure in it. Then we look at what comes *before* that word. Did they earn it? Did they allow it to arrive? Often the revision is not an addition but a refusal to rush—a willingness to stand in the doorway for a second longer.

It’s strange, how often writing instruction boils down to permission. Permission to linger. Permission to let the reader do some of the work. Permission to trust the quiet line.

— JFM
