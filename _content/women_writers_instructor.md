## Preface — How to Use This Book

Dear Mr. Mulhern,

This companion is built to sit on the desk beside you in the hour before each class, and to be read more slowly in the days before that. It is not a script you must follow, and it is no substitute for your own reading of the primary texts, which remain the only indispensable preparation. It is, rather, a working scaffold: a place where the biography, the history, the close-reading, and the likely shape of the room have already been laid out, so that your attention in the week of teaching can go where it belongs — into the texts and into the people in front of you. Each session mirrors the others in structure: an "At a Glance" orientation, a biographical sketch long enough to teach from, historical and cultural context, the readings with freely available sources where they exist, a lecture script in your voice for the opening minutes, close-reading notes on specific passages, discussion questions with model answers, a note on anticipated difficulties, further reading for you, and one line worth memorizing. Use what helps; cross out what doesn't.

A word about the central fact of your situation, because it shapes everything else. You are a man who will spend sixteen weeks teaching a course made entirely of women writers, most often to a room that includes men. This is not a disqualification, and you should not teach as though it were one. A man can read Dickinson as closely as anyone; the dash does not check your sex at the door. But the fact carries responsibilities, and the chief one is this: your job is to bring these writers forward, not to stand in front of them. There is a particular failure mode for the male instructor of women's literature — the impulse to perform one's own enlightenment, to narrate one's own growth, to make the course quietly about the teacher's good intentions. Resist it entirely. The students did not come to hear a man congratulate himself for admiring women. They came for Bradstreet, Chopin, Hurston, O'Connor. Read aloud often; let the writers' own sentences carry the authority; when you must editorialize, keep it short and get out of the way. The best thing you can model in this room is a man reading a woman's work with full seriousness and no apology — neither the apology of condescension ("remarkable, for her time") nor the apology of penance ("as a man, I cannot speak to this"). You can speak to craft, and craft is the subject.

There is a prior question some students — and perhaps you — will want answered at the outset: why is "women's literature" still taught as a separate course at all? Is the segregation not itself a kind of insult, a roping-off of the women into their own pen while the "real" canon proceeds without them? The honest answer is that the separate course is a temporary and imperfect corrective to a real and long injury. The historical record of exclusion is not a grievance invented by theory; it is documented and specific. Bradstreet had to be published by a brother-in-law and apologized for as a "Gentlewoman." Wheatley was put before a panel of men to prove she could write. Chopin's career was ended by reviews. Hurston died in a welfare home and was buried in an unmarked grave. The standard anthologies of the mid-twentieth century could run for hundreds of pages with two or three women among the names. A course like this exists to let students see that record whole — not to replace one partial canon with another, but to make the picture complete. When the completion is finished, the separate course will no longer be needed. We are not there yet.

The danger of such a course runs two ways, and you must steer between them. One temptation is to let the room collapse into appreciation — a warm bath of admiration in which every poem is "beautiful" and every writer "brave," and nothing is actually read. Appreciation is the death of attention; it flatters the reader and ignores the text. The opposite temptation is to let the room collapse into a victim narrative — a recitation of suffering and exclusion in which the writers become exhibits in a museum of injustice and their actual sentences go unexamined. Both temptations share a flaw: they replace the literature with a feeling about the literature. The cure for both is the same, and it is the discipline of this entire course — slow reading aloud, attention to the word on the page, the conviction that these women are masters of craft whose work rewards exactly the scrutiny we would bring to Melville or Whitman. Honor them as artists. The injustice is real and worth naming; it is the frame, not the picture. The picture is the writing, and the writing is extraordinary.

With respect and with confidence in your hands,

*— The Companion*

## Recurring Threads Across the Course

It will help you, and your students, to name a small number of threads at the outset and then pull on them deliberately week after week. The pleasure of a course like this — the thing that turns sixteen separate authors into a single tradition in the students' minds — comes from recognition: *we have seen this before, and now it has changed.* Below are six threads worth tracking aloud. You need not raise all six every week, but you should know where each runs.

**The body and its constraints.** From the start, these writers are unusually conscious of the body — its desires, its labors, its dangers, and the social rules clamped around it. Bradstreet writes from inside a body that bears eight children in a world that reads childbirth as a religious trial. Chopin's Louise Mallard feels freedom arrive as a physical sensation "creeping out of the sky." Gilman's narrator is confined to a bed for her own good. Lucille Clifton writes a praise-poem to her own hips. Watch how the body shifts from something to be transcended (the Puritans), to something to be governed (the domestic ideal), to something to be reclaimed and celebrated (the second-wave poets). Ask the students, late in the term, whether the body in this tradition is ever fully the woman's own, or always partly a contested territory.

**The domestic as subject.** The house, the kitchen, the hearth, the marriage, the nursery — the spaces to which women were so often confined become, in their hands, not a limitation on subject matter but a subject of inexhaustible depth. Stowe makes the domestic interior the moral center of a national argument. Alcott exposes the unpaid female labor that props up male idealism. Wharton anatomizes the drawing room as an instrument of power. Olsen narrates a whole life over an ironing board. The lesson to draw out, repeatedly, is that the "small" domestic subject is not small at all; it is where these writers found the universe. The condescension that called the domestic novel "merely" sentimental was a judgment about who was writing, not about what was written.

**The lyric "I" as a contested space.** For a man, saying "I" in a poem was a birthright; for these women, it was frequently a transgression, an immodesty, a claim to a self that the culture preferred they not assert. Watch the strategies. Bradstreet hedges her "I" with self-deprecation. Wheatley deploys a borrowed neoclassical "I" as both shelter and assertion. Dickinson explodes the lyric self into dozens of personae and a syntax that refuses to settle. By Rich, Lorde, and Clifton the "I" is claimed in full, marked and historical and unapologetic. The arc of the course is in part the story of women winning the right to say "I" and mean *themselves*.

**Voice claimed despite silence.** Silence is everywhere in this tradition — imposed, chosen, strategic, or simply the result of having no time, no room, no permission to write. Tillie Olsen made "silences" her great theme: the books unwritten by women who were too tired, too poor, too interrupted. And yet against that silence runs the astonishing fact of the voices we do have. Track the relationship between the two: how Wheatley's voice survives slavery, how Dickinson's survives near-total non-publication, how Hurston's survives poverty and neglect, how each of these voices reaches us across a silence that very nearly swallowed it. Ask: what did it cost to be heard at all?

**The female bildungsroman.** The novel of growing up — of a young self forming itself against the world — looks different when the self is female and the world is built to limit her. Track the coming-of-age wherever it appears: in Cather's Paul straining against Pittsburgh; in the implied girlhoods behind Dickinson's and Bishop's poems (Bishop's "In the Waiting Room" is a coming-into-selfhood in miniature); in Hurston's Janie, glimpsed in the opening of *Their Eyes Were Watching God*. The question is always the same: what does it mean to become a self in a world that has already decided what you are for?

**The recovery of work the canon set aside.** This is the meta-thread, the one that explains why the course exists. Again and again you will tell, or imply, the same story: a woman writes something extraordinary; the literary culture ignores, misreads, or forgets it; decades later, someone — often another woman — finds it and gives it back. Chopin rediscovered in the 1960s. Hurston restored by Alice Walker in the 1970s. Gilman's "Yellow Wallpaper" resurrected from obscurity. The recovery project is not a footnote to these writers' careers; for many of them it is the reason we can read them at all. Keep asking the students to notice it, and to feel the precariousness — how close each of these voices came to being lost entirely.

Carry these six threads lightly but persistently. They are the rope that ties the sixteen weeks together.

## Session 1 — The First Voices: Anne Bradstreet

### At a Glance
We begin where American literature begins for women — and, in fact, where published American poetry begins, full stop. Anne Bradstreet was the first writer in England's North American colonies to be published, male or female. The session establishes the central premise of the course — that these writers belong in the main current of the tradition, not in a tributary off to the side — and gives students their first model of a woman finding lyric room inside crushing constraint. The aim is to make students hear Bradstreet not as a quaint colonial curiosity but as a poet of genuine feeling, dry intelligence, and quiet daring.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Anne Bradstreet was born Anne Dudley in 1612 in Northamptonshire, England, into a prosperous and unusually literate Puritan family ([Academy of American Poets, "Anne Bradstreet"](https://poets.org/poet/anne-bradstreet); [Wikipedia, "Anne Bradstreet"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_Bradstreet)). Her father, Thomas Dudley, was steward to the Earl of Lincoln and a man who took his daughter's education seriously in a way that was far from universal; she had access to the earl's library and read widely in history, scripture, and poetry. She was especially affected, as the scholars note, by the works of the French Protestant poet Guillaume du Bartas and by the Elizabethan tradition of Spenser and Sidney. At sixteen she married Simon Bradstreet, a Cambridge-educated young man who, like her father, would later serve as governor of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. This matters for the classroom: Bradstreet was not a self-taught primitive. She was a learned woman who had read the best poetry of her age and who knew exactly what she was attempting.

In 1630, at eighteen, she sailed for the New World with her husband and parents as part of the great Puritan migration led by John Winthrop aboard the *Arbella*. She later wrote, with characteristic honesty, that her "heart rose" in protest when she first saw the rawness of the new land — a flicker of resistance she immediately submitted to God's will, but recorded all the same. The family lived first in the rough settlements around Massachusetts Bay, then in Ipswich, and finally settled in Andover, where Bradstreet spent the rest of her life. She bore eight children and raised them through the hardships of frontier life — illness, fire, scarcity, the constant proximity of death — all while writing poetry in whatever hours she could find. This biographical fact alone is worth pausing on with students: the conditions under which this body of work was produced were not those of leisure and a quiet study, but of a working household on the edge of a wilderness.

Her first collection, *The Tenth Muse Lately Sprung Up in America*, was published in London in 1650, making her the first writer in the American colonies — and one of the first women in the English language — to appear in print as a poet ([Wikipedia, "Anne Bradstreet"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_Bradstreet)). The circumstances of that publication are themselves a lesson in the position of the woman writer. The book was carried to London and seen through the press by her brother-in-law, the Reverend John Woodbridge, and was presented to the public as the work of "a Gentlewoman of those Parts" — published, by most accounts, without her prior knowledge or at least without her active arrangement. The framing apparatus of the volume, with its prefatory verses by men vouching for the propriety and authenticity of a woman's writing, tells you everything about the cultural weather she worked in: a woman's poems required male sponsorship and male assurance that she had not neglected her household duties to produce them. You should be careful here, and conservative: the exact degree of Bradstreet's foreknowledge of the publication is not perfectly settled, and the "without her knowledge" story is the traditional account. What is certain is that the book reached print through a man's hands and was wrapped in apologies for the audacity of a woman having written it at all.

The poems of *The Tenth Muse* are largely the ambitious, formal, public poems of her youth — long quaternions on the four elements, the four humors, the four ages of man, the four seasons, and a sequence of historical "monarchies." These are accomplished and learned, and they show her measuring herself against the major poets, but they are not the work for which she is now loved. The poems that have made her permanent are the more personal lyrics, many written later and published posthumously: "To My Dear and Loving Husband," the elegies for her grandchildren, "Verses upon the Burning of Our House," "Before the Birth of One of Her Children," and "The Author to Her Book," in which she addresses her published volume as a slightly embarrassing, ill-formed child sent out into the world in borrowed clothes. A revised and expanded edition, *Several Poems Compiled with Great Wit and Learning*, appeared in Boston in 1678, six years after her death in 1672, and included this more intimate work ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poet/anne-bradstreet)).

It is the intimate poems that you will teach, and they require a particular act of recovery, because their plainness can read, to a modern ear, as simplicity. It is not simplicity. Bradstreet is doing something genuinely difficult and genuinely radical for her moment: she is asserting, inside a theology that subordinated earthly love to divine love and womanly speech to womanly silence, the legitimacy of human feeling — conjugal love, grief for a dead grandchild, attachment to a burned house — as fit matter for poetry. She does not abandon the Puritan frame; her poems characteristically end by submitting the earthly to the heavenly. But the submission is hard-won, and you can hear the resistance underneath it. That tension — between what the heart actually feels and what the doctrine requires it to feel — is the engine of her best work, and it is intensely modern in its honesty.

Her legacy is foundational in the most literal sense. She is the first. Every later woman in this course writes, knowingly or not, in a tradition she opened. The poet John Berryman addressed her directly in his long poem *Homage to Mistress Bradstreet* (1956); Adrienne Rich wrote an important early essay on her. Tell the students that when Virginia Woolf imagined the silenced women writers of the past, the ones who never got to write at all, Bradstreet is the figure who did — who, against the odds of her place and her sex, got the words onto the page and into print, and whose voice survives with a clarity that much of her male contemporaries' verse does not.

### Historical & Cultural Context

To read Bradstreet you must give students Puritan New England and its gender expectations, which were severe and theologically grounded. The Massachusetts Bay Colony was a religious commonwealth in which a woman's sphere was the household and her virtue was understood largely in terms of obedience, modesty, piety, and fruitfulness. Public speech by women was suspect; intellectual ambition in a woman was potentially dangerous. The cautionary example that hangs over Bradstreet's career is Anne Hutchinson, who was tried and banished from the colony in 1638 for, in effect, teaching theology and claiming direct spiritual authority — a woman punished for speaking. Bradstreet, by contrast, survived and was celebrated, and the difference is instructive: she worked within the permitted forms, framed her ambition with self-deprecation, and never directly challenged the order that constrained her. Her radicalism is entirely a matter of tone and emphasis, smuggled in beneath an orthodox surface.

The Puritan understanding of the world was providential and typological: every event, including a house fire or the death of a child, was a message from God, to be read for its spiritual meaning and submitted to. Earthly attachments were a snare; the believer was meant to hold the things of this world loosely, with the heart fixed on the heavenly home. This is the doctrine against which Bradstreet's love poems and grief poems strain. When she writes that she prizes her husband's love "more than whole mines of gold," she is, by strict doctrine, valuing an earthly good too highly, and the poems repeatedly catch themselves and turn the affection heavenward. Students should understand that the constant pivot in her poems from earth to heaven is not a poetic tic; it is the lived shape of Puritan piety, and the poems are dramatizing a real spiritual labor.

Finally, give them the literacy and the books. New England Puritans were, by the standards of the seventeenth century, extraordinarily literate — they were a people of the Book, who valued reading scripture and founded Harvard within a few years of arriving. But that literacy was gendered: education in Latin and the classics flowed primarily to boys destined for the ministry. Bradstreet's wide reading was a privilege of her particular family and a thing she had to be careful not to flaunt. The deprecation in "The Author to Her Book" and the famous concession in her "Prologue" — that "men can do best, and women know it well" — must be read at least partly as protective coloration, the necessary curtsy that bought her the freedom to write at all.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"To My Dear and Loving Husband."** A twelve-line poem in rhymed couplets. It opens with three conditional statements (lines 1–3): *if* ever two people were truly one, *if* ever a man was loved by his wife, *if* ever a wife was happy in a man, then she is that wife — and she challenges other women to match her happiness if they can. The middle (lines 5–8) measures her love: she prizes his love more than whole mines of gold and all the riches of the East; her thirst for his love cannot be quenched by rivers, and only his love can repay her, for which she prays heaven rewards him manifold. The close (lines 9–12) turns the earthly love heavenward: while they live, let them so persevere in love that when they live no more on earth, they may live forever in heaven. The poem's literal arc is thus a stacking of conditions into a boast, then a hyperbolic valuation of married love, then its consecration to eternity.

**"Verses upon the Burning of Our House, July 10th, 1666."** A longer poem (roughly fifty-four lines) in rhymed tetrameter couplets, narrating an actual event. The speaker wakes in the night to cries of "fire," rises, sees the blaze, and prays to God for strength. She watches her house burn and blesses God's name, reasoning that what was lost was His to take. Then she walks past the ruins and cannot help grieving over specific places — where she used to sit, the trunk, the chest, the table where guests once sat and where no guest will sit again, and where no candle or bridegroom's voice will ever be heard again. She chides her own heart for this attachment, reminds herself that her wealth lies "on high" in a house "framed by that mighty Architect," already purchased and paid for by Christ. The poem ends by bidding farewell to earthly "pelf" and fixing her hope on heaven. The arc moves from shock, to dutiful blessing, to honest grief over concrete domestic objects, to a labored but deliberate spiritual consolation.

### Lecture Script

Good evening, and welcome. We are going to spend sixteen weeks together with the women who made American literature, and I want you to notice where we are starting — because we are not starting in some side room, some annex to the main building. We are starting at the front door. The first published poet in the American colonies — not the first *woman* poet, the first poet of any kind — was a woman named Anne Bradstreet, writing in a frontier household in Massachusetts in the middle of the sixteen-hundreds. [pause]

I want you to hold that fact, because it cuts against the story most of us were told. The story goes that American literature begins with the men — with the sermons and then with Emerson and Hawthorne and the rest — and that the women arrive later, as a kind of supplement. But here at the very beginning is a woman, raising eight children at the edge of a wilderness, writing poetry good enough to be carried across an ocean and printed in London. She is not a footnote to the tradition. She is its first page. [pause]

Now, listen to how she opens "To My Dear and Loving Husband." "If ever two were one, then surely we." Notice that little word at the front — "if." She doesn't begin with a declaration; she begins with a condition, a wager. *If* ever two people were truly one, then we are those two. It's a love poem, one of the great frank love poems in the language, and it is completely unembarrassed about earthly, married, bodily love. She says she prizes his love more than "whole mines of gold." And here's the thing you have to understand to feel the daring of it: she was writing inside a religion that told her she should *not* love any earthly thing that much, that her heart was supposed to be fixed on heaven. So this little poem is doing something slightly dangerous. It is insisting that human love — the love of a wife for a husband, in this life, in this body — is worth a poem. [pause]

Watch what she does at the end, though, because it's the move she makes again and again. Having loved him so fiercely, she turns it heavenward — "that when we live no more, we may live ever." She catches her own earthly passion and offers it up to God. And the question I want us to sit with tonight is: does that turn feel honest to you? Does it feel earned — a real act of faith — or does it feel like a woman covering her tracks, making sure no one can accuse her of loving this world too much? I genuinely don't know the answer, and I don't think she entirely did either. That uncertainty is where the poem lives. [pause]

The second poem, "Verses upon the Burning of Our House," is even better at this. Her house burns down. She watches it burn. And the poem records, with painful exactness, the grief of looking at the ruins — the table where guests will never sit again, the trunk where her things were stored — before it turns, with enormous effort, to the doctrine that says none of it mattered, that her real house is "framed by that mighty Architect" in heaven. Watch that turn. Watch how *long* it takes her to get there, and how much she lets herself grieve first. [pause]

Here's what I want you to carry out of tonight. We tend to think of constraint as the enemy of art — that a writer needs freedom. Bradstreet had almost none. She had a theology that policed her feelings, a culture that policed her speech, and a household that consumed her hours. And inside all of that, she found room — small, hard-won, but real — to say true things. That's not a lesser kind of achievement. In some ways it's the harder kind. Let's read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "If ever two were one, then surely we" (the opening of "To My Dear and Loving Husband").** Notice the conditional grammar. What to bring out: the poem builds its certainty out of a hypothesis — *if* such unity is possible anywhere, it is here — which is both more modest and more powerful than a flat assertion. The opening triplet ("If ever two were one... If ever man were loved by wife... If ever wife was happy") uses anaphora to stack three conditions, each raising the stakes, before the poem resolves into the bold "Compare with me, ye women, if you can." That challenge to other wives is the closest Bradstreet comes to open boast.

**2. "I prize thy love more than whole mines of gold, / Or all the riches that the East doth hold."** What to notice: the imagery is worldly, mercantile, even imperial — gold mines, the riches of the East. What to draw out: a Puritan poet reaching for the language of earthly treasure to measure love, then having to discipline that very valuation. The tension between the gorgeous materialism of the image and the theology that distrusts it is the whole drama in miniature.

**3. "And when we live no more, we may live ever" (the close of the same poem).** What to bring out: the turn from earthly to eternal love, accomplished in a single line. Ask whether the heavenly resolution cancels or completes the earthly passion. The best reading holds both: she does not retract her love of his body and presence; she extends it past death. The submission is also a kind of greed — she wants this love *forever*.

**4. "Here stood that trunk, and there that chest, / There lay that store I counted best" ("Verses upon the Burning of Our House").** What to notice: the catalogue of lost domestic objects, named with the deictic precision of someone standing in the ashes pointing — *here, there, there*. What to draw out: how the domestic interior, the woman's particular sphere, becomes the very site of grief and meaning. The poem treats a burned household as worthy of the high elegiac mode. This is the "domestic as subject" thread at its origin point.

**5. The pivot in "Verses": "Then straight I 'gin my heart to chide."** What to bring out: she literally scolds her own grieving heart, then constructs the consoling doctrine — that her treasure is "on high," in a house "purchased and paid for too / By Him who hath enough to do." Note how labored, how willed, the consolation is. The poem does not pretend the comfort comes easily. That honesty about the *difficulty* of faith is what keeps the ending from feeling like a pious formula.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **In "To My Dear and Loving Husband," Bradstreet writes about erotic and spiritual love at once. How does she manage the double register?**
   *Model answer:* She fuses them grammatically — the same hyperbole ("more than whole mines of gold") serves both the bodily and the eternal — and she sequences them, letting the earthly passion run its full course before turning it heavenward in the final line. The spiritual does not replace the erotic; it extends it past death.

2. **Why open the poem with a conditional — "If ever two were one"?**
   *Model answer:* The "if" makes the claim a wager rather than a boast, which is both more modest (appropriate to a Puritan woman wary of pride) and rhetorically stronger, since the reader is invited to test the hypothesis and find it true. It also lets her end with the challenge "compare with me, ye women, if you can" without seeming to have begun in vanity.

3. **"Verses upon the Burning of Our House" begins as an elegy for possessions and ends as theological affirmation. Does the ending feel earned, or forced?**
   *Model answer:* It can be read either way, and the productive answer holds the tension. The consolation is visibly *labored* — she has to chide her own heart into it — which makes it feel honest rather than glib. The length and specificity of the grief before the turn is what earns, or at least dignifies, the faith that follows.

4. **Bradstreet's community was, at best, ambivalent about women's intellectual life. Where do you find traces of that tension?**
   *Model answer:* In the self-deprecation of "The Author to Her Book" and the "Prologue," in the male sponsorship surrounding the published volume, and in the recurring pivots that subordinate her own strong feeling to doctrine. The tension is most audible in the gap between how much she clearly feels and how carefully she frames the feeling as permissible.

5. **What does the catalogue of lost objects accomplish in the "Verses"?**
   *Model answer:* By naming specific household things — trunk, chest, the place where guests sat — she grounds an abstract loss in concrete, domestic detail, making the grief real and particular. It also quietly insists that the domestic sphere, a woman's sphere, is worthy of serious poetry.

6. **How does Bradstreet's learning show itself, and why might she have hidden it?**
   *Model answer:* It shows in her command of form, her classical and biblical allusion, and her ambitious early "quaternion" poems. She downplayed it because open intellectual ambition in a Puritan woman risked the charge of pride and unwomanliness — the cautionary example of Anne Hutchinson loomed — so deprecation was protective.

7. **What surprised you most about these poems — the form, the feeling, or something else?**
   *Model answer:* Open. Useful answers note the surprising emotional directness (the love poem is franker than students expect from a Puritan), the modernity of the grief, or the discovery that the first American poet was a woman writing from a frontier household.

8. **Is the constant turn from earth to heaven a weakness or a strength in her poetry?**
   *Model answer:* It is the source of her power, not a flaw. The drama of her best poems is precisely the friction between strong earthly feeling and the doctrine that demands it be relinquished. Without the turn, the poems would be conventional love or grief lyrics; with it, they dramatize a genuine spiritual struggle.

9. **What does it mean that this body of work was produced in a working frontier household, not a study?**
   *Model answer:* It reframes our idea of the conditions art requires. Bradstreet wrote between the demands of eight children and the labor of survival, which makes her achievement a model of voice claimed against time, exhaustion, and a culture that offered her no room of her own.

10. **Why begin a course on women in American literature with Bradstreet specifically?**
    *Model answer:* Because she is literally first — the first published poet in the colonies — which corrects at the outset the assumption that women entered the tradition late, as a supplement. Beginning here establishes that women were present at the founding, not added afterward.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is that Bradstreet's plainness and her piety can read as dull or simple to modern students, who may mistake the doctrinal pivots for naïveté. The cure is slow reading aloud and a clear framing of the theological stakes — once students see that the heavenly turns are *hard-won* rather than automatic, the poems open. The seventeenth-century diction and inverted syntax will trip a few readers; paraphrase a stanza or two without apology. As the first session with a male instructor leading a women's-literature course, set the tone now: read Bradstreet's own lines aloud far more than you speak your own, treat her as a master rather than a pioneer-to-be-admired, and avoid the reflex of praising her merely "for her time." She is good, period. A male resident may feel the course is "not for him"; the love poem is your friend here, since it speaks to anyone who has loved another person and feared losing them. Lead with the universal door, then let the specific weather of her constraint emerge.

## Session 2 — A Poet in Chains: Phillis Wheatley

### At a Glance
This week we meet a poet who was enslaved as a child and who became, before the age of twenty, one of the most celebrated poets in the English-speaking world. Phillis Wheatley was the first African American to publish a book of poetry, and the first to achieve an international reputation. The session reads her not as a historical curiosity or a "first," but as a genuine master of the neoclassical form, who used the polished surface of the heroic couplet to encode a claim to her own mind and soul. The aim is to teach students to hear the assertion that lives inside the convention.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Phillis Wheatley was born around 1753, most likely in West Africa, in the region of present-day Senegal or Gambia, and was captured by slavers and transported to Boston aboard a slave ship — the schooner *Phillis*, from which she would take her first name — around 1761, when she was seven or eight years old ([Encyclopedia.com, "Wheatley, Phillis"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/women/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/wheatley-phillis-c-1752-1784); [Poetry Foundation, "Phillis Wheatley"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/phillis-wheatley)). She was purchased by John Wheatley, a prosperous Boston merchant tailor, as a domestic servant for his wife, Susanna; from them she received her surname, as was the practice. The detail of the ship's name is worth pausing over with students: she was named for the vessel that carried her into bondage. Her very name on the title page of her book is a wound and a record.

What happened next was extraordinary by any measure. The Wheatleys, recognizing the child's quickness, had her taught to read and write — by their daughter Mary, by most accounts — and Phillis proved a prodigy. Within a remarkably short time she was reading the Bible, Latin, the English poets, and the classical authors in translation; she absorbed Alexander Pope and the neoclassical tradition, the Latin poets Ovid and Virgil, and the scriptures, until she had command of the full apparatus of eighteenth-century learned poetry. Tell students plainly how astonishing this was: a child stolen from her home, sold into slavery, and within a decade writing accomplished verse in the dominant literary idiom of the age — an idiom that took educated free men years of schooling to master.

Her first published poem appeared in the *Newport Mercury* in 1767, when she was about thirteen or fourteen. Her reputation was made in 1770 with an elegy on the death of the famous evangelist George Whitefield, which circulated widely on both sides of the Atlantic. By the early 1770s she had written enough for a volume, but no Boston printer would publish a book by an enslaved African; the prejudice that a Black woman could not possibly have authored such verse was an obstacle in itself. Here we reach the episode that every student should know and that you should narrate with care. In 1772, Wheatley was examined — interrogated, in effect — by a panel of eighteen of Boston's most prominent men, including the governor and John Hancock, who questioned her to determine whether she had really written the poems attributed to her. They concluded that she had, and signed an attestation to that effect, which was printed at the front of her book as proof of its authenticity. Sit with the students in the strangeness and the violence of that scene: a poet made to prove, before a tribunal of powerful white men, that she possessed a mind capable of poetry. The attestation that vouched for her is also a monument to the doubt that required it.

Unable to publish in Boston, she found her support across the ocean. With the patronage of the Countess of Huntingdon, an English aristocrat and evangelical, Wheatley traveled to London in the summer of 1773 — accompanied by the Wheatleys' son — where she was received as a literary celebrity, and where her collection *Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral* was published in September 1773 ([Wikipedia, "Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poems_on_Various_Subjects,_Religious_and_Moral); [National Women's History Museum, "Phillis Wheatley"](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/phillis-wheatley)). It was the first book of poetry published by an African American and made her famous on two continents. Around the time of the book's publication, or shortly after her return to Boston, she was manumitted — freed from slavery. The connection between the literary triumph and the freedom is not coincidental; her achievement and her celebrity made her bondage untenable.

The remainder of her life was harder, and you should not soften it. The American Revolution disrupted the patronage networks and the literary economy on which a poet depended. She wrote a notable poem in 1775 in honor of George Washington's appointment as commander of the Continental Army, and Washington received her graciously and reportedly invited her to visit him. But the Wheatley household dissolved — John Wheatley died in 1778 — and Phillis, now free but without resources, married a free Black man named John Peters. The marriage was beset by poverty; she bore children who died young; she could not find a publisher for a proposed second volume during these years. She died in Boston in 1784, around the age of thirty-one, in poverty and obscurity, having worked at the end as a domestic in a boarding house. The arc is brutal: from international celebrity to a pauper's death in barely a decade.

Her legacy is contested in exactly the way that makes her interesting to teach. For much of the twentieth century she was patronized from two directions: dismissed by some as a derivative imitator of Pope, and criticized by others for the apparent accommodationism of poems like "On Being Brought from Africa to America," which seems to thank God for her removal from "Pagan" Africa. More recent scholarship — and a closer reading — has restored her as a sophisticated artist working under impossible constraints, who used the very forms of her oppressors' culture to stake a claim to full humanity, and who frequently encoded a subtle, double-voiced critique beneath a compliant surface. Tell students that the question of how to read her irony, her sincerity, and her strategy is not a settled matter but a live and serious one — and that the difficulty is a sign of her depth, not her limitation. She is not a "first" to be checked off. She is a major poet whose work rewards the closest reading we can give it.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Students need two contexts at once: the institution of slavery in colonial New England, and the neoclassical literary culture into which Wheatley wrote. On the first: slavery in eighteenth-century Boston was real and ordinary, if smaller in scale than the plantation slavery of the South. Enslaved people were domestic servants, dockworkers, and artisans; the Wheatley household's ownership of Phillis was unremarkable to their neighbors. This is essential background, because it is the ground against which her achievement and her celebrity must be measured: a society that bought and sold human beings was confronted by a human being, its property, who could out-write most of its free citizens.

On the second: the dominant mode of serious English poetry in Wheatley's time was neoclassicism — the tradition of Pope and Dryden, built on the heroic couplet (rhymed iambic pentameter), classical allusion, elevated and Latinate diction, and a poetics of balance, decorum, and public statement. To write in this mode was to claim membership in the highest literary culture of the age. Students raised on Romantic and modern ideals of "authentic," personal expression may find the form artificial or cold; you must teach them that for Wheatley, the choice of this form was itself a powerful argument. By demonstrating total command of the most prestigious literary idiom available, she refuted, line by line, the prevailing belief that Africans were incapable of intellectual and artistic achievement. The form was not a cage she failed to escape; it was a weapon she chose.

There is also the religious context. The eighteenth-century evangelical revival — the Great Awakening, the world of Whitefield and the Countess of Huntingdon — shaped Wheatley's idiom and her patronage. Her poems are saturated in Christian language and frequently take Christian salvation as their theme. Modern readers sometimes find this pious surface alienating or read it as evidence of co-optation. The richer reading sees Wheatley using the universalist claims of Christianity — that all souls are equal before God, that "Negroes... may be refined, and join th' angelic train" — as a theological lever against the racism of her enslavers. If all souls are equal before God, then the institution that treats her as property stands condemned by the very religion her masters profess. Christianity, in her hands, is not only consolation; it is argument.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"On Being Brought from Africa to America."** An eight-line poem in four heroic couplets. Lines 1–4: the speaker says it was "mercy" that brought her from her "Pagan land" and taught her benighted soul to understand that there is a God and a Savior — a salvation she once neither sought nor knew. Lines 5–6: she reports that some people view "our sable race with scornful eye," saying their color is a "diabolic die" (dye) — she quotes the racist judgment directly. Lines 7–8: she answers with an imperative addressed to "Christians": *remember* that "Negroes, black as Cain, may be refined, and join th' angelic train." The literal movement is from apparent gratitude for Christian conversion, to a direct quotation of contemporary racism, to a doctrinal rebuttal that asserts the equal salvific capacity — and thus the full humanity — of Black people.

**"On Imagination."** A longer ode (roughly fifty-three lines) in heroic couplets, personifying Imagination as a queenly, near-divine power. The poem invokes Imagination's "force" and "swiftness," describes how the mind can in an instant survey the whole creation, leave the rolling universe behind, and measure the heavens; it pictures winter and frost-work scenes the fancy can conjure, the mind soaring above the storm and the sun. Near the end the speaker reluctantly checks her flight — "but I reluctant leave the pleasing views" — because the cold realities of her situation (the season, and by implication her bondage) call her back to earth. The arc is a celebration of the boundless, sovereign freedom of the creative mind, ending in a forced and poignant descent.

**"To His Excellency General Washington" (1775).** A public verse address in heroic couplets. The speaker invokes the muse and personifies America as "Columbia," a goddess crowned with olive and laurel, whose cause the nations watch. She praises Washington as the leader "first in peace and honours," describes the martial glory of his army and the justice of the Revolutionary cause, and closes by wishing him victory, a crown, a "mansion," and "a golden throne" — urging "great chief" Washington on to the defense of freedom. The poem claims a confident public voice and positions its author as a participant in the national drama, celebrating a liberty that, pointedly, the new nation withheld from people like her.

### Lecture Script

I want to begin tonight with a name. The poet we are reading was called Phillis — and she was given that name because it was the name of the ship that carried her, as a child of seven or eight, from her home in West Africa into slavery in Boston. The schooner *Phillis*. So before she ever wrote a line, her name was a record of the crime committed against her. Hold that, because everything she wrote, she wrote under that name, in that condition. [pause]

And here is what she did with it. Within about ten years of arriving in Boston unable to speak English — a stolen child, a piece of property — she had taught herself, or been taught, to read Latin, to read the English poets, to read scripture and the classics, and she was writing poetry good enough to make her famous in London. Not "good for an enslaved person." Not "remarkable for her circumstances." Good. As good as most of what free, educated, university-trained men were producing in the same idiom. I want us to be very careful all term about the word "remarkable," because it can become a way of patronizing. So let me say it cleanly: Phillis Wheatley was a master of the neoclassical couplet. Full stop. [pause]

Now I have to tell you the scene that you can never forget once you've heard it. In 1772, the city of Boston could not quite believe that this young Black woman had written these poems. So they convened a panel — eighteen of the most important men in Massachusetts, the governor among them, John Hancock among them — and they examined her. They questioned her, to determine whether she could really have written the verse with her name on it. And they concluded that yes, she had, and they signed a document saying so, and that document was printed at the front of her book, as proof. [pause] Think about what that means. A poet had to stand before a tribunal of powerful men and prove she had a mind. The piece of paper that vouches for her is also the record of the insult that made it necessary. [pause]

So when we read her, we read her double. On the surface, "On Being Brought from Africa to America" looks almost grateful — it seems to thank God, even, for bringing her out of what she calls "Pagan" Africa into Christian light. And for a long time readers held that against her, called her an accommodator, said she'd been brainwashed into thanking her captors. But read it again. Read where it goes. By the end she is reminding her Christian audience that "Negroes, black as Cain, may be refined, and join th' angelic train." She is using their own religion against them. If all souls are equal before God — and that is the heart of the faith they claim — then the people who own her stand condemned. She makes the argument inside their own language, in their own most prestigious poetic form, so politely that they cannot object. That is not naïveté. That is strategy of the highest order. [pause]

Watch, in "On Imagination," what she claims for herself. Imagination — the highest creative faculty, the thing poets and philosophers reserved for genius — she takes it as her own subject and her own power. At a moment when her society was arguing about whether she even possessed a mind, she writes a poem celebrating the soaring, sovereign freedom of her own imagination. The poem is the proof of its own argument. [pause]

So let's read her the way she deserves — not as an exhibit, not as a "first," but as a poet making choices, encoding arguments, surviving inside a form and a faith that she turned, quietly and brilliantly, to her own ends. Open to "On Being Brought from Africa to America." It's eight lines. We're going to spend a long time on eight lines.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "'Twas mercy brought me from my Pagan land" (opening of "On Being Brought from Africa to America").** What to notice: the apparent gratitude, and the word "mercy" applied to an act of violence. What to draw out: the surface compliance that has troubled readers — and then the way the poem complicates it. Ask whether "mercy" is sincere, ironic, or strategically double. The honest answer is that it is genuinely ambiguous, and the ambiguity may be deliberate: she gives her white Christian readers the pious sentiment they expect, then turns it.

**2. "Some view our sable race with scornful eye, / 'Their colour is a diabolic die.'"** What to notice: she quotes the racist view directly, in quotation marks, giving it voice in order to answer it. What to bring out: the rhetorical control — she lets the prejudice speak, then refutes it from inside Christian doctrine. A poet in chains stages a debate and wins it in four lines.

**3. "Remember, Christians, Negroes, black as Cain, / May be refined, and join th' angelic train."** What to notice: the imperative "Remember," addressed directly to "Christians." What to draw out: the reversal of authority — the enslaved poet *instructs* her free Christian readers in their own theology. The equality of souls before God is the lever; "may be refined" insists on her full spiritual humanity and, by implication, condemns those who deny it. End on the daring of that imperative verb aimed at the powerful.

**4. "Imagination! who can sing thy force? / Or who describe the swiftness of thy course?" ("On Imagination").** What to notice: the apostrophe, the elevation of imagination to a near-divine power, and the speaker's command of the sublime register. What to bring out: at a moment when her culture doubted she had a mind at all, she writes a hymn to the boundless freedom of the creative mind. The poem performs the very faculty it praises; its existence is its argument.

**5. "Thee, first in peace and honours,—we demand / The grace and glory of thy martial band" ("To His Excellency General Washington").** What to notice: the confident public voice addressing the most powerful soldier in America, and the personified "Columbia" the poem invokes. What to draw out: the implicit argument of the address itself — an enslaved or newly freed Black woman speaks to Washington as a fellow participant in the national drama, a citizen of the "Columbia" she celebrates, claiming a place in the republic being born even as that republic excludes her. Note the poignancy: she sings the freedom of a nation that does not extend it to her.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **"On Being Brought from Africa to America" is eight lines and has generated decades of argument. Is Wheatley ironic, sincere, or something more complicated?**
   *Model answer:* Most persuasively, something more complicated than either. She offers her Christian readers a sincere-seeming piety and then turns it into an argument for equality, instructing "Christians" in their own doctrine. The poem can be read as both genuine faith and strategic critique, and the doubleness is likely deliberate.

2. **How does Wheatley use neoclassical form — the couplet, the allusion, the elevated diction — as both shelter and assertion?**
   *Model answer:* The form shelters her by making her poems respectable and acceptable to a white literary establishment, and it asserts her humanity by demonstrating total command of the era's most prestigious idiom. Mastering the form was itself a refutation of the claim that Africans could not reason or create.

3. **What does Wheatley do with "imagination" in the poem of that name?**
   *Model answer:* She claims the highest creative faculty as her own and celebrates its sovereign, boundless freedom — at a moment when her society doubted she possessed a mind at all. The poem enacts the power it describes, so its very existence proves its argument.

4. **What is the tone of her address to Washington, and what is the implicit argument?**
   *Model answer:* The tone is confident, public, and celebratory, addressing Washington as an equal participant in a shared national drama. The implicit argument is her own claim to belong to the emerging republic — a claim made poignant and pointed by the fact that the freedom she sings was denied to people like her.

5. **What do you notice about Wheatley's diction? Does the elevated register feel authentic, performed, or both?**
   *Model answer:* Both, productively. The Latinate, formal register is "performed" in that it is a deliberately mastered public style, but it is also authentically hers, the idiom she chose and commanded. The performance is the point: the polish is the proof of the mind behind it.

6. **Why did no Boston printer publish her book, and what does the 1772 examination tell us?**
   *Model answer:* Prejudice held that an enslaved African could not have written such verse, so no local printer would risk it. The examination by eighteen prominent men, and the attestation printed in the book, reveal both the depth of that doubt and the extraordinary burden of proof placed on her humanity.

7. **How does Wheatley use Christianity as argument rather than only consolation?**
   *Model answer:* She leans on the doctrine that all souls are equal before God to insist on her own full spiritual humanity and, by extension, to indict the slaveholding "Christians" she addresses. The faith her enslavers professed becomes, in her hands, the ground of their condemnation.

8. **For much of the twentieth century Wheatley was dismissed as derivative. What does that judgment miss?**
   *Model answer:* It mistakes her strategic mastery of a borrowed form for mere imitation, and it ignores the double-voiced critique encoded beneath the compliant surface. Reading her as derivative also imposes a Romantic ideal of "original" personal expression that was neither available to nor chosen by an eighteenth-century neoclassical poet.

9. **What is the relationship between her literary triumph and her manumission?**
   *Model answer:* They are linked: her international celebrity and demonstrated genius made her continued enslavement untenable, and she was freed around the time of her book's success. The poems, in a real sense, helped purchase her freedom — though that freedom brought poverty rather than security.

10. **Why insist on reading Wheatley as a major poet rather than a historical "first"?**
    *Model answer:* Because treating her as a "first" reduces her to a milestone and patronizes the work, whereas reading her as a master honors the actual achievement — the formal control, the encoded argument, the strategic intelligence. The "first" framing is the frame; the poetry is the picture.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The hardest material here is the apparent piety and gratitude of "On Being Brought from Africa to America," which can offend or confuse modern students who read it as accommodation to slavery. Do not resolve the difficulty too quickly; let students sit with the surface, then guide them to the turn and the double voice, and make clear that scholars genuinely disagree about how to weigh sincerity against strategy. The neoclassical form will feel cold or artificial to readers raised on confessional and Romantic ideals; teach the form as a deliberate and powerful choice rather than a limitation. As a male instructor, your job here doubles: you are leading a white-authored institution's "canon" course while honoring a Black woman enslaved by that society — resist the urge to perform guilt or to lecture about racism in the abstract, and instead let Wheatley's own lines make the argument, which they do with more force than any framing you could supply. Keep the focus on what she *does* on the page. The students will feel the history through the craft.

## Session 3 — Dickinson I: Death and Immortality

### At a Glance
Emily Dickinson published fewer than a dozen poems in her lifetime and left behind nearly 1,800. We give her two full sessions because she earns them and because she is genuinely, productively difficult. This first session takes up her great subject — death, dying, and what (if anything) lies beyond — through four poems that are less worn by anthology overuse than her most famous lyrics, and that show her at her strangest and most exact: "I felt a Funeral, in my Brain," "I heard a Fly buzz — when I died," "My Life had stood — a Loaded Gun," and "There's a certain Slant of light." The aim is to read slowly enough that the dashes, the slant rhymes, and the dislocations of syntax become legible as deliberate art rather than eccentricity.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Emily Dickinson was born in 1830 in Amherst, Massachusetts, into a prominent family; her grandfather had helped found Amherst College, and her father, Edward Dickinson, was a lawyer, treasurer of the college, and a United States congressman ([Emily Dickinson Museum, "Biography"](https://www.emilydickinsonmuseum.org/emily-dickinson/biography/emily-dickinson-her-childhood-and-youth-1830-1855/); [Mass Moments](https://www.massmoments.org/moment-details/mabel-todd-first-describes-emily-dickinson.html)). She grew up in the Homestead, the family house on Main Street, and was educated at Amherst Academy and then for a single year at Mount Holyoke Female Seminary, where the evangelical pressure to declare oneself "saved" met, in her, a quiet and lifelong resistance. She never made the public profession of faith expected of her, and this refusal — to settle into the certainties her culture offered — is one of the keys to her poetry, which circles questions of God, death, and immortality without ever landing on a creed.

The outward facts of her life are famously few, and you should be careful not to let the legend of the reclusive "Belle of Amherst" flatten her. It is true that she withdrew increasingly from society as she aged, that by her late thirties she rarely left the house and grounds, that she came to favor white dresses, and that she conducted many of her most important relationships through letters. But the reclusiveness was not emptiness or madness; it was, by every serious account, a deliberate concentration of a life into its essential activity — reading and writing at a ferocious pitch. She read the Bible, Shakespeare, the metaphysical poets, the Brontës, George Eliot, and the Springfield Republican; she gardened expertly; she baked; she sustained intense epistolary friendships. Within the apparent narrowness of that life she produced one of the largest and most radical bodies of poetry in the language.

The central fact of her career is that she did not publish. Fewer than a dozen of her poems appeared in print during her lifetime, several of them altered by editors who regularized her punctuation and rhyme — which may be part of why she stopped seeking publication. In 1862 she initiated a correspondence with the writer and reformer Thomas Wentworth Higginson, sending him poems and asking whether her verse "breathed"; he recognized something extraordinary but advised against publication and never quite knew what he had. She called publication "the Auction of the Mind of Man" and chose, instead, to circulate poems privately in letters and to sew them into hand-bound manuscript booklets, the "fascicles," which she kept. Tell students this is not failure or timidity: it is one of the strangest and most consequential decisions in literary history, a poet writing at the highest level entirely for a posterity she could only trust would come.

It did come, but only because of an act of disobedience. Dickinson died in 1886, of kidney disease (Bright's disease), at the age of fifty-five. She had reportedly asked that her papers be destroyed. Her sister Lavinia found the cache of nearly 1,800 poems and, instead of burning them, resolved to get them published. The first selection, edited by Mabel Loomis Todd and Higginson, appeared in 1890, four years after Dickinson's death, and was a surprising success — though the editors "smoothed" her dashes, supplied titles, and softened her rhymes. It would take until 1955, with Thomas H. Johnson's variorum edition, and later R. W. Franklin's, for readers to encounter the poems as Dickinson actually wrote them, dashes and capitals and slant rhymes intact. The numbering you will see — "Fr" for Franklin, sometimes "J" for Johnson — refers to these scholarly editions.

A word on her radicalism, because students need to understand why she matters so much. Dickinson did not write decorative nineteenth-century verse. She took the plain hymn-meter of the Protestant hymnbook — the common meter of "Amazing Grace" — and filled it with a content and a syntax that strained it to the breaking point. She made the dash into a new kind of punctuation, a mark for the catch of breath, the swerve of thought, the gap where language fails. She rhymed off-key on purpose (slant rhyme), so that the expected click of closure is withheld and the poem ends slightly ajar. She compressed thought to the point of riddle. She wrote about consciousness itself — perception, grief, dread, the moment of death from the inside — with a precision that no American poet had attempted and few since have matched. She is not a delicate spinster poet of flowers and bees, though she wrote about flowers and bees. She is one of the most formally daring and intellectually ferocious poets in the history of the language.

Her legacy is incalculable and entirely posthumous. She is now universally regarded as one of the two great founders of American poetry, alongside Whitman — the two of them, opposite in nearly every way, between them opening the territory all later American poets have worked. Where Whitman expands, she contracts; where he catalogs the world, she pierces a single instant; where he says "I" and means a nation, she says "I" and means a mind alone with itself. For the women in this course she is a particular kind of ancestor — proof that a woman writing in a single room, refused by or refusing the literary marketplace, could remake the art entirely. We spend two weeks with her because no single session could hold her.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The world Dickinson wrote in was saturated with death in ways modern students rarely grasp, and this is essential to her great subject. In mid-nineteenth-century New England, death was domestic and constant: people died at home, were laid out at home, were mourned at home; infant and child mortality was high; epidemics swept through towns; the Civil War (1861–65) filled the period of her most intense productivity with mass death on an unprecedented scale. The "good death" — the deathbed scene observed for signs of the soul's salvation, the dying person's last words scrutinized for evidence of grace — was a cultural ritual of enormous weight. When Dickinson writes a poem from inside the moment of dying, watching for the expected arrival of "the King" and getting, instead, a fly, she is subverting a ritual every reader of her era knew intimately. The macabre exactness of her death poems is not morbidity; it is a clear-eyed engagement with the central fact of her culture.

The religious context is the second key. Dickinson lived through the long aftermath of Puritan Calvinism and the revivals of the Second Great Awakening, in a community where conversion and public profession of faith were socially expected. Her refusal to convert, her lifelong wrestling with a God she could neither fully believe nor fully dismiss, places her poems in a state of permanent theological suspension. Immortality is, in her own word, the "Flood subject" — the thing she returns to obsessively, never resolving. Students should understand that her poems are not statements of belief or unbelief; they are instruments for thinking about the unthinkable, holding faith and doubt in the same taut line.

Finally, the gendered context. A woman of Dickinson's class was expected to marry, run a household, and subordinate any intellectual life to domestic duty. Dickinson did neither marry nor subordinate; she arranged a life — with the protection of family money and an unmarried daughter's place in the family home — that gave her the one thing Virginia Woolf would later say every woman writer needed: a room of her own and time to think. That she had to become a recluse to secure that freedom tells you something about how little room the era offered a woman who wished, above all, to write.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"I felt a Funeral, in my Brain" (Fr340).** Five quatrains in hymn meter. The speaker feels a funeral taking place inside her own mind: mourners tread back and forth until sense seems "breaking through" (stanza 1); a service begins, a drum beats until her mind goes numb (stanza 2); she hears them lift a box (coffin) and creak across her soul with "those same Boots of Lead" (stanza 3); then space tolls like a bell, all the heavens become a bell and existence "just an Ear," and she and Silence are some strange wrecked race, "Wrecked, solitary, here" (stanza 4); finally a "Plank in Reason" breaks, and she drops down, down, hitting a world at every plunge, and "Finished knowing — then —" (stanza 5). The poem dramatizes a mind's collapse — into madness, despair, or death — as an interior funeral, ending mid-fall with the breaking-off of consciousness itself.

**"I heard a Fly buzz — when I died" (Fr591).** Four quatrains. The speaker, already dead and speaking from beyond, recalls the moment of dying: a fly buzzed (stanza 1); the room was still, the watchers' eyes wrung dry, everyone braced for the solemn arrival of "the King" — God, or death's majesty — to be witnessed (stanza 2); she had signed away her keepsakes, given what was hers to give (stanza 3); and then, instead of revelation, "there interposed a Fly," with a "Blue — uncertain stumbling Buzz," between her and the light, the windows failed, and she "could not see to see" (stanza 4). The expected sublime deathbed sign is replaced by a trivial, buzzing insect — the anticlimax is the point.

**"My Life had stood — a Loaded Gun" (Fr764).** Six quatrains, one of her most enigmatic poems. The speaker's life stood like a loaded gun in corners until an "Owner" identified and carried her away (stanza 1). Now they roam and hunt together; when she speaks (fires) for him, the mountains reply (stanza 2). She guards his head at night, an arrangement better than sharing his pillow (stanza 4); to his foe she is deadly (stanza 5). The famous final stanza turns paradoxical: "Though I than He — may longer live / He longer must — than I — / For I have but the power to kill, / Without — the power to die." The poem allegorizes a self that comes alive only as the instrument of another's will, lethal but unable to die on its own — readable as a parable of female agency, of the artist's power, or of latent violence.

**"There's a certain Slant of light" (Fr320).** Four quatrains. A particular quality of winter afternoon light "oppresses, like the Heft / Of Cathedral Tunes" (stanza 1). It gives "Heavenly Hurt" that leaves no scar but an internal difference "Where the Meanings, are" (stanza 2). None can teach it; it is the "Seal Despair," an "imperial affliction / Sent us of the Air" (stanza 3). When it comes, the landscape and shadows seem to listen and hold their breath; when it goes, it is "like the Distance / On the look of Death" (stanza 4). The poem names, with great precision, a wordless seasonal mood of existential dread provoked by light.

### Lecture Script

Tonight and next week we are going to spend our time with a woman who wrote in a single room in Amherst, Massachusetts, who published almost nothing in her lifetime, and who is now understood to be one of the two or three greatest poets this country has produced. Her name was Emily Dickinson. She left behind nearly eighteen hundred poems, sewn into little hand-bound booklets, found in her drawer after she died. She had asked for her papers to be burned. Her sister disobeyed. Everything we have, we have because of that disobedience. [pause]

I want to clear away a picture first, because most of us were handed it. The picture is of a frail, eccentric spinster in a white dress, writing dainty little poems about flowers and birds. Forget it. The woman who wrote these poems is one of the most ferocious intelligences in American literature. She took the simplest possible form — the meter of the Protestant hymnbook, the meter of "Amazing Grace," the meter a child could clap to — and she filled it with content so strange, so compressed, so daring, that it nearly cracks the form open. She is not delicate. She is dangerous. [pause]

Listen to how this one begins: "I felt a Funeral, in my Brain." Not "I attended a funeral." Not "I imagined." She *felt* a funeral — inside her own head. The whole poem is the experience of a mind coming apart, and she stages it as a funeral happening inside the skull: the mourners pacing, the drum, the coffin lifted and carried across her very soul, and then — listen — "a Plank in Reason, broke, / And I dropped down, and down." And the poem ends not with a conclusion but with the breaking-off of consciousness itself: "And Finished knowing — then —." Then a dash. Then nothing. She ends the poem at the exact instant the mind stops. That dash is doing something no period could do. [pause]

Here is the thing about her dashes, and I want you to hold onto this all term. They are not decoration and they are not bad punctuation. The dash is the mark of the gap — the catch of breath, the swerve of thought, the place where language runs out and she refuses to pretend otherwise. When she ends a poem on a dash, she is telling you the thought continues past where words can follow. Watch for it tonight in every poem. [pause]

Now, her great subject in these four poems is death — and I want you to notice how *unsentimental* she is about it. In her world, death was everywhere: people died at home, in the next room; the Civil War was killing young men by the tens of thousands while she wrote. And there was a whole ritual around the "good death" — you watched the dying person for a holy sign, for the soul's last word. So listen to what she does in "I heard a Fly buzz — when I died." The speaker is already dead, telling you about it. Everyone in the room is braced for the big moment, the arrival of "the King," the holy sign. And what arrives? A fly. A common housefly, with a "Blue — uncertain — stumbling Buzz." The grand revelation everyone expected — and instead, a bug at the window. [pause] She is looking straight at the thing her whole culture wrapped in comfort, and she's reporting what she actually sees. That's not morbid. That's honest, and it's brave.

And then the strangest of the four — "My Life had stood — a Loaded Gun." A life that is a loaded weapon, standing in a corner, until an owner picks it up and it comes alive only by doing his killing. We'll argue tonight about what that means — is it about a woman whose power exists only in someone else's hands? Is it about the poet, lethal and waiting? Hold your reading loosely. This poem has never been fully solved, and that's part of its greatness. Let's read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "And then a Plank in Reason, broke, / And I dropped down, and down — / And hit a World, at every plunge, / And Finished knowing — then —" (close of "I felt a Funeral").** What to notice: the poem ends mid-fall, on a dash, with the breaking-off of consciousness. What to bring out: the dash after "then" leaves the sentence — and the mind — unfinished; the reader is dropped with the speaker. Ask what is more frightening: a stated conclusion, or the refusal to conclude. The form enacts the collapse it describes.

**2. "I heard a Fly buzz — when I died" (opening line).** What to notice: the syntactic collision of the trivial ("Fly buzz") and the absolute ("when I died") in a single line, and the impossible perspective of a speaker narrating her own death. What to draw out: the entire poem's strategy is set here — the sublime undercut by the mundane. The fly is the anti-revelation, and the dash between "buzz" and "when" holds the two registers apart for a beat.

**3. "With Blue — uncertain — stumbling Buzz — / Between the light — and me — / And then the Windows failed — and then / I could not see to see —" (close of "I heard a Fly buzz").** What to notice: the dashes fragmenting the line into the stutter of failing perception, and the haunting "could not see to see." What to bring out: "see to see" means both physical sight failing and the failure of insight — she dies without the spiritual vision the deathbed was supposed to deliver. The fly comes "between the light and me," literally blocking the holy light. Brilliant, bleak, exact.

**4. "For I have but the power to kill, / Without — the power to die" (close of "My Life had stood — a Loaded Gun").** What to notice: the paradox of a thing that can kill but cannot die. What to draw out: read it three ways and let the class choose — (a) a woman whose agency exists only as the instrument of a man's will, lethal in his service but with no autonomous life or death of her own; (b) the poet, whose words outlive her and remain "loaded," dangerous, unable to die; (c) the destructive energy that comes alive only when claimed by an owner. The poem refuses to settle, which is its power.

**5. "There's a certain Slant of light, / Winter Afternoons — / That oppresses, like the Heft / Of Cathedral Tunes" (opening of "There's a certain Slant of light").** What to notice: the precision of "certain Slant" and "Winter Afternoons," and the synesthetic comparison of light's weight ("Heft") to the oppressive weight of organ music. What to bring out: she names a real, wordless mood — the existential heaviness of late-winter afternoon light — with clinical exactness, then calls the feeling it gives "Heavenly Hurt," a wound that "leaves no scar / But internal difference / Where the Meanings, are." The poem locates spiritual dread in a quality of light, and insists the change it works is internal, in the seat of meaning itself.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **"I felt a Funeral, in my Brain" stages a funeral inside the mind. What event is the poem actually describing?**
   *Model answer:* It dramatizes a mind coming apart — readable as the onset of madness, a descent into despair, or the experience of dying — figured as an interior funeral. The point is the process of consciousness breaking down, ending at the instant knowing stops, rather than any single nameable event.

2. **Why does the poem end on a dash after "Finished knowing — then —"?**
   *Model answer:* Because the dash leaves the sentence and the consciousness unfinished, dropping the reader with the falling speaker into a silence no period could supply. It enacts the breaking-off of mind it describes, refusing the closure a completed statement would give.

3. **In "I heard a Fly buzz," what was the room expecting, and what arrives instead?**
   *Model answer:* The watchers expect the solemn arrival of "the King" — a holy, revelatory sign at the deathbed, the spiritual climax the culture demanded. Instead a common fly interposes itself between the dying speaker and the light, replacing revelation with a trivial, stumbling buzz.

4. **What does "I could not see to see" mean?**
   *Model answer:* On one level her physical sight fails as she dies; on another, the phrase names a failure of insight — she dies without the spiritual vision the "good death" was supposed to grant. The doubled "see" makes the loss both literal and metaphysical.

5. **"My Life had stood — a Loaded Gun" is famously unresolved. Offer one reading and defend it.**
   *Model answer:* Open. A strong reading takes it as a parable of female agency: the speaker is a power that comes alive only when claimed and aimed by an "Owner," lethal in service but lacking autonomous life or the power to die. Other defensible readings cast the gun as the poet's outliving words, or as destructive energy awaiting a master.

6. **What do you make of the final paradox — "power to kill, / Without — the power to die"?**
   *Model answer:* It describes a being that can deal death but cannot die itself, an existence that is pure instrumental force without independent mortality or rest. Whether read as the condition of a dependent woman, an immortal poem, or unexpended violence, it figures a self that is potent and yet not free.

7. **How does "There's a certain Slant of light" describe a feeling without naming it directly?**
   *Model answer:* It anchors the mood in a precise external phenomenon — winter-afternoon light — and renders its effect through weight ("Heft"), sound ("Cathedral Tunes"), and a wound that "leaves no scar." By naming the occasion so exactly, she lets the unnamed dread become palpable without stating it as "sadness" or "dread."

8. **Dickinson uses slant rhyme — rhymes that almost rhyme. Find one and describe its effect.**
   *Model answer:* For example, the near-rhyme of "Air" / "Despair" or "scar" / "are" withholds the satisfying click of full rhyme, leaving the stanza slightly ajar and unresolved. The off-key chiming mirrors the poems' refusal of consolation and easy closure.

9. **All four poems take a stance of cold, exact observation toward extreme experience. What does that detachment achieve?**
   *Model answer:* It lends terrible authority and credibility; by refusing lament, she makes the dread and the dying feel observed rather than performed. The clinical precision is more unsettling than emotional outcry would be, because it implies a mind capable of watching its own collapse.

10. **What kind of mind do you encounter in these four poems?**
    *Model answer:* Open. Useful answers note a ferocious, fearless intelligence willing to look directly at death, madness, and dread without the comforts of doctrine; a formal radical who bends a simple meter to strange ends; and a consciousness more interested in honest perception than in consolation.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Dickinson's compression, her dashes, and her dislocated syntax will stop students cold; the cure is patient reading aloud and paraphrase, line by line, until the literal sense surfaces before any interpretation. Resist the urge to "solve" the poems, especially "My Life had stood — a Loaded Gun," whose richness depends on its irresolution; model holding several readings at once. Some students arrive with the sentimental "Belle of Amherst" image and will be surprised by the darkness and daring; lean into that surprise as a teaching opportunity. For the male instructor, Dickinson offers a clean lesson in how to teach a woman writer without condescension: there is nothing to "excuse" and no special pleading to make — she is simply one of the greatest poets in the language, and the room will feel that if you stay on the craft. A few students may find the death poems frightening or personally raw; acknowledge the weight, allow a beat of silence after reading "I heard a Fly buzz" aloud, and let the discussion give the feeling somewhere to go.

## Session 4 — Dickinson II: Interior Weather

### At a Glance
The second Dickinson session moves from death and immortality toward the interior weather of the mind — the textures of pain, numbness, possibility, and truth-telling. These are among her most philosophically precise poems: "After great pain, a formal feeling comes," "It was not Death, for I stood up," "Pain — has an Element of Blank," "I dwell in Possibility," and "Tell all the truth but tell it slant." Together they show Dickinson as an anatomist of consciousness, mapping inner states with an exactness no American poet had attempted. The session also gives you the chance to draw the two weeks together and ask what kind of intelligence the students have met.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

(For Dickinson's full biography, see Session 3; what follows extends it toward the concerns of this session.)

The biographical fact most relevant tonight is the relationship between Dickinson's chosen seclusion and the inwardness of her art. Having arranged a life of deliberate withdrawal — by her forties she rarely left the Homestead and its garden, received few visitors, and conducted intimacy through letters — Dickinson turned the full force of an extraordinary attention inward, onto the operations of her own consciousness. Where another poet might report on the wide world, she reports on the interior one: what grief actually feels like in the body, how pain organizes time, what the mind does when it is too stunned to feel. Her seclusion was not a retreat from experience but a concentration of it; she became, in effect, the leading observer of a territory most people pass through without noticing — the inside of a feeling.

It helps to know that her period of greatest productivity coincided with the Civil War years and with a series of personal losses and crises — the deaths of friends and mentors, an eye ailment that frightened her with the prospect of blindness, and the emotional upheavals recorded in the mysterious "Master" letters, three unsent drafts of passionate address to an unidentified recipient. Scholars debate the identity of the "Master" and the nature of Dickinson's deepest attachments, which included intense bonds with women, most notably her sister-in-law and neighbor Susan Gilbert Dickinson, to whom she sent more poems than to anyone else. You should handle this material with conservative care: the biographical facts of her loves are genuinely uncertain, and the poems are not transcriptions of a life. What is certain is that she knew grief, dread, and longing intimately, and that she made them the subject of an art of unprecedented psychological precision.

The other fact worth carrying into tonight is her relationship to language and limit. Dickinson distrusted direct statement; she believed, as "Tell all the truth but tell it slant" puts it, that truth approached head-on is blinding and must be angled, dimmed, told gradually. This is not evasion but epistemology — a theory of how human beings can bear knowledge at all. Her formal habits (the dash, the slant rhyme, the compression to riddle) are the technical expression of this conviction. She is a poet of the indirect, the partial, the glimpsed — which is why she rewards, and requires, slow reading. By the end of two sessions students should be able to describe not just what individual poems mean but what kind of mind made them: skeptical, exact, fearless before interior extremity, and committed to the truth told slant.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The relevant context tonight is intellectual rather than biographical: Dickinson as a psychologist before the discipline of psychology existed. She wrote in the decades before William James (whose *Principles of Psychology* appeared in 1890, four years after her death) gave America a science of consciousness and the phrase "stream of thought." Working alone, without that vocabulary, Dickinson anatomized states of mind — shock, numbness, dread, the suspension of feeling after trauma — with a precision that anticipates later psychology and modern poetry alike. When she writes that after great pain "the Nerves sit ceremonious, like Tombs," she is describing what we might now call dissociation or traumatic numbing, decades before those concepts existed. Tell students they are reading a pioneer of the interior, mapping country that had no map.

The literary context is her relationship to the meter of belief. Dickinson's hymn-meter forms carry, like a ghost, the religious certainties of the tradition that produced them, even as her content withholds those certainties. The result is a productive tension: the comforting, familiar shape of the hymn houses thoughts that refuse comfort. This is the formal signature of her doubt — orthodox vessel, heterodox content. Students who know "Amazing Grace" can be shown that many Dickinson poems can be sung to its tune, which dramatizes the gap between the form's promise and the poem's refusal of easy faith.

Finally, the gendered context bears restating for this session's emphasis on interiority. The nineteenth century assigned women the realm of "feeling" and men the realm of "thought," and used the division to deny women intellectual seriousness. Dickinson explodes the division: her poems are feeling thought through with the rigor of philosophy, emotion submitted to the coldest analysis. In claiming the interior life as a subject for rigorous art, she refuses the era's condescending sentimentalization of female feeling and insists that the inside of a woman's mind is a fit object for the most serious attention. "I dwell in Possibility — / A fairer House than Prose —" is, among other things, a woman claiming the largest possible house for herself, the house of poetry, with its "numerous" doors and windows and its roof of sky.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"After great pain, a formal feeling comes" (Fr372).** Three stanzas describing the aftermath of severe suffering. Stanza 1: after great pain a "formal feeling" arrives; the nerves "sit ceremonious, like Tombs," the stiff heart asks whether it was *it* that bore the pain, and "Yesterday, or Centuries before?" — time has come unmoored. Stanza 2: the feet go round mechanically, on ground or air or "Ought," a "Wooden way," with "a Quartz contentment, like a stone" — numb, mineral, indifferent. Stanza 3: this is "the Hour of Lead," remembered, if survived, "As Freezing persons, recollect the Snow — / First — Chill — then Stupor — then the letting go." The poem maps the stages of traumatic numbness and ends with the sequence of freezing to death as the figure for how shock ends.

**"It was not Death, for I stood up" (Fr355).** Five-plus quatrains of negation. The speaker tries to name an extreme inner state by eliminating what it is not: not Death (she stood up), not Night (the bells were ringing noon), not Frost (she felt hot winds), not Fire (her marble feet were cold). She finds it resembles all of them and is "like" the figures she has seen "set orderly, for Burial." Finally she likens her condition to midnight, to a stopped and "stopless" cool, to "Chaos — Stopless — cool — / Without a Chance, or Spar — / Or even a Report of Land — / To justify — Despair." The poem enacts the impossibility of naming a state of total desolation by ruling out every available name, arriving at a hopeless, shoreless chaos.

**"Pain — has an Element of Blank" (Fr760).** A short poem (two stanzas) on the phenomenology of pain. Pain contains "an Element of Blank"; it cannot recollect when it began or whether there was ever a time without it. It has no future but itself; its "Infinite contain[s] / Its Past — enlightened to perceive / New Periods — of Pain." The poem argues that pain abolishes time: in its grip there is no memory of painlessness and no expectation of relief, only an eternal present of suffering that perceives the future merely as more of itself.

**"I dwell in Possibility" (Fr466).** Three quatrains, a poem of poetic vocation. The speaker says she dwells in "Possibility — / A fairer House than Prose," one with more numerous windows and "superior — for Doors." Its chambers are "as the Cedars" impregnable to the eye; its roof is "the Gambrels of the Sky." Its visitors are "the fairest," and her occupation is "This — / The spreading wide my narrow Hands / To gather Paradise." The poem celebrates poetry (Possibility) over prose as a more open, capacious dwelling, and defines the poet's work as the gathering of the infinite ("Paradise") with the finite human reach of "narrow Hands."

**"Tell all the truth but tell it slant" (Fr1263).** A single eight-line poem. It advises telling all the truth but telling it "slant" — indirectly — because "Success in Circuit lies." Truth's "superb surprise" is too bright for our "infirm Delight," just as lightning must be explained gently to frightened children. Truth must "dazzle gradually / Or every man be blind." The poem is Dickinson's compact ars poetica and epistemology: total, direct truth blinds; it must be approached at an angle, dimmed, delivered by degrees, if it is to be borne at all.

### Lecture Script

Last week we read Emily Dickinson on death. Tonight we read her on something harder, in a way — the weather inside the mind. What does it actually feel like to be in great pain? What happens to a person in the moment after a catastrophe, when feeling shuts off? What does the mind do when it cannot bear what it knows? Dickinson is the great cartographer of these interior states. She mapped country that, in her time, had no map — this is decades before psychology was a science, before anyone had the word "trauma" in the sense we mean it. She did it alone, in a room, with nothing but a hymnbook meter and a ferocious gift for exactness. [pause]

Listen to this. "After great pain, a formal feeling comes — / The Nerves sit ceremonious, like Tombs." If you have ever been through a shock — a death, a diagnosis, a sudden loss — you know this feeling, even if you never had words for it. It is not grief, exactly. It is the strange formality that descends afterward, the numbness, the sense of going through the motions while something in you has gone to stone. "A Quartz contentment, like a stone," she calls it. She is describing what a clinician today might call dissociation, and she nailed it a hundred and fifty years before the textbooks. [pause]

And notice how the poem ends — not with recovery, but with an image of freezing to death. "As Freezing persons, recollect the Snow — / First — Chill — then Stupor — then the letting go." That is the sequence of dying of cold, and she uses it as the figure for how the hour of lead either kills you or passes. "The letting go" — is that death, or is that survival, the moment the numbness finally releases? She won't tell you. The dash before it holds both. [pause]

Here is the poem I most want you to carry, because it's a little instruction manual for everything she does. "Tell all the truth but tell it slant — / Success in Circuit lies." She is saying: the whole truth, told straight at you, is too much. It blinds. Like lightning, like the sun — you can't look directly at it. So the truth has to come at an angle, dimmed, by degrees — "The Truth must dazzle gradually / Or every man be blind." [pause] Now hold that next to her dashes, her riddles, her refusals to name things directly — and you understand them completely. They aren't decoration and they aren't shyness. They are her theory of how a human being can survive knowing anything true at all. You tell it slant because told straight it would burn out your eyes. [pause]

And then, against all that pain and dread, one poem of pure joy — "I dwell in Possibility." She compares poetry to a house, a house "fairer" than prose, with more windows, more doors, a roof made of sky. And she ends by describing her own work, her vocation: "The spreading wide my narrow Hands / To gather Paradise." Think about that image. A woman in a small house in a small town, who has chosen to give her whole life to this, opening her two narrow human hands as wide as they'll go — to gather the infinite. That's the ambition under all the modesty. That's what she was actually doing in that room. [pause]

So tonight let's read these five poems slowly, and at the end let's try to answer a question together: after two weeks, what kind of mind have we been keeping company with? How would you describe her, this woman, to someone who has never read a line? Let's read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "The Nerves sit ceremonious, like Tombs — / The stiff Heart questions 'was it He, that bore,' / And 'Yesterday, or Centuries before'?" ("After great pain").** What to notice: the simile of nerves as tombs, the heart's confusion about time and even about who suffered. What to bring out: trauma unmoors time and self — "Yesterday, or Centuries before" makes the recent unbearable event feel ancient or unplaceable. This is a clinical observation rendered as image. The "formal feeling" is the mind's protective ceremony over its own wound.

**2. "First — Chill — then Stupor — then the letting go —" (close of "After great pain").** What to notice: the three-stage sequence, separated by dashes, of freezing to death used as the figure for the aftermath of pain. What to draw out: the dashes meter out the stages like a slow clock; "the letting go" is deliberately ambiguous — death, or release from numbness, or simply forgetting. The poem ends in that ambiguity rather than resolving it.

**3. "'Twas like a Maelstrom, with a notch... / Without a Chance, or Spar — / Or even a Report of Land —" ("It was not Death").** What to notice: after the long list of negations (not Death, not Night, not Frost, not Fire), the poem arrives at a shoreless chaos with no rescue and no land in sight. What to bring out: the entire poem proceeds by elimination — naming what the state is *not* — because the state itself has no name. The failure of naming *is* the poem; despair is what's left when every available word has been ruled out, and there is not even "a Report of Land — / To justify — Despair."

**4. "Pain — has an Element of Blank — / It cannot recollect / When it begun — or if there were / A time when it was not —" ("Pain — has an Element of Blank").** What to notice: pain as a state that abolishes memory and time. What to draw out: the "Blank" is the erasure of any before or after; in pain there is only an eternal present that "perceive[s] / New Periods — of Pain." This is the phenomenology of chronic suffering — the way it convinces the sufferer it has always been and will always be. Note how the abstraction "Pain" is treated as an entity with its own faulty memory.

**5. "I dwell in Possibility — / A fairer House than Prose — ... The spreading wide my narrow Hands / To gather Paradise" ("I dwell in Possibility").** What to notice: the extended architectural metaphor (poetry as a house superior in windows and doors, roofed by the sky) and the closing image of vocation. What to bring out: "Possibility" is poetry itself, more open and capacious than "Prose"; the poem is an ars poetica disguised as a real-estate boast. The final image — narrow human hands spread wide to gather the infinite — defines the poet's impossible, magnificent task and quietly claims it for a woman in a small house.

**6. "Tell all the truth but tell it slant — / Success in Circuit lies" (opening of "Tell all the truth").** What to notice: the paradoxical advice to tell *all* the truth but *slant*, and "Circuit" as the indirect route. What to bring out: this is her method stated outright — indirection as the only bearable approach to overwhelming truth. Connect it to every dash and riddle in the preceding poems; "dazzle gradually / Or every man be blind" is the rationale for her entire difficult, oblique style.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **"After great pain" describes the stages of shock with cold detachment. Why does detachment serve the subject better than lamentation?**
   *Model answer:* Because the poem's subject *is* detachment — the numb, formal aftermath of pain in which feeling shuts down. A lamenting style would contradict the state being described; the cold, exact tone enacts the very numbness it reports, making it credible and chilling.

2. **What does the closing image of freezing — "First — Chill — then Stupor — then the letting go" — accomplish?**
   *Model answer:* It gives the aftermath of pain a precise physiological analogue and a sequence, ending in an ambiguous "letting go" that may be death, survival, or forgetting. The dashes pace the stages like a slow clock, and the refusal to specify which outcome "letting go" names keeps the poem honest about not knowing.

3. **"It was not Death" proceeds entirely by saying what the state is *not*. Why?**
   *Model answer:* Because the inner condition has no name; it resembles Death, Night, Frost, and Fire without being any of them. The strategy of negation dramatizes the failure of language before total desolation, and the poem lands in a shoreless "Chaos" precisely because no positive word will hold it.

4. **What is the "Element of Blank" in pain, and what does the poem claim about pain and time?**
   *Model answer:* The "Blank" is the erasure of memory and anticipation; pain abolishes any sense of a before or after. The poem claims that in suffering there is only an eternal present that perceives the future as nothing but more pain — a precise account of how chronic pain distorts time.

5. **How does "I dwell in Possibility" use the metaphor of a house, and what is the poet's "occupation"?**
   *Model answer:* Poetry is figured as a house "fairer than Prose," with more windows and doors and a roof of sky — more open, more admitting of light and possibility. The poet's occupation is "the spreading wide [of] narrow Hands / To gather Paradise": using finite human means to take in the infinite.

6. **In what sense is "I dwell in Possibility" a woman's claim as much as a poet's?**
   *Model answer:* A nineteenth-century woman was confined to the literal house and the domestic sphere; here Dickinson claims instead the largest possible house — poetry, roofed by the whole sky — as her dwelling and her work. It converts the domestic metaphor of the house into a declaration of boundless imaginative ambition.

7. **Unpack "Tell all the truth but tell it slant." Is this evasion or epistemology?**
   *Model answer:* It is epistemology, not evasion: the poem argues that the whole truth told directly blinds, as lightning or the sun would, and so must be angled and dimmed to be survivable. Indirection is presented as the condition under which truth can be received at all — a theory of human limits, not a dodge.

8. **How does "Tell all the truth" explain Dickinson's difficult style?**
   *Model answer:* Her dashes, riddles, slant rhymes, and compressions are the technical means of telling it slant — approaching overwhelming matter at an angle so it "dazzle[s] gradually." The poem is, in effect, the manifesto behind the obliquity of everything else we have read.

9. **Several of these poems treat abstractions (Pain, Possibility, Truth) as if they were entities or places. What does that habit do?**
   *Model answer:* It externalizes inner experience so it can be examined, giving feelings a structure, a memory, an architecture. By turning states of mind into agents and dwellings, Dickinson makes the invisible interior analyzable — the move of a psychologist working in metaphor.

10. **After two sessions, how would you describe Dickinson's mind to someone who has never read her?**
    *Model answer:* Open. Useful answers: a fearless, skeptical intelligence that looks directly at death, pain, and dread without religious consolation; a formal radical who bends hymn-meter to strange ends; a pioneer of the inner life who tells the truth "slant" because she believes that is the only way the truth can be borne.

### Anticipated Difficulties

These poems are even more abstract than last week's, and students may feel they cannot "get" them; reassure them that partial understanding is the right relationship to Dickinson and that paraphrasing the literal sense first (which the summaries above let you do instantly) unlocks most of the difficulty. "It was not Death" can frustrate readers who want a name for the state it describes — turn that frustration into the lesson, since the unnameability *is* the poem. Some students will resist "Tell all the truth" as a license for obscurity; clarify that she is making a claim about human limits, not endorsing willful difficulty. Because tonight's poems touch pain, trauma, and depression, a few students may find them close to home; "After great pain" in particular can land hard. Acknowledge that, allow silence, and balance the session by ending on "I dwell in Possibility," whose joy is the deliberate counterweight to the darkness. For the male instructor, the gendered reading of "I dwell in Possibility" is worth voicing but not belaboring — name it once, then return to the craft.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Helen Vendler, *Dickinson: Selected Poems and Commentaries* (Harvard University Press, 2010) — covers several of tonight's poems directly.
- Sharon Cameron, *Lyric Time: Dickinson and the Limits of Genre* (Johns Hopkins University Press, 1979) — on Dickinson's handling of time, directly relevant to "After great pain" and "Pain — has an Element of Blank."
- Christanne Miller, *Emily Dickinson: A Poet's Grammar* (Harvard University Press, 1987) — on the dash, syntax, and compression.
- Wendy Martin, ed., *The Cambridge Companion to Emily Dickinson* (Cambridge University Press, 2002).
- Alfred Habegger, *My Wars Are Laid Away in Books: The Life of Emily Dickinson* (Random House, 2001) — the fullest reliable biography, conservative on the uncertain attachments.

### One Thing to Memorize

"Tell all the truth but tell it slant — / Success in Circuit lies." — the two lines that explain Dickinson's entire method and, arguably, the strategy of every careful writer in this course.

## Session 5 — The Book That Started a War: Harriet Beecher Stowe

### At a Glance

Tonight we meet the most consequential American novel of the nineteenth century, and one of the most consequential ever written in English: *Uncle Tom's Cabin*. We read only the first two chapters — the Kentucky parlor where Tom and little Harry are sold, and the cabin where the news arrives — but those two chapters contain the whole engine of the book: the casual commerce in human beings, the sentimental appeal to the mother's heart, and the Christian conscience set against the bill of sale. The session asks students to take seriously a kind of writing the twentieth century taught us to disdain — the sentimental, the frankly persuasive, the tear-jerking — and to ask what it accomplished. This is also our first encounter with the most contested word in American literature, and with the way a white woman's book about enslaved people both galvanized abolition and froze a damaging type into the culture. We do not resolve that tension; we hold it.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Harriet Beecher Stowe was born in Litchfield, Connecticut, in 1811, the seventh child of Lyman Beecher, one of the most famous Calvinist preachers in America, and the sister of Henry Ward Beecher, who would become the most famous preacher of the next generation ([Britannica, "Harriet Beecher Stowe"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Harriet-Beecher-Stowe)). She grew up in a household where religion, argument, and moral seriousness were the very air, and where the daughters were educated more rigorously than was usual — her sister Catharine Beecher founded influential schools for women, and Harriet taught in them. The family's piety was not abstract; it carried the assumption that a Christian was obligated to act on conscience in the world.

In 1832 the family moved to Cincinnati, Ohio, where Lyman Beecher headed a theological seminary. Cincinnati sat on the Ohio River, directly across from the slave state of Kentucky, and there Stowe encountered slavery at close range — fugitives crossing the river, the auction block within a day's travel, the testimony of formerly enslaved people, and the fierce local conflict between abolitionists and their enemies. She married Calvin Stowe, a seminary professor and biblical scholar, in 1836, and bore seven children. The death of her infant son Charley in 1849, in a cholera epidemic, was a turning point she herself named: it taught her, she said, what an enslaved mother felt when her child was torn away — and it is no accident that the separation of mother and child is the moral center of her great novel ([Harriet Beecher Stowe Center](https://www.harrietbeecherstowecenter.org/harriet-beecher-stowe/)).

The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850, which compelled Northerners to assist in returning escaped slaves and criminalized those who helped them, was the immediate provocation. *Uncle Tom's Cabin; or, Life Among the Lowly* was first serialized in the antislavery newspaper *The National Era* from 1851 to 1852, then published as a book in 1852 ([Britannica, "Harriet Beecher Stowe"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Harriet-Beecher-Stowe)). Its success was without precedent — hundreds of thousands of copies in the first year, translations across Europe, stage adaptations beyond counting. When Stowe met Abraham Lincoln, he is said to have greeted her as "the little woman who wrote the book that started this great war" — a story almost certainly embroidered, but one we keep because it captures the scale of the book's effect, and we should tell students it may be apocryphal ([Smithsonian Magazine, "The Little Woman Who Wrote the Book"](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/history/uncle-toms-cabin-180970146/)). Stowe wrote many other books — *Dred*, another antislavery novel; *The Minister's Wooing*; New England regional fiction that influenced Jewett, whom we meet in Session 8 — but she was, and remains, the author of one book above all. She lived until 1896, long enough to see the war fought, slavery ended, and Reconstruction abandoned. The legacy is double, and we must teach both halves: a novel that moved a nation toward emancipation, and a title character whose name became, in the twentieth century, an epithet for servility. We will not let either half cancel the other.

### Historical & Cultural Context

To understand why this book detonated, students must understand the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850. Part of the Compromise of 1850, it required citizens of free states to assist in capturing escaped slaves, denied the accused a jury trial or the right to testify, and penalized those who sheltered them ([National Archives, "Fugitive Slave Act"](https://www.archives.gov/milestone-documents/compromise-of-1850)). It made slavery not a distant Southern institution but a legal obligation pressing on every Northerner's conscience — exactly Stowe's argument, that there were no clean hands.

The novel arrives within a flourishing tradition of sentimental and domestic fiction, written largely by women and read largely by women, which located moral authority in the home, the heart, and the mother. Twentieth-century criticism dismissed this writing as mawkish; recent scholarship, notably Jane Tompkins's influential essay "Sentimental Power," has argued that the sentimental novel was a serious instrument of cultural work, mobilizing feeling toward political ends ([Jane Tompkins, "Sentimental Power," in *Sensational Designs*](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2925985)). Stowe's strategy is precisely that: to make the reader *feel* the wrongness of slavery in the body — to weep at the auction of a child — and to convert that feeling into action. This is rhetoric in the oldest sense, and it worked.

We must also place the book honestly in the history of racial representation. *Uncle Tom's Cabin* was anti-slavery, but its portraits drew on and helped codify racial types — the patient, pious, suffering Tom; the comic or pathetic child — that minstrel-stage adaptations (the "Tom shows," over which Stowe had no control) coarsened further, until "Uncle Tom" curdled into a slur ([Britannica, "Uncle Tom's Cabin"](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Uncle-Toms-Cabin)). The instructor's task is to let students hold the book's historical courage and its racial limitations in the same hand, without flattening either.

### Summary of the Work(s)

We read **Chapters I and II**. Chapter I, "In Which the Reader Is Introduced to a Man of Humanity," opens on a February afternoon at the Kentucky estate of **Mr. Shelby**, a genteel slaveholder in debt to a coarse slave trader named **Haley** ([SparkNotes, *Uncle Tom's Cabin*](https://www.sparknotes.com/lit/uncletom/section1/)). To settle what he owes, Shelby proposes selling his most trusted enslaved man, **Tom** — honest, devout, dependable. Haley insists one slave is not enough. When a beautiful four-or-five-year-old boy, **Harry**, enters and is made to sing and dance for the men's amusement, Haley demands the child too. Harry's mother, **Eliza**, comes to fetch him; Haley, struck by her beauty, suggests Shelby sell her as well, but Shelby refuses, saying his wife is too fond of her. Shelby reluctantly agrees to think over the sale of Tom and Harry and to talk again that evening. Eliza, having overheard fragments, goes anxiously to her mistress, **Mrs. Shelby**, who — ignorant of her husband's debts — laughingly assures her that no one will be sold, least of all Harry. The chapter's terrible irony is that the kind mistress's promise is already void.

Chapter II, "The Mother," gives Eliza's history: raised indulgently by Mrs. Shelby, married to **George Harris**, a brilliant, literate enslaved man on a neighboring estate who invented a labor-saving machine only to be punished for it by a jealous master ([SparkNotes, *Uncle Tom's Cabin*](https://www.sparknotes.com/lit/uncletom/section1/)). Having lost two children already, Eliza clings to Harry. George, degraded and forbidden to see her, tells Eliza he intends to escape to Canada and send for them. The two chapters establish the novel's whole moral architecture: human beings priced like livestock in a gentleman's parlor, and the bonds of marriage and motherhood that the system treats as nothing.

### The Text(s) for This Session

- *Uncle Tom's Cabin*, Chapters I–II, available free in full at [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/203). Chapter I is "In Which the Reader Is Introduced to a Man of Humanity"; Chapter II is "The Mother."

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] Tonight we read a book that almost no one assigns whole anymore, and that almost everyone has an opinion about — most of those opinions formed without reading a page of it. So let us do the radical thing and read it.

I want to begin with the title of the first chapter, because it is the whole novel in miniature: "In Which the Reader Is Introduced to a Man of Humanity." [pause] The "man of humanity" is Haley, the slave trader. Stowe's irony is not subtle — she does not want it to be. She wants you to feel the gap between the word and the man, between "humanity" and what this man does for a living, and she wants that gap to hurt. [pause]

Now, look at where we are. A parlor in Kentucky. Two gentlemen — and Stowe is careful to make Shelby a gentleman, kind by the standards of his class — discussing a debt over wine. And the way they settle the debt is by selling people. A man named Tom. A child named Harry, who is brought in and made to dance for them. [pause] I want you to notice how quietly Stowe does this. There is no whip in this chapter, no overseer, no plantation horror of the kind we expect. There is only a pleasant room and a polite conversation, and in the middle of that conversation a mother's child is sold. The horror is in the ordinariness. That is the argument: that the system corrupts even the kind, that there are no clean hands, that "I am a humane man" is what every man in the room believes about himself. [pause]

Some of you will have come in tonight knowing that "Uncle Tom" is an insult. You are right that it became one. I am not going to ask you to pretend otherwise, and I am not going to ask you to love this book. But I am going to ask you to separate two things: what Stowe wrote, and what a century of minstrel adaptations and political shorthand made of it. [pause] The Tom of the actual novel is not a coward and not a fool; he is a man whose religious conviction is the one thing his owners cannot buy or beat out of him. Whether that makes him a hero or a problem is one of the great arguments in American literature, and tonight you get to have it — but have it about the text, not about the cartoon.

Here is what I most want you to carry. [pause] This is a book that set out to make its readers *feel* something so strongly that they would *act*. The whole nineteenth-century tradition of sentimental fiction — domestic, tearful, written by women for women and dismissed by the men who ran the magazines — is a tradition that believed feeling was a moral force, that a tear could be the beginning of justice. We were taught to sneer at that. [pause] Stowe is the great test case. Because whatever else you think of this book, it did the thing. It moved a nation that did not want to be moved. So tonight I want us to ask, with real seriousness and no condescension: how does sentiment do its work? And when is feeling not weakness, but power? [pause] Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **The chapter title, "In Which the Reader Is Introduced to a Man of Humanity."** The irony is structural, not decorative. By naming Haley "a man of humanity," Stowe forces the reader to define the word against him — and, crucially, against Shelby too, who believes himself the better man for being reluctant. The point is that reluctance is not innocence. Ask students who the "humane" people in the room actually are.

2. **Harry made to perform.** The child is told to sing and to imitate, for the men's entertainment, and the moment is played as charming before we register its horror — a child performing for the men who are pricing him ([SparkNotes, *Uncle Tom's Cabin*](https://www.sparknotes.com/lit/uncletom/section1/)). Stowe lets the reader enjoy the boy's liveliness, then closes the trap. This is sentimental technique at its most deliberate: charm as the setup for grief.

3. **Haley's economic theology.** Haley argues that he is *more* humane than reformers because he keeps families together when it is convenient and profitable to do so, and reasons that Black people do not feel separation as whites would. Stowe gives the proslavery rationalization its own voice and lets it condemn itself. Note for students how she refutes by quotation rather than by lecture.

4. **Mrs. Shelby's false comfort.** At the close of Chapter I, the kind mistress assures Eliza that Harry is safe — a promise already worthless because she does not know her husband's debts ([BookRags, *Uncle Tom's Cabin*](https://www.bookrags.com/notes/utc/part1.html)). The irony indicts not malice but the willed ignorance of the benevolent slaveholder, who can afford kindness only because she does not look at the ledger.

5. **George Harris and the invented machine (Ch. II).** George's intelligence and his punishment for it — removed from the factory because his master resents a slave who excels — dismantles the proslavery fiction that the enslaved were suited to bondage ([SparkNotes, *Uncle Tom's Cabin*](https://www.sparknotes.com/lit/uncletom/section1/)). His gifts are precisely what the system must crush. Pair this with Wheatley from Session 2: genius in chains.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does Stowe set the opening in a "kind" household rather than a brutal one?**
   *Model answer:* Because her argument is that slavery corrupts even the benevolent, and that there are no clean hands. A brutal master would let readers comfort themselves that the problem is bad individuals; the kind Shelby shows that the system itself, not merely cruelty, sells children. The ordinariness is the indictment.

2. **What is the effect of the chapter title's irony?**
   *Model answer:* It forces the reader to weigh the word "humanity" against the man it names, and then against everyone in the room. It establishes Stowe's method — refutation by ironic juxtaposition — and warns the reader that the polite surface conceals an atrocity.

3. **How does the scene of Harry performing work on the reader?**
   *Model answer:* It first charms, presenting the child as delightful, then horrifies, as we realize he is being appraised for sale. The technique is the essence of the sentimental: pleasure converted into grief so that the reader feels the wrong rather than merely understanding it.

4. **Haley claims to be humane. How does Stowe answer him?**
   *Model answer:* She lets him talk. His own reasoning — that he keeps families together when profitable, that Black people feel less — condemns him without authorial comment. Stowe refutes proslavery argument by giving it voice and trusting the reader's conscience.

5. **What does George Harris's story add to the moral case?**
   *Model answer:* It refutes the claim that the enslaved were fit for slavery by showing a man of obvious genius punished precisely for excelling. His invention and degradation reveal that the system must suppress Black ability to sustain itself, linking him to Wheatley's "genius in bondage."

6. **Is the novel's sentimentality a weakness or a strength? Argue both.**
   *Model answer:* As weakness: it can manipulate, simplify, and substitute feeling for analysis. As strength: as Jane Tompkins argues, sentiment was the era's most effective instrument of moral persuasion, and it moved a resistant public toward abolition. The honest answer is that it is both, and that its political effectiveness is undeniable.

7. **How should a modern reader hold the novel's antislavery courage alongside its racial stereotyping?**
   *Model answer:* By refusing to collapse one into the other. The book advanced emancipation and humanized the enslaved for white readers who had never considered their inner lives; it also drew on and entrenched racial types that later adaptations made worse. A mature reading honors both truths without using either to erase the other.

8. **Why has "Uncle Tom" become a slur, and is that fair to Stowe's character?**
   *Model answer:* The slur derives largely from the minstrel "Tom shows" and from political usage, not from the novel's Tom, who is a man of unbreakable conviction rather than servility. It is fair to note the slur's history and unfair to assume Stowe's text is its source; the gap between the written Tom and the cultural "Uncle Tom" is itself a lesson in how texts are used.

9. **Stowe makes motherhood the moral center. Why is that strategically shrewd?**
   *Model answer:* Because the separation of mother and child was an experience her largely female, largely Christian readership could feel without instruction, and because it located the argument in a sphere — the home — where women's moral authority was uncontested. She turned the era's domestic ideology into an antislavery weapon.

10. **What does it mean that a novel "started a war"?**
    *Model answer:* The claim is exaggerated and the Lincoln anecdote likely apocryphal, but it points to a real truth: that *Uncle Tom's Cabin* shifted Northern public feeling on a scale no political tract had managed, making the moral stakes of slavery vivid to ordinary readers. It is the strongest case in American history for literature as a political force.

### Anticipated Difficulties

This session contains the course's hardest material to handle in a mixed room: the most contested word in American English appears in the text, and the legacy of the "Uncle Tom" stereotype is alive in students' minds. Decide in advance how you will handle the language — most instructors read around the slur and address it directly as a subject rather than voicing it — and say your policy aloud at the start, with adult composure and no apology. Some students arrive certain the book is racist trash; others, that it is a sacred abolitionist text; your task is to slow both down and return them to the actual two chapters. As a male instructor of a course of women writers, you face a particular version of the central challenge here: a white-authored book about Black suffering, taught by a white man. Name your own position briefly if it helps, then get out of the way and let the text and the scholarship — especially Black critics like James Baldwin and recent scholars — carry the argument. Do not perform either guilt or defensiveness; model the careful reading that the inflamed reputation of this book makes so rare.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Jane Tompkins, "Sentimental Power: *Uncle Tom's Cabin* and the Politics of Literary History," in *Sensational Designs: The Cultural Work of American Fiction, 1790–1860* (Oxford University Press, 1985) — the essay that rehabilitated the sentimental.
- James Baldwin, "Everybody's Protest Novel" (1949), in *Notes of a Native Son* (Beacon Press) — the indispensable dissent, indicting the novel's sentimentality and its Tom.
- Joan D. Hedrick, *Harriet Beecher Stowe: A Life* (Oxford University Press, 1994) — Pulitzer Prize–winning biography, the standard life.
- Thomas F. Gossett, *Uncle Tom's Cabin and American Culture* (Southern Methodist University Press, 1985) — on the book's vast and tangled afterlife, including the Tom shows.
- David S. Reynolds, *Mightier than the Sword: Uncle Tom's Cabin and the Battle for America* (W. W. Norton, 2011) — on the novel's political impact and reception.

### One Thing to Memorize

The first chapter's title — "In Which the Reader Is Introduced to a Man of Humanity" — and the fact that the "man of humanity" is the slave trader. It teaches everything about Stowe's irony and her method in a single line.

## Session 6 — The Wife Who Brought in the Harvest: Louisa May Alcott

### At a Glance

Tonight we read Louisa May Alcott not as the beloved author of *Little Women* but as a satirist with a knife. "Transcendental Wild Oats" is her wry, devastating account of Fruitlands — the utopian farm community her father Bronson Alcott helped found in 1843 — written thirty years later by the daughter who, as a ten-year-old girl, watched the men philosophize while the women did the work. The piece is short, funny, and quietly furious, and it lets us examine one of the recurring threads of this course in its purest form: the gap between high ideals proclaimed by men and the unglamorous labor performed by women that makes any life possible at all. We also get to complicate the saintly image of the Transcendentalists, and to watch a woman writer use comedy — the most deniable of weapons — to tell the truth about her own family.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Louisa May Alcott was born in Germantown, Pennsylvania, in 1832, the second of four daughters of Amos Bronson Alcott and Abigail "Abba" May Alcott ([Britannica, "Louisa May Alcott"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louisa-May-Alcott)). Her father was a Transcendentalist philosopher and education reformer of genuine originality and almost no practical sense — a man who held high views about the soul and low earning power, and who repeatedly subjected his family to poverty in pursuit of his ideals. The Alcotts moved often, and Louisa grew up in the orbit of Concord's great men: Ralph Waldo Emerson, who quietly supported the family financially and lent Louisa books, and Henry David Thoreau, who took the children botanizing. Her mother, Abba, an early social worker and a woman of fierce practicality, held the household together through her husband's experiments and was the emotional and economic anchor of Louisa's life.

The defining catastrophe of Louisa's childhood was Fruitlands. In 1843, when she was ten, Bronson Alcott and the English reformer Charles Lane established a "consociate family" on a farm near Harvard, Massachusetts, devoted to the most rigorous principles: no animal products of any kind — no meat, milk, eggs, leather, or wool; no animal labor, so the land was to be worked by human hands; no commerce that exploited any creature ([Walden Woods Project, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.walden.org/work/lmalcott-transcendental-wild-oats-the-independent-18-dec-1873/)). The experiment lasted barely seven months and collapsed in the winter, nearly destroying the family. The labor fell almost entirely on Abba and the girls while the men discoursed; when the men eventually wandered off after inspiration, it was the women who tried to bring in the meager harvest before the cold. Louisa never forgot it.

Necessity made Louisa a professional writer. To support her family she wrote pseudonymous "blood and thunder" thrillers — sensational tales of passion and revenge published under the name A. M. Barnard, which scholars rediscovered only in the twentieth century — alongside more respectable work. She served as a Union nurse during the Civil War until typhoid (and the mercury used to treat it) wrecked her health; her *Hospital Sketches* (1863) brought early recognition. Then in 1868–69 came *Little Women*, the semi-autobiographical novel of the four March sisters that made her famous and, finally, financially secure, and that has never gone out of print ([Britannica, "Louisa May Alcott"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Louisa-May-Alcott)). She wrote sequels and many other books, supported her parents and sisters, was an active abolitionist and an advocate of women's suffrage — the first woman registered to vote in Concord — and never married. She died in Boston in 1888, two days after her father, her own health long broken by her war service.

"Transcendental Wild Oats" was published in the New York newspaper *The Independent* on December 18, 1873, three decades after the events it describes ([Walden Woods Project, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.walden.org/work/lmalcott-transcendental-wild-oats-the-independent-18-dec-1873/)). It is the work of a mature professional looking back with affection, irony, and an unmistakable verdict on whose labor the dream had cost. We should be conservative about reading it as straight autobiography — it is a shaped, fictionalized sketch with renamed characters — but its emotional accounting is hers.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Transcendentalism was the great American intellectual movement of the 1830s and 1840s, centered in Concord and led by Emerson: a Romantic, idealist faith in the divinity of the individual soul, the authority of intuition over institution, and the moral primacy of nature ([Britannica, "Transcendentalism"](https://www.britannica.com/event/Transcendentalism-American-movement)). It produced Emerson's essays, Thoreau's *Walden*, and a cluster of communal experiments — most famously Brook Farm, and, at its most austere, Fruitlands — that tried to live the philosophy. These utopias were sincere and often noble; they were also, almost without exception, designed by men whose ideals depended on women's unacknowledged labor.

This is the context students must grasp: the period that produced America's most exalted philosophy of self-reliance also produced households in which self-reliance for the man meant total reliance, by the woman, on her own unending work. Alcott's sketch is the inside view of that arrangement. The "cult of domesticity" we will define in the glossary — the doctrine that woman's sphere was the home, her power moral and private — assigned women the labor of sustaining life while denying them the public standing of the men whose ideas that labor underwrote. Fruitlands is the reductio ad absurdum: a community so committed to purity it would not exploit an ox, while exploiting, without noticing, its women and children.

Alcott chose comedy, and the choice is itself historical. Satire was one of the few modes in which a woman could criticize revered men — Emerson and her own father among them — and survive socially. By renaming Bronson "Abel Lamb" and Charles Lane "Timon Lion," she gains the deniability of fiction while making her point unmistakable. Comedy, here, is the weapon of the person who cannot afford to attack directly.

### Summary of the Work(s)

"Transcendental Wild Oats" opens on a stormy first of June, 1843, as a small band of "modern pilgrims" makes its way to a New England farm ([UNIGE, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.unige.ch/vls/anthology/alcott)). The party comprises **Abel Lamb** (Bronson Alcott), an impractical idealist of "the devoutest faith"; his wife, **Sister Hope** (Abba Alcott), "unconverted but faithful to the end," who wants only rest and a home for her children; their daughters; and **Timon Lion** (Charles Lane), who means to found a colony that will "regenerate the world and glorify his name for ever," along with his son. Alcott quotes the community's grandiloquent founding letter, with its promises to "supersede the labor of cattle by the spade and the pruning-knife" and to enter "the kingdom of peace" through "self-denial."

The "prospective Eden" turns out to be a run-down red farmhouse, a dilapidated barn, and ten old apple trees — christened, with sublime optimism, "Fruitlands" ([Walden Woods Project, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.walden.org/work/lmalcott-transcendental-wild-oats-the-independent-18-dec-1873/)). The rules are absolute: unleavened bread, porridge, vegetables, and water; no tea, coffee, wine, milk, butter, cheese, meat, or even salt and spice; no wool or leather; no animal labor and no animal manure. When Sister Hope asks what work her husband and Brother Timon will actually do, the men reply that "being" rather than "doing" is the great aim, achieved through "resigned willingness" rather than "willful activity." A member named **Jane Gage** is caught eating a bit of fish and sneaking milk and cheese, is publicly reproved, and leaves. The men's purity soon bends to necessity — a team of oxen (one of which proves to be a cow) is pressed into service when the men discover that working the land by hand is hard.

By summer's end the experiment is failing. The men follow their "inspirations" away from the farm — toward the Shakers and elsewhere — leaving **Sister Hope** and the children to rush in what little harvest there is before a storm ([Literary Ladies Guide, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.literaryladiesguide.com/full-texts-of-classic-works/transcendental-wild-oats-louisa-may-alcott-1973-full-text/)). The community dissolves; the family is left without food, friends, or money, having given everything to "the experiment." Abel Lamb, broken, lies near despair until his wife rouses him; the sketch ends on the single word "Hope!" — both the family's only remaining resource and the rechristened name of the wife who has, throughout, been the one actually holding life together.

The full text is also available with scholarly framing at the [Walden Woods Project](https://www.walden.org/work/lmalcott-transcendental-wild-oats-the-independent-18-dec-1873/).

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] Last week we read a book that helped start a war. Tonight we read something much smaller and much funnier, and I want to argue that it is just as serious. It is a daughter's account of the time her father tried to build paradise and very nearly starved his family doing it.

Most of you know Louisa May Alcott as the author of *Little Women* — Jo March, the attic, the four sisters. Hold onto that, because the woman who wrote that warm book also wrote pseudonymous thrillers full of revenge and poison to pay the bills, and tonight she is in a third register entirely: the satirist. [pause] "Transcendental Wild Oats" is her revenge — gentle, comic, but real — on Fruitlands, the utopian community her father Bronson Alcott founded with an Englishman named Charles Lane in 1843, when Louisa was ten years old.

Here is what Fruitlands believed. [pause] No meat. No milk, no eggs, no cheese, no butter. No wool, because that exploits the sheep; no leather, because that exploits the cow. No animal labor — you may not yoke an ox, because the ox did not consent. No manure, for the same reason. They would work the land with their bare hands and the spade. They named the place Fruitlands, on the strength of ten old apple trees. [pause] Now, I want to be fair to these men. Their principles were, in a sense, ahead of their time — what they were reaching toward we would now call ethical veganism, and you may find it admirable. Alcott does not simply mock them. But here is the thing she cannot let pass, and the thing I want you to watch for tonight. [pause]

When Sister Hope — that is Alcott's name for her own mother, Abba — asks the men what *labor* they intend to perform, the answer comes back: "Being in preference to doing is the great aim." [pause] Being, not doing. And so the men sat in the parlor and *were*, and discussed the soul, and waited for inspiration — and the woman and the children planted, and weeded, and cooked the unleavened bread, and when the storm came at the end of the summer and the men had wandered off after their inspirations toward the Shakers, it was the wife and the little girls who ran out to bring in the harvest. [pause]

That is the whole course in one image, and I want you to sit with it. The men have the philosophy. The woman does the work. The men get the glory or the failure of the *idea*; the woman gets the actual hunger when the idea fails. [pause] Alcott is not arguing against ideals. She is arguing that an ideal which floats free of the labor that sustains daily life — an ideal that will not exploit an ox but will exploit, without noticing, its own wife — is not high-minded. It is a luxury, and somebody else pays for it.

And notice how she does it. [pause] She does not write an angry essay. She could not have; you did not, in 1873, publish a frontal attack on your sainted father and on Emerson's circle. So she writes comedy. She changes the names — Bronson becomes "Abel Lamb," Charles Lane becomes "Timon Lion," so you can tell which one bites. She lets the absurdities speak for themselves: the cow disguised as an ox, the man caught eating a fish tail. Comedy is the weapon of the person who is not allowed to carry a sword. [pause] Watch her use it. Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **"Being in preference to doing is the great aim."** This is the sketch's thesis disguised as a quotation. Abel Lamb's lofty principle is, in practice, a justification for not working — and Alcott places it in direct answer to his wife's wholly practical question about who will do the labor ([UNIGE, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.unige.ch/vls/anthology/alcott)). The comedy is in the juxtaposition: metaphysics offered as a reply to "who will hoe the field?"

2. **The christening of "Fruitlands."** Ten ancient apple trees become, by the alchemy of optimism, "Fruitlands" — a name that promises orchards from "inner consciousness" ([Walden Woods Project, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.walden.org/work/lmalcott-transcendental-wild-oats-the-independent-18-dec-1873/)). The gap between the grand name and the dilapidated reality is Alcott's basic satiric engine, and it recurs in every paragraph: the sublime label, the shabby fact.

3. **The renamed characters: Abel Lamb and Timon Lion.** The names do double work. "Lamb" suggests gentleness and sacrifice (and is near to "Alcott"); "Lion," with "Timon" recalling Shakespeare's misanthrope, marks the one "who more often bares his teeth" ([Louisa May Alcott Is My Passion](https://louisamayalcottismypassion.com/2026/02/05/call-them-by-their-names-transcendental-wild-oats-by-edward-rowett-lifts-the-veil-from-alcotts-short-story-and-makes-the-alcotts-come-to-life/)). Naming is Alcott's deniable judgment — fiction's license to tell the truth.

4. **The ox that is a cow.** The community forbids animal labor on principle, then quietly yokes a team when hand-tilling proves too hard — and one of the "oxen" turns out to be a cow ([UNIGE, "Transcendental Wild Oats"](https://www.unige.ch/vls/anthology/alcott)). The detail captures how absolute principle bends the instant it meets necessity, and how the bending is never admitted.

5. **The ending: "Hope!"** The sketch closes on the wife rousing her despairing husband, and on the single word "Hope" — which is both the family's last resource and the name Alcott gave her mother in the story ([Louisa May Alcott Is My Passion](https://louisamayalcottismypassion.com/2026/02/05/call-them-by-their-names-transcendental-wild-oats-by-edward-rowett-lifts-the-veil-from-alcotts-short-story-and-makes-the-alcotts-come-to-life/)). The final word belongs to the woman who held everything together — the quiet last word of the satire's argument.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What is the central irony of Fruitlands as Alcott presents it?**
   *Model answer:* The community would not exploit an animal — no leather, no wool, no ox labor — yet it exploited, without noticing, the labor of its own women and children. The men's scruple about the ox is exact while their blindness to the wife is total, and that asymmetry is the sketch's whole argument.

2. **Why does Alcott choose satire rather than direct denunciation?**
   *Model answer:* Because in 1873 a woman could not safely publish a frontal attack on her revered father and Emerson's circle. Comedy gave her deniability — "it is only a sketch, the names are changed" — while letting the absurdities indict the men. Satire is the available weapon of those denied direct ones.

3. **What does the line "Being in preference to doing is the great aim" reveal?**
   *Model answer:* It exposes how high philosophy can function as an excuse for idleness. Placed as the answer to his wife's practical question about labor, the line shows the men's "being" resting entirely on the women's "doing," and turns a lofty principle into a comic evasion.

4. **How does Alcott's naming of the characters work?**
   *Model answer:* By renaming Bronson "Abel Lamb" and Charles Lane "Timon Lion," she signals her judgment — the gentle lamb, the biting lion — while preserving the fiction's protective distance. The names are a quiet verdict delivered under cover of invention.

5. **Is Alcott mocking the ideals of Transcendentalism, or something else?**
   *Model answer:* Not the ideals themselves, which she treats with some respect, but the men's failure to connect ideals to the labor that sustains life. Her target is an idealism that floats free of who actually feeds and clothes the community — a critique of irresponsibility, not of aspiration.

6. **How does this sketch illuminate the course's thread about women's labor?**
   *Model answer:* It presents the thread in its starkest form: men supply the philosophy and the public meaning; women supply the unglamorous, life-sustaining work; and when the idea fails, women bear the material cost — the hunger, the harvest run, the broken family. It is the gendered division of labor made visible.

7. **Why does the sketch end on the word "Hope"?**
   *Model answer:* Because "Hope" is both the family's only remaining resource after the experiment ruins them and the name Alcott assigned her mother in the story. The double meaning gives the last word, literally and morally, to the woman who held the family together while the men chased inspiration.

8. **How reliable is this sketch as history?**
   *Model answer:* It is a shaped, fictionalized account written thirty years later, with renamed characters and comic exaggeration, so we should be cautious about treating specific details as documentary. Its emotional accounting, however — who labored, who philosophized, who paid — is consistent with the historical record of Fruitlands.

9. **Compare Alcott's satire with Stowe's sentiment as instruments of women's writing.**
   *Model answer:* Both are strategies for a woman to exert moral pressure in a world that limited her direct authority. Stowe weaponizes feeling to move readers toward justice; Alcott weaponizes comedy to expose hypocrisy she could not denounce outright. Each chose the deniable, socially survivable mode available to her.

10. **What is gained by reading the author of *Little Women* in this satiric register?**
    *Model answer:* It corrects the sentimental flattening of Alcott into a writer of cozy domestic fiction and reveals the sharp, working professional beneath — a woman who wrote thrillers for money and satire about her own family, and who understood, from the inside, exactly what her father's ideals had cost the women around him.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is tonal: students may read the sketch as simple ridicule and miss its affection, or as nostalgia and miss its anger. Guide them to the doubled tone — Alcott loves these people and indicts them in the same sentence — because that doubleness is the lesson. Some students will want to defend the Transcendentalists, especially Emerson and Thoreau, and you should let them: Alcott is not attacking the philosophy so much as the irresponsibility, and the distinction is worth drawing carefully. For the male instructor, the temptation here is to side too eagerly with Alcott against the foolish men — to perform agreement. Resist it; let the comedy do the work and keep your own commentary dry. The frank fact that the women's labor was invisible to good and idealistic men is more powerful stated plainly, once, than underlined repeatedly. Finally, the historical detail of Fruitlands may swallow the literary discussion; keep returning to *how* Alcott writes — the renaming, the juxtaposition, the final word — and not merely *what* happened on the farm.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Harriet Reisen, *Louisa May Alcott: The Woman Behind Little Women* (Henry Holt, 2009) — accessible, well-researched modern biography.
- Madeleine B. Stern, *Louisa May Alcott: A Biography* (originally 1950; University of Oklahoma Press, rev. ed.) — the scholar who rediscovered Alcott's pseudonymous thrillers.
- Richard Francis, *Fruitlands: The Alcott Family and Their Search for Utopia* (Yale University Press, 2010) — the fullest account of the experiment itself.

### One Thing to Memorize

"Being in preference to doing is the great aim" — the men's principle, offered as the answer to the question of who will do the work. It contains the whole comedy and the whole critique.

## Session 7 — The Cost of a Woman's Desire: Kate Chopin

### At a Glance

Tonight we read Kate Chopin — but not the three stories everyone teaches. (We are deliberately avoiding "The Story of an Hour," "Désirée's Baby," and "The Storm," which belong to another course in this program; if students have met them, so much the better, but our texts tonight are different.) We read two of her finest short stories, "A Pair of Silk Stockings" and "Regret," and we frame both against two short excerpts from her great, scandalous novel *The Awakening* — its opening and its close. The thread tonight is desire: the desire of a woman for something of her own — an afternoon, a child, a self — and the cost the world exacts, or the woman exacts from herself, for wanting it. Chopin is the most economical of our prose writers, a master of the unsaid; tonight is a lesson in how much a story can carry in how few words, and in how a woman writer at the turn of the century told the truth about female longing and was punished for it.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Kate Chopin was born Katherine O'Flaherty in St. Louis, Missouri, in 1850, the daughter of an Irish immigrant father, who died in a railway accident when she was a child, and a mother of French-Creole descent ([Britannica, "Kate Chopin"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kate-Chopin)). She was raised largely by women — her mother, grandmother, and great-grandmother — in a bilingual, Catholic, storytelling household, and she read widely and early. In 1870 she married Oscar Chopin, a Louisiana cotton factor, and moved with him to New Orleans and later to the rural parish of Natchitoches, where she absorbed the Creole and Cajun life of the region that would fill her fiction. She bore six children. When Oscar died of fever in 1882, leaving debts, Kate ran the plantation store herself for a time before returning to St. Louis.

She did not begin writing seriously for publication until her late thirties, after her husband's death and her mother's, when a family friend who was a doctor suggested writing as therapy and outlet ([KateChopin.org, "Biography"](https://www.katechopin.org/biography/)). Once she began, she worked fast and prolifically. Her stories of Louisiana life — local-color sketches rich in dialect, landscape, and the textures of Creole and Acadian society — found ready markets in the national magazines of the 1890s: *Vogue*, *The Atlantic*, *Century*, and others. She published two collections, *Bayou Folk* (1894) and *A Night in Acadie* (1897), and was, for a time, a respected regionalist writer in the mode of Jewett, whom we meet next week.

Then in 1899 she published *The Awakening*, and her career was effectively destroyed. The novel tells the story of Edna Pontellier, a wife and mother on the Louisiana Gulf coast who awakens to her own sensual, artistic, and sexual selfhood, refuses the roles of wife and mother, and finds no place in her world to live the self she has discovered. The reviews were harsh — the book was called morbid, vulgar, unhealthy — and while the story that it was formally banned from libraries is now disputed by scholars and should be told with caution, its reception was undeniably hostile enough to wound her deeply ([KateChopin.org, "The Awakening"](https://www.katechopin.org/the-awakening/)). She published little afterward and died in St. Louis in 1904, at fifty-four, of a brain hemorrhage following a day at the World's Fair, regarded as a minor regionalist whose one ambitious book had failed.

The recovery is one of the great stories of twentieth-century literary scholarship. In the 1960s and 1970s, critics — notably the Norwegian scholar Per Seyersted, who edited her complete works in 1969 — rediscovered *The Awakening* and recognized it as a masterpiece, decades ahead of its time in its frank treatment of female interiority and desire ([Per Seyersted, *Kate Chopin: A Critical Biography*](https://www.lsupress.org/)). Chopin is now firmly in the canon, taught everywhere, the subject of a vast scholarship. We should tell students both halves: a fine writer broken by the reception of her best book, and a reputation resurrected sixty years too late for her to know.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Chopin wrote in the 1890s, the decade of the "New Woman" — a term we will define in the glossary — when the educated, independent, sometimes employed or unmarried woman became a visible cultural figure, and a contested one. The New Woman frightened a society organized around the doctrine of separate spheres, which held that woman's proper place was the home and her proper role that of wife and mother. Chopin's fiction repeatedly tests that doctrine by imagining women who want something the doctrine cannot accommodate.

She wrote, too, within the tradition of regionalism or local color, the post–Civil War mode that found literary value in the speech, customs, and landscapes of particular American places ([Britannica, "Local Color"](https://www.britannica.com/art/local-color)). Chopin's Louisiana — its Creole gentry, its Acadian farmers, its mingling of French and English, its Catholic and sensual culture — gave her both a market (magazine readers loved exotic regional fiction) and a cover: she could explore daring themes under the protective coloring of "quaint" local material. "Regret" and the *Awakening* excerpts are saturated in this Louisiana world.

The reception of *The Awakening* belongs to a larger pattern students should recognize: the punishment of women writers who treated female desire honestly. Where a male naturalist like Theodore Dreiser could write of a "fallen" woman and be defended as bold, Chopin's portrait of a wife who simply wants a self was received as obscene. The double standard is the context. So is the era's literary naturalism — the deterministic vision of human beings shaped by environment, biology, and circumstance — which colors *The Awakening*'s sense that Edna's longing meets a world with no room for it.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"A Pair of Silk Stockings"** (published in *Vogue*, 1897). "Little Mrs. Sommers," a poor, harried mother who was once better off, unexpectedly comes into fifteen dollars — to her a small fortune ([LitCharts, "A Pair of Silk Stockings"](https://www.litcharts.com/lit/a-pair-of-silk-stockings/summary-and-analysis)). She plans carefully, over sleepless nights, to spend it all on her children: shoes for Janie, fabric, shirt-waists, hats, so they may look "fresh and dainty and new for once in their lives." On the day of shopping, faint because she has forgotten to eat, she rests at a counter and her hand brushes a pair of silk stockings. She buys them — for herself. The single transgression unspools into a whole afternoon of self-indulgence: she changes into the stockings, buys fine boots and fitted kid gloves, costly magazines, then a good lunch with wine in a restaurant she would never normally enter, then a theater matinee where she shares chocolates with a stranger ([GradeSaver, "A Pair of Silk Stockings"](https://www.gradesaver.com/kate-chopins-short-stories/study-guide/summary-a-pair-of-silk-stockings)). Riding the cable car home at the end, she feels "a poignant wish, a powerful longing that the cable car would never stop anywhere, but go on and on with her forever." The story ends there — at the dread of return — with the children's clothes unbought and never mentioned again.

**"Regret"** (written 1894; published in *Century*, May 1895; collected in *A Night in Acadie*, 1897). Mamzelle Aurélie is a strong, self-sufficient, fifty-year-old unmarried woman who runs her own Louisiana farm, wears a man's hat and an old army coat, and has "never lived to regret" turning down a marriage proposal at twenty ([KateChopin.org, "Regret"](https://www.katechopin.org/regret/)). One morning her neighbor Odile, summoned to a dying mother, leaves her four small children — Élodie, Ti Nomme, Marcéline, Marcélette — in Aurélie's reluctant care. Aurélie, who thinks at first that children can be managed like livestock ("little children are not little pigs"), is inept and frustrated; she confides to her cook, Aunt Ruby, that she would rather run a dozen plantations ([Interesting Literature, "Regret"](https://interestingliterature.com/2022/06/kate-chopin-regret-summary-analysis/)). But over two weeks she adapts — retrieving aprons and a sewing basket, learning the child's kisses, letting the baby sleep beside her. The day she has grown accustomed to them, Odile returns and takes them home. In the sudden stillness, Aurélie sits at her table and weeps — "not softly, as women often do," but "like a man, with sobs that seemed to tear her very soul" — not noticing her dog licking her hand. The title names what she has only now discovered she feels.

**The Awakening, opening and close.** We read the novel's opening pages — the Grand Isle summer resort, the caged parrot crying "Allez-vous-en! Sacré bonso" at the chapter's start, Léonce Pontellier reading his newspaper and regarding his wife Edna "as one looks at a valuable piece of personal property which has suffered some damage" — and the final chapter (XXXIX), in which Edna returns alone to Grand Isle, undresses on the empty beach, and walks naked into the Gulf, swimming out until her strength fails ([KateChopin.org, "The Awakening"](https://www.katechopin.org/the-awakening/)). The frame stories of longing and constraint prepare students for the novel's full statement: a woman who awakens to a self her world cannot house.

- "Regret" is freely available, with publication detail, at [KateChopin.org](https://www.katechopin.org/regret/) and in full text at the [Central New Mexico Community College open text](https://nmoer.pressbooks.pub/introductiontoliterature/chapter/kate-chopin-regret-1897/).

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] Tonight's writer is the one whose name you may already associate with a single famous story about a woman who hears her husband is dead. We are not reading that one. We are reading two stories you may not know, and pieces of the novel that ruined her, and I want to tell you at the outset why I made that choice. It is because Kate Chopin is far larger than her most anthologized page, and because the texts I have chosen let us watch her do, in miniature, the thing she does in the great book: tell the truth about what a woman wants. [pause]

Start with "A Pair of Silk Stockings." A poor mother comes into fifteen dollars. She plans, carefully, over several sleepless nights, to spend every cent of it on her children. And then her tired hand touches a pair of silk stockings, and she buys them for herself — and the afternoon comes apart. Boots, gloves, magazines, a real lunch with wine, the theater. [pause] Now, I want to be careful here, because there is a lazy reading of this story in which Mrs. Sommers is selfish, a bad mother. That is not what Chopin wrote. Watch how the story ends. It does not end in guilt. It ends on the cable car home, with her "powerful longing that the cable car would never stop anywhere, but go on and on with her forever." [pause] She does not regret the stockings. She dreads the return — the return to the self that exists only for others. Chopin has shown you, in the space of a shopping trip, the whole hunger of a woman who has not been a person, only a function, for years. The silk stockings are not vanity. They are the brief, smuggled experience of being a self. [pause]

Then "Regret." Mamzelle Aurélie — fifty, unmarried, runs her own farm, wears a man's hat, turned down a proposal at twenty and never looked back. A capable, contented, independent woman; the kind the nineteenth century had no comfortable category for. Four children are left in her care for two weeks. She is terrible at it, then learns it, then loves it — and the day she has learned to love it, they are taken back. And she sits down and cries. [pause] Listen to how Chopin describes the crying: "not softly, as women often do," but "like a man, with sobs that seemed to tear her very soul." [pause] What is she regretting? Not exactly the children. Something larger and unnameable — a road not taken, a self not lived, a love she had told herself for fifty years she did not want. Chopin does not spell it out. The title does the work the woman cannot do for herself.

Both stories are about the same thing, and it is the thing *The Awakening* is about. [pause] A woman discovers she wants something the world has told her she should not want, or has told her she does not want, or has simply never given her room to want — an afternoon, a child, a self. And the discovery costs. Sometimes the cost is dread; sometimes it is grief; in the novel, it is a woman walking into the Gulf because there is no place on land to be the person she has become. [pause] Notice how little Chopin says, and how much she means. She is the most economical writer in this course. Tonight is a lesson in the unsaid — in what a great story leaves out, and trusts you to feel. Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **The ending of "A Pair of Silk Stockings": the cable car.** The "poignant wish... that the cable car would never stop... but go on and on with her forever" relocates the story's meaning from indulgence to dread ([LitCharts, "A Pair of Silk Stockings"](https://www.litcharts.com/lit/a-pair-of-silk-stockings/summary-and-analysis)). What she fears is arrival — the resumption of the self that exists only for others. Ask students why Chopin never mentions the children's unbought clothes again; the omission is the point.

2. **"Little Mrs. Sommers" and the lost past.** The diminutive "little," repeated, and the hints that she "had known better days" before her marriage, establish a woman diminished by domestic life ([Literary Ladies Guide, "A Pair of Silk Stockings"](https://www.literaryladiesguide.com/full-texts-of-classic-works/a-pair-of-silk-stockings-kate-chopin/)). The afternoon is not a new appetite but the recovery of an old self. The stockings restore, briefly, the woman she was.

3. **Mamzelle Aurélie's masculine dress.** The man's hat, the army overcoat, the top-boots, the gun mark Aurélie as a woman who has stepped outside the feminine role and prospered there ([KateChopin.org, "Regret"](https://www.katechopin.org/regret/)). Chopin makes her competence and independence vivid precisely so that the final breakdown will register as the eruption of something the masculine costume had kept buried.

4. **"Little children are not little pigs."** Aurélie's initial instinct to manage children like livestock — shooing them to bed as she shoos hens — is comic, but it measures the distance she must travel ([Interesting Literature, "Regret"](https://interestingliterature.com/2022/06/kate-chopin-regret-summary-analysis/)). The animal comparisons chart her education in a kind of feeling she has organized her life to avoid.

5. **"She cried like a man."** The story's last gesture — Aurélie weeping "not softly, as women often do," but "like a man, with sobs that seemed to tear her very soul" — is Chopin's most daring stroke ([KateChopin.org, "Regret"](https://www.katechopin.org/regret/)). The simile reverses the gender coding the whole story has built: the masculine, capable woman is undone by the most "feminine" of losses, and grieves in the register the story has assigned to men. The unattended dog at her hand seals the loneliness.

6. **The Awakening's first and last images: the caged parrot and the open sea.** The novel opens with a caged bird crying in French and Spanish, "Go away! For God's sake!" and closes with Edna swimming out into the boundless Gulf ([KateChopin.org, "The Awakening"](https://www.katechopin.org/the-awakening/)). Cage and sea, confinement and dissolution, frame the whole book; read the two passages back to back and the architecture of Edna's awakening becomes visible in a single class.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Is Mrs. Sommers a selfish mother, or something else?**
   *Model answer:* Something else. Chopin withholds any judgment and ends not on guilt but on dread of return, which reframes the afternoon as the recovery of a self long submerged in service to others. The silk stockings are less an indulgence than a brief experience of personhood, and the story asks us to feel the hunger behind them rather than condemn it.

2. **Why does Chopin never return to the children's unbought clothes?**
   *Model answer:* Because the omission enacts the story's meaning: for one afternoon Mrs. Sommers is not thinking as a mother at all, and Chopin refuses to drag us — or her — back to that frame. The silence about the children is the measure of how completely, and how briefly, she has become a self.

3. **What does Mamzelle Aurélie's masculine dress signify?**
   *Model answer:* It marks her as a woman who has stepped outside the feminine role of wife and mother and built a competent, independent life. Chopin establishes that costume and competence so that the final eruption of grief will register as the return of a buried longing the masculine self-presentation had successfully held down for thirty years.

4. **What exactly does Mamzelle Aurélie regret?**
   *Model answer:* Not simply the departed children, but the larger life she chose against — marriage, motherhood, a love she told herself for fifty years she did not want. The two weeks reveal a capacity for tenderness she had organized her life to avoid, and the title names the feeling she has only now discovered she has carried all along.

5. **Analyze the simile "she cried like a man."**
   *Model answer:* It reverses the gender coding the story has built: the masculine, capable Aurélie is undone by the loss of children, the most conventionally feminine of griefs, yet grieves in a manner the narrator codes as masculine. The line collapses the categories Aurélie has lived by and exposes the person beneath the costume.

6. **How do the two stories illuminate one another?**
   *Model answer:* Both turn on a woman discovering a desire her life has had no room for — Mrs. Sommers for a self of her own, Aurélie for love and motherhood — and both end on the ache of that discovery rather than its fulfillment. Read together, they show Chopin's central subject: female longing meeting a world, or a self, that cannot accommodate it.

7. **What do the framing excerpts from *The Awakening* add?**
   *Model answer:* They show the full, tragic statement of the theme the stories handle in miniature. The caged parrot and the final swim frame Edna's awakening to a self her world cannot house, so that students read the short stories as studies for, or variations on, the longing that the novel carries to its fatal conclusion.

8. **Why was *The Awakening* received so harshly when male naturalists treated similar material?**
   *Model answer:* Because of a double standard: a male author depicting a "fallen" woman could be praised as bold, while Chopin's sympathetic portrait of a wife who simply wants a self was read as obscene and unhealthy. The hostility measures the era's refusal to grant women the interior life and desire it granted male characters.

9. **What makes Chopin's economy a distinctive strength?**
   *Model answer:* She conveys an entire inner history through a single gesture or omission — a touch of silk, an unmentioned errand, a simile about weeping — trusting the reader to supply the feeling. The restraint is not coldness but compression; the unsaid carries the emotional weight, which is why her best stories reward close reading so richly.

10. **Are these stories tragic, and if so, in what key?**
    *Model answer:* They are tragic in a quiet, domestic key: no catastrophe, only the recognition of a desire that comes too late or can be lived only for an afternoon. The tragedy is the gap between what a woman discovers she wants and the room her world allows her to want it — a gap *The Awakening* widens into death.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The frankest material tonight is *The Awakening*'s treatment of female sexual and existential awakening and its ending in Edna's death, which some will read as suicide and others as escape; handle the ending with adult composure and present the interpretive options without resolving them prematurely. Some students reflexively judge Mrs. Sommers or Edna as bad mothers; your task is to slow that judgment and direct attention to what Chopin actually wrote, which withholds condemnation. The central challenge for a male instructor surfaces here in a pointed form: the subject is specifically female desire and its punishment, and there is a temptation either to handle it gingerly or to over-explain it. Do neither. Read the passages aloud, let Chopin's economy speak, and ask questions rather than delivering conclusions about what women want — the writer is the authority in the room, not you. If male students find Edna's choices hard to sympathize with, treat that resistance as material for discussion rather than as a failing to be corrected.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Per Seyersted, *Kate Chopin: A Critical Biography* (Louisiana State University Press, 1969) — the biography that launched the modern recovery; indispensable.
- Emily Toth, *Unveiling Kate Chopin* (University Press of Mississippi, 1999) — the fullest modern biography, careful about the legends (including the library-ban story).
- Barbara C. Ewell, *Kate Chopin* (Ungar, 1986) — a clear critical study of the whole body of work, useful on the short stories.
- Margo Culley, ed., *The Awakening: A Norton Critical Edition* (W. W. Norton, 2nd ed. 1994) — text plus contemporary reviews and key criticism in one volume.
- The scholarly resource [KateChopin.org](https://www.katechopin.org/), maintained by the Kate Chopin International Society, for reliable composition and publication facts on individual stories.

### One Thing to Memorize

The final image of "A Pair of Silk Stockings" — Mrs. Sommers's "powerful longing that the cable car would never stop anywhere, but go on and on with her forever." It contains Chopin's whole subject: the woman who, for one afternoon, is a self, and dreads the stop.

## Session 8 — Loyalty to the Wild: Sarah Orne Jewett

### At a Glance

Tonight we read Sarah Orne Jewett, the great regionalist of the Maine coast, and a writer whose quiet mastery is easy to underrate because nothing in her work shouts. We pair her most famous short story, "A White Heron," with the opening chapter of her acknowledged masterpiece, *The Country of the Pointed Firs*. The thread tonight is the inner life lived in and through place: a child's loyalty to the wild against the claims of a charming, gun-carrying man and his money, and a woman writer's return to a community of women on a remote shore. Jewett is our finest example of the post–Civil War tradition of women writing about constraint, rootedness, and the value of a small, particular world — and of the local-color mode used not for quaintness but for serious art. She also belongs in the room because she is the bridge between Stowe, who influenced her, and Cather, whom she influenced; she stands at the center of a female literary lineage we have been tracing all term.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Sarah Orne Jewett was born on September 3, 1849, in South Berwick, Maine, a once-prosperous shipbuilding town on the Piscataqua River, and she lived there for most of her life ([Britannica, "Sarah Orne Jewett"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sarah-Orne-Jewett)). Her father, Theodore Herman Jewett, was a country doctor and professor of obstetrics, and as a girl Sarah often accompanied him on his rounds through the Maine countryside — an education in the lives, speech, and landscapes of rural and coastal New England that would feed all her fiction. Frequent ill health (a rheumatic condition) interrupted her formal schooling but deepened her habit of close observation. She read widely, and she credited Harriet Beecher Stowe's New England regional novel *The Pearl of Orr's Island* with showing her, when she was young, that the life around her was worthy of literature.

She began publishing early. Her first significant story, "Mr. Bruce," appeared in *The Atlantic Monthly* in 1869, when she was around nineteen or twenty, under a pseudonym ([Britannica, "Sarah Orne Jewett"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sarah-Orne-Jewett)). Encouraged by the *Atlantic*'s editors, she gathered her early Maine sketches into *Deephaven* (1877), and over the next two decades produced the body of work — stories, sketches, and novels of New England life — on which her reputation rests. "A White Heron," the title story of an 1886 collection, became her best-known short work; *A Country Doctor* (1884) drew on her father's vocation; and *The Country of the Pointed Firs* (1896), a loosely linked sequence of sketches set in the fictional coastal village of Dunnet Landing, is now widely regarded as her masterpiece and one of the finest works of American regionalism ([Wikipedia, "The Country of the Pointed Firs"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Country_of_the_Pointed_Firs)).

Jewett never married. After the death of the publisher James T. Fields in 1881, she formed a lifelong domestic partnership with his widow, Annie Adams Fields, a Boston literary hostess; the two women lived together and traveled together in what their era called a "Boston marriage" — a long-term cohabitation between two women, common enough to have a name, whose precise nature in any individual case we cannot and need not pin down ([Britannica, "Sarah Orne Jewett"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sarah-Orne-Jewett)). Through Annie Fields, Jewett moved at the center of New England's literary world. Her own deepest literary friendship at the end of her life was with the young Willa Cather, whom she met in 1908 and whom she advised to write from her own ground and her own deep memory — counsel Cather credited with shaping her art, and which we will pick up next week. Jewett's writing life was cut short in 1902 by a carriage accident that left her with lasting injury; she suffered a stroke and died in South Berwick in 1909. We should be conservative in characterizing her private life and let the work, which is reticent by design, set the tone: a literature of restraint, attention, and deep attachment to place and to other women.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Jewett is the central figure of American regionalism, or local color — the post–Civil War literary movement that turned to the speech, customs, landscapes, and folkways of particular American regions, often those being changed or erased by industrialization and migration ([Britannica, "Local Color"](https://www.britannica.com/art/local-color)). The mode flourished in the magazines after the war, and it gave many women writers — Jewett, Mary Wilkins Freeman, Kate Chopin in her Louisiana stories, later Cather on the prairie — a literary territory of their own. Critics long condescended to local color as minor and feminine; recent scholarship has reclaimed it as a serious art that preserved vanishing communities and, crucially, centered the lives of women, the old, and the rural poor whom the dominant literature ignored.

The Maine that Jewett writes is a world in economic decline. The great age of New England shipbuilding and sea trade — the source of South Berwick's and Dunnet Landing's former prosperity — was ending, the men gone to sea, to cities, or to the dead, leaving coastal communities composed largely of women, the elderly, and memory. *The Country of the Pointed Firs* is, among other things, an elegy for and a celebration of such a community: a society of women holding a place together after the men have largely departed. This demographic fact — a women's world made by historical circumstance — gives Jewett's regionalism its feminist undercurrent without any polemic.

"A White Heron," meanwhile, stages a quieter and more universal conflict: the encounter between the rooted, intuitive, feminine world of nature and the mobile, acquisitive, masculine world of science, money, and collection. The young ornithologist who would kill the rare heron to possess it embodies a whole nineteenth-century relation to nature — to know is to capture, to capture is to kill and to own. The girl's choice is the story's argument, and it is one students will find newly resonant in an ecological age.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"A White Heron"** (1886). Sylvia ("Sylvy"), a shy nine-year-old, has come from a crowded, noisy mill town to live with her grandmother, Mrs. Tilley, on a remote Maine farm, where she has flourished among the woods and animals ([SparkNotes, "A White Heron"](https://www.sparknotes.com/short-stories/a-white-heron/summary/)). One June evening, driving home the cow, Mistress Moolly, she is startled by the aggressive whistle of a young man — an ornithologist from the city, carrying a gun — who is lost and asks for lodging. Mrs. Tilley takes him in. He is collecting birds, which he kills and stuffs, and he is hunting a rare white heron he has glimpsed nearby; he offers ten dollars — a large sum to the poor household — to anyone who will lead him to its nest ([Encyclopedia.com, "A White Heron"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/white-heron-sarah-orne-jewett-1886)). Sylvia, who has seen the heron, says nothing, though she is drawn to the kind, knowledgeable young man (he gives her a jack-knife) even as she cannot understand why he kills the birds he claims to love.

Before dawn the next day, Sylvia steals out alone and climbs a great solitary pine — a frightening, dangerous ascent — to find the heron's nest so she can show the hunter. At the top, in a passage of rapturous vision, she sees the whole world spread beneath her — the sea, the ships, the hawks, the farms — and then she sees the white heron itself rise from below and fly to a nearby bough, calling to its mate ([SuperSummary, "A White Heron"](https://www.supersummary.com/a-white-heron/summary/)). She climbs down with the secret. But when she returns to the house and the hunter and her grandmother press her — when "she must really be made to tell" — Sylvia is silent. She will not betray the heron, though it costs her the money her family needs and the regard of the young man. He leaves disappointed. The narrator closes by asking whether the birds were better friends than the hunter might have been, and by calling on the woodlands to bring their gifts to "this lonely country child."

**The Country of the Pointed Firs, Chapter I, "The Return."** The brief opening chapter establishes the frame ([Wikisource, "The Country of the Pointed Firs/Chapter 1"](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Country_of_the_Pointed_Firs/Chapter_1)). An unnamed narrator — a woman writer from the city, as later chapters reveal — describes the particular charm of the coastal Maine town of Dunnet Landing, which she had first visited briefly some summers before on a yachting cruise and to which she now returns, drawn by "all that mixture of remoteness, and childish certainty of being the centre of civilization." On a June evening she lands, the single passenger, at the steamboat wharf at high tide, watched with "subdued excitement" by the townsfolk, and makes her way up the narrow street of the "salt-aired, white-clapboarded little town." The chapter is pure arrival and atmosphere — the return to a beloved, unchanged place — and it sets the elegiac, observant key of the whole book, in which the narrator will be taken into a community of women centered on her landlady, the herbalist Almira Todd.

- "A White Heron" (1886) is freely available in full at [Marshall University's open Women Writers text](https://pressbooks.marshall.edu/womenwriters/chapter/sarah-orne-jewetts-a-white-heron/) and in the scanned first edition of *A White Heron and Other Stories* at the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/whiteheronothers00jeweiala).
- *The Country of the Pointed Firs*, Chapter I ("The Return"), is freely available in full at [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/367) and at [Wikisource](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Country_of_the_Pointed_Firs/Chapter_1).

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] Tonight's writer is the quietest master in this whole course, and I want to begin by warning you against the very thing that will tempt you when you read her: the sense that not much happens. A girl finds a bird. A woman gets off a boat. If you are reading for plot, Sarah Orne Jewett will seem to give you almost nothing. [pause] So do not read for plot. Read for attention. Jewett's subject is the inner life as it is lived through a place — through woods and water and weather and the people who have stayed when everyone else has gone — and her art is the art of noticing. Tonight is a lesson in slowing down.

Start with "A White Heron." A shy child, Sylvia, has been taken out of a noisy mill town to live with her grandmother on a remote Maine farm, and there she has bloomed. She knows the woods the way you know your own house in the dark. [pause] One evening a young man appears — an ornithologist, a bird scientist, from the city. He is charming, he is kind, he knows a great deal, and he carries a gun. He is hunting a rare white heron, and he wants to kill it and stuff it and add it to his collection. He offers the family ten dollars — real money, money they need — to anyone who will show him the nest. And Sylvia knows where the heron is. [pause]

Now watch what Jewett does with this small situation, because it is everything. Sylvia is drawn to the young man. She is nine years old and on the edge of something — call it the first stirring of attraction to the world of men, and money, and the wider life he represents. Jewett does not pretend that pull is not real; the girl watches him with "loving admiration." [pause] And so, to win his regard, she climbs — before dawn, alone, up the tallest pine in the forest, a real and dangerous climb — to find the heron for him. And at the top, she has a vision. She sees the whole world: the sea, the ships, the hawks below her. And she sees the heron rise, and fly, and call to its mate. [pause]

And then she comes down, and they ask her to tell, and she does not. [pause] That is the story. She chooses the bird over the boy, the wild over the world, loyalty to the living creature over the money and the man. And here is what I want you to notice about how Jewett tells it: she does not let it be easy. The last lines do not congratulate Sylvia. They ask — the narrator asks, openly — whether she was right. "Were the birds better friends than their hunter might have been, — who can tell?" [pause] Jewett honors the cost. The girl gave something up. The story is not a tidy fable about the goodness of nature; it is about a real choice with real loss on both sides, and that honesty is what makes it art rather than a moral.

Then we turn to the opening of *The Country of the Pointed Firs*, and I want you to read it as a different kind of arrival. [pause] A woman — a writer — comes back by boat to a small, declining coastal town that she loves. That is the whole chapter: a return. But listen to the prose, the way it loves the "salt-aired, white-clapboarded little town," the way it makes a remote place "the centre of civilization." [pause] What Jewett is about to give us, over the whole book, is a world made almost entirely of women — the men gone to sea or to the cities or to the grave — holding a place and a way of life together. It is an elegy and a celebration at once. Tonight we read only the door opening. But notice how much a great writer can make you feel before anything has even happened. Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **The hunter's whistle.** Sylvia hears "a boy's whistle, determined, and somewhat aggressive," and Jewett pointedly distinguishes it from "a bird's friendly whistle" ([SuperSummary, "A White Heron"](https://www.supersummary.com/a-white-heron/summary/)). The contrast — human, willful, aggressive sound against the birds' friendly notes — encodes the story's whole opposition between the masculine world of mastery and the natural world Sylvia belongs to, before a word of conflict is spoken.

2. **"She would have liked him vastly better without his gun."** The line names Sylvia's divided heart exactly ([Encyclopedia.com, "A White Heron"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/white-heron-sarah-orne-jewett-1886)). She is drawn to the man and repelled by what he does; Jewett refuses to simplify the attraction. The gun is the precise emblem of the cost of his world — to know and to possess the bird is to kill it.

3. **The climb up the pine.** The ascent is rendered in heroic, near-mythic terms, the old pine personified as loving its "new dependent" and steadying her against the wind ([SuperSummary, "A White Heron"](https://www.supersummary.com/a-white-heron/summary/)). The climb is Sylvia's quest and her initiation; at the top she is "imagistically identified with the bird," seeing as the heron sees. The passage is the story's spiritual center and rewards being read aloud entire.

4. **The silence.** When pressed — when "she must really be made to tell" — Sylvia simply does not speak ([Encyclopedia.com, "A White Heron"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/white-heron-sarah-orne-jewett-1886)). Connect this to the course's thread of silence and voice: here silence is not powerlessness but the only available form of power, a refusal that is the girl's first moral act. She keeps faith by withholding speech.

5. **The narrator's closing question.** "Were the birds better friends than their hunter might have been, — who can tell?" ([SparkNotes, "A White Heron"](https://www.sparknotes.com/short-stories/a-white-heron/summary/)). Jewett declines to certify Sylvia's choice as simply right; she counts the loss — the foregone money, the foregone human companionship — and leaves the question open. This refusal to moralize is what raises the story above fable.

6. **"The Return": the arrival at Dunnet Landing.** The single passenger landing at the wharf at high tide, watched by the town, in a place that is "the centre of civilization" to those who love it ([Wikisource, "The Country of the Pointed Firs/Chapter 1"](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Country_of_the_Pointed_Firs/Chapter_1)). The chapter establishes the narrator as an outsider returning to be taken in, and the village as a small, complete, beloved world — the elegiac frame of the entire book set in a paragraph.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What is the central conflict of "A White Heron"?**
   *Model answer:* It is the conflict between Sylvia's loyalty to the wild, intuitive world of nature and the claims of the masculine world of science, money, and human companionship that the hunter represents. The plot externalizes this as the question of whether she will reveal the heron's nest, but the real drama is internal — a child choosing which world she belongs to.

2. **Why does Jewett make the hunter sympathetic rather than villainous?**
   *Model answer:* Because the story's power depends on the choice being genuinely hard. The hunter is kind, knowledgeable, and attractive, and Sylvia is really drawn to him; if he were a brute, her silence would cost nothing. By making him likable, Jewett ensures that Sylvia sacrifices something real, which is what gives her decision moral weight.

3. **What is the significance of the climb up the pine?**
   *Model answer:* It is Sylvia's quest, initiation, and moment of vision. The dangerous ascent and the panoramic sight from the top — sea, ships, hawks, and the heron itself — identify her imaginatively with the bird and grant her a knowledge larger than the hunter's. She climbs intending to betray the heron and descends having been changed by what she saw.

4. **How does the story treat Sylvia's attraction to the hunter?**
   *Model answer:* Frankly and without disapproval. Jewett shows the nine-year-old watching him with "loving admiration" and notes she would have liked him "vastly better without his gun," registering a genuine pull toward the man and the wider world he embodies. The attraction is the cost of her final loyalty, not a flaw to be corrected.

5. **What does Sylvia's silence mean in the context of the course's thread on voice?**
   *Model answer:* Here silence is power, not powerlessness. Pressed to tell, Sylvia withholds speech as her one means of protecting the heron, making refusal her first deliberate moral act. It inverts the usual association of women's silence with suppression: this silence is chosen, and it is strength.

6. **Why does the narrator end on a question rather than an affirmation?**
   *Model answer:* Because Jewett refuses to reduce the story to a moral. By asking whether the birds were truly better friends than the hunter might have been, she acknowledges the real loss — the money, the human connection — and honors the difficulty of the choice. The open ending is what distinguishes art from fable.

7. **How does the demographic reality of coastal Maine shape *The Country of the Pointed Firs*?**
   *Model answer:* With the men gone to sea, to cities, or to death, Dunnet Landing is largely a world of women and the elderly, and Jewett makes that world her subject. The book becomes both an elegy for a declining maritime economy and a celebration of a female community sustaining a place and a culture — a feminist undercurrent carried entirely by circumstance and tone, without polemic.

8. **What does the first chapter, "The Return," accomplish before any events occur?**
   *Model answer:* It establishes the narrator as a loving outsider coming back to be absorbed into the community, and it renders Dunnet Landing as a small, complete, cherished world worth the closest attention. Through atmosphere and cadence alone it sets the elegiac, observant key of the whole book, proving how much Jewett can make a reader feel before any plot begins.

9. **How is Jewett positioned within the female literary lineage of this course?**
   *Model answer:* She is the bridge: she credited Stowe's New England fiction with showing her that local life was worthy of literature, and she became the mentor who advised Willa Cather to write from her own ground. Reading her, students see one generation of American women writers handing the art directly to the next.

10. **Why was regionalism long undervalued, and what does Jewett show is at stake in reclaiming it?**
    *Model answer:* Local color was dismissed as minor, quaint, and "feminine" — small worlds for small talents. Jewett shows that the mode could preserve vanishing communities and center the lives of women, the old, and the rural poor with the full resources of serious art. Reclaiming regionalism means recognizing that attention to a small, particular place can be a major literary achievement.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is that students conditioned to read for plot and conflict may find Jewett uneventful and undervalue her; meet this head-on by naming attention, not action, as her subject, and by reading the pine-climb and the arrival passages aloud so the prose can do its persuading. Some will want "A White Heron" to be a simple environmental parable; press them toward the open ending, which counts the cost of Sylvia's choice and refuses the easy moral. As a male instructor, you face a subtle version of the central challenge: the story sets a girl's intuitive, natural world against a man's acquisitive one, and it would be easy either to over-identify with the sympathetic young hunter or to flatten him into a straw man. Do neither; let the text hold both his charm and his violence. On Jewett's private life and her partnership with Annie Fields, stay conservative and brief — name the "Boston marriage" as the historical category, decline to speculate beyond what the record supports, and return attention to the reticent, deeply felt work itself, which is where Jewett wanted it.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Paula Blanchard, *Sarah Orne Jewett: Her World and Her Work* (Addison-Wesley, 1994) — the standard modern biography.
- Sarah Way Sherman, *Sarah Orne Jewett, an American Persephone* (University Press of New England, 1989) — influential feminist reading, strong on myth and the female community.
- Marjorie Pryse, ed., *The Country of the Pointed Firs and Other Stories* (Norton/Anchor) — reliable text with useful introduction; Pryse and Judith Fetterley's work on regionalism is essential.
- Judith Fetterley and Marjorie Pryse, *Writing Out of Place: Regionalism, Women, and American Literary Culture* (University of Illinois Press, 2003) — the key reframing of regionalism as serious women's art.
- Willa Cather's preface to *The Best Stories of Sarah Orne Jewett* (Houghton Mifflin, 1925), in which Cather ranks *The Country of the Pointed Firs* with *The Scarlet Letter* and *Huckleberry Finn* — a primary document of the lineage.

### One Thing to Memorize

The narrator's closing question in "A White Heron": "Were the birds better friends than their hunter might have been, — who can tell?" It is the line that turns a nature parable into literature by honoring the cost of the girl's loyalty.

## Session 9 — The Comedy of a Woman's Past: Edith Wharton

### At a Glance

Tonight we read Edith Wharton — the great anatomist of old New York and the first woman to win the Pulitzer Prize for fiction — in a sharp, funny, deceptively light short story, "The Other Two." A man marries a woman who has been divorced twice; over the course of the story he is thrown, by accident and circumstance, into the company of both her former husbands, and must learn to live in a world where his wife's past will not stay buried. The thread tonight is the double standard made visible through manners: a man can "shed his past," Wharton's protagonist realizes, but a woman cannot — society reads her through the men she has belonged to. Wharton is our master of social comedy with a tragic undertow, and tonight is a lesson in how a brilliant woman writer used wit, irony, and the surfaces of polite society to expose what that society did to women. She is also our pivot from the regionalists to the high modern: a writer of the leisure class dissecting its own cruelties from within.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Edith Wharton was born Edith Newbold Jones in New York City in 1862, into the wealthy, rigidly conventional society of old New York — the world of inherited money, brownstones, and elaborate social rules that she would later make her great subject ([Britannica, "Edith Wharton"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edith-Wharton)). (The family connection to the phrase "keeping up with the Joneses" is part of her legend, if not strictly verifiable.) She was educated privately, read voraciously in her father's library, and absorbed European languages and culture during long family stays abroad — an education that left her formidably learned and, by the standards of her class, faintly improper for a girl. Her society expected a young woman to marry well and conduct herself decoratively; it did not expect, or welcome, a woman who wished to write.

In 1885 she married Edward "Teddy" Robbins Wharton, a Boston gentleman thirteen years her senior. The marriage was a poor match — Teddy suffered from worsening mental illness and was no intellectual companion — and it ended in divorce in 1913, a step that, for a woman of her class, carried real cost. Wharton's escape and her vocation were writing. After early work on design (her first book, with Ogden Codman, was *The Decoration of Houses*, 1897), she turned to fiction, and with *The House of Mirth* (1905), the tragedy of the beautiful, doomed Lily Bart, she achieved both critical and popular success ([Britannica, "Edith Wharton"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Edith-Wharton)). There followed a remarkable body of work: the bleak New England novella *Ethan Frome* (1911), the satirical *The Custom of the Country* (1913), and, after the First World War, *The Age of Innocence* (1920), her masterpiece of old New York, for which in 1921 she became the first woman to win the Pulitzer Prize for the Novel ([The Pulitzer Prizes, "Edith Wharton"](https://www.pulitzer.org/winners/edith-wharton)).

Wharton moved permanently to France, where she ran extensive relief efforts during the First World War — organizing hostels, work programs, and care for refugees — and was made a Chevalier of the French Legion of Honor for her work. She maintained a long, deep friendship with Henry James, who admired and sometimes sparred with her, and she wrote constantly across genres: novels, short stories, travel books, criticism, and a memoir, *A Backward Glance* (1934). "The Other Two" appeared in her 1904 collection *The Descent of Man and Other Stories*, in the period of her first full maturity ([Encyclopedia.com, "The Other Two"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/other-two-edith-wharton-1904)). She died in France in 1937. We can be confident in the outlines of this life — wealth, a constraining society, a failed marriage, expatriation, prodigious achievement — and should resist the temptation to read her fiction too directly as autobiography; she was an artist of observation, not confession.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Wharton's subject is the leisure class of old New York and its rituals — the dinners, the calls, the boxes at the opera, the elaborate code governing who may be received and who may not. To understand "The Other Two," students must understand that in this world divorce, while increasingly possible by the turn of the century, still carried a stigma that fell unequally on women. A divorced man remained himself; a divorced woman carried the trace of each marriage, and her social standing depended on the men who had claimed and released her. Wharton's comedy depends on this asymmetry.

The story sits at the hinge of two eras. The "New Woman" was emerging — more educated, more independent, more able to leave an unhappy marriage — and Alice Waythorn is, in her quiet way, a product of that change: a woman who has remade herself twice through divorce and remarriage and risen socially each time. But the social machinery that judges her is older, and Wharton's irony plays the new freedom against the old judgment. The reader is invited to admire Alice's adaptability and, at the same time, to feel the faint chill of what that adaptability has cost her and what it reveals about a world in which a woman's identity is assembled from her husbands.

Wharton wrote as an insider turned anatomist. Unlike Stowe or Jewett, she did not write from outside power or from the margins; she wrote from the very center of American wealth and dissected it with a scalpel. This is the particular authority of "The Other Two": its critique of how society uses women comes from a woman who knew that society's rules from the inside, had been bound by them, and had paid to break free. The leisure-class comedy of manners, in her hands, becomes a precise instrument for exposing the machinery of gender.

### Summary of the Work(s)

"The Other Two" (collected in *The Descent of Man and Other Stories*, 1904). Mr. Waythorn, a solid, middle-aged New York broker, has recently and happily married Alice, a woman who has been divorced twice before ([GradeSaver, "The Other Two"](https://www.gradesaver.com/roman-fever-and-other-stories/study-guide/summary-the-other-two)). As the story opens, the couple's honeymoon has been cut short because Alice's young daughter from her first marriage, Lily, has fallen ill with typhoid fever. Because Lily cannot travel, her father — Alice's first husband, Haskett — must be allowed to visit her in the Waythorn home, a prospect Waythorn finds distasteful but accepts.

Through a series of coincidences, Waythorn is thrown repeatedly into contact with both ex-husbands ([Encyclopedia.com, "The Other Two"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/other-two-edith-wharton-1904)). He meets Alice's second husband, the socially polished but shallow Gus Varick, on the train and again at lunch; then, when his own business partner Sellers falls ill, Waythorn is obliged to handle Varick's investments and work with him directly. Meanwhile Haskett — whom Alice had let Waythorn believe was a brute — turns out to be a dull, shabby, but gentle and devoted father, who comes to discuss their daughter's upbringing and worries about her French governess's influence. In the course of these encounters Waythorn discovers small lies Alice has told, and he comes to a series of realizations: that Alice cannot, as a man could, simply "shed her past"; that her very perfection as a wife — her tact, her adaptability — was schooled by her previous marriages; and that he is, in effect, "indebted to his predecessors." He passes into a kind of rueful acceptance.

The story's famous closing scene gathers all three husbands. Waythorn comes home to find Haskett waiting in the library to see Alice; he offers him a cigar. Then Varick arrives on business and is offered a cigar too. The three men stand together awkwardly when Alice breezes in, momentarily startled to find all three husbands in one room, then composes herself and proposes tea. She pours and hands round the cups; she gives Waythorn his — the third — and he takes it "with a laugh" ([Study.com, "The Other Two"](https://study.com/academy/lesson/the-other-two-summary.html)). The laugh is the story's perfectly ambiguous ending: acceptance, absurdity, surrender, and complicity all at once.

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] After the hush of Jewett's Maine, tonight we step into a New York drawing room and into the company of one of the wittiest and most pitiless intelligences in American letters. Edith Wharton was born at the very top of the society she spent her life dissecting, and tonight's story shows you her method in miniature: she takes a polite social situation, lets it become quietly excruciating, and by the end has exposed something true and uncomfortable about how the world treats women. And she does it while making you laugh.

Here is the setup. [pause] A man, Waythorn, has married a woman, Alice, who has been divorced twice. He is pleased with her; she is graceful, tactful, "unflappable." And then, through a run of coincidences that Wharton handles like a comic playwright, he keeps colliding with her former husbands. The first husband, Haskett, has a legal right to visit their sick daughter, so he must come to Waythorn's house. The second husband, Varick, Waythorn keeps running into — on the train, at lunch — and then is forced, through business, to work with directly. [pause] So this man finds himself surrounded by the other two. The other two men who have had his wife.

Now watch what Wharton does to Waythorn's mind, because the comedy is also a dawning recognition. [pause] First, he learns that Haskett — whom Alice had let him believe was a brute — is no brute at all. He is dull, shabby, a small businessman, but gentle, and devoted to his daughter. So one of Alice's stories quietly comes apart. Then Waythorn begins to notice the small lies, the little adjustments of the truth. And then he arrives at the realization that is the heart of the story. [pause] He realizes that Alice's very perfection — the tact, the grace, the smoothness he so admires — was learned. Schooled. In her previous marriages. He is, as Wharton puts it, "indebted to his predecessors." He has only a third of his wife; the other two men made the woman he married.

And here is the line I most want you to carry out of tonight. [pause] Waythorn understands that, unlike a man, Alice cannot "shed her past." A man marries, divorces, remarries, and remains simply himself — a clean slate each time. A woman carries every marriage with her; society reads her through the men she has belonged to. She is assembled out of her husbands. That is the double standard, and Wharton does not lecture you about it — she lets you feel it, through the comedy, until it has a little chill in it. [pause]

Then comes the ending, which is one of the great closing scenes in the American short story. [pause] All three husbands end up in the same room. The two exes, Waythorn, and then Alice walks in. And what does this remarkable woman do? She does not flee, she does not blush, she does not apologize. She proposes tea. She pours it out and hands a cup to each of her three husbands, and gives Waythorn his — third — and he takes it "with a laugh." [pause] Now: what is that laugh? Is it acceptance? Defeat? Absurdity? Complicity? Wharton will not tell you. The laugh is the whole story compressed into a single ambiguous sound — a man laughing because there is nothing else to do, because the social machinery has won, because his wife is magnificent and unknowable and partly made by other men. [pause] Hold onto that laugh. We will spend the hour deciding what it means. Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **The shortened honeymoon and Lily's illness.** The story's machinery starts with a sick child who cannot travel, forcing the first husband into the house ([GradeSaver, "The Other Two"](https://www.gradesaver.com/roman-fever-and-other-stories/study-guide/summary-the-other-two)). Wharton uses a domestic accident to crack open the sealed world of the new marriage, and the daughter, Lily, is the living evidence of Alice's first marriage that cannot be wished away.

2. **The re-description of Haskett.** Alice had implied Haskett was a brute; he proves to be a "small businessman... dull and colorless but gentle and intensely devoted" to his daughter ([Encyclopedia.com, "The Other Two"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/other-two-edith-wharton-1904)). The gap between Alice's account and the man himself is the first crack in Waythorn's image of his wife — and Wharton's first quiet indictment of how stories about ex-husbands get shaped.

3. **"He had not shed his past as she had shed hers" — the double standard.** Waythorn's central realization is that Alice cannot escape her history as a man can; she is read through her successive husbands ([Study.com, "The Other Two"](https://study.com/academy/lesson/the-other-two-summary.html)). This is the thematic core: the asymmetry between how society treats a divorced man and a divorced woman, delivered not as a thesis but as a husband's uneasy insight.

4. **"Indebted to his predecessors."** Waythorn recognizes that Alice's prized tact and adaptability were trained by her earlier marriages, so that he enjoys a wife the other two men helped to make ([eNotes, "The Other Two"](https://www.enotes.com/topics/other-two/in-depth)). The line is Wharton at her most coolly ironic: a husband's admiration curdling into the sense that he owns only "a third" of his wife.

5. **The final tea, and Waythorn's laugh.** Alice serves tea to all three husbands and hands Waythorn his cup third; he takes it "with a laugh" ([Study.com, "The Other Two"](https://study.com/academy/lesson/the-other-two-summary.html)). The laugh is deliberately unreadable — acceptance, absurdity, defeat, complicity — and the whole story's meaning gathers in it. Ask students to argue for each reading; the ambiguity is the art.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What is the central irony of "The Other Two"?**
   *Model answer:* That the very qualities Waythorn most admires in Alice — her tact, grace, and adaptability — were schooled by the marriages he resents, so that he is "indebted to his predecessors." His ideal wife is partly the creation of the other two men, and his pleasure in her is inseparable from her history.

2. **How does Wharton dramatize the sexual double standard?**
   *Model answer:* Through Waythorn's realization that he can "shed his past" while Alice cannot. A divorced man remains simply himself; a divorced woman is read through the men who have claimed her. Wharton delivers this not as argument but as a husband's gradually dawning, uneasy insight, which makes it land harder.

3. **Why does Wharton make Haskett gentle rather than brutish?**
   *Model answer:* To expose Alice's earlier characterization as self-serving and to deepen Waythorn's disillusion. If Haskett were the brute Alice implied, her account would be vindicated; because he is a dull but decent and devoted father, Waythorn must confront that his wife has shaped the truth, and the reader must reconsider who the wronged parties are.

4. **What role does the daughter, Lily, play?**
   *Model answer:* Lily is the irreducible evidence of Alice's past — a living person who cannot be divorced away — and her illness is the plot device that forces the first husband back into the house. Through Lily, Wharton makes the point that a woman's history is not abstract but embodied and ongoing.

5. **Is Alice a victim, a manipulator, or a survivor?**
   *Model answer:* Wharton allows all three readings. She is a victim of a system that judges women by their marriages; she is a manipulator who has shaped stories and told small lies; and she is a survivor who has adroitly remade herself and risen each time. The richness of the story lies in refusing to settle which she is.

6. **Analyze the final scene and Waythorn's laugh.**
   *Model answer:* The gathering of all three husbands over tea is social comedy at its sharpest, and Waythorn's laugh as he takes the third cup is its perfectly ambiguous resolution. It can be read as acceptance, as absurdist surrender, as complicity in the social machinery, or as rueful admiration. The unreadability is deliberate; Wharton ends on a sound rather than a statement.

7. **How does Wharton's insider position shape the story's critique?**
   *Model answer:* Because she wrote from the center of the leisure class she anatomizes, her critique carries the authority of intimate knowledge rather than outside protest. The comedy of manners becomes a precise instrument: she exposes how her own society used women because she had lived inside its rules and paid to break them.

8. **What makes this a comedy with a "tragic undertow"?**
   *Model answer:* The surface is light — coincidences, awkward meetings, a witty closing tableau — but underneath runs the chilling recognition that a woman cannot own her own past or be known apart from her men. The laughter never quite dispels the cold fact, and that doubleness is Wharton's characteristic key.

9. **How does Alice exemplify and complicate the figure of the New Woman?**
   *Model answer:* She embodies the New Woman's freedom to leave unhappy marriages and remake herself, rising socially each time. But Wharton complicates the figure by showing that this freedom is exercised within, and judged by, an older social code that still defines a woman through her husbands — so that Alice's independence is real but never clean.

10. **Why is "The Other Two" a fitting pivot between the regionalists and the moderns?**
    *Model answer:* It leaves behind the rural margins of Jewett for the metropolitan center of wealth and power, and it trades local color for the cool, ironic dissection of manners that points toward modern fiction. Wharton writes from inside privilege about privilege, anatomizing the social order rather than preserving a vanishing one, which marks the turn the course is taking.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is that students may take the story's light surface at face value and miss the chill beneath; guide them to the double-standard realization and the ambiguity of the final laugh, which is where the comedy turns serious. Some will want to condemn Alice as a manipulator or, conversely, to make her a pure victim; resist both and hold the story's deliberate refusal to decide. The frank material here — divorce, a woman's sexual and marital history — is handled by Wharton with such polished indirection that the difficulty is the opposite of crudeness: students may not notice how much the story is actually saying about female sexuality and social judgment, so you may need to make the subtext explicit with adult composure. For the male instructor, the useful move is to read closely with the grain of Waythorn's perceptions while making clear that the story is critiquing, not endorsing, his proprietary view of his wife ("a third of his wife"). Let Wharton's irony, not your commentary, carry the judgment.

## Session 10 — The Boy Who Wanted Beauty: Willa Cather

### At a Glance

Tonight we read Willa Cather — one of the great American novelists, the chronicler of the immigrant prairie and the artist's vocation — in her most famous and most anthologized short story, "Paul's Case." It is the study of a Pittsburgh schoolboy who cannot bear the drabness of his respectable middle-class street and hungers, with his whole being, for beauty, glamour, and the world of art; who steals to live that life for a few brilliant days in New York; and who, when the money runs out and his father comes to take him home, chooses death over the return. The thread tonight is desire again — but here the desire is for beauty itself, for a life larger and more luminous than the one assigned, and the cost of that desire in a world with no room for it. Cather, a woman who built her life and her art outside the conventional female role, gives us a story whose protagonist is a boy, and that choice — a woman writing a male outsider — is itself part of tonight's lesson. We also pick up the lineage from last week: Cather was Jewett's protégée, and she carried forward the older woman's counsel to write from one's own deepest ground.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Willa Cather was born in 1873 in Virginia and moved at age nine with her family to the Nebraska frontier, near the town of Red Cloud — a transplanting that shaped her imagination permanently ([Britannica, "Willa Cather"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Willa-Cather)). The vast prairie and the immigrant farming communities of the Great Plains — Bohemian, Scandinavian, German — became the deep material of her finest novels. As a girl she was strikingly unconventional: for a period in her teens she cut her hair short, dressed in masculine clothes, signed herself "William Cather," and pursued interests then coded as male, including a serious early ambition toward medicine. She attended the University of Nebraska, where she began publishing criticism and journalism.

After graduating she worked as a journalist and magazine editor, moving to Pittsburgh — the setting of "Paul's Case" — where she also taught high-school English, and then to New York, where she became managing editor of the influential *McClure's Magazine* ([Britannica, "Willa Cather"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Willa-Cather)). It was a decisive meeting with Sarah Orne Jewett, in 1908, that turned her decisively toward fiction: Jewett advised her to leave journalism, to write from her own country and her own deepest memory, and to find her own "quiet centre of life." Cather later dedicated *O Pioneers!* (1913) to Jewett, and credited her with the counsel that freed her art. There followed the great prairie novels — *O Pioneers!*, *The Song of the Lark* (1915), and *My Ántonia* (1918) — and, in 1923, the Pulitzer Prize for *One of Ours*. Her *Death Comes for the Archbishop* (1927) is widely regarded among her masterpieces.

"Paul's Case" was first published in 1905 in Cather's first story collection, *The Troll Garden*, and later reprinted in *Youth and the Bright Medusa* (1920) ([Encyclopedia.com, "Paul's Case"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/pauls-case-willa-cather-1905)). It is the only one of her stories she permitted to be anthologized in her lifetime. Cather lived for decades in New York in a long domestic partnership with the editor Edith Lewis, with whom she shared a home until her death; about the nature of that relationship, and about Cather's own intensely guarded private life, we should be conservative — she destroyed many of her letters and forbade the publication of others, and we honor that reticence by declining to over-read it. She grew, in her later years, into a fierce defender of the value of art against a commercializing age. She died in New York in 1947. The verifiable facts — Virginia birth, the formative Nebraska prairie, the masculine self-fashioning of her youth, the Jewett mentorship, the major novels, the Pulitzer — are abundant and secure.

### Historical & Cultural Context

"Paul's Case" is set in turn-of-the-century Pittsburgh, the grim, prosperous capital of American steel — a city of smoke, money, and Calvinist respectability. The story's "Cordelia Street," where Paul lives, is Cather's emblem of the American middle-class ideal: clean, decent, comfortable, and, to a boy hungry for beauty, suffocating in its sameness and its worship of "dollars and cents." The cultural backdrop is the Gilded Age and its aftermath — an America of industrial fortunes and self-made men, in which the only sanctioned ambition was to get on, to prosper, to be a sound young man of business like the model youths held up to Paul. Against this Cather sets the rival values of art, the theater, and Carnegie Hall, where Paul ushers and feels himself, for a few hours, alive.

The story belongs to a long literary tradition of the sensitive young person crushed by a philistine environment, but Cather complicates it. Paul is not a misunderstood genius; he has, the narrator is careful to say, no real talent or desire to make art — only an overpowering hunger to be *near* it, to inhabit its glamour, to escape the ugly and the common. This is a study of aestheticism as appetite, of the craving for beauty divorced from the discipline of creation, and Cather refuses to sentimentalize it.

Readers since have also asked what is unspoken in "Paul's Case." Cather codes Paul as different in ways her era could not name directly — his fastidiousness, his theatricality, his revulsion from the masculine world of Cordelia Street, the hints of something the schoolmasters find unnatural in him — and many critics read the story as one of the earliest and subtlest American treatments of a queer sensibility under pressure. We should present this as a serious and widely held interpretation, grounded in the text, while being careful not to flatten the story into a single key or to make biographical claims the evidence does not support.

### Summary of the Work(s)

"Paul's Case" (first published in *The Troll Garden*, 1905). Paul, a Pittsburgh high-school student, appears before his teachers to be reinstated after a suspension; he is insolent, theatrical, oddly dressed, with a red carnation in his buttonhole, and the teachers, baffled and antagonized, feel there is something "not right" about him ([SuperSummary, "Paul's Case"](https://www.supersummary.com/pauls-case/summary/)). Paul's real life is at Carnegie Hall, where he ushers, and at the local stock theater, where he haunts the actors; there, amid music and glamour, he comes alive. He despises his home on Cordelia Street — its respectable dullness, its smell of cooking, its worship of money — and the model "sound" young businessmen his father admires.

When Paul's behavior worsens, his father removes him from school and from the Hall and the theater, putting him to work as a clerk ([studylib, "Paul's Case"](https://studylib.net/doc/8428845/paul-s-case)). Paul cannot endure it. Entrusted to deposit a large sum of his employer's money, he steals roughly a thousand dollars instead and boards a train to New York. There he buys fine clothes, takes a suite at the Waldorf, surrounds himself with flowers, attends the opera, and for eight shining days lives "exactly the kind of boy he had always wanted to be," freed at last of the dread that has shadowed him ([Encyclopedia.com, "Paul's Case"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/pauls-case-willa-cather-1905)). But the money runs out, and he reads in a Pittsburgh paper that the theft has been discovered, that his father has repaid the firm and is coming to bring him home.

Paul faces the choice he cannot bear: the return to Cordelia Street, where now "all the world had become Cordelia Street." He briefly considers a revolver he has bought but rejects so theatrical an end. Instead he takes the train to the outskirts, gets out near the Pennsylvania Railroad tracks, watches his red carnations — bought in defiance of the winter — droop and fail, buries one in the snow, and, as a train approaches, leaps in front of it ([Encyclopedia.com, "Paul's Case"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/pauls-case-willa-cather-1905)). In the final sentence he feels "the folly of his haste" as everything drops away and "the picture-making mechanism was crushed" — he is "dropped back... into the immense design of things."

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] Tonight's story is about a boy who could not bear his own life. Not because it was cruel — it was not cruel — but because it was ordinary, and he had inside him an appetite for beauty so large that the ordinary felt like suffocation. His name is Paul, and the story is called "Paul's Case," and that title is doing something I want you to notice from the first minute: it is the language of diagnosis. A "case." As if Paul were a patient, or a problem, a thing for the teachers and the doctors and the city of Pittsburgh to solve. Cather adopts that clinical voice and then quietly turns it against the people who would use it. [pause]

Here is who Paul is. He lives on Cordelia Street, which is the most respectable, decent, comfortable street you can imagine — and to Paul it is a kind of death. He cannot stand the sameness of it, the smell of cooking, the talk of dollars and cents, the sound young men of business his father wants him to become. [pause] Where Paul comes alive is at Carnegie Hall, where he ushers, and at the theater. Not because he wants to make art — and this is crucial, this is where Cather is so much better than the cliché she is working with — Paul has no talent and no real wish to create anything. He just wants to be *near* beauty. To stand inside the glamour. To wear the carnation, to breathe the perfume of the larger life. It is an appetite, not a vocation. [pause]

So when his father takes the theater away from him and sits him down on a clerk's stool, Paul does the thing the whole story has been building toward. He steals — a thousand dollars of his employer's money — and he runs to New York. And for eight days he lives the life. [pause] The Waldorf. The fine clothes. The flowers everywhere. The opera. And listen to how Cather describes it, because she does not condemn it — for those eight days, Paul is happy. He is, for the first time, free of the dread that has hunted him his whole life. He becomes "exactly the kind of boy he had always wanted to be." Cather lets the beauty be real. [pause]

And then the money runs out, and the newspaper arrives, and his father is coming. And Paul understands that he must go back — and that now "all the world had become Cordelia Street." There is no New York anymore; there is only the return. [pause] And he will not go. He goes out to the railroad tracks in the snow. He has bought a revolver, but he decides that is too theatrical, too cheap an ending — even now, his sense of style governs him. He watches his red carnations, which he bought against the winter, droop and die in the cold. He buries one in the snow. And when the train comes, he jumps. [pause]

I want you to sit with how Cather ends it: he feels "the folly of his haste," and then "the picture-making mechanism was crushed," and he drops "back into the immense design of things." [pause] No sermon. No verdict. A boy who wanted beauty more than he wanted life, in a world that offered him only Cordelia Street or death, and could not imagine a third thing. Tonight we ask the hard questions. Is Paul a tragic figure or a shallow one? Is the story on his side, or on the world's? And why did Willa Cather — a woman who fought her whole life to live outside the role assigned to her — choose to tell this through a boy? Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **The title and the clinical frame.** "Paul's Case" names him as a problem to be diagnosed, and the opening — Paul before the tribunal of his teachers, who sense something "not right" — adopts the language of pathology ([SuperSummary, "Paul's Case"](https://www.supersummary.com/pauls-case/summary/)). Cather uses the diagnostic voice ironically, inviting the reader to ask who is really sick: the boy, or the world that can only see him as a case.

2. **Cordelia Street.** Paul's home street is the emblem of respectable middle-class American life — clean, comfortable, and, to him, unbearable in its worship of "dollars and cents" ([Fu Jen study notes, "Paul's Case"](http://english.fju.edu.tw/lctd/asp/works/51/study.htm)). Cather makes drabness, not cruelty, the antagonist; the horror is ordinariness, which sharpens the story's critique of the American ideal of sound, prosperous decency.

3. **The carnation.** The red carnation Paul wears against the gray of his world — and the carnations he buys in New York in defiance of winter — are the story's central image of beauty as defiance and as doomed fragility ([Encyclopedia.com, "Paul's Case"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/pauls-case-willa-cather-1905)). When, at the end, he watches the flowers droop and buries one in the snow, the image enacts his own coming death and the unsustainability of his hunger for beauty in a cold world.

4. **"Money was everything, the wall... between all he loathed and all he wanted."** In New York Paul grasps that wealth is the only thing standing between Cordelia Street and the luminous life ([Fu Jen study notes, "Paul's Case"](http://english.fju.edu.tw/lctd/asp/works/51/study.htm)). The line exposes the story's economic hard core: beauty, in this America, is purchasable, and the boy who cannot afford it must steal it or die without it.

5. **The rejected revolver.** Paul considers and rejects shooting himself as too theatrical ([Encyclopedia.com, "Paul's Case"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/pauls-case-willa-cather-1905)). The detail is devastating: even in choosing death, Paul is governed by taste, by his horror of the cheap gesture. His aestheticism rules him to the very end and is, in part, what kills him.

6. **The final image: "the picture-making mechanism was crushed."** Cather's closing — Paul feeling "the folly of his haste" as he drops "back into the immense design of things" — refuses both melodrama and moral ([Encyclopedia.com, "Paul's Case"](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/pauls-case-willa-cather-1905)). The phrase "picture-making mechanism" reduces consciousness to a device, and the "immense design" sets one boy's hunger against an indifferent cosmos. The understatement is the art.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What does Paul actually want, and why does it matter that he has no artistic talent?**
   *Model answer:* He wants not to make art but to live inside its glamour — to be near beauty, wealth, and the luminous life, and to escape the ordinary. Cather's insistence that he has no talent or wish to create keeps the story from being a simple tale of the misunderstood genius; it makes Paul's hunger an appetite rather than a vocation, which is both more honest and more disturbing.

2. **How does the title's clinical language shape our reading?**
   *Model answer:* "Paul's Case" frames him as a problem to be diagnosed, echoing the teachers and the society that can see him only as a pathology. Cather adopts the diagnostic voice ironically, turning the question back on the reader: whether the sickness is in the boy or in the world that has no category for him but "case."

3. **Why does Cather make Cordelia Street comfortable rather than cruel?**
   *Model answer:* Because her target is the American ideal of respectable, prosperous decency itself, not mere abuse. By making Paul's home decent and dull, she locates the horror in ordinariness and conformity, and makes his revulsion harder to dismiss — he is fleeing not pain but a life he experiences as spiritual suffocation.

4. **What is the significance of the carnations?**
   *Model answer:* They are beauty as defiance and as doomed fragility — red against the gray, flowers bought in defiance of winter. When Paul watches them droop and buries one in the snow before his death, the image mirrors his own fate and dramatizes the impossibility of sustaining his hunger for beauty in a cold, indifferent world.

5. **Is Paul a tragic figure or a shallow one? Argue both.**
   *Model answer:* As tragic: he is a sensitive being destroyed by a world that offers him only conformity or death, with no room for his nature. As shallow: his "beauty" is borrowed glamour and consumer luxury, his theft is selfish, and his suicide is partly vanity. Cather's refusal to resolve this is the source of the story's enduring power.

6. **What is the effect of Cather letting Paul's eight days in New York be genuinely happy?**
   *Model answer:* It denies the reader the comfort of seeing his rebellion as obviously hollow. For those days Paul is free of dread and fully alive, and Cather makes the beauty real, so that his choice carries weight. The happiness implicates the reader, who is made to feel the appeal of what destroys him.

7. **How should we handle the queer reading of "Paul's Case"?**
   *Model answer:* As a serious, text-grounded interpretation: Paul's fastidiousness, theatricality, revulsion from the masculine world, and the teachers' sense of something "unnatural" in him invite reading the story as an early treatment of a queer sensibility under pressure. We should present it as a widely held and persuasive reading while avoiding both flattening the story to one key and making biographical claims the guarded record cannot support.

8. **Why might Cather, a woman, have told this story through a boy?**
   *Model answer:* The displacement onto a male protagonist let Cather explore the outsider's hunger for a larger life — a hunger she knew intimately from her own unconventional youth — at a protective distance, and it universalized the theme beyond the specifically female constraints the course usually examines. The choice itself models how a woman writer could claim subjects and selves beyond her assigned role.

9. **What does the rejected revolver reveal about Paul?**
   *Model answer:* That his aestheticism governs even his death; he refuses the "theatrical" gesture of shooting himself as a matter of taste. The detail shows how completely his craving for the right style controls him, and suggests that the very sensibility that makes ordinary life unbearable is also what destroys him.

10. **How does the story's final sentence shape its meaning?**
    *Model answer:* By rendering Paul's death in cool, almost scientific understatement — "the picture-making mechanism was crushed," dropped "back into the immense design of things" — Cather refuses both melodrama and moral judgment. The phrasing diminishes the boy against an indifferent universe and leaves the reader to weigh the loss without instruction, which is the source of its restraint and power.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is the suicide, which must be handled with care and adult composure; be aware that some students may have personal experience near this material, name the subject plainly at the outset, and allow gravity and silence rather than rushing past it. Students often split sharply over Paul — pitying him as a victim or dismissing him as a shallow thief — and your task is to hold both readings open, since Cather built the story to sustain them. The queer subtext requires tact: present it as a serious, widely accepted, textually grounded interpretation, not as the single "answer," and avoid speculation about Cather's own life beyond the guarded record. For the male instructor, the most useful note is that Cather, a woman, chose a male outsider as her vehicle; this lets you discuss how women writers claim selves and subjects beyond the assigned female role, and it keeps the session from narrowing into a single identity reading. Above all, return students to Cather's prose — the clinical title, the carnations, the final sentence — and let her restraint set the tone of the discussion.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Hermione Lee, *Willa Cather: Double Lives* (Pantheon, 1989) — outstanding critical biography, strong on Cather's guardedness and her art.
- James Woodress, *Willa Cather: A Literary Life* (University of Nebraska Press, 1987) — the standard full-length biography.
- Sharon O'Brien, *Willa Cather: The Emerging Voice* (Oxford University Press, 1987) — influential study of Cather's formation, including her early masculine self-fashioning.
- Joan Acocella, *Willa Cather and the Politics of Criticism* (University of Nebraska Press, 2000) — a bracing account of how Cather has been claimed by competing critical camps; valuable for handling the queer-reading question with balance.
- Willa Cather, *Not Under Forty* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1936), including "148 Charles Street," her tribute to Annie Fields and Jewett — a primary document of the lineage from Session 8.

### One Thing to Memorize

Paul's recognition, when the money runs out, that "all the world had become Cordelia Street" — the line in which the luminous life collapses back into the ordinary he cannot survive.

## Session 11 — The Self Against the Category: Zora Neale Hurston

### At a Glance

Tonight we read Zora Neale Hurston — anthropologist, folklorist, novelist, and the most exuberant prose stylist of the Harlem Renaissance — in her brilliant short essay "How It Feels to Be Colored Me" (1928), framed against the opening of her masterpiece, *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937). The thread tonight is the self asserting itself against the category: a Black woman writer who refuses to be "tragically colored," who insists on her own vivid individuality against the era's pressure to make her a representative or a victim, and who writes a heroine's quest for voice and selfhood in glorious vernacular prose. Hurston is a writer of joy, and that joy is itself a radical claim. She also gives us our richest example of the recovery project that runs through this course: she died in poverty and obscurity and was literally rediscovered, decades later, by another Black woman writer who went looking for her unmarked grave. Tonight is a lesson in voice, in dialect as literary instrument, and in the refusal of the assigned story.

### The Author: A Biographical Sketch

Zora Neale Hurston was born in 1891 in Notasulga, Alabama, but she grew up in Eatonville, Florida — one of the first all-Black incorporated towns in the United States, a self-governing community of Black mayors, merchants, and storytellers ([Britannica, "Zora Neale Hurston"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zora-Neale-Hurston)). (Hurston herself sometimes gave her birth year as later, and Eatonville as her birthplace; scholars have settled the facts, and we should note that the self-mythologizing is itself characteristic of her.) Eatonville was decisive: because she grew up where Black people governed themselves and Blackness was simply the normal condition of life, Hurston came to her sense of self without the daily wound of being a minority, and that grounding shapes the confidence of everything she wrote. Her mother, who told her to "jump at de sun," died when Zora was young, scattering the family and beginning years of hardship and wandering work.

Hurston made her own education by force of will, finishing high school and attending Howard University before winning a place at Barnard College, where she studied anthropology under the great Franz Boas and became, in 1928, the first Black graduate of Barnard ([Britannica, "Zora Neale Hurston"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zora-Neale-Hurston)). Her anthropological training is inseparable from her art: she went back into the rural South and the Caribbean to collect Black folklore, songs, sermons, and "lies" — the oral culture she would transmute into literature, gathered in *Mules and Men* (1935) and *Tell My Horse* (1938). In the Harlem of the 1920s she was a central, electric figure of the Renaissance, famous for her wit and presence.

Her masterpiece, the novel *Their Eyes Were Watching God*, appeared in 1937, written in a few weeks in Haiti ([Britannica, "Zora Neale Hurston"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zora-Neale-Hurston)). It tells the story of Janie Crawford's search, through three marriages, for love and her own voice, in a luminous blend of standard narration and Black Southern vernacular. In its own day it was attacked by some leading Black male critics — Richard Wright among them — for not being a protest novel, for portraying Black life as joyful rather than as indictment. Hurston's insistence on writing Black interiority and pleasure, rather than racial suffering for a white audience, cost her support, and her career declined. She died in 1960 in Fort Pierce, Florida, poor and largely forgotten, and was buried in an unmarked grave ([Britannica, "Zora Neale Hurston"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zora-Neale-Hurston)). In 1973 the novelist Alice Walker traveled to Florida, found the approximate site, and placed a marker reading "A Genius of the South" — an act that began the full recovery of Hurston's reputation, which now stands at the very center of American literature. The recovery is one of the defining literary events of the late twentieth century, and we should tell it plainly.

### Historical & Cultural Context

The Harlem Renaissance of the 1920s was the flowering of African American literature, art, music, and intellectual life centered in Harlem — the era of Langston Hughes, Countee Cullen, Claude McKay, Jean Toomer, and Hurston, supported by figures like W. E. B. Du Bois and the philosopher Alain Locke, who heralded the "New Negro" ([Britannica, "Harlem Renaissance"](https://www.britannica.com/event/Harlem-Renaissance-American-literature-and-art)). It was a moment of extraordinary creativity and of intense debate about the purpose of Black art: whether it should serve racial "uplift" and protest, presenting dignified images to persuade white America, or whether it should be free to portray the full, unidealized range of Black life — including its folk roots, its humor, its sensuality. Hurston stood firmly on the side of freedom, the folk, and joy, which set her against the more programmatic visions of some contemporaries.

Crucial context is the debate over dialect and representation. To write Black vernacular speech was risky: it had been the language of the minstrel stage and racist caricature, and some Black intellectuals wanted to leave it behind in favor of standard English that would command white respect. Hurston, the trained anthropologist, did the opposite: she treated Black Southern speech as a rich, expressive, fully literary language, and made it the medium of high art. Her use of vernacular is a deliberate political and aesthetic claim — that this language, and the lives it expressed, were worthy of literature on their own terms.

"How It Feels to Be Colored Me" appeared in 1928 in the magazine *The World Tomorrow*, at the height of the Renaissance. Students should read it against the era's pressure to be a "race representative." Hurston's essay is a declaration of individuality that some have found politically naive and others profoundly liberating — and that tension is exactly the discussion to have. We must present both responses fairly: the essay's joyful refusal of victimhood can read as either a brave assertion of selfhood or an underestimation of structural racism, and Hurston's complexity allows both.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"How It Feels to Be Colored Me"** (1928). The essay is a first-person meditation on race and identity, moving by vivid scenes rather than argument ([LitCharts, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me"](https://www.litcharts.com/lit/how-it-feels-to-be-colored-me/summary-and-analysis)). Hurston opens with characteristic wit — "I am colored but I offer nothing in the way of extenuating circumstances except the fact that I am the only Negro in the United States whose grandfather on the mother's side was not an Indian chief" — and recalls her childhood in all-Black Eatonville, where she did not feel "colored" because everyone was; she was simply "Zora," a confident girl who would greet and perform for white travelers passing through. She locates the change at age thirteen, when she was sent to school in Jacksonville: "I was not Zora of Orange County any more, I was now a little colored girl," her identity suddenly reduced to a category imposed from outside.

But Hurston refuses the role of victim. "I am not tragically colored," she declares; "There is no great sorrow dammed up in my soul, nor lurking behind my eyes." She describes feeling "most colored" when thrown against a sharp white background — famously, sitting in a jazz club ("The New World Cabaret") where the music takes her over completely while her white companion remains merely "far away over there," able only to remark, "Good music they have here" ([Interminable Rambling, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me"](https://interminablerambling.com/2018/11/20/hurston-how-it-feels-to-be-colored-me/)). At other times she feels she has "no race"; she is simply herself. The essay closes on its central metaphor: that all people are like brown, red, yellow, white bags of miscellany, each holding a jumble of small things — "a first-water diamond," "an empty spool," bits of glass — and that if the bags were emptied and refilled at random, the great contents would scarcely change. Skin color, she argues, is the bag; the self is what is inside, and the inside is gloriously, irreducibly individual.

**Their Eyes Were Watching God, opening.** The novel begins with one of the most celebrated openings in American fiction, distinguishing the dreams of men from those of women: "Ships at a distance have every man's wish on board... That is the life of men. Now, women forget all those things they don't want to remember, and remember everything they don't want to forget. The dream is the truth. Then they act and do things accordingly" ([Britannica, "Their Eyes Were Watching God"](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Their-Eyes-Were-Watching-God)). The frame then shows Janie Crawford returning to her town of Eatonville at dusk, walking back alone after a long absence, watched and judged by the gossiping porch-sitters; she sits with her friend Pheoby and begins to tell the story of her life and three marriages — the narrative the novel unfolds. The opening establishes Janie as a woman come home to claim her own story, telling it in her own voice, and it sets the book's deep theme: a woman's quest for love, selfhood, and the power to speak.

### The Text(s) for This Session

- "How It Feels to Be Colored Me" (1928) is freely available in full at [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/73549).
- **"Sweat" (1926)** — to give students a complete Hurston *story* with a free link rather than a copyrighted novel excerpt, pair the essay with this masterpiece of the Florida years, freely available in full at [American Literature](https://americanliterature.com/author/zora-neale-hurston/short-story/sweat). It dramatizes a Black washerwoman's hard-won independence and moral triumph over an abusive husband, and complements "How It Feels to Be Colored Me" beautifully.
- *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937) remains in copyright but is freely accessible: the novel can be borrowed at no cost (free Internet Archive account, one-hour loans) at the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/theireyeswerewat0000hurs). For class, the opening two or three pages — through Janie's return and her sitting down with Pheoby — are sufficient; "Sweat" above is the primary free assignment.

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] Tonight we read a writer who will feel, after the cool ironies of Wharton and the clinical restraint of Cather, like throwing open a window. Zora Neale Hurston wrote with more joy than almost anyone in American literature, and I want to begin by saying that her joy is not lightness. It is a position. It is an argument. In a moment when a Black writer was expected to document suffering — to hold up the wound for white readers to see and feel ashamed — Hurston insisted on writing pleasure, confidence, vitality, the full richness of Black life lived from the inside. That insistence cost her her career. Tonight we honor it.

Start with the essay, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me." [pause] Listen to the first sentence: "I am colored but I offer nothing in the way of extenuating circumstances except the fact that I am the only Negro in the United States whose grandfather on the mother's side was not an Indian chief." [pause] That is a joke — a sharp, sophisticated joke that mocks the way light-skinned Black Americans claimed Native ancestry to distance themselves from Blackness. And right away you know you are in the hands of a writer who is going to refuse the solemn, the pitiable, the expected.

Here is the heart of the essay. Hurston grew up in Eatonville, Florida, an all-Black town — Black mayor, Black storekeepers, Black everybody — and so as a child she did not feel "colored." She was just Zora. [pause] And then at thirteen she was sent to school in Jacksonville, and, in her words, "I was not Zora of Orange County any more, I was now a little colored girl." Notice what happened. Her name became a category. Her self became a type. That is the wound the whole essay is built around — not a hatred from outside, but a reduction, a flattening of a particular person into a general thing. [pause]

And then comes the sentence I most want you to carry: "I am not tragically colored. There is no great sorrow dammed up in my soul." [pause] Now — you can read that two ways, and we are going to argue about both tonight. You can read it as one of the bravest assertions of selfhood in American letters: a refusal to be defined by victimhood, an insistence on joy as a form of resistance. Or you can read it, as some of her critics did, as a dangerous looking-away from the real structures of racism, a privatizing of a public wound. [pause] I do not think you have to choose tonight. I think Hurston is large enough to hold both, and the discomfort of holding both is the lesson.

She ends with a metaphor I find unforgettable. [pause] All of us, she says, are like bags — brown, red, yellow, white bags — and each bag is full of a jumble of small things: a diamond, an empty spool, a key to a door long since crumbled. And if you emptied all the bags and filled them again at random, the great Stuffer of Bags would hardly notice the difference. The color is the bag. The self is the contents. And the contents are gloriously, particularly individual. [pause]

Then we turn to the opening of *Their Eyes Were Watching God*, and I want you to hear the difference in the prose — how she moves between a grand, almost biblical narration and the living music of Black Southern speech. The book opens by telling us that men's dreams are ships on the horizon, but a woman's dream "is the truth," and then it brings Janie home, walking back into town alone to tell her own story in her own voice. [pause] That is the whole subject: a woman claiming the right to tell her life. We read only the threshold tonight. But listen to how the voice sounds, because that voice — that a Black woman's vernacular could carry the highest literary art — is Hurston's great gift to everyone who came after. Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **The opening joke about the "Indian chief" grandfather.** Hurston's first sentence mocks the era's habit of claiming Native ancestry to soften Blackness ([LitCharts, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me"](https://www.litcharts.com/lit/how-it-feels-to-be-colored-me/summary-and-analysis)). It immediately establishes her wit, her refusal of shame, and her willingness to satirize her own community's anxieties — the comic register that is itself a claim to full, unguarded selfhood.

2. **"I was not Zora of Orange County any more, I was now a little colored girl."** The pivot of the essay: the moment a particular, named child becomes a general category imposed from outside ([interminablerambling, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me"](https://interminablerambling.com/2018/11/20/hurston-how-it-feels-to-be-colored-me/)). The grammar enacts the reduction — proper name to generic noun — and locates racial consciousness not in birth but in a social encounter at thirteen.

3. **"I am not tragically colored."** The essay's most quoted and most debated line ([LitCharts, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me"](https://www.litcharts.com/lit/how-it-feels-to-be-colored-me/summary-and-analysis)). Present it as genuinely double: a brave refusal of the victim role and an assertion of joy as resistance, and, simultaneously, what some critics read as an underacknowledgment of structural injustice. The richness is in holding both.

4. **The jazz cabaret scene.** Hurston is swept up bodily by the music while her white companion remains detached, able only to say "Good music they have here" ([interminablerambling, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me"](https://interminablerambling.com/2018/11/20/hurston-how-it-feels-to-be-colored-me/)). The scene dramatizes her feeling "most colored when... thrown against a sharp white background," and locates racial difference in depth of feeling and cultural participation rather than in any deficiency.

5. **The "bags of miscellany" metaphor.** The closing image — colored bags each full of a random jumble, interchangeable in their contents — argues that skin is mere container and the self is the irreducibly individual stuff within ([LitCharts, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me"](https://www.litcharts.com/lit/how-it-feels-to-be-colored-me/summary-and-analysis)). It is Hurston's anti-essentialist credo, rejecting the idea that race determines the inner life.

6. **The opening of *Their Eyes Were Watching God*: ships, dreams, and Janie's return.** The famous distinction between men's dreams (ships on the horizon) and women's ("The dream is the truth") frames a novel about a woman who acts on her own desire, and the image of Janie walking home alone to tell her story sets the book's quest for voice ([Britannica, "Their Eyes Were Watching God"](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Their-Eyes-Were-Watching-God)). Note the modulation between elevated narration and vernacular — Hurston's signature double register.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does Hurston open with a joke?**
   *Model answer:* The joke about the "Indian chief" grandfather mocks the impulse to dilute or apologize for Blackness, and it announces her own refusal of shame and solemnity. By beginning in comedy, Hurston claims the right to a full, unguarded selfhood and signals that she will not perform the expected role of the suffering race representative.

2. **What happens to Hurston at age thirteen, and how does she render it?**
   *Model answer:* Sent from all-Black Eatonville to school in Jacksonville, she ceases to be "Zora of Orange County" and becomes "a little colored girl" — a named individual reduced to an external category. The grammar itself enacts the flattening, and Hurston locates racial consciousness not in birth but in a specific social encounter with a white-majority world.

3. **How should we read "I am not tragically colored"?**
   *Model answer:* As a deliberately double statement. It is a courageous refusal of victimhood and an insistence on joy as resistance; it is also, as some contemporaries charged, open to the criticism that it underplays structural racism. The most honest reading holds both at once, treating the line as the crux of a real and unresolved debate rather than choosing a side.

4. **What does the jazz cabaret scene argue about race?**
   *Model answer:* That racial difference lies in depth of cultural participation and feeling, not in deficiency. Hurston is bodily transported by the music while her white companion stays detached; she feels "most colored" precisely in that contrast. The scene reframes Blackness as fullness and intensity rather than as lack or wound.

5. **Explain the "bags of miscellany" metaphor and its claim.**
   *Model answer:* Hurston imagines all people as differently colored bags holding a random jumble of small objects, interchangeable if emptied and refilled. The color is mere container; the self is the contents, which are irreducibly individual. The metaphor is her anti-essentialist credo: race does not determine the inner life, and shared humanity lies beneath the surface of color.

6. **Why was Hurston's joyful vision controversial among Black intellectuals of her time?**
   *Model answer:* Many believed Black art should serve protest and uplift, documenting suffering to indict white America; Hurston's celebration of folk life, pleasure, and individuality could seem to neglect that political duty. Critics like Richard Wright faulted her for not writing protest, and her refusal cost her support — a conflict over the very purpose of Black literature.

7. **What is the significance of Hurston's use of Black vernacular in *Their Eyes*?**
   *Model answer:* By treating Black Southern speech as a rich, fully literary language rather than as minstrel caricature, Hurston made a political and aesthetic claim that this language and the lives it expressed deserved high art on their own terms. Her command of vernacular, grounded in her anthropological fieldwork, became a model for generations of later writers.

8. **What does the novel's opening say about men's and women's dreams?**
   *Model answer:* It contrasts men's dreams, which remain distant ships that may never come in, with women's, which become "the truth" and prompt action. The distinction frames *Their Eyes* as the story of a woman who acts on her own desire, and it positions Janie's homecoming narration as the reclaiming of a life and a voice.

9. **How does Hurston exemplify the course's "recovery" thread?**
   *Model answer:* She died poor and forgotten in 1960 in an unmarked grave, and was literally rediscovered in 1973 when Alice Walker sought out the site and marked it "A Genius of the South." Her restoration to the center of American literature is one of the clearest cases of the recovery project this course traces — work the canon set aside, brought back by a later woman writer.

10. **Are joy and protest opposed, or can joy itself be protest?**
    *Model answer:* Hurston's example suggests joy can be protest: in a context that expected Black writing to display suffering, insisting on pleasure, confidence, and full interior life is itself a refusal of the terms imposed from outside. The question to press is whether that stance can coexist with naming structural injustice, or whether it risks obscuring it — a tension Hurston embodies rather than resolves.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The central difficulty is the debate around "I am not tragically colored," which can divide a room sharply; your task is to keep both readings alive — joy-as-resistance and the critique of underplaying structural racism — rather than letting the class settle prematurely on either. For the male instructor, there is an additional and obvious dimension: this is a Black woman's text taught by a white man, and the temptation is either to over-claim authority on the experience of race or to retreat into excessive deference. Do neither; speak to craft, to the essay's wit and structure and Hurston's vernacular artistry, which is the subject you can authoritatively teach, and let the writer's own confident voice lead on matters of experience. Handle the dialect with respect and never with performance — read vernacular passages plainly, or have students read them, and frame Hurston's use of Black speech as the deliberate literary achievement it is, not as quaintness. Finally, give the recovery story its due weight; the unmarked grave and Alice Walker's pilgrimage move students and make the stakes of the whole course concrete.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Valerie Boyd, *Wrapped in Rainbows: The Life of Zora Neale Hurston* (Scribner, 2003) — the definitive modern biography.
- Robert E. Hemenway, *Zora Neale Hurston: A Literary Biography* (University of Illinois Press, 1977) — the pioneering scholarly life, with a foreword by Alice Walker.
- Alice Walker, "In Search of Zora Neale Hurston," *Ms.* magazine (March 1975), reprinted in *In Search of Our Mothers' Gardens* (Harcourt, 1983) — the essay that recovered Hurston; essential and moving.
- Cheryl A. Wall, *Women of the Harlem Renaissance* (Indiana University Press, 1995) — strong on Hurston's place among her contemporaries.
- Henry Louis Gates Jr., *The Signifying Monkey: A Theory of African-American Literary Criticism* (Oxford University Press, 1988) — on vernacular tradition and Hurston's "speakerly text."

### One Thing to Memorize

"I was not Zora of Orange County any more, I was now a little colored girl." It is the sentence in which a self becomes a category, and it sets up the whole essay's defiant reclaiming of the individual.

## Session 12 — The Eye and the Form: Marianne Moore and Elizabeth Bishop

### At a Glance

Tonight we read two great American poets of precision — Marianne Moore and her younger friend and protégée Elizabeth Bishop — and we trace a literary lineage in real time, since Moore mentored Bishop directly. We read Moore's famous ars poetica, "Poetry" ("I, too, dislike it"), and three of Bishop's finest poems: "The Fish," the villanelle "One Art," and "In the Waiting Room." The thread tonight is the disciplined eye and the made form: a tradition of women's poetry that is not confessional outpouring but exact observation, emotional reticence, and consummate craft — feeling held under pressure of form, the way a diamond is carbon held under pressure. After the heat of Hurston, this is a session about restraint as its own kind of power, and about how two women claimed authority in poetry not by raising their voices but by refining them to an almost unbearable accuracy. We also meet a quiet but important counter-tradition: against the confessional school we will study later, Moore and Bishop show feeling mastered rather than displayed.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Marianne Moore (1887–1972)** was born near St. Louis, Missouri, and raised by her mother after her father's institutionalization before her birth ([Poetry Foundation, "Marianne Moore"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/marianne-moore)). She graduated from Bryn Mawr College in 1909, taught for a time, and moved with her mother to New York, eventually settling in Brooklyn, where she became one of the central figures of American modernism. From 1925 to 1929 she edited *The Dial*, the most important literary magazine of the era, shaping the taste of a generation. Her own poetry — syllabic rather than metrical, built of exact description, quotation, and a naturalist's eye for animals and objects — is among the most distinctive of the century; her *Collected Poems* (1951) won the Pulitzer Prize, the National Book Award, and the Bollingen Prize. Moore became a public figure of singular charm, famous in her later years for her tricorne hat and cape, even asked (comically) to name a new Ford automobile. She never married and lived devoted to her mother and to her art. Her work is grouped with that of T. S. Eliot, Wallace Stevens, William Carlos Williams, and Ezra Pound, and she was the crucial early mentor of Elizabeth Bishop.

**Elizabeth Bishop (1911–1979)** was born in Worcester, Massachusetts, into early and lasting loss: her father died when she was an infant, and her mother was permanently institutionalized when Bishop was five, so that she was raised by relatives in Nova Scotia and New England ([Poetry Foundation, "Elizabeth Bishop"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/elizabeth-bishop)). She graduated from Vassar College in 1934, where a meeting with Marianne Moore — arranged by the college librarian — began a formative friendship and mentorship; Moore encouraged Bishop's exactitude and helped launch her career. Independently wealthy enough to travel, Bishop lived for many years in Brazil with the architect Lota de Macedo Soares, a relationship of great importance to her life and work that ended in Soares's death. Bishop published sparingly and revised obsessively — sometimes for years or decades — producing a small, flawless body of work: *North & South* (1946), *A Cold Spring* (1955), *Questions of Travel* (1965), and *Geography III* (1976). She won the Pulitzer Prize in 1956 and the National Book Award, and late in life taught at Harvard. Her poetry is celebrated for its painterly precision, its emotional reticence, and the way enormous feeling is disciplined into exact observation. Bishop guarded her privacy fiercely and resisted being anthologized as a "woman poet" or a "lesbian poet," preferring to stand simply as a poet — a stance students should know and respect even as we note the facts of her life. We should be conservative about reading her guarded biography into her poems, while acknowledging the losses that the poems themselves clearly transmute.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Moore and Bishop belong to the central line of American modernist and post-modernist poetry, but they occupy a particular and instructive position within it. The dominant story of twentieth-century American poetry often runs through the confessional poets of the 1950s and 1960s — Robert Lowell, Sylvia Plath, Anne Sexton — who made private anguish the explicit material of the poem. Moore and Bishop represent the powerful counter-current: a poetics of impersonality, restraint, and exact observation, in which feeling is implied through the precision of seeing rather than declared. Notably, Bishop and Lowell were close friends and admirers across this divide, and Bishop's reticence was in part a deliberate choice against the confessional mode her friend helped found.

This restraint has a gendered dimension worth drawing out carefully. Women poets had long been pressured into, or dismissed as, "poetesses" of sentiment and feeling. Moore and Bishop's cool, intellectual, formally exacting verse was, among other things, a way of claiming the full authority of the art and refusing the sentimental box. Their precision is a kind of armor and a kind of dignity. At the same time, the deepest feeling is never absent from their work; it is held under pressure, which is why a poem like "One Art" can be so devastating precisely because it refuses to weep.

Students should also understand the modernist value placed on the image and the object. Moore's famous formulation — that poets should present "imaginary gardens with real toads in them" — captures the modernist demand that the abstract be grounded in the concrete, that poetry earn its feeling through exact, verifiable detail. Bishop, the painter-poet, carries this to its height: her poems see so accurately that the seeing becomes a form of knowing and, finally, of feeling.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Marianne Moore, "Poetry."** Moore's much-revised ars poetica famously opens by admitting distaste for its own subject: "I, too, dislike it: there are things that are important beyond all this fiddle" ([Poets.org, "Poetry"](https://poets.org/poem/poetry)). Reading it, however, "with a perfect contempt for it," the speaker discovers "in / it, after all, a place for the genuine." The poem then catalogs concrete, even unpoetic images — hands that can grasp, eyes that can dilate, a wild horse, a tireless wolf, a tree, a baseball fan, a statistician — insisting these are valuable because they are "useful" and real, not because they have been made "poetic." It warns against poetry that becomes "so derivative as to become unintelligible," and arrives at its central demand: that poets be "literalists of the imagination" who can present "imaginary gardens with real toads in them." The poem argues, in short, for a poetry rooted in genuine, observed reality rather than in poetic posturing — a fitting manifesto for the precision Moore and Bishop both practiced. (Moore notoriously cut the poem over decades, in one late version to just three lines; we read the fuller, familiar version.)

**Elizabeth Bishop, "The Fish."** The speaker catches "a tremendous fish" and holds it beside the boat, describing it in extraordinarily exact, defamiliarizing detail — its battered brown skin "like ancient wallpaper," the barnacles and sea-lice on it, its terrible gills, its pink swim-bladder "like a big peony," and especially its eyes and its lower lip, from which hang five old fish-hooks with broken lines, "a five-haired beard of wisdom" marking it as a survivor of many previous battles ([Poetry Foundation, "Elizabeth Bishop"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/elizabeth-bishop)). As the speaker stares, a sense of "victory" fills the little rented boat, until the oil slick in the bilge spreads "a rainbow" and "everything / was rainbow, rainbow, rainbow!" — and, in the famous last line, "And I let the fish go." The poem moves from minute observation to a sudden, unexplained release, the act of mercy or recognition arising directly out of the intensity of seeing.

**Elizabeth Bishop, "One Art."** A villanelle — a strict nineteen-line form with two repeating refrain lines — on the subject of loss ([Wikipedia, "One Art"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Art)). The poem begins with the ironic assertion that "The art of losing isn't hard to master," and proceeds to a mounting catalog of losses, escalating from the trivial to the grave: lost door-keys, an hour badly spent; then places, names, a watch, "my mother's watch"; then "three loved houses," "two cities," "two rivers, a continent." The escalation culminates in the loss of a beloved person ("you / (the joking voice, a gesture I love)"), and the poem's controlled surface finally cracks in the last line's parenthetical, stammering self-command: "though it may look like (Write it!) like disaster." The rigid form, which seemed to promise mastery over grief, instead measures exactly how much the speaker is straining not to break — the discipline making the feeling more, not less, unbearable.

**Elizabeth Bishop, "In the Waiting Room."** A narrative poem recalling a childhood moment in 1918: the nearly-seven-year-old Elizabeth waits in a dentist's office in Worcester while her Aunt Consuelo is treated inside ([Poetry Foundation, "Elizabeth Bishop"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/elizabeth-bishop)). To pass the time the child reads a *National Geographic*, studying photographs of a volcano, of explorers, and of bare-breasted African women with "horrifying" necks and breasts. Suddenly she hears a cry of pain — from her aunt inside, but seeming also to come from herself — and is plunged into a vertiginous recognition: that she is an "I," a person, an "Elizabeth," and at the same time "one of them," one of the human family in the magazine and the room. The ordinary waiting room tips into existential vertigo — "the sensation of falling off / the round, turning world" — as the child apprehends, all at once, individual selfhood and her terrifying connection to all other humans.

### Lecture Script

Good evening. [pause] After Hurston's warmth and music, tonight we turn the temperature down and the resolution up. We are with two poets whose great instrument is not the voice raised but the eye sharpened — Marianne Moore and her younger friend Elizabeth Bishop — and I want to argue tonight that restraint can be as powerful as any outpouring, that a feeling held back can hit harder than a feeling let loose.

Let us begin with Moore's little manifesto, "Poetry," because it tells us how to read everything that follows. [pause] It opens — and I love this — by confessing dislike for its own subject: "I, too, dislike it." A poem about poetry that begins by saying poetry is largely "fiddle." But read on, she says, with "a perfect contempt for it," and you may find "a place for the genuine." And then she gives us her great phrase, the one I want you to keep: real poetry presents "imaginary gardens with real toads in them." [pause] Think about that. The garden may be made up — the poem is an invention — but the toad must be real. The feeling must be grounded in something exact, observed, verifiable. No vague beauty, no poetic posturing. A real toad, with its real warts, sitting in the imagined garden. That is the whole aesthetic of this session.

Now watch Bishop do it. "The Fish." [pause] A person catches a fish and holds it by the boat, and the entire poem is just looking — looking with a painter's patience at this battered creature, its skin like old wallpaper, the barnacles on it, and then the detail that turns the whole poem: hanging from its lip are five old fish-hooks with bits of broken line, like medals, like "a five-haired beard of wisdom." This fish has been caught before, and fought, and got away, five times. [pause] And as the speaker stares and stares, something fills the little boat — she calls it "victory" — and the oil in the bilge spreads a rainbow, and suddenly "everything / was rainbow, rainbow, rainbow!" And then: "And I let the fish go." [pause] Notice: she never tells you why. There is no explanation, no moral. The release comes straight out of the seeing. She looked so hard, so long, that she could not keep the fish. The feeling is entirely in the looking. That is Bishop's method.

Then "One Art," and here is where restraint becomes almost unbearable. [pause] It is a villanelle — one of the strictest forms in English, with two lines that must keep coming back. And the subject is loss. The poem starts almost flippant: "The art of losing isn't hard to master." And then it lists losses, and they get bigger — keys, an hour, then your mother's watch, then houses, cities, rivers, a whole continent — the form so calm, so controlled, repeating its refrain like someone gripping the railing. And at the very end the loss is a person, the beloved, and the control finally cracks, just slightly, in a parenthesis: "though it may look like (Write it!) like disaster." [pause] That "(Write it!)" — the poet ordering herself to get the word down, to finish the line, not to break — is one of the most devastating moments in American poetry, precisely because the form held until it almost couldn't. The discipline is what makes you feel how much is being held back.

And finally "In the Waiting Room," where a child of nearly seven, reading a magazine in a dentist's office, suddenly falls through the floor of ordinary life into the terrifying recognition that she is a self, an "Elizabeth," and at the same time "one of them" — one of all the people in the world. [pause] An entire crisis of identity, staged in a waiting room, in 1918, told in the plainest words. Tonight we learn how much these poets can make a clear eye and a made form carry. Let us read.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

1. **Moore, "I, too, dislike it."** The opening concession disarms the reader and clears away false reverence for "poetry" as posture ([Poets.org, "Poetry"](https://poets.org/poem/poetry)). By admitting distaste, Moore relocates poetic value away from the "poetic" and toward "the genuine" — a programmatic move that licenses her own catalog of unglamorous, real things.

2. **Moore, "imaginary gardens with real toads in them."** The poem's central demand: the made object (the garden) must contain something actual and observed (the toad) ([Poets.org, "Poetry"](https://poets.org/poem/poetry)). It is the manifesto of the precision both poets practice — feeling earned through exact, verifiable detail rather than asserted.

3. **Bishop, the fish's "five-haired beard of wisdom."** The five old hooks with broken lines hanging from the fish's lip, "like medals," reveal a history of survival and confer a strange dignity ([Poetry Foundation, "Elizabeth Bishop"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/elizabeth-bishop)). The detail is the hinge of the poem: pure observation that suddenly carries the moral weight that motivates the release.

4. **Bishop, "everything / was rainbow, rainbow, rainbow! / And I let the fish go."** The triple "rainbow" and the abrupt final line move from saturated seeing to an unexplained act of mercy ([Poetry Foundation, "Elizabeth Bishop"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/elizabeth-bishop)). Bishop withholds all motive; the release arises directly from the intensity of attention, demonstrating her conviction that feeling lives in observation.

5. **Bishop, "though it may look like (Write it!) like disaster."** The parenthetical self-command, the stammered repetition of "like," and the held-off word "disaster" enact the breaking-point of a controlled surface ([Wikipedia, "One Art"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Art)). The villanelle's machinery, which promised mastery over loss, instead measures how nearly the speaker fails to master it — form as the visible strain of composure.

6. **Bishop, "you are an I, / you are an Elizabeth, / you are one of them."** The child's vertiginous double recognition — separate self and member of the human family — is the crux of "In the Waiting Room" ([Poetry Foundation, "Elizabeth Bishop"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/elizabeth-bishop)). Bishop stages a full crisis of identity in the flattest possible diction and the most ordinary setting, proving that existential terror needs no raised voice.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **Why does Moore begin a poem called "Poetry" by saying she dislikes poetry?**
   *Model answer:* The concession clears away false reverence and the merely "poetic," relocating value in "the genuine." By starting from distaste, Moore earns the right to redefine poetry as a matter of real, useful, observed things rather than elevated posturing, and she disarms a reader who might otherwise resist a grand claim for the art.

2. **What does "imaginary gardens with real toads in them" demand of poetry?**
   *Model answer:* That invention be grounded in actuality — that the made structure of the poem contain something exact, observed, and verifiable. It is a demand for concreteness over abstraction and for feeling earned through precise detail, and it serves as the aesthetic key to both Moore's and Bishop's work.

3. **How does "The Fish" generate emotion without stating it?**
   *Model answer:* Through sustained, exact observation that culminates in the discovery of the five old hooks, which confer a history and dignity on the creature. The "victory" and the rainbow arise from the act of seeing itself, and the unexplained release at the end lets the accumulated attention, rather than any declared feeling, carry the emotional weight.

4. **Why does Bishop refuse to explain why she lets the fish go?**
   *Model answer:* Because the release is meant to feel like a direct consequence of having seen the fish so completely, not the result of a stated moral. Explaining it would convert observation into argument; by withholding motive, Bishop keeps the feeling located in perception and trusts the reader to feel the recognition the speaker cannot, or need not, articulate.

5. **How does the villanelle form shape the meaning of "One Art"?**
   *Model answer:* The strict repeating refrains promise control and mastery over loss, and the escalating catalog tests that promise against ever-greater losses. When the form's composure finally strains in the last line's "(Write it!)," the discipline itself becomes the measure of the grief being held back — so the tight form makes the feeling more devastating, not less.

6. **Analyze the line "though it may look like (Write it!) like disaster."**
   *Model answer:* The parenthetical command is the poet ordering herself to complete the line and not break down, the stammered "like... like" enacts the difficulty of saying it, and the long-delayed word "disaster" lands with finality. The moment dramatizes a controlled surface cracking just enough to reveal the magnitude of what is being mastered.

7. **What crisis does the child undergo in "In the Waiting Room," and how is it rendered?**
   *Model answer:* She apprehends, all at once, that she is a singular self — "an Elizabeth" — and simultaneously "one of them," bound to every other human being, and the recognition produces vertigo, "the sensation of falling off / the round, turning world." Bishop renders this existential terror in plain diction and an utterly ordinary setting, showing that the deepest crisis can be staged without any raising of the voice.

8. **How do Moore and Bishop relate to the confessional poets?**
   *Model answer:* They form the powerful counter-current to confessionalism: where poets like Lowell, Plath, and Sexton made private anguish explicit, Moore and Bishop imply feeling through impersonality, restraint, and exact observation. Bishop was Lowell's close friend yet deliberately chose reticence, demonstrating that the period offered more than one way to write a serious modern poem.

9. **What is gendered about Moore's and Bishop's choice of precision and restraint?**
   *Model answer:* Women poets had long been dismissed as "poetesses" of sentiment; cool, intellectual, formally exact verse was a way of claiming the full authority of the art and refusing the sentimental box. Their precision functions as both dignity and armor, asserting that a woman's poem could be as rigorous and impersonal as anyone's while still carrying profound, if disciplined, feeling.

10. **What does the Moore–Bishop relationship show about literary tradition in this course?**
    *Model answer:* It is another living link in the female lineage the course traces: Moore mentored the younger Bishop directly, encouraging her exactitude and helping launch her career, much as Jewett guided Cather. Reading them together lets students see craft and standards handed personally from one woman writer to the next, a tradition built through actual relationships.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is that students trained to equate poetry with self-expression may find Moore and Bishop cold or may "not get" poems that withhold their feeling; meet this by teaching restraint as a deliberate and powerful strategy, and by reading the cracking moments — the fish's release, the "(Write it!)" — aloud so the buried emotion surfaces. Moore's "Poetry" can frustrate as abstract; ground it immediately in Bishop's practice, so the manifesto and the achieved poems illuminate each other. "In the Waiting Room" rewards patience; walk through its plain narrative before its metaphysics. For the male instructor, the relevant note is light but real: resist the impulse to over-explain feeling in poets whose whole art is to imply it, and respect Bishop's own refusal to be read primarily through her biography or her sexuality — discuss the losses the poems transmute without pinning each poem to a documented event. Let the craft lead; these poets reward the instructor who trusts the text's surface and reads slowly.

- Brett C. Millier, *Elizabeth Bishop: Life and the Memory of It* (University of California Press, 1993) — the standard biography, careful and thorough.
- Thomas Travisano, *Love Unknown: The Life and Worlds of Elizabeth Bishop* (Viking, 2019) — recent, authoritative life.
- Linda Leavell, *Holding On Upside Down: The Life and Work of Marianne Moore* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2013) — award-winning Moore biography.

### One Thing to Memorize

Moore's demand for "imaginary gardens with real toads in them" — the phrase that defines the whole tradition of precision, and the test you can apply to any image in any poem all term.

## Session 13 — Flannery O'Connor: "Parker's Back," "Revelation," and the Writer on Her Craft

### At a Glance

A full session on Flannery O'Connor, the great Catholic writer of the Southern grotesque, built around two of her late masterpieces — "Parker's Back" and "Revelation," both from the posthumous collection *Everything That Rises Must Converge* (1965) — paired with O'Connor speaking in her own voice: an essay excerpt from *Mystery and Manners* (1969) and a letter from *The Habit of Being* (1979). We deliberately avoid "A Good Man Is Hard to Find," which students meet elsewhere, and turn instead to the stories O'Connor wrote at the very end of her short life, when her vision was at its most concentrated. The pairing of the two stories with the prose lets students hear what O'Connor *meant* to be doing — her theory of grace, violence, and the grotesque — and then test that theory against the fiction itself. Approximate seminar time: one full, ambitious session. The stories are short; budget time to read O'Connor's prose aloud.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

Mary Flannery O'Connor was born on March 25, 1925, in Savannah, Georgia, the only child of devout Roman Catholic parents in the overwhelmingly Protestant South — a doubleness of belonging and not-belonging that marks all her work ([Flannery O'Connor — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flannery_O'Connor)). When she was a child the family moved to Milledgeville, the antebellum former capital of Georgia, and after her father, Edward, was diagnosed with the autoimmune disease lupus, the household drew close around his illness; he died when Flannery was fifteen. The same disease would later claim her. She dropped the "Mary," signing herself simply Flannery O'Connor, and she remained, all her life, a writer of fierce, orthodox Catholic conviction working in a Protestant, often fundamentalist, rural world she observed with unsparing comic exactness.

She took her degree at Georgia State College for Women in Milledgeville and then won a place at the State University of Iowa's renowned Writers' Workshop, where she studied under Paul Engle and began the novel that became *Wise Blood* ([Flannery O'Connor — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flannery_O'Connor)). In the late 1940s she lived for a time at Yaddo, the artists' colony, and in Connecticut with the poet Robert Fitzgerald and his wife Sally, who would later become O'Connor's literary executor and editor. In 1950, gravely ill, she was diagnosed with lupus and returned to Milledgeville, where she lived for the rest of her life with her mother, Regina, on the family dairy farm, Andalusia. There, on crutches in her last years, she wrote in the mornings, raised peafowl — the subject of her delightful essay "The King of the Birds" — and conducted an extraordinary correspondence.

Her published fiction is small in bulk and enormous in influence: two novels, *Wise Blood* (1952) and *The Violent Bear It Away* (1960), and two story collections, *A Good Man Is Hard to Find* (1955) and the posthumous *Everything That Rises Must Converge* (1965). *The Complete Stories*, gathered after her death, won the National Book Award for Fiction in 1972, a rare posthumous honor ([Flannery O'Connor — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flannery_O'Connor)). She also left a body of essays and lectures, collected by the Fitzgeralds as *Mystery and Manners* (1969), and a magnificent volume of letters, *The Habit of Being* (1979). She died of lupus on August 3, 1964, at the age of thirty-nine.

It is worth pausing on the figure O'Connor cut, because students often arrive with a flattened picture of her. She was, by the testimony of her letters, calm, funny, ferociously intelligent, devout but never pious, and entirely without sentimentality about herself or anyone else. Her great correspondent "A." — long anonymous, now known to be the Atlanta clerk Betty Hester, who first wrote to O'Connor in 1955 admiring her work — drew from her some of the clearest statements of belief and craft we possess from any American writer ([Flannery O'Connor — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flannery_O'Connor)). She maintained correspondences with writers including Robert Lowell and Elizabeth Bishop — the very Bishop we read last session — a reminder that these writers were not isolated figures but a living, intersecting community. O'Connor did not regard her illness as a tragedy to be discussed; she regarded it, with characteristic theological steel, as a circumstance to be used. "I have never been anywhere but sick," she wrote. "In a sense sickness is a place." That sentence is a key to the whole sensibility.

### Historical & Cultural Context

O'Connor wrote out of the mid-twentieth-century American South — segregated, agrarian, intensely Protestant, economically poor, and self-conscious about its decline — and she wrote about it as an orthodox Catholic, which gave her a deliberate angle of vision on the world around her. She called her region "Christ-haunted" rather than Christ-centered: a place where belief and unbelief alike were saturated in the language and pressure of fundamentalist Christianity ([Flannery O'Connor — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flannery_O'Connor)). Her characters are farm women, salesmen, tattoo artists, intellectual sons, country preachers, and the rural poor of both races, and she rendered their speech and their pretensions with an ear as exact as any in American fiction.

Two contexts matter especially for today's stories. The first is the racial world of the segregated South, which O'Connor depicts without flinching and without flattery; her white characters' casual and vicious racism is recorded precisely, as a moral disease the stories diagnose. Students should know that O'Connor's own attitudes, as revealed in some private letters, were a product of her time and place and have been the subject of serious recent debate; the honest course is to read the fiction's unsparing moral judgment of figures like Mrs. Turpin while neither excusing the author's blind spots nor reducing the art to them.

The second context is theological. O'Connor wrote in the tradition of Catholic literary modernism — she read Aquinas nightly and admired the French novelists François Mauriac and Georges Bernanos — and her central subject is the action of divine grace breaking violently into the lives of self-satisfied or spiritually blind people ([Flannery O'Connor — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flannery_O'Connor)). She is also the great American theorist and practitioner of the literary *grotesque*: the distorted, the freakish, the shocking, used not for sensation but to make the reader see. As she argued in *Mystery and Manners*, "to the hard of hearing you shout, and for the almost-blind you draw large and startling figures." That sentence governs both stories on today's syllabus, and it connects this session to the course's recurring concern with the *female grotesque* and with women writers who refuse the consolations of sentiment.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"Parker's Back"** (1965). O. E. Parker is a sullen, irreligious Georgia farm worker whose one passion is tattoos; his body is covered with them, front and sides, though his back remains bare. Mysteriously, he has married Sarah Ruth, a stern, plain, fiercely fundamentalist preacher's daughter who despises tattoos as vanity and idolatry and who is, in his bafflement, the only thing he cannot leave. The story flashes back to the origin of Parker's obsession — a county-fair tattooed man whose patterned skin gave the teenage Parker a sense of awe and direction — and to his joyless marriage. While baling hay, Parker, lost in distraction, crashes the tractor into a tree; the machine and the tree burst into flame, his shoes burn, and in a moment that echoes Moses at the burning bush he cries out to God. Shaken, he drives to the city and orders, on his bare back, an enormous tattoo of the face of Christ — the Byzantine Christ, severe and all-eyed — believing it will please, or master, Sarah Ruth. He suffers through the long ordeal of the work, is mocked by the men at the pool hall, and returns home transformed and frightened. When Sarah Ruth demands to know who is on his back and he says "God," she rejects the image as idolatry — "He don't look. He's a spirit. No man shall see his face" — and beats him across the new tattoo with a broom until the face of Christ is welted and bleeding. The story ends with Parker, who has refused his own baptismal name (Obadiah Elihue) all his life, leaning against the tree, "crying like a baby," his Christ-bearing back the site of a grace he cannot understand ([Parker's Back — full text, John Paul the Great Catholic University course page](https://jpcatholic.edu/NCUpdf/courses/HUMA122-ParkersBack.pdf)).

**"Revelation"** (1964). Mrs. Ruby Turpin, a complacent, self-satisfied white farm woman, enters a crowded doctor's waiting room with her husband Claud, who has an ulcer on his leg. As she waits, she silently sorts the other patients into social and racial categories, thanking Jesus that He made her herself — comfortable, respectable, a homeowner, and white — rather than "white-trash" or Black. She strikes up a smug conversation with a well-dressed "pleasant" lady, while the lady's homely, acne-scarred teenage daughter, Mary Grace, a Wellesley student, glares at Mrs. Turpin over a book titled *Human Development* with mounting fury. As Mrs. Turpin exclaims aloud her gratitude — "Thank you, Jesus, for making everything the way it is!" — Mary Grace hurls the book at her, striking her over the eye, then lunges and tries to strangle her. Subdued and sedated, the girl delivers her oracular message: "Go back to hell where you came from, you old wart hog." Shaken to her core, Mrs. Turpin spends the afternoon obsessively brooding; that evening, hosing down her hogs in the pig parlor, she confronts God directly, demanding, "Who do you think you are?" In answer she receives a vision: a vast horde of souls streaming up a streak of light toward heaven, and at the front of the procession are all those she despised — "white-trash," Black people, "freaks and lunatics" — while she and her respectable kind bring up the rear, their virtues being "burned away." The story ends with the cricket-choruses sounding "like the voices of souls climbing upward into the starry field and shouting hallelujah" ([Revelation — summary and text references, GradeSaver study guide](https://www.gradesaver.com/flannery-oconnors-stories/study-guide/summary-revelation)).

**Essay excerpt — "The Nature and Aim of Fiction," from *Mystery and Manners* (1969).** In this lecture-essay (assembled by the Fitzgeralds from O'Connor's talks on writing), O'Connor lays out her theory of fiction: that the fiction writer works through "the concrete," that meaning must be embodied in the literal world of the story rather than stated, and that the writer of "grotesque" fiction in particular distorts appearances precisely in order to reveal a deeper, normally invisible reality. It is here, and in the companion essay "The Fiction Writer & His Country," that she gives her famous defenses of shock and distortion — "to the hard of hearing you shout, and for the almost-blind you draw large and startling figures" — and insists that her subject is "the action of grace in territory held largely by the devil" ([Mystery and Manners — Internet Archive free borrow](https://archive.org/details/mysterymanners00flan)).

**Letter excerpt — to "A." (Betty Hester), from *The Habit of Being* (1979).** In her letters to "A." beginning in 1955, O'Connor states her convictions with a directness her fiction deliberately withholds. In one celebrated passage she writes of the Eucharist, recounting a dinner party at which a sophisticated guest called the Host merely a symbol; O'Connor's reply — "Well, if it's a symbol, to hell with it" — is one of the most quoted sentences she ever wrote, and it crystallizes the literal, sacramental seriousness behind all her fiction. The letters explain, better than any critic, why a writer so funny is also so violent: for O'Connor, the supernatural is not metaphor but fact, and her stories are attempts to make a skeptical modern reader feel that fact ([The Habit of Being — Internet Archive free borrow](https://archive.org/details/habitofbeinglett00ocon)).

### Lecture Script

I want to start today not with a story but with a sentence O'Connor wrote about her own work. [pause] She said: "To the hard of hearing you shout, and for the almost-blind you draw large and startling figures." Hold that. Because everything that is shocking, grotesque, and violent in the two stories we read today — the tattooed Christ, the book hurled at a woman's face, the vision over the pig parlor — is O'Connor shouting, drawing large, because she believed her readers were deaf and blind to the thing she most wanted them to see: the action of grace. [pause] She is not a cruel writer. She is a writer who thought the modern reader could only be reached by a kind of holy shock.

Let me give you O'Connor herself, in her own voice, because we are reading her essays and letters alongside the fiction. [pause] In one of her letters — to her friend known only as "A." — she describes a dinner party where someone called the communion Host "a symbol," a nice metaphor. And O'Connor, who almost never raised her voice, wrote: "Well, if it's a symbol, to hell with it." [pause] I want you to feel the force of that. For O'Connor, the supernatural is not a symbol. It is a fact, as solid as a tractor or a broom. And once you understand that she means it literally — that grace is real, that the devil is real, that a tattoo can be a sacrament — the stories stop being weird Southern gothic and become exactly what she said they were: attempts to make a skeptical reader feel a reality he has stopped believing in.

So take "Parker's Back." [pause] Here is O. E. Parker — a man who covers his whole body in tattoos, everywhere but his back, chasing a feeling he can't name. He marries a woman, Sarah Ruth, who hates tattoos, hates vanity, hates idols — a fierce fundamentalist who believes God is pure spirit and that "no man shall see his face." Why does Parker marry her? He doesn't know. O'Connor knows. [pause] And then the turning point — Parker, driving a tractor, crashes into a tree, and the tree bursts into flame, and his shoes burn off his feet, and he calls out to God. Listen to what that is. It is the burning bush. It is Moses on holy ground, taking off his shoes. O'Connor has dropped Exodus into a Georgia hayfield. [pause]

And what does Parker do? He goes and has the face of Christ — the old Byzantine Christ, severe, with all those eyes — tattooed across his bare back. And he brings it home like an offering. And Sarah Ruth, the believer, looks at the face of God on her husband's back and calls it idolatry and beats it with a broom until it bleeds. [pause] Sit with that irony. The irreligious tattooed man is carrying Christ on his body; the religious woman beats Christ off his back. O'Connor is telling us something hard about literalism and spirit, about who really receives grace and who refuses it. And the last image — Parker, who has hidden his real name, Obadiah Elihue, his whole life, "leaning against the tree, crying like a baby." He has been broken open. That crying is the beginning of his soul. [pause]

Now "Revelation." [pause] Mrs. Turpin. One of the great comic monsters in American literature, and I mean comic — she is funny, and O'Connor wants us to laugh at her before she breaks her. Mrs. Turpin sits in a doctor's waiting room and sorts everyone into boxes: white-trash below her, Black people below her, and herself, comfortable and respectable, thanking Jesus that He made her exactly herself. [pause] And there is a girl in the room — Mary Grace, ugly, furious, glaring over a book called *Human Development* — who finally cannot bear it, and throws the book straight into Mrs. Turpin's face, and tries to strangle her, and says: "Go back to hell where you came from, you old wart hog." [pause] An ugly girl named Grace is the instrument. That is not an accident. Grace, in O'Connor, almost always arrives as an assault.

And the ending is one of the greatest in the language. Mrs. Turpin, humiliated, goes out to her pig parlor — to her hogs — and she rages at God: "Who do you think you are?" And she gets her answer. A vision: a great streak of light to heaven, and crowds of souls climbing it — and at the front, leading the procession into glory, are all the people she despised: the white-trash, the Black field hands, the freaks and lunatics. And bringing up the rear, last of all, are the respectable people, people like her, "and their virtues were being burned away." [pause] Even the good are saved only by having their pride burned off. That is the revelation. Not that Mrs. Turpin is damned — she's saved — but that she is saved last, and only by fire. [pause]

So here is what I want you to carry out of this room. O'Connor is not writing about the South to mock it, and she is not writing about freaks to disturb you. She is writing, every single time, about the moment grace breaks in — usually violently, usually unwanted — and breaks a proud or blind person open. The tattoo, the book in the face, the burning bush in the hayfield: these are her large and startling figures, drawn for the almost-blind. [pause] And the question she leaves with every reader is the one she would ask us tonight: when grace comes for you like that — sideways, violent, unwelcome — will you, like Sarah Ruth, beat it off with a broom? Or will you, like Parker, lean against the tree and finally cry?

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The burning tree in "Parker's Back."** When Parker crashes the tractor, "the tree reached out for him and... he heard himself yelling in an unbelievably loud voice, 'GOD ABOVE!'" and his shoes are left burning. O'Connor stages a deliberate echo of Moses and the burning bush (Exodus 3): the irreligious Parker is, without understanding it, standing on holy ground ([Parker's Back — JPCatholic course PDF](https://jpcatholic.edu/NCUpdf/courses/HUMA122-ParkersBack.pdf)). Have students mark how O'Connor embeds scripture in farm machinery, making the supernatural literal — exactly her stated method.

**2. The Byzantine Christ on Parker's back.** Parker rejects the gentle, modern images in the tattooist's book and chooses "the haloed head of a flat stern Byzantine Christ with all-demanding eyes." The choice matters: not a comforting Jesus but a severe, all-seeing one, an icon that looks back. The "all-demanding eyes" make the tattoo an image that judges its bearer, turning Parker's vanity into an unwilling confession.

**3. Sarah Ruth with the broom.** When Parker finally names the figure — "It's anybody. It's just God" — Sarah Ruth answers, "Idolatry!... He don't look. He's a spirit. No man shall see his face," and beats the tattooed face until "large welts had formed on the face of the tattooed Christ." The believer assaults the image of God; O'Connor's irony is total and theological, dramatizing the difference between a faith that cannot bear the incarnate and a grace embodied in flesh ([Parker's Back — JPCatholic course PDF](https://jpcatholic.edu/NCUpdf/courses/HUMA122-ParkersBack.pdf)).

**4. "Go back to hell where you came from, you old wart hog."** Mary Grace's oracular insult is the hinge of "Revelation." It is delivered after an assault, by a girl whose very name is *Grace*, and it lands on Mrs. Turpin "as if she had been singled out for the message" ([Revelation — GradeSaver](https://www.gradesaver.com/flannery-oconnors-stories/study-guide/summary-revelation)). Discuss how O'Connor makes revelation arrive as violence and humiliation, never as comfort, and why a proud soul could be reached no other way.

**5. The final vision over the pig parlor.** Mrs. Turpin sees "a vast horde of souls" ascending, with the despised — "white-trash... bands of black... freaks and lunatics" — at the front, and her own respectable kind last, their "virtues... being burned away" ([Revelation — GradeSaver](https://www.gradesaver.com/flannery-oconnors-stories/study-guide/summary-revelation)). The hog parlor as the site of vision is pure O'Connor: the holy breaks in at the lowest, dirtiest place. Note the closing image of cricket-voices "shouting hallelujah," which lets grace sound even after the vision fades.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What does O'Connor mean by "to the hard of hearing you shout," and how do these stories enact it?**
   *Model answer:* She means that a modern reader deaf to the reality of grace can only be reached through shock and distortion, so she "draws large and startling figures." The tattooed Christ, the hurled book, and the burning tree are exactly such figures — violent, grotesque images designed to make a skeptical reader feel a spiritual reality that quiet statement could not convey.

2. **Why does Parker, who is irreligious, end up bearing the face of Christ on his back?**
   *Model answer:* Parker is driven by a longing he cannot name, and after the burning-tree experience he has the severe Byzantine Christ tattooed on the one bare part of his body, believing it will master his wife. O'Connor's point is that grace works through Parker's confusion and vanity rather than his understanding; the icon with "all-demanding eyes" claims him whether or not he grasps what has happened.

3. **What is the significance of Sarah Ruth beating the tattoo?**
   *Model answer:* The fiercely religious Sarah Ruth condemns the image of Christ as idolatry — "He's a spirit... No man shall see his face" — and beats it until it bleeds, so that the believer assaults the incarnate God while the unbeliever bears Him. The irony dramatizes O'Connor's Catholic, sacramental conviction that grace is embodied in flesh, and exposes a faith so spiritualized it cannot tolerate God made visible.

4. **Why does O'Connor name the girl in "Revelation" Mary Grace?**
   *Model answer:* The name signals that the violent, ugly girl is the unlikely instrument of grace, which in O'Connor almost always arrives as assault rather than comfort. By having "Grace" hurl a book titled *Human Development* into a self-satisfied woman's face, O'Connor fuses theology and comedy and insists that revelation can come through the repellent and the shocking.

5. **What exactly is revealed to Mrs. Turpin, and is she damned or saved?**
   *Model answer:* She is granted a vision of souls ascending to heaven with the people she despised — the poor, Black people, the "freaks" — at the front, and her own respectable kind last, their virtues "being burned away." She is saved, not damned, but saved last and only by having her pride purged, which overturns her entire system of self-righteous social ranking.

6. **How does O'Connor use the grotesque, and how is it different from mere shock?**
   *Model answer:* The grotesque in O'Connor — the freakish, distorted, and violent — is never sensation for its own sake but a means of revelation, distorting surfaces to expose a normally invisible spiritual reality. As she argues in *Mystery and Manners*, distortion is how the writer makes the "almost-blind" see, so the grotesque is a moral and theological instrument rather than a thrill.

7. **How do O'Connor's essay and letter change the way we read the fiction?**
   *Model answer:* In *Mystery and Manners* she states that her real subject is "the action of grace in territory held largely by the devil" and that meaning lives in the concrete, while her letter to "A." — "if it's a symbol, to hell with it" — reveals her literal, sacramental belief. Knowing this, we read the violence not as Gothic atmosphere but as the literal eruption of a grace O'Connor believed to be real.

8. **How does O'Connor handle race in "Revelation," and how should we read it?**
   *Model answer:* She records Mrs. Turpin's casual racism precisely and then condemns it through the vision that puts Black souls ahead of the respectable whites, so the story's moral judgment falls squarely on Turpin's bigotry. At the same time, students should know O'Connor's own private attitudes have been seriously debated, so the honest approach is to credit the fiction's judgment while neither excusing the author's blind spots nor reducing the art to them.

9. **Why are humor and violence so closely joined in O'Connor?**
   *Model answer:* O'Connor makes us laugh at her proud, self-deceived characters before she breaks them, so that comedy lowers our guard for the violent arrival of grace. The humor is not relief from the seriousness but part of it: by exposing pretension as ridiculous, she prepares the reader, like the character, for a humbling revelation.

10. **What single question do both stories finally put to the reader?**
    *Model answer:* Both ask how a person will respond when grace breaks in unbidden, sideways, and violent — whether one will refuse it, like Sarah Ruth with her broom, or be broken open by it, like Parker crying against the tree or Turpin purged in her vision. O'Connor insists that grace rarely comes as comfort, and that its reception, not its arrival, is what the soul is judged by.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is that O'Connor's violence and her grotesque can read as cruelty or mere shock unless the theological purpose is made clear; ground the class early in her own statement that she "draws large and startling figures" for the almost-blind, and the stories cohere. The frank racial material in "Revelation" must be handled with adult composure: present Mrs. Turpin's bigotry as the disease the story diagnoses, credit the fiction's unsparing judgment, and acknowledge honestly the documented debate over O'Connor's own attitudes without either excusing them or collapsing the art into them. For a male instructor, the relevant note is restraint about belief: O'Connor's Catholicism is not optional decoration but the engine of the work, so present it accurately and seriously without either endorsing or mocking it, and let students of any conviction meet it on the page. Honor O'Connor as one of the supreme American stylists and a rigorous thinker about her own craft — a master, not a regional eccentric — and resist the temptation to "explain away" the religion that makes her strange.

### One Thing to Memorize

O'Connor's defense of her own method: "To the hard of hearing you shout, and for the almost-blind you draw large and startling figures." It explains every violent, grotesque turn in her fiction — the shock is not cruelty but the means by which she makes a deaf and blind reader see the action of grace.

## Session 14 — Tillie Olsen and Grace Paley: "I Stand Here Ironing" and "A Conversation with My Father"

### At a Glance

Two short stories by two New York–shaped, politically engaged writers of the mid-century left: Tillie Olsen's "I Stand Here Ironing" (1961) and Grace Paley's "A Conversation with My Father" (1974). Both are short, both are about mothers and the stories we can or cannot tell about a child's life, and both turn the ordinary materials of domestic labor and family talk into searching meditations on time, helplessness, and the shape of a story. We read them together because they make a fine argument with each other: Olsen's narrator stands trapped in the linear, irreversible flow of a life lived; Paley's narrator argues with her dying father about whether a story — and a life — must have a fixed, tragic plot. Approximate seminar time: one session. Both texts are brief and reward reading aloud.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Tillie Olsen (1912–2007)** was born Tillie Lerner in Nebraska to Jewish immigrants from Russia who had fled after the failed 1905 revolution; her father became state secretary of the Nebraska Socialist Party ([Literary Ladies Guide](https://www.literaryladiesguide.com/author-biography/olsen-tillie/)). She grew up poor, second of six children, worked from childhood, and dropped out of high school to help support the family. Politically active from her teens, she joined the Young Communist League, was jailed for organizing packinghouse workers, and contracted tuberculosis from factory conditions ([Literary Ladies Guide](https://www.literaryladiesguide.com/author-biography/olsen-tillie/)). She married Jack Olsen, raised four daughters, and worked for decades at the kind of jobs — laundry, factory, clerical — that left little time or energy for writing. This long enforced silence became her great subject; her 1978 book *Silences* studies the social conditions, especially class and gender, that mute the creative work of the poor and of women.

Olsen wrote "I Stand Here Ironing" in the early 1950s and published it as the opening story of *Tell Me a Riddle* (1961), a slender collection of four stories that appeared when she was nearly fifty ([Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/education/news-wires-white-papers-and-books/i-stand-here-ironing)). The title story won the O. Henry Award and remains her best-known work. Her output was small and hard-won, which is part of its meaning: here was a working-class mother who wrote against every obstacle and who insisted that the world hear what such silences cost. She later taught at Stanford, MIT, and Amherst, and worked to recover out-of-print women's writing, helping return Rebecca Harding Davis's *Life in the Iron Mills* to print ([Literary Ladies Guide](https://www.literaryladiesguide.com/author-biography/olsen-tillie/)).

**Grace Paley (1922–2007)** was born Grace Goodside in the Bronx, the youngest child of Jewish socialist immigrants from Ukraine; Russian, Yiddish, and English filled the household, and that polyphony enters her ear-perfect dialogue ([Oxford Research Encyclopedia of Literature](https://academic.oup.com/edited-volume/61883/chapter/547845560)). She studied briefly at Hunter College and with W. H. Auden at the New School, married young, and raised two children in Greenwich Village. Paley came to fiction in her thirties, publishing her first collection, *The Little Disturbances of Man*, in 1959; "A Conversation with My Father" appears in her second, *Enormous Changes at the Last Minute* (1974) ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/grace-paley)). She published only three slim story collections in her lifetime, plus poems and essays, and she was famously unhurried — her stories are short because, she said, she could only write the truth she had.

Paley was also a lifelong activist: an anti-war organizer, feminist, and pacifist who was arrested more than once for civil disobedience, and who taught for many years at Sarah Lawrence College, a post she held from 1965 to 1988 ([Oxford Research Encyclopedia of Literature](https://academic.oup.com/edited-volume/61883/chapter/547845560)). She later served as the first official New York State Author. Like Olsen, she wrote from inside ordinary urban women's lives — mothers, neighbors, ex-husbands, the talk of the stoop and the kitchen — and made of that talk an art of extraordinary compression and warmth. The two women shared a generation, a left politics, a Jewish immigrant inheritance, and a conviction that the lives of unimportant people are the proper and sufficient subject of serious literature.

### Historical & Cultural Context

Both writers belong to the American literary left and to a mid-century moment when the lives of working-class and immigrant women were largely absent from the serious literary record. Olsen's silences were literal: two decades in which child-rearing and wage labor left her almost no time to write, an experience she later theorized as a class-and-gender problem rather than a personal failing ([Literary Ladies Guide](https://www.literaryladiesguide.com/author-biography/olsen-tillie/)). "I Stand Here Ironing," written in the early 1950s and published in 1961, emerges from Depression-era poverty, the disruptions of the WPA years and World War II, and the era's confident expert advice to mothers — advice the story quietly indicts.

Paley's "A Conversation with My Father" comes out of the early 1970s, the height of the women's movement and of an experimental, metafictional turn in American fiction. The story stages a debate between an old man — a dying father who wants the consolations of the classic, plotted, "tragic" story — and his daughter, a writer who refuses to foreclose her characters' futures, insisting that "everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life" ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/grace-paley)). That argument is at once personal (a daughter and her dying father), aesthetic (old realism versus new open form), and political (whether women's lives must end in defeat). Both writers also draw on a specifically Jewish immigrant world of socialist conviction, family obligation, and verbal richness, connecting this session to the female-voice and domestic-labor threads that run through the whole course.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**"I Stand Here Ironing."** The entire story is the interior monologue of a mother as she stands ironing, prompted by a request — from a teacher, counselor, or social worker — to come in and talk about her nineteen-year-old daughter, Emily, who is said to need help. Moving back and forth in time as the iron moves back and forth over the cloth, the mother reconstructs Emily's childhood: a beautiful baby, breast-fed and beloved, whose father left when she was eight months old during the Depression; the mother's desperate poverty and need to work; the long separations when Emily was sent to relatives and, later, to a grim convalescent home for run-down children, where affection was rationed and letters were not allowed to be kept. She recalls Emily's loneliness, her jealousy of a prettier, easier younger sister, her slow, late-blooming gift for comic mime, which finally won her applause at a school amateur show. The mother's meditation circles a central helplessness: so much of what shaped Emily was beyond her control — poverty, war, time, the demands of other children — and she cannot, in the time it takes to iron a dress, "total it all." She refuses the summons to explain her daughter, asking only that Emily be helped to know "that she is more than this dress on the ironing board, helpless before the iron" ([Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/education/news-wires-white-papers-and-books/i-stand-here-ironing)).

**"A Conversation with My Father."** The narrator, a writer, sits at the bedside of her eighty-six-year-old father, who is gravely ill. He asks her to write him "a simple story" of the old-fashioned kind — "the kind de Maupassant wrote, or Chekhov" — recognizable people with a plain plot. She first produces a bare two-sentence story about a woman whose son becomes a drug addict and who, to keep close to him, becomes one too; then the son gives up drugs and leaves her, and she is alone. Her father objects that she has left out everything — the people's looks, their history, the texture of their lives — so she rewrites the story at greater length, naming the mother a once-respectable Manhattan woman who joins her teenage son in the East Village drug culture. The father pronounces the ending tragic: the son recovers and abandons his mother to her ruin. But the daughter refuses that closure. She insists the woman could change — could stop being an addict, could become, in fact, the receptionist in a storefront clinic, valued for her experience. The father, who knows he is dying, presses her: she is afraid to look tragedy in the face; in real life this woman would simply be dead, or finished. The story ends in their loving, unresolved disagreement about whether a life, like a story, must have a fixed and tragic end ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/grace-paley)).

### Lecture Script

I want to start with a woman ironing. [pause] That is all the situation Tillie Olsen gives us: a mother at the ironing board, the iron moving back and forth, back and forth, while she thinks. Someone — a school, a counselor — has asked her to come in and talk about her daughter Emily, who is in some kind of trouble. And the whole story is what passes through the mother's mind as she irons. Notice that the iron's motion is the story's motion: back and forth in time, smoothing, pressing, never quite getting the wrinkles out. The form is the meaning. [pause]

Olsen is writing about something the literature before her largely left out: the actual texture of a poor mother's life. The father left when Emily was eight months old, in the Depression. The mother had to work. The baby was sent away — to relatives, then to a convalescent home where the children couldn't even keep the letters their parents sent. [pause] And here is the heart of it: the mother knows she failed Emily in a hundred ways, and she also knows that most of those failures were not failures of love but of circumstance — poverty, time, war, the needs of the other children. She says she cannot "total it all." She cannot do the math of a life. [pause]

This is not a story that lets the mother off the hook, and it is not a story that hangs her on it. It does something harder. It holds her in the place where love and helplessness meet, and it refuses to resolve them. The last lines are among the most quietly powerful in American fiction. She will not go in and explain Emily. She asks only this — that someone help Emily to know "that she is more than this dress on the ironing board, helpless before the iron." [pause] The dress, the daughter, the mother — all helpless before the iron, before time, before circumstance. And yet the wish that the child be more than that is itself an act of fierce, defeated love.

Now turn to Grace Paley, who gives us a different argument entirely — gives us, in fact, an argument, out loud, between a daughter and her dying father. [pause] The father is eighty-six and ill. He asks his daughter, who is a writer, to write him a simple story, "the kind de Maupassant wrote, or Chekhov." Plain people, a plot. So she tries. She writes him two sentences about a woman whose son becomes an addict, and who becomes one too to stay close to him, and then he quits and leaves her alone. [pause] Her father says: you've left out everything. Where are the people? So she writes it again, fuller, with a real woman, a real neighborhood.

And here is where the story becomes about everything. The father reads the new version and says: tragic. The son saved, the mother destroyed. The end. And the daughter says — no. She won't let the woman be finished. She insists the woman could change, could get clean, could become the receptionist at a clinic, valued for exactly what she suffered. [pause] The father, who is dying, looks at his daughter and says, in effect: you can't face it. You can't look tragedy in the eye. In life this woman would just be dead.

Do you hear what they're really arguing about? [pause] On the surface it's craft — old realism versus open form. Underneath it's the father's death, which the daughter cannot bear, and which she keeps trying to write her way out of. And underneath that it's a question this whole course has been circling: must a woman's story end in defeat? The father says the honest ending is tragic. The daughter says — and Paley says — that "everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life." [pause] She will not close the door on her character, because to close it would be to close it on her father, and on all the women whose stories were always allowed only one ending. Paley refuses. Gently, lovingly, while ironing out no wrinkle and winning no argument, she refuses. [pause] Put these two stories side by side, and you have the whole drama: Olsen's mother trapped in the irreversible flow of a life already lived, and Paley's daughter fighting to keep the future open. Helplessness and hope, the closed plot and the open destiny. That is our session.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. The iron as form.** "I stand here ironing, and what you asked me moves tormented back and forth with the iron." The opening sentence fuses the physical task with the mental one: the back-and-forth of the iron is the back-and-forth of memory, and the whole story's nonlinear structure is announced in its first line ([Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/education/news-wires-white-papers-and-books/i-stand-here-ironing)). Have students track how the iron returns at intervals, anchoring the drift of recollection in a body at work.

**2. "She was a child of anxious, not proud, love."** Olsen's mother is unsparing about the conditions of Emily's infancy — the colicky months, the enforced separations, the rationed affection of the convalescent home. The phrasing refuses sentimentality about motherhood while insisting on the love beneath the anxiety. It is a model of how to write hard truths about family without either accusation or excuse.

**3. "Helpless before the iron."** The closing wish — "Only help her to know... that she is more than this dress on the ironing board, helpless before the iron" — gathers the story's central metaphor into a plea ([Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/education/news-wires-white-papers-and-books/i-stand-here-ironing)). The dress is shaped by forces it cannot resist; so was Emily; so, the mother knows, was she. The line lets students feel determinism and the refusal of determinism in the same breath.

**4. The two versions of Paley's embedded story.** Paley gives us the bare two-sentence version and then the fuller rewrite, and the gap between them is a lesson in fiction itself: what detail adds, what plot forecloses. Ask students to compare the two and to notice that the father's "better" story is also the more hopeless one — fullness and fatalism arriving together ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/grace-paley)).

**5. "Everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life."** This is the daughter's — and Paley's — credo, set against the father's tragic realism. It is at once an aesthetic position (against closed, plotted endings), a feminist one (against the single defeated ending allotted to women), and an act of love (against admitting that her father, too, has a fixed and nearing end). Let students hold all three meanings at once.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **How does the act of ironing structure Olsen's story?**
   *Model answer:* The iron's back-and-forth motion mirrors the mother's movement back and forth through memory, so the story's nonlinear structure grows directly out of a body at domestic labor. The chore is not mere setting; it is the form, embodying both the relentlessness of a working mother's day and the impossibility of "totaling" a life while one's hands are full.

2. **What forces shaped Emily, according to her mother?**
   *Model answer:* Poverty, the father's desertion when Emily was eight months old, the Depression, the mother's need to work, long enforced separations, a loveless convalescent home, and the arrival of easier younger siblings. The mother's point is that most of these were circumstance, not failures of love, which is why she resists being summoned to "explain" her daughter.

3. **Why does the mother refuse to go in and talk about Emily?**
   *Model answer:* She knows that no conversation can "total" the tangle of love, poverty, and time that made Emily who she is, and she distrusts the implication that a mother can be made to account for her child on demand. Her refusal is also a defense of Emily's complexity against being reduced to a case.

4. **What does the closing image — "helpless before the iron" — mean?**
   *Model answer:* The dress is shaped by a force it cannot resist, as Emily was shaped by circumstance and as the mother herself was; the image makes determinism vivid. Yet the mother's wish that Emily "know she is more than this" is a refusal of total determinism — a defeated but real assertion of human possibility.

5. **What kind of story does Paley's father want, and why?**
   *Model answer:* He wants a "simple story" with recognizable people and a clear plot, "the kind de Maupassant wrote, or Chekhov" — classic realism with a shapely, often tragic arc. As a dying man, he wants the consolation of form, the sense that a life, like a story, has a recognizable and complete shape.

6. **Why does the daughter resist giving her character a tragic ending?**
   *Model answer:* She insists that "everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life" and refuses to declare her character finished. Her resistance is at once aesthetic (against closed plots), feminist (against the single defeated ending so often allotted to women), and personal — refusing her character's death is a way of refusing her father's.

7. **How is the argument about fiction also an argument about death?**
   *Model answer:* The father, who is dying, identifies honesty with accepting the tragic, fixed end; the daughter's refusal to close her character's fate is her refusal to accept his. Their loving disagreement about whether a story must end in defeat is really their unspoken negotiation with his nearing death, which is why neither can simply win.

8. **How do the two stories speak to each other?**
   *Model answer:* Olsen's mother is caught in the irreversible flow of a life already lived and cannot undo what circumstance has done; Paley's daughter fights to keep the future open against a father who insists the ending is fixed. Read together, they stage the course's deep question — whether a woman's life and story must end in defeat — from opposite directions, one looking back in helplessness, the other forward in stubborn hope.

9. **What is distinctive about how both writers handle voice?**
   *Model answer:* Both build their stories almost entirely out of a woman's speaking or thinking voice — Olsen's interior monologue, Paley's quick, ear-perfect dialogue — and both draw on a Jewish immigrant verbal world of warmth and argument. The art is in the talk: ordinary speech compressed until it carries the full weight of love, helplessness, and conviction.

10. **Why are these stories so short, and is that a limitation?**
    *Model answer:* Brevity is a deliberate poetics for both writers — Olsen wrote against the silences imposed by poverty and labor, Paley said she could only write the truth she had — and the compression concentrates rather than thins the material. The shortness is itself meaningful: it embodies lives lived in scarce time and insists that a few pages of ordinary speech can hold an entire moral world.

### Anticipated Difficulties

Students may find these stories "small" — domestic, plotless, undramatic — and miss how much moral and formal weight they carry; meet this by teaching the form as the meaning (the iron, the embedded story) rather than hunting for event. The frank material — addiction in Paley, maternal failure and institutional neglect in Olsen — should be handled with adult composure and without melodrama, keeping the focus on the writers' control. For a male instructor, the live note is restraint about maternal guilt: present Olsen's mother without either excusing or condemning her, and resist explaining a mother's interior life as though you had privileged access to it; let the text speak. Honor both writers as deliberate artists of compression, not as charming miniaturists or sociological witnesses — the brevity is a hard-won aesthetic, and the politics are inseparable from the craft. Finally, let Paley's argument stay genuinely open; do not resolve the father-daughter debate, because its unresolvability is the point.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Tillie Olsen, *Silences* (Delacorte Press, 1978) — her foundational meditation on how class and gender silence creative work; essential background to "I Stand Here Ironing."
- Panthea Reid, *Tillie Olsen: One Woman, Many Riddles* (Rutgers University Press, 2010) — the major, and notably unsparing, biography.
- Grace Paley, *The Collected Stories* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1994) — gathers all three collections; a National Book Award and Pulitzer finalist.
- Grace Paley, *Just As I Thought* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1998) — essays and talks that lay out her politics and her poetics of the open destiny.
- Judith Arcana, *Grace Paley's Life Stories: A Literary Biography* (University of Illinois Press, 1993) — useful on the life-and-work relation.
- Constance Coiner, *Better Red: The Writing and Resistance of Tillie Olsen and Meridel Le Sueur* (Oxford University Press, 1995) — strong on the literary left and both writers' politics.

### One Thing to Memorize

Paley's credo against the closed, tragic plot: "Everyone, real or invented, deserves the open destiny of life." Set it beside Olsen's "helpless before the iron," and you hold the session's whole argument — determinism and the refusal of determinism, in two unforgettable phrases.

## Session 15 — Adrienne Rich, Audre Lorde, and Lucille Clifton

### At a Glance

Three poets of the second-wave era, read together: Adrienne Rich's "Diving into the Wreck" and "Power"; Audre Lorde's "Coal" and "A Litany for Survival"; and Lucille Clifton's "won't you celebrate with me" and "homage to my hips." These are poems of self-claiming — of language as descent and recovery, of the body and the named self as ground to stand on, of survival as a deliberate art. They make a powerful trio: Rich the formal poet who broke her own line open under political pressure; Lorde the self-described "Black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet" who fused identities the culture wanted kept separate; Clifton the spare, exuberant celebrant of the Black female body and self. Approximate seminar time: one session, poetry-paced and read aloud throughout. All six poems are available in reliable online texts and in standard editions.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Adrienne Rich (1929–2012)** was born in Baltimore, the daughter of a Jewish physician father and a Protestant pianist mother, and educated at Radcliffe College, from which she graduated in 1951 — the same year W. H. Auden chose her first book, *A Change of World*, for the Yale Series of Younger Poets ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/book/diving-wreck)). Her early verse was formally polished and praised for its decorum; Auden famously said her poems "do not tell fibs." She married the Harvard economist Alfred Conrad, had three sons before she was thirty, and then, through the 1960s, transformed both her life and her art — moving to New York, becoming deeply involved in anti-war, civil rights, and feminist activism, and breaking her tight metrical line open into a freer, more confrontational free verse ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrienne_Rich)). She separated from her husband in 1970, the year he died by suicide. In 1973 she published *Diving into the Wreck*, which won the National Book Award; declining to accept it for herself, she joined her fellow nominees Alice Walker and Audre Lorde in accepting it on behalf of all women "whose voices have gone and still go unheard" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrienne_Rich)). From 1976 until her death she shared her life with the writer Michelle Cliff. Rich became one of the most widely read and influential poets of the second half of the twentieth century, and one of its most important feminist thinkers.

**Audre Lorde (1934–1992)** was born in New York City to West Indian immigrant parents from Grenada; she described herself, in a famous and deliberate formulation, as "Black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet" — identities the culture preferred to keep apart and that she insisted on holding together ([Norton Anthology of American Literature](https://eclass.uoa.gr/modules/document/file.php/ENL605/Audre%20Lorde%20%E2%80%93%20Coal.pdf)). She began writing poems as a child, attended Hunter College High School, took a B.A. from Hunter College in 1959 and a master's in library science from Columbia in 1960, and worked for years as a librarian. She married, had two children, divorced in 1970, and built a life as a writer, teacher, and activist, eventually professor of English at Hunter College ([Norton Anthology of American Literature](https://eclass.uoa.gr/modules/document/file.php/ENL605/Audre%20Lorde%20%E2%80%93%20Coal.pdf)). Her breakthrough volume *Coal* (W. W. Norton, 1976) and *The Black Unicorn* (1978) established her; in prose she wrote *The Cancer Journals* (1980), the "biomythography" *Zami: A New Spelling of My Name* (1982), and the essays of *Sister Outsider* (1984), which include "The Master's Tools Will Never Dismantle the Master's House" ([Queer Portraits in History](https://www.queerportraits.com/bio/lorde)). She co-founded Kitchen Table: Women of Color Press, was a major voice in what we now call intersectional feminism, became New York State Poet in 1991, and died of cancer in 1992.

**Lucille Clifton (1936–2010)** was born Lucille Sayles in Depew, New York, near Buffalo, into a working family with deep memory and little money; she went to Howard University in the 1950s, a flowering time for Black letters, where her cohort and circle included Toni Morrison — later her editor at Random House — and the poet Sterling Brown ([Emory Libraries Blog](https://scholarblogs.emory.edu/woodruff/rose-library/come-celebrate-with-me-an-exhibition-on-the-work-of-lucille-clifton)). She married Fred Clifton, raised six children, and wrote in the spare, lower-case, deceptively simple style that became her signature. Her first book, *Good Times* (1969), was named one of the year's ten best by *The New York Times*; "homage to my hips" appears in *Two-Headed Woman* (1980), and "won't you celebrate with me" in *Book of Light* (1993) ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucille_Clifton); [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/articles/69412/lucille-clifton-wont-you-celebrate-with-me)). She served as Poet Laureate of Maryland, won the National Book Award for *Blessing the Boats* (2000), and was the first author to have two books named finalists for the Pulitzer in the same year. Her work celebrates the Black female body and self with unguarded joy and confronts loss with unflinching plainness.

### Historical & Cultural Context

These three poets write out of the second wave of American feminism and the long civil rights and Black freedom struggles, roughly the late 1960s through the 1980s — a period when poetry became, for many women, a primary instrument of self-definition and political witness. Rich's career enacts the era's pressure on form: a poet trained in mid-century New Critical decorum who, under the force of the women's movement and the Vietnam War, broke her line open and turned her art toward what had been unspeakable in women's lives ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/book/diving-wreck)). Her 1974 National Book Award acceptance — refusing the prize as an individual and sharing it with Walker and Lorde on behalf of all silenced women — is itself a primary document of the moment's politics ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrienne_Rich)).

Lorde and Clifton extend the frame to the specific situation of Black women, insisting — against a feminism that too often spoke for white middle-class women and a Black freedom movement that too often centered men — that race, gender, sexuality, and class are lived together, not separately. Lorde's insistence on naming all her identities at once, and her essays urging white feminists to confront racism, are foundational to intersectional thought ([Queer Portraits in History](https://www.queerportraits.com/bio/lorde)). Clifton's celebrations of the Black female body answered a culture that had long denied that body beauty or worth, and her poem of self-celebration was written, as the Poetry Foundation notes, against the inherited weight of "exile and difference" ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/articles/69412/lucille-clifton-wont-you-celebrate-with-me)). This session gathers the course's threads of the contested lyric "I," the body and its constraints, and voice claimed despite silence, and brings them into the contemporary world.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Rich, "Diving into the Wreck"** (1973): The speaker, having read "the book of myths," loads a camera and a knife, puts on a diver's mask and flippers, and descends alone down a ladder into the sea. The poem narrates the dive step by step — the awkward gear on land, the descent past blue light into the dark, the body adapting to the depths — until the speaker reaches the wreck itself. The goal, she insists, is not the story of the wreck or its myth but "the wreck and not the story of the wreck, / the thing itself and not the myth." At the bottom the speaker's identity becomes fluid — "I am she: I am he" — merging genders and merging with the drowned, mermaid and merman, examining the damage and "the treasures that prevail." The dive is an extended allegory of recovering a buried truth about self and history that the official "book of myths," in which "our names do not appear," has omitted ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrienne_Rich)).

**Rich, "Power"** (1978): A short poem that opens with the speaker reading, in a backhoe-unearthed bottle from the soil, "a hundred-year-old / cure for fever or melancholy," then turns to Marie Curie, who discovered radium and died of the radiation her own work had exposed her to — "her wounds came from the same source as her power." The poem meditates on the tragic cost of a woman's achievement and on the denial that accompanies it: Curie, the poem suggests, "must have known" yet "denied... her wounds came from the same source as her power," a final image of the price women pay for their own strength.

**Lorde, "Coal"** (1968/1976): A poem of identity built on a central metaphor: "I / is the total black, being spoken / from the earth's inside." Coal — black, compressed, capable of becoming diamond and of giving light and heat — becomes the figure for the speaker's Black self and for her words, which are "open" like diamonds or charged like fuse-wire. The poem catalogs different kinds of words and ends by claiming language as power: "I am Black because I come from the earth's inside / now take my word for jewel in the open light," a declaration of self and of the worth of Black speech ([Norton Anthology of American Literature](https://eclass.uoa.gr/modules/document/file.php/ENL605/Audre%20Lorde%20%E2%80%93%20Coal.pdf)).

**Lorde, "A Litany for Survival"**: Addressed to "those of us who live at the shoreline," the marginalized who were "never meant to survive," the poem catalogs the fears that haunt people taught to live on the edges — fear in love, in speech, in silence. Its turning recognition is that fear is constant whether one speaks or stays silent — "when we are loved we are afraid / love will vanish... when we are alone we are afraid / love will never return" — and so it ends with the great imperative that names the poem's purpose: "So it is better to speak / remembering / we were never meant to survive." Speech, however dangerous, is chosen over a silence that protects no one.

**Clifton, "homage to my hips"** (1980): A short, exuberant poem in which the speaker celebrates her "big hips," hips that "need space to move around in," that "don't fit into little / petty places," that are "free hips," that "have never been enslaved" and "go where they want to go" and "do what they want to do." The closing image turns the hips magical and powerful: "i have known them / to put a spell on a man and / spin him like a top." It is a poem of bodily self-celebration, claiming beauty, freedom, and power for a Black woman's body the culture had denied ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucille_Clifton)).

**Clifton, "won't you celebrate with me"** (1993): A single-sentence sonnet-like poem in which the speaker invites the reader to "celebrate with me / what i have shaped into / a kind of life." Having "no model" — "born in babylon / both nonwhite and woman" — she made herself up, "here on this bridge between / starshine and clay," with "one hand holding tight / the other hand." The poem ends with a stark, triumphant survival: "come celebrate / with me that everyday / something has tried to kill me / and has failed." It is the course's purest statement of self-creation against erasure ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/articles/69412/lucille-clifton-wont-you-celebrate-with-me)).

- **Adrienne Rich, "Power"** — in *The Dream of a Common Language: Poems 1974–1977* (W. W. Norton, 1978); text at the [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/adrienne-rich).
- **Audre Lorde, "Coal"** and **"A Litany for Survival"** — in *Coal* (W. W. Norton, 1976) and *The Black Unicorn* (1978) respectively; both gathered in *The Collected Poems of Audre Lorde* (Norton, 1997); texts at the [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/audre-lorde).

### Lecture Script

I want to begin underwater. [pause] Adrienne Rich gives us a woman putting on a diver's mask and flippers, taking up a camera and a knife, and going down a ladder, alone, into the sea. And she tells us exactly why she is going. Not for the story of the wreck. Not for the myth. "The thing itself and not the myth." She wants the wreck — the actual damage, and the treasures that survived it. [pause] Hold that distinction, because it is the key to this whole session. These three poets are all diving past the official "book of myths" — the stories the culture told about women, about Black people, about bodies — to reach the thing itself.

And listen to what happens to the diver at the bottom. "I am she: I am he." The self becomes fluid, both and neither, merged with the drowned. Rich is doing something radical with the lyric "I" — the same "I" we watched Dickinson invent and Bishop discipline. She is saying the self that goes down to recover the truth is not the tidy, single self of the old poems. It is plural, fluid, and it goes armed — camera and knife, to record and to cut. [pause] Notice, too, the line that ends "a book of myths / in which / our names do not appear." That is the wound this whole course has been probing: the record from which women's names were left out. Rich dives to put them back.

Then turn to "Power," a much shorter poem, and one of the hardest truths in the book. [pause] Marie Curie discovered radium. Radium killed her. Rich's final image: "her wounds came from the same source as her power." Read that twice. The very thing that made her great is the thing that destroyed her — and, Rich suggests, she denied it, because to face it would be unbearable. This is Rich's warning to every woman claiming power: the source of your strength may also be the source of your wound. It is not a poem of triumph. It is a poem of cost. [pause]

Now Audre Lorde, who claimed power differently. Lorde called herself "Black, lesbian, mother, warrior, poet" — all at once, refusing to choose. In "Coal," she builds a whole self out of a single image. "I / is the total black, being spoken / from the earth's inside." Coal: black, buried, compressed — and capable of becoming a diamond, capable of light and heat. [pause] Her words come out of that blackness like jewels in the open light. This is a poem that takes the very thing the culture devalued — blackness, the underground, the buried — and says: this is where the diamond comes from. This is where the word comes from. Take my word for a jewel.

And then "A Litany for Survival," which I cannot read without my voice changing. [pause] It is for "those of us who live at the shoreline... who were never meant to survive." And Lorde walks through every fear of those taught to live on the edge — fear when we speak, fear when we are silent, fear in love. And she arrives at the great recognition: we are afraid whether we speak or not. The silence will not save us. "So it is better to speak / remembering / we were never meant to survive." [pause] That is one of the bravest endings in American poetry. Speech is dangerous. Silence is also dangerous. Choose speech.

And finally Lucille Clifton, who brings joy. [pause] After all this depth and danger, Clifton gives us "homage to my hips" — a poem that struts. "these hips are big hips... these hips are free hips... they don't like to be held back." And the last image: these hips have "put a spell on a man and / spin him like a top." It is funny, and it is defiant, and it is a Black woman claiming her own body as beautiful and powerful in a culture that told her otherwise. [pause] And then her masterpiece, "won't you celebrate with me." Listen: "i had no model. / born in babylon / both nonwhite and woman / what did i see to be except myself? / i made it up." She made herself up, with no model, "one hand holding tight / the other hand." And the ending — "come celebrate / with me that everyday / something has tried to kill me / and has failed." [pause] That is where this session lands, and very nearly where the course lands. Not in triumph exactly, and not in defeat. In survival, deliberately celebrated. Something has tried to kill me, every day, and has failed. Won't you celebrate with me.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "the thing itself and not the myth" (Rich, "Diving into the Wreck").** The poem's central distinction: the speaker descends not for the comforting narrative but for the unmediated reality, "the wreck and not the story of the wreck" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrienne_Rich)). Ask students how a poem — itself made of language and therefore "story" — can claim to reach past story to the thing itself; the tension is productive and deliberate.

**2. "I am she: I am he" (Rich).** At the wreck the speaker's gender dissolves into plurality, merging with the drowned mermaid and merman. The line reimagines the lyric "I" as fluid and collective rather than singular, and it lets students connect Rich's experiment to the whole course's argument about the contested first person. The closing return to "a book of myths / in which / our names do not appear" names the omission the dive sets out to repair.

**3. "her wounds came from the same source as her power" (Rich, "Power").** Curie's radium is both her glory and her death. The line crystallizes a tragic structure students can carry across many of the women on the syllabus: the gift and the wound issuing from one source. Note Rich's emphasis on Curie's denial — the difficulty of facing that the source of one's power is also lethal.

**4. "I / is the total black, being spoken / from the earth's inside" (Lorde, "Coal").** The grammar is deliberately strange — "I is," not "I am" — making the self both subject and substance, a thing spoken out of the earth ([Norton Anthology of American Literature](https://eclass.uoa.gr/modules/document/file.php/ENL605/Audre%20Lorde%20%E2%80%93%20Coal.pdf)). Coal's transformation into diamond underwrites the poem's claim that the devalued, buried, black thing is the source of the jewel and of the word. Read the strangeness as meaning, not error.

**5. "something has tried to kill me / and has failed" (Clifton, "won't you celebrate with me").** The poem's triumphant close earns its joy precisely because it names the daily threat ([Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/articles/69412/lucille-clifton-wont-you-celebrate-with-me)). Pair it with "i made it up" and "i had no model": self-creation without precedent, survival as achievement. The lower-case, unpunctuated style enacts a self made plainly and from scratch, "between starshine and clay."

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **What does the diver seek in "Diving into the Wreck," and why is the distinction important?**
   *Model answer:* She seeks "the thing itself and not the myth" — the actual wreck, its damage and surviving treasures, not the official story preserved in "the book of myths." The distinction matters because the poem is about recovering a buried truth the culture's myths have omitted, including the women whose "names do not appear," making the dive an allegory of feminist and historical recovery.

2. **How does Rich transform the lyric "I" in the poem?**
   *Model answer:* At the wreck the speaker declares "I am she: I am he," dissolving singular gendered identity into a fluid, plural self merged with the drowned. Rich thereby reimagines the lyric first person — the same contested "I" the course has tracked from Dickinson onward — as collective and androgynous rather than unitary.

3. **What is the central insight of "Power"?**
   *Model answer:* Using Marie Curie, who died of the radiation that made her famous, Rich observes that "her wounds came from the same source as her power" — that a woman's strength and her destruction can issue from one source. The poem is a sober meditation on the cost of achievement and on the human tendency to deny that cost.

4. **How does the coal metaphor work in Lorde's "Coal"?**
   *Model answer:* Coal is black, buried, and compressed, yet it becomes diamond and yields light and heat; Lorde uses it to figure her Black self and her words as something valuable born from what the culture devalues. The odd grammar "I / is the total black" fuses self and substance, and the poem ends by offering her word "for jewel in the open light," claiming worth for Black speech.

5. **What recognition organizes "A Litany for Survival"?**
   *Model answer:* The poem recognizes that those "who were never meant to survive" live in constant fear whether they speak or stay silent, so silence offers no real safety. From that recognition it draws its imperative — "it is better to speak / remembering / we were never meant to survive" — choosing dangerous speech over a silence that protects no one.

6. **How does Clifton claim the body in "homage to my hips"?**
   *Model answer:* She celebrates her "big," "free" hips that refuse to be confined or "held back" and that "have never been enslaved," ending with their magical power to "put a spell on a man." The poem reclaims beauty, freedom, and power for a Black woman's body in defiance of a culture that denied it, turning self-celebration into a political act done with humor and swagger.

7. **What does "i made it up" mean in "won't you celebrate with me"?**
   *Model answer:* Born "in babylon / both nonwhite and woman" with "no model," the speaker had to invent her own selfhood from scratch, "between starshine and clay," with "one hand holding tight / the other hand." Self-creation without precedent is the poem's subject, and the act of making oneself up becomes the very thing worth celebrating.

8. **How do these three poets handle the relationship between identity and language?**
   *Model answer:* All three treat language as the instrument of self-claiming: Rich dives armed with a camera and knife to record and cut; Lorde turns the self into a word offered "for jewel in the open light"; Clifton makes herself up in plain, lower-case speech. For each, to write is to assert a self the official record omitted, so the poem is not description but act.

9. **What is gained by reading these three poets together?**
   *Model answer:* Together they show the range of second-wave self-claiming — Rich's allegorical descent and tragic cost, Lorde's intersectional naming of all her identities at once, Clifton's exuberant bodily joy — and they extend the course's threads of the contested "I," the body, and voice-against-silence into the contemporary moment. Reading them as a trio also honors the real solidarity among them, signaled by Rich, Walker, and Lorde sharing the 1974 award on behalf of all silenced women.

10. **How do these poems answer the silence and constraint the course began with?**
    *Model answer:* Where Bradstreet apologized and Dickinson hid her poems and Wheatley wrote under examination, these poets speak openly, claim the body, and name the self without apology. Lorde's "it is better to speak," Clifton's "something has tried to kill me / and has failed," and Rich's dive to restore the missing names all answer the earlier silences directly, completing the arc from constraint toward claimed voice.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The frank material — lesbian identity in Lorde and Rich, the unabashed celebration of the Black female body in Clifton — must be handled with adult composure and without either prurience or apology; teach the poems as poems, attending to image, grammar, and form, and the rest follows. For a male instructor leading this session, the key is to lead without speaking over the writers, and especially without explaining Black women's experience to the room as though from authority; let Lorde and Clifton define their own terms, quote them precisely, and ask rather than assert. Some students may treat these poets as slogans or as historical markers of a "movement"; resist that by honoring them as exacting artists — Rich's broken line, Lorde's strange grammar, Clifton's compression are craft, not mere statement. Where male-resident discomfort surfaces around "homage to my hips" or the lesbian content, name the poems' artistry and their joy, model unembarrassed seriousness, and keep the discussion on the work. The aim throughout is reverence for mastery, not the waving of a flag.

### Suggested Further Reading for the Instructor

- Adrienne Rich, *On Lies, Secrets, and Silence: Selected Prose 1966–1978* (W. W. Norton, 1979) — essential for understanding Rich's poetics and politics, including "When We Dead Awaken."
- Hilary Holladay, *The Power of Adrienne Rich: A Biography* (Nan A. Talese/Doubleday, 2020) — the major life.
- Audre Lorde, *Sister Outsider: Essays and Speeches* (Crossing Press, 1984) — includes "The Master's Tools Will Never Dismantle the Master's House" and "Poetry Is Not a Luxury."
- Alexis De Veaux, *Warrior Poet: A Biography of Audre Lorde* (W. W. Norton, 2004) — the authoritative biography.
- Lucille Clifton, *The Collected Poems of Lucille Clifton 1965–2010*, ed. Kevin Young and Michael S. Glaser (BOA Editions, 2012) — the complete poems with a fine introduction.
- Mary Jane Lupton, *Lucille Clifton: Her Life and Letters* (Praeger, 2006) — a useful critical biography.

### One Thing to Memorize

Clifton's close to "won't you celebrate with me": "come celebrate / with me that everyday / something has tried to kill me / and has failed." It is the course's whole arc — silence, constraint, and survival — compressed into four lines of plain, triumphant speech.

## Session 16 — Capstone: Toni Morrison, Tracy K. Smith, and Joy Harjo

### At a Glance

The capstone session gathers three contemporary masters to close the arc and to look forward: a short excerpt from Toni Morrison (the opening of *Beloved*), a poem by Tracy K. Smith (from the Pulitzer-winning *Life on Mars*), and Joy Harjo's "Remember." We read them not to "cover" the present but to ask what the whole tradition has been moving toward — the novelist who made Black women's interior lives the center of American fiction and won the Nobel Prize; the astrophysicist's daughter who turned cosmology into elegy and became Poet Laureate; the Muscogee poet and musician, the first Native American Poet Laureate, whose "Remember" is a litany of belonging. The session is designed to send students back across the whole course with the question: what does it mean, now, to have these voices fully present in the canon they were once kept out of? Approximate seminar time: one session, plus time for synthesis and final discussion.

### The Author(s): Biographical Sketches

**Toni Morrison (1931–2019)** was born Chloe Ardelia Wofford in Lorain, Ohio, into a working-class Black family rich in storytelling and song; she took the name Toni at Howard University, where she studied English before earning a master's at Cornell ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toni_Morrison)). She taught, married, had two sons, divorced, and in the late 1960s became the first Black woman to be a senior fiction editor at Random House, where she championed Black writers — among them Lucille Clifton — while writing her own first novel, *The Bluest Eye* (1970), at her kitchen table after her children were asleep ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toni_Morrison)). *Sula* (1973) and the National Book Critics Circle Award–winning *Song of Solomon* (1977) built her national reputation; *Beloved* (1987), her novel of slavery and its aftermath, won the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction in 1988. In 1993 she became the first Black woman to win the Nobel Prize in Literature, honored for fiction of "visionary force and poetic import." She continued to write and teach, latterly at Princeton, until her death in 2019. Morrison did more than any other writer to place Black women's interior lives at the center of American literature, and to insist — in her criticism as in her fiction — that the African American presence is constitutive of American writing, not marginal to it.

**Tracy K. Smith** (b. 1972) was born in Falmouth, Massachusetts, and raised in Northern California, the youngest of five children; her father was an engineer who worked on the Hubble Space Telescope, a detail that shapes her most celebrated book ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poet/tracy-k-smith)). She studied at Harvard, where she was part of the Dark Room Collective of young Black writers, and took an M.F.A. at Columbia. Her collections include *The Body's Question* (2003), *Duende* (2007), and *Life on Mars* (2011), an elegy for her father that fuses cosmology, science fiction, and David Bowie and won the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry in 2012. She later published the memoir *Ordinary Light* (2015), a National Book Award finalist. Smith served two terms as United States Poet Laureate (2017–2019), during which she traveled to rural communities to bring poetry to readers far from literary centers, and she has taught at Princeton and Harvard. Her work joins intellectual reach to deep feeling, and extends the elegy and the meditative lyric into the spaces of science and the cosmos.

**Joy Harjo** (b. 1951) was born in Tulsa, Oklahoma, and is an enrolled member of the Muscogee (Creek) Nation; she is a poet, saxophonist, and playwright whose work draws on Native histories, myth, music, and the landscapes of Oklahoma and the Southwest ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poet/joy-harjo)). She studied at the Institute of American Indian Arts and at the University of New Mexico, then took an M.F.A. at the Iowa Writers' Workshop. Her many collections include *She Had Some Horses* (1983), *In Mad Love and War* (1990), and *An American Sunrise* (2019); "Remember," among her most beloved poems, appears in *She Had Some Horses* ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poem/remember-0)). A musician who performs her poems with her band, Harjo weaves song and chant into her writing. In 2019 she was named United States Poet Laureate — the first Native American to hold the office — and served three terms. Her poetry insists on memory, kinship, and the living presence of the land and the ancestors, joining personal voice to communal and ceremonial speech.

### Historical & Cultural Context

This session stands at the end of the arc the course has traced and asks what the tradition has become. Morrison, Smith, and Harjo are not "emerging" or "marginal" writers appended to a settled canon; they are central figures — a Nobel laureate, two United States Poets Laureate — whose presence at the center of American letters represents the completion, within living memory, of a long movement from exclusion toward authority ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toni_Morrison); [Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poet/tracy-k-smith)). Where Bradstreet apologized for her sex and Wheatley wrote under examination, these writers occupy the highest official seats of the national literature.

The three also widen the frame the course has worked within. Morrison made the inner lives of Black women — their love, memory, and the wound of slavery — the explicit center of major American fiction, and argued in her criticism that this presence was always constitutive of the literature ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toni_Morrison)). Smith brings a Black woman's voice into the registers of science, cosmology, and elegy, claiming intellectual and metaphysical territory once coded as outside women's province. Harjo brings Native American memory, kinship, and ceremony into the lyric, reminding us that "American literature" includes the literatures of this continent's first peoples and that the recovery the course has charted is ongoing, not finished ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poet/joy-harjo)). Together they let the capstone do its proper work: to look back across Bradstreet, Wheatley, Dickinson, Chopin, Hurston, Rich, and the rest, and to see where their long labor of claiming voice has arrived.

### Summary of the Work(s)

**Toni Morrison, the opening of *Beloved*** (1987): The novel begins, famously, "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom." We are at 124 Bluestone Road, a house outside Cincinnati in 1873, haunted by the ghost of a baby. Sethe, a former slave who escaped from a Kentucky plantation called Sweet Home, lives there with her daughter Denver; her two sons have already fled the house's hauntings, and Baby Suggs, Sethe's mother-in-law, has died. The spectral presence is the ghost of Sethe's dead infant daughter, whose gravestone bears only the word "Beloved." The opening pages establish the haunted house, the isolation of the women, and the weight of an unspeakable past — the reader does not yet know that Sethe killed her own child rather than let her be returned to slavery. Morrison's narration moves in fragments, withholding the full story and immersing us at once in grief, memory, and the supernatural reality of a trauma that will not stay buried ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toni_Morrison)). (Assign one to three pages of the opening; the excerpt is meant to give students Morrison's voice and method, not the whole novel.)

**Tracy K. Smith, a poem from *Life on Mars*** (2011): The collection is an elegy for Smith's father, an engineer who worked on the Hubble Space Telescope, and it sets private grief against the vastness of the cosmos. In poems such as the title sequence and "The Universe as Primal Scream," Smith moves between the intimate and the astronomical — using black holes, deep space, David Bowie, and science fiction as figures for loss, mystery, and the longing to know what lies beyond ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poet/tracy-k-smith)). The book's recurring gesture is to look up, through her father's telescopes, into a universe at once indifferent and consoling, and to ask whether the dead, like distant light, persist. For class, assign "The Universe as Primal Scream," freely available in full at the Poetry Foundation, and read it whole — a poem that hears, in the noise of a Sunday-afternoon apartment building, a longing for a God who may or may not answer.)

**Joy Harjo, "Remember"** (1983): The poem is a litany built on the repeated imperative "Remember." It instructs the listener to remember the sky and the stars under which they were born, the moon, the sun's birth at dawn, sundown and night; to remember their own birth and their mother's struggle to give them life; to remember their father, their relatives, the earth whose skin we are, the plants, trees, and animals with their own stories, the wind, the languages, and that all things are alive and connected — that "all is in motion, is growing, is you." The poem gathers personal, familial, natural, and cosmic memory into a single chant of belonging, ending with the recognition that to remember is to know oneself woven into all of creation ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poem/remember-0)).

### The Text(s) for This Session

All three are under copyright; use authoritative editions and assign brief, focused excerpts.

- **Toni Morrison, *Beloved*** — assign only the opening pages of Part One; the novel is freely accessible (free Internet Archive account, one-hour loans) at the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/beloved0000morr). Morrison's free, estate-authorized [Nobel Lecture (1993)](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/literature/1993/morrison/lecture/) makes an excellent free companion text on language and storytelling.
- **Tracy K. Smith, "The Universe as Primal Scream"** (from *Life on Mars*, Graywolf Press, 2011) — full text freely available at the [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/55518/the-universe-as-primal-scream); Smith also reads the poem aloud, free, at [poets.org](https://poets.org/tracy-k-smith-reads-universe-primal-scream).
- **Joy Harjo, "Remember,"** in *She Had Some Horses* (Thunder's Mouth Press, 1983; reissued W. W. Norton, 2008) — full text at the [Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poem/remember-0).
- For Morrison's criticism, pair with *Playing in the Dark: Whiteness and the Literary Imagination* (Harvard University Press, 1992), her argument that the Black presence is constitutive of American literature.

### Lecture Script

We have come a long way. [pause] We began with Anne Bradstreet apologizing for daring to hold a pen, asking the world not to scorn a woman's "single thread." We are ending with a Nobel laureate and two Poets Laureate of the United States. I want you to feel the distance we have traveled today — not as progress exactly, because these writers would distrust that word, but as arrival. The voices that the canon kept at its edge are now standing at its center. [pause]

Begin with Morrison, and with one of the most famous opening lines in American fiction: "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom." [pause] Notice what she does. No throat-clearing, no scene-setting. A house, named only by a number, is alive and angry. A baby is dead and full of venom. We are inside the haunting before we know there is anything to be haunted by. This is the opposite of Bradstreet's apology. Morrison assumes her authority absolutely. She does not explain the Black women in this house to a white reader; she puts us inside their reality and makes us learn it. [pause] And what is the reality? A mother, Sethe, who escaped slavery, and a house haunted by the daughter she lost — a loss so terrible we will spend the novel learning that she caused it herself, killing her child rather than let her be taken back into bondage. Morrison took the most unspeakable wound in American history and made it the center of a great novel, told from inside the woman who bears it. Think of how far that is from Stowe, who wrote about slavery from the outside, for a white audience, in the language of sentiment. Morrison writes from within, in language that withholds and haunts and trusts us to bear it. [pause]

Then Tracy K. Smith, who points us at the stars. [pause] Her father helped build the Hubble Space Telescope, the instrument that let us see to the edge of the visible universe. And when he died, she wrote *Life on Mars* — an elegy that grieves a single man against the scale of deep space, black holes, the cosmos. A Black woman poet claiming the language of physics and the infinite as her own ground for mourning. [pause] Remember how we said women were so long confined to the domestic, the small, the indoor? Smith takes the whole universe as her subject and her father's death as her occasion, and she fuses them. The telescope her father built becomes the instrument of her grief and her wonder. She looks up and asks whether the dead, like the light of distant stars, go on reaching us long after their source is gone. [pause]

And finally Joy Harjo, and the poem I want to leave ringing in your ears. "Remember." [pause] It is a litany — that ancient form, the chant, the prayer. Remember the sky you were born under. Remember the moon. Remember your mother's struggle to give you life. Remember the earth whose skin you are. Remember the plants, the trees, the animals, each with their own stories. Remember that all things are alive, and that "all is in motion, is growing, is you." [pause] Harjo is the first Native American Poet Laureate of this country, and she reminds us that the literature of this continent did not begin with the English settlers — that there were voices and songs here long before Bradstreet's ship, and that they are still speaking. Her poem is not about the lonely lyric "I" we have tracked all term. It is about the "I" woven into everything — into family, land, ancestors, the wind. [pause]

So look at the three of them together, and look back across the whole course. Bradstreet's single thread. Wheatley examined for proof she could write. Dickinson hiding her poems in a drawer. Chopin's reputation ruined by *The Awakening*. And here, at the end, Morrison, Smith, Harjo — at the center of the national literature, claiming the wound of slavery, the reach of the cosmos, the memory of the first peoples, with complete authority and no apology. [pause] That is the arc. Not a story of simple triumph — these writers know too much for that — but a long, hard, magnificent claiming of voice. And the last word, I think, should be Harjo's. Remember. Remember the women who came before, whose long labor made these voices possible. Remember that the work of recovery is not finished. Remember that all is in motion, is growing — is you. [pause] That is where we end.

### Close-Reading Notes on Key Passages

**1. "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom."** Morrison's opening refuses all preamble: a house, named by number, is given malign life, and a dead baby is given venom, before any explanation arrives ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toni_Morrison)). The technique — immersion, withholding, the supernatural treated as fact — assumes the reader will work, and it places us inside a Black woman's haunted reality without translation. Contrast with Stowe's explanatory, sentimental address to a white audience to measure the distance the tradition has traveled.

**2. The gravestone marked "Beloved."** The single carved word, the only thing Sethe could afford to have engraved, gives the novel and the ghost their name. It compresses love, loss, and the unspeakable act into one word, and models Morrison's method of letting an enormous history hang on a small, concrete detail. Let students feel how much is withheld and how the withholding generates the book's dread.

**3. Smith's cosmic scale against private grief (*Life on Mars*).** Smith repeatedly sets the death of one man — her father — against black holes, deep space, and the Hubble's reach, so that the elegy's intimacy is magnified rather than dwarfed by the cosmic frame ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poet/tracy-k-smith)). The move claims for a contemporary Black woman poet the full intellectual range of science and metaphysics. Ask how grief and wonder reinforce each other in her images of light still traveling from extinguished stars.

**4. The anaphora of "Remember" (Harjo).** The poem's power is formal: the repeated imperative builds a chant, a ceremonial speech-act rather than a private confession ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poem/remember-0)). Each "remember" widens the circle — body, mother, father, earth, plants, animals, wind, language — until the self is dissolved into relation. Read it aloud to let students hear the litany do its gathering work.

**5. "all is in motion, is growing, is you" (Harjo).** The poem's close collapses the boundary between self and world: to remember fully is to know oneself continuous with all creation ([Academy of American Poets](https://poets.org/poem/remember-0)). Set against the isolated, contested "I" the course has tracked from Dickinson through Rich, Harjo's communal, ecological self offers a different model of identity entirely — a fitting note on which to end.

### Discussion Questions With Model Answers

1. **How does Morrison's opening differ from the way slavery was written earlier in the course, as in Stowe?**
   *Model answer:* Morrison plunges us inside a Black woman's haunted reality with no explanation — "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom" — assuming authority and trusting the reader to learn the world, whereas Stowe wrote about slavery from the outside, for a white audience, in the consoling language of sentiment. The contrast measures the tradition's movement from speaking about Black women to speaking from within their interior lives.

2. **What is the effect of Morrison withholding the central act?**
   *Model answer:* By revealing only gradually that Sethe killed her own child to save her from slavery, Morrison generates dread and forces the reader to inhabit grief and haunting before understanding their cause. The withholding makes the unspeakable feel unspeakable, and it respects the trauma by refusing to deliver it as plain information.

3. **How does Tracy K. Smith use the cosmos in her elegy?**
   *Model answer:* Drawing on her father's work on the Hubble Telescope, Smith sets his death against black holes, deep space, and the scale of the universe, so that private grief is magnified rather than diminished. The cosmic frame lets her ask whether the dead, like light from extinguished stars, continue to reach us, fusing mourning with metaphysical wonder.

4. **What does it mean that a Black woman poet claims the language of science and the cosmos?**
   *Model answer:* It extends the territory available to women's poetry far beyond the domestic and the personal spaces to which women were long confined, claiming physics, astronomy, and the infinite as fit ground for a woman's meditation. Smith thereby answers one of the course's recurring constraints — the smallness women were assigned — by taking the whole universe as her subject.

5. **What form does Harjo's "Remember" take, and why does it matter?**
   *Model answer:* It is a litany built on the anaphora of "Remember," a chant or prayer rather than a private confession, rooted in ceremonial and communal speech. The form matters because it enacts the poem's meaning: identity is not the isolated lyric "I" but the self woven into family, land, ancestors, and all living things.

6. **How does Harjo's model of the self differ from the lyric "I" the course has tracked?**
   *Model answer:* Where Dickinson, Rich, and others wrestled with a singular, often isolated and contested first person, Harjo dissolves the self into relation — "all is in motion, is growing, is you." Her communal, ecological self offers an alternative to Western individualism and reminds us that the literatures of this continent's first peoples carry different conceptions of identity.

7. **Why end the course with these three writers in particular?**
   *Model answer:* A Nobel laureate and two United States Poets Laureate, they stand at the center of the national literature rather than at its margins, embodying the arrival the whole course has traced from Bradstreet's apology toward fully claimed authority. They also widen the frame to Black women's interior lives, the cosmos, and Native memory, showing that the tradition is plural and the recovery ongoing.

8. **What does it mean to call this a "completion" of an arc rather than simple progress?**
   *Model answer:* These writers would distrust a tidy story of progress, knowing how much wound and loss the history contains; "completion" names instead the fact that voices once excluded now hold the highest seats of the literature. The arc is real — from single thread to laureate — but it is a hard, costly claiming of voice, not an untroubled ascent.

9. **How do Morrison, Smith, and Harjo each answer the silences the course began with?**
   *Model answer:* Morrison gives full interior voice to the Black woman's experience that earlier literature spoke about from outside; Smith claims intellectual and cosmic registers denied to women's poetry; Harjo restores Native voice and a communal self to "American literature." Each occupies, without apology, ground that Bradstreet, Wheatley, and Dickinson could approach only under constraint.

10. **What is the right note on which to leave students at the end of the course?**
    *Model answer:* Harjo's imperative — "Remember... all is in motion, is growing, is you" — gathers the whole tradition into an act of memory and belonging, asking students to remember the women whose labor made these voices possible and that the work of recovery continues. It leaves the course not on triumph but on living connection and responsibility, which is the truest place to end.

### Anticipated Difficulties

The chief difficulty is scale: three major writers and the weight of the whole course in a single session, so resist trying to "teach" *Beloved* or *Life on Mars* entire — use short, well-chosen excerpts to give students each writer's voice and let the synthesis, not coverage, be the goal. The material is grave — slavery and infanticide in Morrison, a father's death in Smith — and must be handled with adult composure and without melodrama; let Morrison's withholding teach the room how to sit with the unspeakable. For a male instructor closing a course on women writers, the live note is humility: lead the synthesis without summarizing the writers' experience as though from authority, and especially, with Morrison and Harjo, do not explain Black or Native experience to the room — quote precisely, ask, and let the texts speak. Honor all three as full masters at the center of the literature, never as a diversity coda; the point of the capstone is that these voices are central, and the course has been about how they came to be heard.

## Glossary for the Instructor

A working vocabulary for the course. These are the terms I reach for most often at the board; each is given in a form you can say aloud to students without a footnote.

**Cult of domesticity.** The nineteenth-century middle-class ideology — also called the "doctrine of separate spheres" — that assigned women a private, domestic realm of piety, purity, and homemaking while men occupied the public world. It is the cultural air Stowe, Alcott, and Chopin breathe and, often, push against; understanding it is essential to seeing what their heroines are escaping or refusing.

**Domestic novel.** A genre of nineteenth-century fiction centered on the home, family, marriage, and women's daily moral life, often written by and for women. Frequently dismissed as minor, the form was in fact a serious instrument for examining women's constrained choices, and the course treats it as literature rather than as period decoration.

**Sentimental tradition.** A mode of writing — dominant in mid-nineteenth-century America — that aimed to move readers to feeling and moral action through pathos, especially scenes of suffering, family, and death. *Uncle Tom's Cabin* is its towering example; the tradition was politically powerful and later disparaged, and part of our work is to weigh both its force and its limits.

**Bluestocking.** An eighteenth- and nineteenth-century term, originally neutral and later mocking, for an intellectual or literary woman. The word carries the long cultural suspicion of female learning that Bradstreet and Wheatley faced directly and that echoes through the whole course.

**New Woman.** A term from the 1890s–1910s for the educated, independent, often unmarried or unconventional woman who claimed work, mobility, and sometimes sexual autonomy. Chopin's Edna Pontellier, Wharton's divorced Alice, and Cather's own life all touch this figure; she marks the turn from the domestic ideal toward modern womanhood.

**Female bildungsroman.** A novel (or story) of a woman's formation and coming-of-age — her growth toward selfhood — distinguished by the way social constraint so often blocks or complicates the heroine's development. Where the classic bildungsroman ends in the hero's arrival in the world, its female version frequently ends in marriage, retreat, or death, and that difference is itself the subject.

**Regionalism / local color.** A late-nineteenth-century mode devoted to the precise rendering of a particular place — its landscape, dialect, customs, and types. Jewett's Maine is its finest American example; the course treats regionalism not as quaint miniature but as a serious art of attention, and notes how often women writers were confined to it by critics.

**Naturalism.** A turn-of-the-century mode, descended from realism, that shows human beings determined by heredity, environment, and economic and biological force, often moving toward defeat. Chopin and Wharton borrow its pressures; the female characters' "fate" in these works is frequently a naturalist machinery of money, marriage, and the body.

**Confessional school.** A mid-twentieth-century poetic movement (Lowell, Plath, Sexton, and others) that made private anguish — mental illness, family, the body, sexuality — the explicit material of the poem. The course defines it chiefly to mark its opposite: the reticence of Moore and Bishop, who imply feeling rather than confess it.

**Second-wave feminism.** The feminist movement of roughly the 1960s through the early 1980s, concerned with equality, the body, sexuality, work, and language, and with recovering women's silenced history. It is the direct context for Rich, Lorde, and Clifton, and the moment that produced much of the scholarship this course relies on.

**Écriture féminine.** Literally "women's writing"; a concept from French feminist theory (Cixous, Irigaray) proposing a mode of writing rooted in the female body and outside patriarchal logic. The course uses the term lightly and with caution, mainly to give students a name for the idea that form itself can be gendered.

**The gaze.** The act of looking understood as a relation of power — most often, in feminist criticism, the "male gaze" that makes woman the object of sight rather than the seeing subject. Useful for Chopin's Edna, Wharton's much-observed Alice, and Bishop's deliberate reversals of who looks at whom.

**Gynocriticism.** Elaine Showalter's term for a criticism focused on women as writers — on the history, themes, genres, and traditions of women's own literature, rather than on women as represented by men. It names, in effect, the project of this entire course.

**Recovery project.** The scholarly and editorial effort, especially from the 1970s onward, to restore to print and to the canon women writers whom literary history had neglected or forgotten. Olsen's reprintings, the Feminist Press, and Alice Walker's recovery of Hurston are central examples; the course is itself an act of recovery.

**Female grotesque.** The deployment of the distorted, excessive, monstrous, or unruly female body and self — in revolt against the ideal of the contained, decorous woman. Useful for O'Connor's Mary Grace and Mrs. Turpin, and for thinking about how women writers weaponize the unbeautiful.

**Madwoman in the attic.** From Gilbert and Gubar's landmark study; the figure of the confined, raging woman (originally Bertha Mason in *Jane Eyre*) read as the buried, furious double of the "proper" heroine and of the woman writer herself. A key to the anger beneath many decorous surfaces in this course.

**Voice.** In this course, the achieved, recognizable presence of a writer or speaker on the page — and, more pointedly, the hard-won right to speak at all. The arc from Bradstreet's apology to Clifton's celebration is, above all, the claiming of voice.

**Persona.** The constructed speaker of a poem or story, distinct from the author. Dickinson's "I," Wheatley's pious public voice, and Rich's diver are all personae; teaching the distinction protects students from reading every "I" as simple autobiography.

**Woman of letters.** A woman who makes her life and living in literature across genres — poetry, fiction, essays, editing, criticism. Wharton, O'Connor, Rich, and Morrison all qualify; the term dignifies the full professional identity these writers achieved.

**Salonnière.** The (usually French) woman who hosted and presided over a literary or intellectual salon, exercising real cultural power through conversation and patronage. The course uses it to remind students that women shaped literary life even when barred from authorship.

**The anonymous (Woolf's).** Virginia Woolf's claim, in *A Room of One's Own*, that "Anonymous was a woman" — the proposition that much unsigned work, and much silenced talent, was female. A touchstone for the whole problem of women's erased authorship.

**The cover / the pseudonym.** The use of a false or male name, or of anonymity, to publish — a recurring survival strategy for women writers ("Currer Bell," "George Eliot"). The course notes how the cover both enabled publication and registered the cost of being a woman in print.

**The closet.** The condition of concealed identity, especially sexual identity, and the act of "coming out" of it. Relevant to Bishop's guarded reticence and to Lorde's and Rich's deliberate openness; the term lets students discuss what is hidden, half-said, or declared in a writer's work and life.

**The canon.** The body of works treated as the essential, authoritative tradition of a literature — what gets taught, anthologized, and preserved. The course's premise is that the canon long underrepresented women, and that these writers belong at its center.

**The counter-canon.** An alternative or oppositional canon assembled to include what the dominant canon excluded — here, a tradition of American women writers. The course is, finally, an argument that there is no need for a separate counter-canon: these writers *are* the canon, fully and without apology.

## Essential Further Reading for the Instructor

*A note on access: the per-session "Suggested Further Reading" lists above are for your own preparation and include scholarly books best found through a library. The student **readings**, by contrast — every primary text assigned in the course — are all freely accessible online; see the "All Course Readings" note immediately below.*

### All Course Readings — Free-Access Confirmation

**All readings in this course are freely accessible online without purchase or library card. Links verified.** Public-domain texts (Bradstreet, Wheatley, Dickinson, Stowe, Alcott, Chopin, Jewett, Wharton, Cather, Hurston's "How It Feels to Be Colored Me" and "Sweat") are linked to Project Gutenberg, Wikisource, the Academy of American Poets, the Poetry Foundation, the Internet Archive, americanliterature.com, and university open-text pages. Copyrighted poems (Moore, Bishop, Rich, Lorde, Clifton, Harjo, Tracy K. Smith) are linked to the Poetry Foundation and poets.org, which host them with permission. Copyrighted prose freely hosted online (Olsen, Paley, O'Connor's "Parker's Back") is linked to legitimate free sources, including university course pages. **The O'Connor selections from *Mystery and Manners* and *The Habit of Being*, the O'Connor stories in *Everything That Rises Must Converge*, Hurston's *Their Eyes Were Watching God*, and Morrison's *Beloved* require a free Internet Archive account, which permits anyone to borrow each book at no cost (one-hour loans, renewable); see archive.org.** No student needs to buy a book, photocopy a handout, or visit a library to complete any reading in this course.

### General — On Women Writers and the American Tradition

- Elaine Showalter, *A Jury of Her Peers: American Women Writers from Anne Bradstreet to Annie Proulx* (Alfred A. Knopf, 2009) — the single most useful one-volume history for this course; humane, readable, and comprehensive.
- Sandra M. Gilbert and Susan Gubar, *The Madwoman in the Attic: The Woman Writer and the Nineteenth-Century Literary Imagination*, 2nd ed. (Yale University Press, 2000) — the foundational study of female authorship, anger, and concealment.
- Wendy Martin, *An American Triptych: Anne Bradstreet, Emily Dickinson, Adrienne Rich* (University of North Carolina Press, 1984) — traces a single female poetic lineage across three centuries; ideal for framing the course's through-line.
- Virginia Woolf, *A Room of One's Own* (1929; widely available, including free at [Project Gutenberg Canada](https://gutenberg.ca/ebooks/woolfv-aroomofonesown/woolfv-aroomofonesown-00-h.html)) — still the indispensable essay on women, writing, and the material conditions of art.
- Cheryl A. Wall, *Women of the Harlem Renaissance* (Indiana University Press, 1995) — essential context for Hurston and for the Black women writers of the period.
- Sandra M. Gilbert and Susan Gubar, eds., *The Norton Anthology of Literature by Women: The Traditions in English* (W. W. Norton, multiple editions) — a comprehensive teaching anthology and reference.

### Author- and Topic-Specific

- Charlotte Gordon, *Mistress Bradstreet: The Untold Life of America's First Poet* (Little, Brown, 2005) — a lively, scholarly life of Bradstreet.
- Vincent Carretta, *Phillis Wheatley: Biography of a Genius in Bondage* (University of Georgia Press, 2011) — the authoritative Wheatley biography.
- Helen Vendler, *Dickinson: Selected Poems and Commentaries* (Harvard University Press, 2010) — the best close-reading companion to Dickinson.
- Joan D. Hedrick, *Harriet Beecher Stowe: A Life* (Oxford University Press, 1994) — Pulitzer Prize–winning biography.
- Hermione Lee, *Edith Wharton* (Alfred A. Knopf, 2007) and *Willa Cather: Double Lives* (Pantheon, 1989) — superb lives of both novelists by a master biographer.
- Valerie Boyd, *Wrapped in Rainbows: The Life of Zora Neale Hurston* (Scribner, 2003) — the standard Hurston biography.
- Brad Gooch, *Flannery: A Life of Flannery O'Connor* (Little, Brown, 2009), with O'Connor's own *Mystery and Manners* (FSG, 1969) and *The Habit of Being* (FSG, 1979) — the life and the indispensable self-portrait in essays and letters.
- Hilary Holladay, *The Power of Adrienne Rich: A Biography* (Nan A. Talese/Doubleday, 2020) and Alexis De Veaux, *Warrior Poet: A Biography of Audre Lorde* (W. W. Norton, 2004) — authoritative lives of two central second-wave poets.

## Notes for Future Iterations

A few honest notes to my future self, for the next time I teach this course.

The substitutions this time around — O'Connor's "Parker's Back" and "Revelation" in place of the over-taught "A Good Man Is Hard to Find," and the fresh Dickinson and Chopin selections — worked well and should stay; they keep the material from overlapping with the *Art of Telling* seminar and let students meet these writers somewhere other than the anthology's worn first page. I am glad O'Connor is back; pairing the two stories with her own essays and letters is the richest hour in the term, and I would protect that time.

The free-access mandate proved a clarification rather than a constraint, and the course is fairer for it. Next time, verify each link again before the term begins — web pages move — and walk students through creating a free Internet Archive account in the first week, since several of our richest texts depend on it.

If the calendar allows, I would like to add a session on Gwendolyn Brooks, whose absence I feel, and perhaps to give Hurston a full two sessions rather than one. And I should keep listening: the discussion questions that land best are the ones that trust students to do the close reading themselves, and I should write more of those and lecture a little less.

*End of the Instructor Companion.*
